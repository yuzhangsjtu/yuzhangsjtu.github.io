> This study uses cross-national data from over 150 countries between 1960 and 2019 to systematically examine, for the first time, how governments’ actual **compliance with constitutions** affects economic performance. The results show that **non-compliance significantly lowers per capita GDP**, especially in the areas of **property rights and the rule of law**; **investment and foreign capital inflows** are the main mechanisms through which this occurs. How a constitution is enforced matters more than the text itself.

**Title:** Economic effects of (non-)compliance with constitutions

**Journal:** *Journal of Comparative Economics*

**Authors:**

- <u>Anna Lewczuk</u>: Assistant Professor at University of Warsaw.
- <u>Katarzyna Metelska-Szaniawska</u>: Professor of Political Economy, Faculty of Economic Sciences, University of Warsaw.

## Introduction

#### **The rise of constitutional compliance research**

Over the past few decades, constitutional economics has made considerable progress, and a growing body of research shows that **constitutional frameworks** play a key role in state governance and economic development. For example, different types of constitutional design—such as a presidential versus a parliamentary system, or how electoral rules are structured—are closely linked to economic growth, policy-making, and public spending. However, as countries around the world have witnessed marked instances of “unconstitutional” behavior or a failure to implement constitutional provisions, the **“gap between constitutional texts (de jure) and actual enforcement (de facto)”** has become increasingly prominent. In other words, while some governments or top decision-makers promise various political, economic, and social rights in their constitutions, they do not abide by those commitments in practice.

This phenomenon of **“non-compliance with the constitution”** has drawn particularly keen attention in recent years. Some describe these cases as **“sham constitutions”** (texts filled with written clauses that serve merely as empty words), while others define them as “constitutional underperformance” (where actual constitutional safeguards fall below the levels promised in the text). Voigt (2021) introduced a more systematic concept: the **de jure–de facto gap**, i.e., the degree of deviation between government actions and constitutional provisions. As such deviations appear across multiple countries and repeatedly involve blatant or covert violations of core areas—**property rights**, the **rule of law**, **judicial independence**, and **human rights**—scholars have begun to re-examine the real extent of constitutional enforcement.

#### **Gaps and aims**

The previous literature has largely focused on **what factors** lead governments to violate constitutions, including:

- Whether the constitutional text itself provides sufficiently strict or effective sanctions;
- Whether environmental factors such as a country’s political culture, social conflicts, or level of economic development influence government compliance;
- Whether external “watchdogs” like the public, courts, or international organizations are able to curb government misconduct.

By contrast, fewer studies **directly explore how “unconstitutional actions affect the economy itself.”** Constitutional economics proposes that constitutions provide **credible commitment** and **reduce uncertainty**; once a government breaches the constitution, it undermines public trust in national institutions, raising political and policy risks, which in turn can potentially damage the investment climate and long-term growth.

Yet until now, these relationships have lacked systematic quantitative analysis and cross-national evidence. Two main reasons account for this gap: first, the **absence of data measuring government compliance in practice**, and second, the difficulty of **identifying causality**: why do governments choose to breach the constitution? Is the relationship between constitutional violations and economic performance one of causation, or do they merely influence each other?

Addressing this research gap, this paper sets out the following questions:

- Is there a systematic link between the **degree to which governments abide by constitutional texts** and a **country’s per capita GDP**?
- If such a relationship exists, which **areas of the constitution**—such as property rights / the rule of law, political rights, civil liberties, or basic human rights—matter most for economic development?
- Does the compliance effect vary across different **types of countries**—distinguished by democratic / authoritarian systems or income levels (high, middle, low)?

The authors argue that when a government frequently violates the constitution, that constitution loses its role in providing **long-term commitment** and institutional coordination, triggering adverse economic consequences. Specifically, they hypothesize:

**Higher constitutional compliance—a narrower de jure–de facto gap—is significantly and positively correlated with higher per capita GDP.**

#### **Feasibility**

The study became feasible thanks chiefly to the release of the **Comparative Constitutional Compliance Database (CCCD)**. This database:

- Supplies country-year information starting from 1900 on the congruence between constitutional texts and actual enforcement for a large portion of the world’s countries;
- By matching constitutional provisions—such as property rights, electoral rights, and freedom from torture—with countries’ actual enforcement, it provides quantitative insight into whether governments truly implement what the constitution mandates.

Using this dataset, the authors can work within a large cross-national panel of **over 150 countries spanning 1960–2019** and apply dynamic panel regressions, including both fixed effects and methods such as GMM, to gauge the impact of constitutional compliance on economic performance—proxied by per capita GDP.

## Literature background

#### **Research trajectories in constitutional economics**

Since Persson and Tabellini (2000, 2003) made a path-breaking investigation into the economic effects of explicit constitutional rules (such as electoral systems and the separation of powers), the academic community has been **framing conceptual advances or policy deliberations and systematically studying** how constitutions shape governmental decisions and thus shape key economic variables such as **economic growth**, **public spending**, and **macroeconomic stability**.

- The intellectual roots here trace back to **James Buchanan**’s constitutional economics; together with Tullock (1962), he argued that the constitution is a key institutional device to constrain government power and restrain “predatory politicians” or “myopic rulers,” a vision captured in their theory of public choice and constitutions.
- In turn, numerous studies have verified that constitutions serve as a safeguard against the **time-inconsistency problem** in policy-making: for example, a constitution that pushes the government to honor its previous commitments can, in principle, heighten the investment appeal and innovative potential of the economy by countering this inconsistency. If a constitution delivers a responsive fiscal framework, the claim ultimately predicts new evidence linking adherence-oriented setups alongside development.
