> Using cross-national data from over 150 countries between 1960 and 2019, this study is the first to systematically examine how governments' actual **compliance with constitutions** affects **economic performance**. The results show that **non-compliance significantly lowers per capita GDP**, with the domains of **property rights and the rule of law** being most critical; **investment and foreign capital flows** are the main mechanisms. How a constitution is enforced matters more than the text itself.

**Title:** Economic effects of (non-)compliance with constitutions

**Journal:** *Journal of Comparative Economics*

**Authors:**

- <u>Anna Lewczuk</u>: Assistant Professor at University of Warsaw.
- <u>Katarzyna Metelska-Szaniawska</u>: Faculty of Economic Sciences, University of Warsaw; Professor of Political Economy. Faculty of Economic Sciences, University of Warsaw.

**Published online:** 31 December 2024

## Introduction

#### **The Rise of the Constitutional Compliance Problem**

Over the past few decades, constitutional economics has made considerable progress, and a growing body of research shows that **constitutional frameworks** play a key role in state governance and economic development. For example, different types of constitutional design (presidential versus parliamentary systems, how electoral rules are arranged, and so on) are highly correlated with economic growth, policy-making, public spending, and other dimensions. However, as some countries around the world have exhibited clear "unconstitutional" behavior or have failed to implement constitutional provisions, the **"gap between the constitutional text (de jure) and its actual enforcement (de facto)"** has become increasingly prominent. In other words, although the governments or top decision-makers of certain countries have promised various political, economic, and social rights in their constitutions, they do not abide by those commitments in actual governance.

This phenomenon of **"non-compliance with the constitution"** has drawn particular attention in recent years. Some have summarized these cases as **"sham constitutions"** (texts in which various clauses are written on paper but remain a dead letter), while other researchers define them as "constitutional underperformance" (where the actual constitutional guarantees fall below the levels promised in the text). Voigt (2021) offers a more systematized concept: the **de jure-de facto gap**, that is, the degree of deviation between government behavior and constitutional provisions. When such deviations appear across multiple countries and repeatedly involve "blatant" or "covert" violations of core content such as **property rights**, the **rule of law**, **judicial independence**, and **human rights**, scholars have begun to re-examine the true extent of constitutional enforcement.

#### **Gaps and aims**

The previous literature has mostly focused on **what factors** lead governments to choose to violate the constitution. For example:

- Does the constitutional text itself establish sufficiently strict or effective sanction mechanisms?
- Do environmental factors such as a country's political culture, social conflict, and level of economic development affect government compliance?
- Can external "watchdogs" such as the public, the courts, or international organizations constrain government violations?

By contrast, fewer studies **directly explore "what impact unconstitutional behavior has on the economy itself."** Constitutional economics holds that constitutions can perform the functions of **credible commitment** and **reducing uncertainty**; once a government breaches the constitution, it undermines society's trust in national institutions, raises political and policy risks, and thereby potentially damages the investment climate and long-term growth.

However, these mechanisms have previously lacked systematic quantitative analysis and cross-national evidence. The reasons for this past lack of research are twofold: first, the **absence of data measuring governments' actual compliance**, and second, the difficulty of **causal identification**: why do governments violate the constitution? Is the relationship between constitutional violations and economic performance one of causation, or do the two merely influence each other?

Addressing this research gap, this paper explicitly poses:

- Is there a systematic association between **"the degree to which a government complies with the constitutional text"** and **"a country's per capita GDP"**?
- If such a relationship does exist, which **constitutional domains** (such as property rights / the rule of law, political rights, civil liberties, or basic human rights) most significantly affect economic development?
- Does the economic effect of compliance differ across different **types of countries** (distinguished by democratic / authoritarian regimes, or by high/middle/low income levels)?

The authors argue that when a government frequently violates the constitution, the constitution struggles to perform its functions of **long-term commitment** and **institutional coordination**, thereby producing negative economic consequences. Specifically, the authors hypothesize:

**Higher constitutional compliance (i.e., a smaller de jure-de facto gap) is significantly and positively correlated with higher per capita GDP.**

#### **Feasibility**

This study was able to be carried out largely thanks to the recently released **Comparative Constitutional Compliance Database** (CCCD). This database:

- Provides year-level information on the "degree of match between the constitutional text and actual enforcement" for most countries worldwide since 1900;
- By combining constitutional provisions (such as property rights, electoral rights, freedom from torture, etc.) with each country's actual enforcement, it makes it possible to quantitatively observe whether a government "has truly implemented what the constitution mandates."

Based on this dataset, the authors are able to test the impact of "constitutional compliance" on economic performance (per capita GDP) in a large cross-national sample covering **more than 150 countries over 1960–2019**, using **dynamic panel regressions (including fixed-effects and GMM instrumental methods, among others)**.

## Literature Background

#### **The Research Lineage of Constitutional Economics**

Ever since Persson and Tabellini (2000, 2003) made pioneering investigations into the economic effects of specific constitutional rules (such as electoral systems and the separation of powers), the academic community has **systematically studied** at multiple levels how constitutions shape government decisions and thereby affect key economic variables such as **economic growth**, **public spending**, and **macroeconomic stability**.

- The intellectual origins of this research can be traced back to the constitutional economics championed by **James Buchanan**, who, together with Tullock (1962), put forward the theory of "public choice and the constitution," viewing the constitution as a key institutional means of constraining government power and restraining "predatory politicians" or "myopic politicians."
- Furthermore, many studies have also verified the role of constitutions in preventing the **time-inconsistency** problem; for example, if a constitution can guarantee that the government honors its "prior commitments," it can provide market participants with more stable expectations and strengthen consistent confidence in economic policy.

Within this lineage, scholars have extensively discussed how **different constitutional clauses or constitutional designs** (for example, presidential vs. parliamentary systems, proportional representation vs. majoritarian electoral systems, etc.) affect economic policy and social welfare. But this literature has often **focused only on the "constitutional text"** (de jure), that is, how the provisions are written on paper, while paying less attention to their **actual degree of enforcement** (de facto).

#### **From "Constitutional Text" to "Constitutional Compliance"**

Empirical research shows that **"the constitutional text" and "actual constitutional enforcement" are not always consistent.** Earlier research on areas such as judicial independence and central bank independence had already revealed that "the independence written into the law or constitution" and "whether genuine independence can be achieved in practice" are often two different things. In many cases, governments or politicians evade, weaken, or openly violate legal and constitutional provisions in actual practice.

Echoing this, in recent years a body of literature has emerged that focuses on "**the degree to which governments respect (or do not respect) the constitution**" and its consequences, for example:

- Law and Versteeg (2013): First proposed the concepts of "**sham constitutions**" and "**constitutional underperformance**," and attempted to quantify the gap between "the freedoms and rights written into the constitution" and "the extent to which a country actually respects those rights."
- Chilton and Versteeg (2020): Comparing the constitutional provisions for "individual rights" versus "organizational rights," they argued that the latter are more easily enforced because of "the self-protection of interest groups or social organizations," resulting in a smaller "de jure-de facto gap."
- Voigt (2021): Proposed an approach for the systematic study of "constitutional (non-)compliance," defining the degree of constitutional compliance as "**the degree of congruence between constitutional provisions and the behavior of each country's government in practice**." He further explored aspects such as the **institutional design, environmental factors, and external oversight** that affect constitutional compliance.

**In short**, research in constitutional economics has in recent years begun to turn toward the key question of "**whether governments actually comply with the constitution**"—what this paper calls "(non-)compliance"—and has attempted to link it with economic consequences.

The existing research on "why governments violate constitutions" can be divided into three analytical perspectives:

- **Design factors**: For example, whether the constitutional text embeds operable and enforceable sanctions, impeachment mechanisms, court-enforcement mechanisms, and so on. If the cost of violating the constitution is high, the probability of a government doing so may be lower.
- **Environmental factors**: A country's history, culture, level of economic development, political conflict, social identity, and so on. Certain cultural traditions or political backgrounds may be more tolerant of a leader "bypassing the constitution," or violations may more readily occur when oversight capacity is insufficient.
- **Non-state actors**: For example, the public, civil society groups, the media, and international organizations—the extent to which they can publicly expose and pressure unconstitutional behavior also affects compliance.

Although this literature is very important, it mostly only **explains "why non-compliance occurs"** rather than studying **"the economic impact of non-compliance itself."**

#### **Existing Preliminary Discussions of the "Economic Consequences of Constitutional Compliance"**

The literature studying the economic consequences of "constitutional (non-)compliance" remains limited:

- **Voigt (2021)** was the first to argue, at the level of economic theory, how constitutional non-compliance might undermine a government's credibility, damage coordination between the government and citizens, increase transaction costs, and thereby reduce economic performance.
- **Metelska-Szaniawska (2021)** used post-socialist transition countries as a sample and, employing a relatively simple "rights gap" indicator (focusing mainly on seven civil and political rights), found that the larger this gap, the less smooth the economic transition, and that democratization, social conflict, and the like all affect this gap. However, that study was limited to transition countries, the time period extended only to around 2012, and the measurement indicator was relatively limited.

At the same time, a large body of literature has explored the relationship between "**institutional quality**" and "**economic development**" from a more macro political-economy perspective. For example, North (1990), Acemoglu et al. (2001), and Rodrik et al. (2004) all concur that a stable, transparent, and predictable institutional environment is conducive to investment and long-term growth. But this research has not drilled down to the finer-grained question of "constitutional compliance / non-compliance."

#### **The Contribution of This Paper**

As the foregoing literature review shows, the causal relationship between **"constitutional compliance"** and **"economic development"** appears theoretically significant, yet there is still a lack of large-scale, long-term cross-national quantitative evidence. Compounded by the problems of data scarcity or one-sided measurement that past research faced in measuring "constitutional compliance," discussion of its economic consequences has remained rather preliminary or confined to small samples. The contributions of this paper are:

- It uses the **Comparative Constitutional Compliance Database (CCCD)** provided by **Gutmann et al. (2024a)**, covering compliance measurement data for the vast majority of countries worldwide over a span of a century;
- Drawing on the theories of North, Buchanan, and others regarding the constitution as a "**credible commitment**" mechanism, it proposes systematic hypotheses;
- It adopts the dynamic panel research method of **Acemoglu et al. (2019)** for studying democracy and economic development, thereby testing in a large sample "whether constitutional non-compliance leads to a significant decline in GDP."

In the end, the authors position this study at the intersection of **constitutional political economy**, **new institutional economics**, and **development economics**: it both fills the gap in measuring "constitutional compliance" and connects institutional quality (especially core institutions such as property rights and the rule of law) with empirical research on economic performance, offering a new perspective for understanding the real role that constitutions play in economic development.

## Research Hypotheses

#### **The Main Research Hypothesis**

The authors first put forward a **main hypothesis**, which can be summarized as:

> **When a country's government behavior does not match its constitutional provisions (i.e., when there is a clear de jure-de facto gap), the country's per capita GDP level tends to be lower; conversely, the higher the constitutional compliance, the better the economic performance.**

The theoretical basis for this hypothesis is that one of the constitution's primary functions is to help the government provide a **credible commitment** to society and the market, overcome the time-inconsistency problem, and supply certain "rules of the game" for social transactions. Once a government frequently violates the constitution, members of society or external investors will come to doubt the government's creditworthiness and the credibility of its commitments, which may in turn reduce investment and innovation activity and harm economic growth. At the same time, constitutional violations also weaken the constitution's own **coordination** function and **legitimacy**, increase social conflict and governance costs, and thereby reduce economic efficiency. It is thus clear that these mechanisms all suggest that the negative effects of violating the constitution should manifest at the economic level.

After establishing the main hypothesis, the authors further put forward three major research questions (Q1, Q2, Q3) to examine more closely the relationship between constitutional compliance and economic development:

#### **Research Question Q1: Which specific domain of constitutional compliance is most critical?**

The authors point out that constitutional provisions can be roughly distinguished into four main aspects:

- **Property rights & rule of law**: Concerned with how the government protects individual property and ensures the fair enforcement of the law;
- **Political rights**: Concerned with citizens' rights to participate in political life, such as freedom of assembly and association;
- **Civil rights**: Concerned with basic individual freedoms, such as freedom of speech, the media, and religion;
- **Basic human rights**: Concerned with the most fundamental personal protections, such as the right to life and freedom from slavery and torture.

The role that constitutional provisions in different domains play in the economy may not be the same. Some literature (such as Blume and Voigt, 2007) has likewise noted that property rights and rule-of-law provisions are especially important for economic growth, while the impact pathways of certain social rights and political rights each have their own characteristics.

#### **Research Question Q2: What are the specific mechanisms through which constitutional compliance affects the economy?**

Possible transmission channels include:

- **Investment** (including domestic investment and foreign direct investment, FDI): higher compliance → credible government commitment → strengthened investor confidence → increased investment → higher per capita GDP
- **Total factor productivity (TFP)**: a credible and robust legal environment → encourages innovation and technological progress → raises productivity
- **Trade**: if the rule of law and policy transparency are relatively high, it may attract more foreign trade; if compliance is low, partner countries may worry about risk and reduce trade
- **Tax revenue and government expenditure** (Tax revenue, Government expenditure)
  - If a government's unconstitutional behavior is severe, citizens or firms may reduce their trust in the government, leading to larger-scale tax evasion or non-cooperation with taxation, and a relative reduction in government fiscal revenue;
  - And if compliance is high, taxation capacity may be strengthened, but there is also another possibility: in order to safeguard certain rights stipulated in the constitution, government expenditure may also rise correspondingly. The authors hope to distinguish these effects in different directions empirically.

- **Social unrest**: the more a government violates the constitution, the more it may trigger internal disorder, protests, or conflict, leading to rising instability and thereby suppressing economic growth.

#### **Research Question Q3: In what types of countries does compliance matter most for the economy?**

Here the authors further distinguish countries:

- By **income level**: high-income, upper-middle-income, lower-middle-income, low-income
- By **political regime**: democratic or authoritarian (non-democratic, autocratic)

Some countries may also be a "low-income + authoritarian" combination, or "high-income + democratic," and so on. Under different political-economic environments, the degree of social impact caused by "unconstitutional behavior" is not the same. For example, in mature democracies, judicial and voter oversight is stronger and the cost of government constitutional violations is higher; whereas in some authoritarian regimes, public checks are relatively weak, government non-compliance may be more prevalent, but its economic impact may also have different characteristics. Likewise, for low-income countries, where basic institutions are fragile, changes in compliance may produce a greater shock to the economy.

## Data and Methods

#### **Data Construction**

**Constitutional compliance (constitutional_compliance)**

The paper's **core independent variable** comes from the **Comparative Constitutional Compliance Database** (CCCD) (Gutmann et al., 2024a). This database compares the constitutional text (de jure) of countries around the world with actual enforcement (de facto) year by year, coding each key provision as 0/1/0.5 and the like, and then synthesizing them into an **overall compliance index** through factor analysis. This index covers most countries worldwide over roughly 1900–2020, and is divided into four compliance domains: property rights & rule of law, political rights, civil rights, and basic human rights.

In the final model, the authors first use the **overall compliance indicator**, and then run regressions on **domain-specific indicators** to test for differences in impact across domains.

#### **Dependent Variable: Per Capita GDP (gdp_pc)**

The authors take the logarithm of the "PPP-adjusted per capita GDP (in 2017 US dollars)" provided by **Feenstra et al. (2015, updt. 2021)** as the standard indicator for measuring economic performance. The time range of the study is **1960–2019**, with the country sample reaching up to 153.

#### **Other Control Variables**

- Social unrest (social_unrest): whether there are large-scale riots, revolutions, and other events;
- Trade openness (trade_gdp): the ratio of trade to GDP (logged);
- The ratio of investment to GDP (investment_gdp), the ratio of FDI to GDP (fdi), etc.;
- Demographic structure: the share of the population under 15 and over 64 in the total population;
- Leadership turnover (leader): used to identify whether there has been a change of political leader, which may affect the policy environment;
- The transition shock of the former Soviet Union and its satellite states (soviets_post_1989 and other dummies);
- The ratio of tax revenue to GDP (tax_revenue_gdp), the ratio of government expenditure to GDP (gov_expenditure_gdp), total factor productivity (tfp), etc., used for the subsequent mechanism analysis.

#### **Methods**

The study mainly adopts a **dynamic panel model**, inspired by the research of **Acemoglu et al. (2019)** on "the impact of democracy on economic growth." Since "constitutional compliance" is likewise a political-institutional variable that may interact with economic variables, the researchers need a set of econometric methods that can mitigate the **endogeneity** problem, control for potential **reverse causality**, and account for temporal dynamics.

The core specification of the model is as follows:
$$
gdp\_pc_{it} = \beta constitutional\_compliance_{it-1} + \sum_{j=1}^{\rho} \gamma gdp\_pc_{it} + \alpha_i + \delta_t + \epsilon_{it}
$$
where:

- $gdp\_pc_{it}$ is the per capita GDP of country $i$ in year $t$;
- $constitutional\_compliance_{it-1}$ is the one-period-lagged degree of constitutional compliance;
- $\sum_{j=1}^{\rho} \gamma gdp\_pc_{it}$ is a sum of lagged per capita GDP terms, used to control for the dynamics of GDP;
- $\alpha_i$ is the country fixed effect, and $\delta_t$ is the time fixed effect.

To address the **Nickell bias** and endogeneity problems that may exist in dynamic panels, the authors use two estimation methods:

- **Fixed effects (Within estimator)**: when the panel length (T) is sufficiently large, the Nickell bias is relatively small.
- **Arellano-Bond GMM**: applying GMM estimation to the differenced equation can further mitigate endogeneity.

## Results

#### **Baseline Results**

Whether using fixed effects or Arellano-Bond GMM, the coefficient on constitutional compliance is positive and statistically significant. This means that **when constitutional compliance is higher, the level of per capita GDP also rises accordingly**. Although the coefficient itself is not large in magnitude, considering that the measure of constitutional compliance is a composite index and that the scale of GDP is enormous, its ability to remain significant under robust multiple tests is sufficient to show that compliance does indeed make a positive contribution to the economy.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20250102213618580.png)

The authors also conducted a series of robustness checks, including the inclusion of additional controls or interaction terms, for example:

- **The transition effect of the former Soviet Union and its satellite states** (soviets_post_1989/1990/1991/1992): considering that these countries underwent fundamental institutional change between 1989 and 1992 and that new constitutions were generally promulgated, this may have simultaneously shocked both compliance and economic performance.
- **Social unrest** (social_unrest): unrest or conflict may precede unconstitutional behavior, and may also lead to prolonged economic downturn;
- **Trade openness**, **net financial flows**, **demographic structure**, **leadership turnover**, etc.

The results show that even after controlling for these factors, the significant positive effect of the constitutional compliance indicator on GDP still holds, indicating that the conclusion that **non-compliance produces negative economic consequences** is not affected by the main potential confounding variables.

#### **Domain-Specific Estimation**

After the main regression confirmed that the overall constitutional compliance indicator has a positive effect on economic performance, the authors used the CCCD data to break constitutional compliance down into four categories: **property rights & rule of law**, **political rights**, **civil rights**, and **basic human rights**:

The regression results show that constitutional compliance in **property rights & rule of law** has the strongest and most robust positive effect on per capita GDP; the impact of the others—such as political rights, civil rights, and basic human rights—on GDP is, across different model specifications, either insignificant, of lower significance, or has a smaller coefficient.

The authors explain: **property rights & rule of law** are most critical for investor confidence, contract enforcement, and social stability, and if a country violates the constitution in this area, it greatly damages the predictability and security of economic activity. Therefore, this domain-specific finding also echoes the judgment in the earlier literature (such as Blume and Voigt, 2007) that "property rights and the rule of law" are core institutional factors for economic growth.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20250102214119003.png)

#### **Mechanism Analysis**

The authors selected several mechanism variables (investment, FDI, TFP, trade, tax revenue, government expenditure, social unrest) and constructed a similar dynamic panel model:

$$
X_{it} = \beta constitutional\_compliance_{it} + \sum_{j=1}^{\rho}  X_{it} + \alpha_i + \delta_t + \epsilon_{it}
$$

The regressions show that the channel of **foreign direct investment (FDI)** is the most significant: the higher the compliance, the larger the share of FDI in GDP. That is to say, international capital cares most about whether a constitution is actually enforced; if a country repeatedly exhibits constitutional violations or arbitrary government interference with property, foreign investment will retreat out of concern over policy and legal risks.

Other mechanisms, such as **general investment (investment_gdp)** and **productivity (tfp)**, also show some significance in certain regressions, but the authors believe that the strongest and most consistent evidence is concentrated on FDI. In addition, for channels such as **tax revenue**, **government expenditure**, and **social unrest**, some support is also obtained under certain models, but it does not remain significant across all specifications.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20250102214638144.png)

The authors ran the same mechanism regressions for the "property rights & rule of law" domain-specific indicator, and found that, in addition to FDI, compliance in "property rights / rule of law" also significantly raises **domestic investment** and **total factor productivity**. At the same time, higher compliance can provide investors and innovators with a predictable legal environment, promoting capital formation and technological improvement.

#### **Heterogeneity Analysis**

To answer research question Q3, the authors grouped countries for regression in the following ways:

- **Income level**: low-income, lower-middle-income, upper-middle-income, high-income
- **Political regime**: democracy and autocracy
- The intersection of the two: for example, "low-income + democracy," "high-income + autocracy," etc.

The conclusions can be summarized as follows:

- **Low-income and lower-middle-income countries** (especially democracies): compliance helps raise the share of **tax revenue** in GDP, thereby promoting economic development. The reason may be that when government compliance is high, citizens trust the government more and are more willing to pay taxes; conversely, if the government frequently violates the constitution, taxpayers will question the government's legitimacy and evade taxes, causing the tax base to shrink.
- **High-income autocracies**: represented by Middle Eastern oil-producing countries, if these countries strengthen compliance in constitutional property / rule of law, they can attract more FDI, raise productivity, and reduce their dependence on military spending (thereby optimizing resource allocation).
- **Upper-middle-income democracies**: compliance in property rights and the rule of law can significantly improve these countries' investment climate and economic growth.

This indicates that **after compliance improves, the specific benefit channels obtained by different types of countries are not the same.** For low-income democracies, improvements in **tax administration** and enhanced government legitimacy matter most; for high-income autocracies, **foreign capital inflows** and **productivity** gains are key.


## Conclusion

- **Non-compliance with constitutions has negative economic consequences**: when a government does not comply with the constitution (i.e., when there is a large de jure-de facto gap), the country's per capita GDP level is significantly lower.
- **The domain of property rights and the rule of law is especially important**: after splitting the compliance indicator, the authors find that compliance in property rights and the rule of law (property rights and the rule of law) has a particularly prominent and robust positive effect on per capita GDP. If a government violates the constitution in this domain, it severely damages investor confidence and society's trust in the government.
- **The main transmission channel**: investment and foreign direct investment (FDI). The empirical evidence shows that constitutional non-compliance lowers a country's credibility in international capital markets, leading to reduced FDI. Where compliance in property rights and the rule of law is higher, not only does FDI flow in more readily, but domestic investment and total factor productivity (TFP) also rise accordingly.
- **Heterogeneity analysis**: for low-income democracies, constitutional compliance plays a larger role in enhancing government legitimacy and strengthening tax administration capacity, thereby promoting economic development. For high-income autocracies, raising constitutional compliance can bring more foreign investment and efficiency gains, and may reduce dependence on military spending.
