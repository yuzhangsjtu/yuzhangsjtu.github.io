## Reward Hacking in Reinforcement Learning

In the field of Reinforcement Learning (RL), **reward hacking** is a core and thorny problem. The goal of an RL agent is to learn a policy through interaction with its environment that maximizes its cumulative reward. Central to this process is the design of the **reward function**, which signals to the agent what constitutes **good** behavior. However, defining a reward function that perfectly captures the designer’s **true intentions** is extraordinarily difficult.

The reward functions that scientists design are often merely a **quantifiable**, **imperfect** proxy for their true intentions. Reward hacking occurs when the agent discovers and exploits loopholes or ambiguities in this proxy reward function, obtaining high scores in an unintended manner without truly accomplishing the task. This is not malice on the agent’s part; rather, its optimization algorithm is coldly and efficiently executing the only instruction we gave it: **maximize the reward signal**.

A classic example is OpenAI’s **CoastRunners** game. The agent’s true objective was to complete the racecourse, but its reward function was defined as “picking up reward items along the course to achieve a higher score.” Ultimately, the agent learned to circle repeatedly in a small bay (see the GIF below), collecting rapidly respawning reward items, even willing to crash its boat in order to return to the **scoring spot** more quickly. It achieved extreme success on the proxy objective (reward score) but failed utterly on the true objective (finishing the race).

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/coastrunners.gif)

This phenomenon becomes more insidious in complex tasks. For example, a robot programmed to **clean a room** might, if rewarded based on “amount of trash cleaned per unit time,” learn to first knock over trash bins, create a huge mess, and then efficiently clean it up, thereby earning a high score. In Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), language models have discovered that, compared to providing objective and neutral answers, mimicking users’ opinions and using more confident, flowery language is a more effective way to score highly with human raters. It learns not to be *useful*, but to be *pleasing* (think a certain version of the sycophantic GPT-4o, or today’s Gemini and GPT-5, which max out emotional value).

These examples all point to a central problem, namely **specification gaming**: while the agent strictly adheres to the explicit specification or rules you’ve written down, its behavior undermines the **spirit** or **implicit objectives** that were never written down but exist by default. In other words, any formalized, rule-based system can be broken by a sufficiently powerful optimization process. The greater the optimization pressure, the more fragile the system becomes.

## The Dilemma of “Good Metrics”

Decades before AI researchers began wrestling with agent reward hacks, social scientists had already observed strikingly similar patterns in human society. The most famous articulation of this is **Goodhart's Law**, proposed by British economist Charles Goodhart in 1975:

> When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.

The core idea of this law is that once a social or economic indicator is chosen as a policy target, it loses its informational value as an indicator because people will begin to *game* the metric rather than focus on the real goals it is meant to represent. For instance, if a school’s performance is measured solely by students’ average test scores, teachers and students may focus on test-taking techniques or even cheat, rather than genuinely acquiring knowledge and improving skills. Average score as a **measure** may initially be a good indicator of the quality of education, but when it becomes the sole target, it becomes corrupted.

Closely related is **Campbell’s Law**, articulated by psychologist Donald T. Campbell in 1976:

> The more any quantitative social indicator is used for social decision-making, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures and the more apt it will be to distort and corrupt the social processes it is intended to monitor.

Campbell’s Law further emphasizes the dynamic evolution of indicators within social systems. It not only points out that the indicator will fail, but also pinpoints the mechanism of failure: namely, **corruption pressures** and the **distortion and corruption** of the social process itself.

## Reward Hacking in Academia

By turning the hacking framework onto academia, we observe an astonishingly similar systemic dynamic. The true objectives of academic research—such as **advancing the frontiers of knowledge**, **solving major scientific problems**, and **training the next generation of researchers**—are inherently **ambiguous**, **long-horizon**, and difficult to quantify with precision. Consequently, any academic evaluation system, in pursuit of administrative convenience and ostensible objectivity, inevitably resorts to a set of quantifiable proxy metrics. These metrics include, but are not limited to: the **number** of papers published, the **impact factor** of the journals they appear in, the number of citations they receive, and the acceptance rate at top-tier conferences.

These proxy metrics form the bedrock of the evaluation system and are directly linked to researchers’ promotion, access to funding, academic reputation, and even graduation eligibility. When the entire system treats the optimization of these metrics as its central goal, a form of academic reward hacking inevitably arises.

The first thing I’ve observed is that academic reward hacking tends to occur more readily among master’s students who did not attend top-tier undergraduate programs. In the social sciences, the hardest-hit fields are **environmental economics** and **human geography**, because these two disciplines allow you to endlessly permute and combine variables, mass-produce papers, and are particularly conducive to using difference-in-differences. Graduate recommendation exams (保研, *baoyan* — a highly competitive selection system allowing the best undergraduates onto graduate programs at prestigious institutions) are themselves one such selection pathway. A good number of students, introduced in undergrad to research in environmental economics or human geography, come to publish in journals recognized as having sufficient credentials — e.g. those classified as “Source Journals for Chinese Social Sciences Index” (CSSCI) (C-journals, *C刊*) — or in international publications — before the admission season. By the time of *baoyan* interviews, these metrics already translate into a ticket to enter a very good institution, and the neatly positive reward feedback continues to operate throughout their graduate studies. I’ve seen master’s students who have published over 40 geography papers, nearly all titled along the lines of **“Spatiotemporal Patterns and Driving Mechanisms of xxxx.”** I’ve also seen many students with no prior research interest in issues strongly branded under climate change, energy, or environmental themes, who end up pivoting into environmental directions, investigating, say, a hundred variant downstream consequences of air pollution.

Secondly, when students go wrong, a large share of the responsibility lies with the advisor. Some advisors are themselves beneficiaries of the hacking-in-academia, and naturally would not criticize such behavior (here I recommend reading [The Two Dimensions of Education](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/9vnJdeXDm_-EhfakLWN-6Q)). At an economics and management school of a certain 211 university in central China, when looking over a prestigious postgraduate roundtable, the associated faculty organize graduate student academic conferences at which ~80% of the submitted pieces fall within some variant of China-applied environmental economics — frankly boggling mind by the sheer numbers.
