> When people encounter phenomena they cannot explain, they engage in thinking driven by curiosity, attempting to uncover these mysteries. This process enhances human knowledge and technological levels, thereby driving economic growth.
>
> More complex thought processes and more developed technologies have been found in societies more affected by **solar eclipses**; individuals who witnessed a total solar eclipse during childhood are more likely to enter scientific careers.

**Title:** Solar Eclipses and the Origins of Critical Thinking and Complexity

**Journal:** Economic Journal

**Authors:**

- Anastasia Litina, University of Macedonia, AP
- Èric Roca Fernández, Université Clermont Auvergne, AP

**Online Date:** 27 December 2023

<br/>

**Gaps:** Scientific progress and human capital are universally recognized in both theory and empirical evidence as crucial drivers of economic growth. However, the deeper determinants of **critical thinking**, **science**, and ultimately, the **creation of human capital**, remain unclear.

**Aims:**

- This paper posits that **curiosity** is a natural precursor to human capital, linking curiosity to economic development by investigating its impact on pre-modern human capital formation and technological progress.
- Measuring curiosity is elusive, especially for pre-modern societies. To circumvent this limitation, the paper focuses on triggers of human curiosity, so-called **natural phenomena**. Natural events are promising candidates for approximating curiosity—humans have consistently sought to understand the world around them. Thus, the paper argues that pre-modern societies with greater exposure to perplexing natural phenomena gained a **comparative advantage**.——**Solar eclipses** are among the most impressive natural phenomena.

**Data:**

- Ethnic group data: Drawn from Murdock's (1967) *Ethnographic Atlas* and later supplements, covering the livelihoods, marriage customs, labor divisions, etc., of over 1,000 ethnic groups worldwide, which can be used to construct the following outcome variables:
    - Population density: an ordinal variable with 8 levels, ranging from fewer than 50 individuals to over 50,000.
    - Settlement pattern: an ordinal variable with 8 levels, from nomadic camps to villages, etc.
    - Proxies for organizational complexity: jurisdictional hierarchy levels (small societies, chiefdoms, states, etc.), class stratification, level of political integration.
    - Presence of strategy games (as opposed to chance or physical strength games).
    - Concepts reflecting curiosity in folklore.
    - Level of religious belief within the ethnic group.
- Panel data:
    - Intertemporal population density, administrative levels, jurisdictional levels, and information on writing and geometric abilities at the polity level.
    - Technological levels of various regions from 1 AD to 1000 AD, including general technology and non-agricultural technology levels.
- Individual data: Wikipedia entries for individuals collected from 1600 BCE to 1800 AD, including birth date, birthplace, and occupation information.
    - Occupation classification: scientific occupations or religious occupations.
    - Dummy variable for individuals witnessing a solar eclipse between ages 5 and 15.
    - Dummy variable for individuals experiencing a volcanic eruption.
- Solar Eclipses: From Espenak and Meeus's (2006) "Five Millennium Catalog of Solar Eclipses," which provides ephemerides for all solar eclipses occurring between 1999 BCE and 3000 AD:
    - Characteristics of solar eclipses: exogenous, harmless, locally distributed.
    - Differentiates between total and annular solar eclipses.
    - Over a study span of 3,500 years, a total of 5,635 solar eclipses occurred.
    - Within a 100km*100km grid, an average of 1.17 solar eclipses occur per century.
    - Key variable: the number of total solar eclipses observable in a region.

**Results:**

- Economic development: In locations with more solar eclipses, population densities tend to be higher, settlement patterns more complex, and jurisdictional hierarchy levels higher.
- Human capital and technology:
    - A 1% increase in the number of solar eclipses increases the probability of an ethnic group playing strategy games or developing a writing system by 0.23 and 0.40, respectively.
    - In societies with more frequent solar eclipses, the vocabulary for concepts related to calendars is richer in their folk tales.
    - There tends to be a more pronounced development trend in geometry as the number of solar eclipses increases.
    - Societies experiencing more solar eclipses performed better (or exhibited greater complexity) in technological development, including monetary systems, technical facilities, general technologies, non-agricultural technologies, communication, industry, etc.
- Curiosity:
    - The more solar eclipses an ethnic group experiences, the more eclipse-related concepts exist in its folklore.
    - A 1% increase in the number of solar eclipses increases the number of **curiosity**-related terms in folk tales by 0.11.
    - Individuals who witnessed a solar eclipse between the ages of 5 and 10 are more likely to choose a scientific career in adulthood.
    - The association between observing a total solar eclipse during childhood (ages 5-15) and pursuing a scientific career⬇️: This (scientist) effect is positively significant before the 7th century AD.

**Robustness‌:**

- Replacing the explanatory variable with **lunar eclipses** does not produce the previous regression results.
- The impact of solar eclipses on **religion** is not statistically significant.
- Running the analysis with the frequency of concepts like lightning, rocks, colors (white and purple) in folklore as outcome variables shows no significant results, indicating the previously observed effect of solar eclipses on related concepts is not attributable to a **placebo effect**.

**Conclusion‌:**

- Societies with a higher frequency of solar eclipses exhibit more complex social development.
- Societies more affected by solar eclipses demonstrate greater complexity in strategy games, technology, and the prevalence of curiosity-related concepts.
- Witnessing a solar eclipse during childhood increases an individual's likelihood of choosing a scientific career.
