> When people face phenomena they cannot explain, they think out of curiosity, trying to unravel these mysteries. This process raises the level of human knowledge and technology, thereby driving economic growth.
>
> More complex thought processes and more developed technologies are found in societies more susceptible to the influence of **solar eclipses**; people who observed a total solar eclipse during childhood are more likely to enter scientific careers.

**Title:** Solar Eclipses and the Origins of Critical Thinking and Complexity

**Journal:** Economic Journal

**Authors:**

- Anastasia Litina, University of Macedonia, AP
- Èric Roca Fernández, Université Clermont Auvergne, AP

**Online date:** 27 December 2023

<br/>

**Gaps:** Scientific progress and human capital are both theoretically and empirically widely regarded as important drivers of economic growth. However, the deeper determinants of **critical thinking**, **science**, and ultimately the **creation of human capital** remain unclear.

**Aims:**

- This paper proposes that **curiosity** is a natural precursor to human capital, and by examining the impact of curiosity on pre-modern human capital formation and technological progress, it links curiosity to economic development.
- The metric for curiosity is elusive, especially for pre-modern societies. To circumvent this limitation, this paper focuses on the triggers of human curiosity, in particular **natural phenomena**. Natural events are good candidates for approximating curiosity, as humans have always sought to understand the world around them. Therefore, this paper argues that pre-modern societies more exposed to perplexing natural phenomena acquired a **comparative advantage**. **Solar eclipses** are among the most impressive of natural phenomena.

**Data:**

- Ethnic group data: From Murdock's (1967) *Ethnographic Atlas* and later supplements, covering the livelihoods, marriage customs, and division of labor, etc., of more than 1,000 ethnic groups around the world, from which the following explained variables can be constructed:
  - Population density: an ordinal variable with 8 levels, ranging from fewer than 50 people to more than 50,000 people.
  - Settlement pattern: an ordinal variable with 8 levels, from nomadic camps to villages, etc.
  - Proxy variables for organizational complexity: jurisdictional hierarchy (small societies, chiefdoms, states, etc.), class stratification, level of political integration.
  - Presence of strategy games (as opposed to games of chance or games of physical strength).
  - Concepts reflecting curiosity in folktales.
  - The level of religious belief of the ethnic group.
- Panel data:
  - Cross-period population density, administrative level, jurisdictional hierarchy, and information on writing and geometric ability at the polity level.
  - The technological level of various regions from 1 AD to 1000 AD, including general technology and non-agricultural technology levels.
- Individual data: Wikipedia entries for individuals collected from 1600 BCE to 1800 AD, including date of birth, place of birth, and occupational information.
  - Occupational classification: scientific occupations or religious occupations.
  - A dummy variable for individuals seeing a solar eclipse between the ages of 5 and 15.
  - A dummy variable for individuals experiencing a volcanic eruption.
- Solar eclipses: From Espenak and Meeus's (2006) "Five Millennium Catalog of Solar Eclipses," which provides ephemerides corresponding to all solar eclipses that occurred between 1999 BCE and 3000 AD:
  - Characteristics of solar eclipses: exogenous, harmless, locally distributed.
  - Distinguishes between total and annular solar eclipses.
  - Over the study period spanning 3,500 years, a total of 5,635 solar eclipses occurred.
  - Within a 100km*100km grid, an average of 1.17 solar eclipses occur per century.
  - Key variable: the number of total solar eclipses observable in a region.

**Results:**

- Economic development: In places where more solar eclipses occur, population density is higher, settlement patterns are more complex, and the jurisdictional hierarchy is also higher.
- Human capital and technology:
  - A 1% increase in the number of solar eclipses increases the probability of an ethnic group playing strategy games or developing a writing system by 0.23 and 0.40, respectively.
  - In societies where solar eclipses occur more frequently, the vocabulary in folktales related to calendar concepts is richer.
  - As the number of solar eclipses increases, the development trend of geometry is more pronounced.
  - Societies that experienced more solar eclipses performed better (or were more complex) in technological development, including monetary systems, technical facilities, general technology, non-agricultural technology, communication, industry, etc.
- Curiosity:
  - The greater the number of solar eclipses an ethnic group experienced, the more eclipse-related concepts there are in its folklore.
  - A 1% increase in the number of solar eclipses increases the number of **curiosity**-related terms in folktales by 0.11.
  - Individuals who saw a solar eclipse between the ages of 5 and 10 are more likely to choose a scientific career as adults.
  - The association between observing a total solar eclipse during childhood (ages 5-15) and pursuing a scientific career⬇️: this (scientist) effect is positively significant up until the 7th century.

**Robustness‌:**

- Replacing the explanatory variable with **lunar eclipses** fails to reproduce the earlier regression results.
- The impact of solar eclipses on **religion** is not significant.
- Replacing the explained variable with the occurrence of concepts of lightning, rocks, and colors (white and purple) in folktales yields insignificant results, indicating that the effect of solar eclipses on the related concepts above is not a **placebo effect**.

**Conclusion‌:**

- Societies in which solar eclipses occur more often have more complex social development.
- Societies more affected by solar eclipses have more complex strategy games, technology, and concepts of curiosity.
- Seeing a solar eclipse during childhood increases an individual's odds of choosing a scientific career.
