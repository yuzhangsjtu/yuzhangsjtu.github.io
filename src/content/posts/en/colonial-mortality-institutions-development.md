> What determines differences in levels of regional development? A large literature holds that the impact of institutions on economic performance is crucially important; however, whether this relationship is **causal** still faces many **endogeneity** challenges.
>
> By introducing European **settler mortality** during the colonial period as an instrumental variable, this paper innovatively analyzes the profound influence of colonial policies on modern institutions and economic performance. The study finds that high-mortality regions typically established **extractive** institutions, lacking private property protection and constraints on government, which led to poorer modern economic performance; low-mortality regions, by contrast, established Europe-like **inclusive** institutions, which promoted economic development. Institutional differences among the former colonies explain roughly three-quarters of today's per capita income gap. Once institutional factors are controlled for, geographic factors are no longer important.

**Title:** The Colonial Origins of Comparative Development: An Empirical Investigation

**Journal:** *American Economic Review*

## Institutions as the Fundamental Cause of Cross-Country Per Capita Income Differences: Theory and Challenges

#### **The Importance of Institutions**

- Why do per capita incomes differ so enormously across countries? Although there is no consensus on the answer to this question, differences in **institutions** and **property rights** have received widespread attention in recent years;
- Countries with better institutions (such as more secure property rights and fewer **policy distortions**) invest more in physical and human capital and use these factors more efficiently, thereby achieving higher income levels. This view is supported by some empirical studies, which find a positive correlation between property rights protection and economic development;
- To some extent, the importance of institutions is self-evident. In reality, the starkly different development paths of **South Korea and North Korea**, and of **East Germany and West Germany**, illustrate how one part of a country stagnated under a planned economy and collective ownership while the other part developed rapidly under private property rights and a market economy.

#### **Empirical Challenges**

- There is still a lack of reliable evidence that institutional differences play a **decisive** role in cross-country per capita incomes;
- **Reverse causality**: it may well be that rich economies choose good institutions, or are able to afford the costs that establishing good institutions entails;
- Perhaps more importantly, economic development is the **combined result** of many factors, which simultaneously affect both institutions and per capita income levels.

## Core Hypothesis and Historical Background

#### **The Core Hypothesis of This Paper**

- To estimate the impact of institutions on economic development, one needs a source of **exogenous** variation that explains institutional differences. This paper attempts to use the mortality rates of European settlers in the colonies during the Age of Exploration as the source of exogenous variation, taking countries that experienced colonization as the study sample. This yields a sample of economies that had similar per capita income levels 400 years ago but display enormous per capita income gaps today;

- The figure below shows the negative correlation between settler mortality in the colonies and per capita GDP across countries in 1995: ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240611134907740.png)

- The detailed explanation is as follows:

  - Institutional differences across countries can be traced back to the different colonial policies of the European colonial era. In some regions, European colonizers could not settle and therefore established extractive institutions, which lacked private property protection and constraints on government power and were designed to transfer the colony's resources to the colonizers. Such institutions were more common in high-mortality regions (such as Africa and the tropics);
  - Conversely, in colonies where Europeans could settle in large numbers (such as North America, Australia, and New Zealand), the settlers established Europe-like institutions that emphasized private property protection and constraints on government power. These institutions promoted economic development;
  - These institutional differences have persisted to this day and influence the economic performance of modern countries (the complete logical chain is shown below ⬇️);

  ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240611101333608.png)

#### **Historical Background**

Mortality influenced settlement activity; settlement activity influenced early institutional forms; and early institutions persisted and shaped the institutions that exist today.

**Argument 1: Mortality Influenced Settlement Activity**

- The mortality rate of European settlers was the key factor determining whether they could settle in a colony. High mortality meant settlement was difficult; conversely, settlement proceeded relatively smoothly. Philip D. Curtin's research documents in detail the mortality rates of European settlers in different colonial regions, and these data are very important for understanding settlement patterns;
- Britain's early settlement plans in West Africa failed because of extremely high mortality. Curtin records a series of examples: in the "Province of Freedom" (Sierra Leone), the European mortality rate in the first year was 46%, and on Bulama Island (April 1792 to April 1793) the European mortality rate was 61%. In the first year of the Sierra Leone Company (1792-1793), 72% of the European settlers died;
- The Pilgrim Fathers (the Puritans) chose to migrate to America rather than Guyana, in part because of Guyana's high mortality;
- In 1795, the Beauchamp Committee decided to send British convicts to Australia rather than to the island in the Gambia River, where mortality was high.

**Argument 2: Colony Types and Settlement Activity**

Differences in settler mortality shaped different types of colonies, chiefly extractive colonies and settler colonies.

- **Extractive colonies**: colonies whose main purpose was resource extraction. European colonizers did not intend to settle there long term, but instead sought to extract as many resources as possible from the local area. These regions typically lacked protection of private property and constraints on government power.
  - **Congo**: Belgium implemented extremely extractive policies in the Congo to obtain rubber, ivory, and other resources. The colonial government built a strict system of control that forced the local population into high-intensity labor;
  - **Latin America**: the colonial policies of Spain and Portugal likewise centered on resource extraction, especially in regions rich in gold and silver deposits. The Spanish Crown imposed strict monopolies and trade controls in these regions, with the main aim of extracting wealth from the colonies.
- **Settler colonies**: colonies that attracted large numbers of European migrants to settle and that attempted to replicate European institutions. Institutions in these regions typically emphasized private property protection and constraints on government power, resembling those of Europe.
  - **Australia**: most early migrants to Australia were convicts, but they fought for and won legal protections and political rights similar to those in Britain, and ultimately established institutions emphasizing private property protection and constraints on government power;
  - **New Zealand, Canada, and the United States**: these countries attracted large numbers of European migrants and established Europe-like institutions, emphasizing private property protection and constraints on government.

> Manning (1988) summarizes: "On the European continent, the doctrines of representative democracy triumphed over those of absolutist autocracy... but in Africa, what the European conquerors established were French-style absolutist governments in the manner of Louis XIV."

**Argument 3: Colonial-Era Institutions Exhibited Persistence**

- Cases of good institutions persisting: the legal order, private property rights, and other institutions that took shape during the colonial period in Australia, Canada, New Zealand, and the United States shaped these countries' present-day institutions;
- **The persistence of bad institutions in non-settler colonies**:
  - **Spanish America**: in Spanish America, post-independence institutions largely carried over the extractive institutions of the colonial period. For example, in the 19th century, Mexico, Peru, and Bolivia all retained forced labor policies;
  - **Africa**: many countries continued to operate the colonial era's extractive institutions after independence. For example, the Democratic Republic of the Congo maintained the colonial era's autocratic rule and resource extraction policies after independence;

> Arthur Lewis (1965) argued: "For most (of the newly independent African governments), independence meant only that they had won their struggle against the puppet dictatorships propped up by Britain and France. In turn, they molded themselves into governments broadly the same as the arrogant and despotic ones that the governors and other colonial officials had established."

**Why Institutions Persist**

- **The cost problem**: establishing institutions that limit government power and protect property rights entails high costs. If those costs were already borne by the colonizers during the colonial period, the post-independence elite may be unwilling to change the existing institutions and may instead continue to exploit the existing extractive institutions. Once extractive institutions have been established, the cost to post-independence rulers of maintaining them is relatively low, because they can continue to use the existing control structures and resource-extraction mechanisms;
- **Elite interests**: in many extractive colonies, the colonizers delegated the day-to-day operation of the state to a small group of local elites. After independence, these elites took control of the state and continued to support extractive institutions in order to advance their own interests. Such small elite groups are more inclined to support a centralized system of power, because it guarantees their control over resources and their allocation;
- **Irreversibility of investment**: if individuals have made irreversible investments under a particular set of institutions, they will tend to support those institutions and allow them to persist. For example, individuals who have already invested human and physical capital under the existing institutions will support institutions that protect property rights.

## Empirical Strategy and Data Sources

#### **The Instrumental Variable Method**

In the empirical part, the authors mainly use the **instrumental variable** (IV) method to estimate the impact of institutions on economic performance.

An ideal empirical study would randomly assign good institutions to some countries and bad institutions to others, and then observe their economic performance. But this is impossible in reality. The authors therefore use an alternative approach: finding a variable (an instrumental variable) that satisfies two conditions:

- First, the variable must be correlated with the endogenous variable (here, institutions);
- Second, the variable must affect the dependent variable (here, economic performance) only indirectly, through its influence on the endogenous variable. In other words, the instrumental variable itself must not directly affect the dependent variable.

In this study, the authors use the mortality of European settlers as the instrumental variable for institutions. They justify the validity of this instrument as follows:

- Settler mortality was closely related to early European settlement patterns, which in turn influenced the early institutions the settlers established, and those early institutions in turn influenced today's institutions. Settler mortality is therefore correlated with institutional quality;
- Settler mortality mainly reflects the disease environment **of that time**, which was exogenously given and does not itself directly affect **today's** economic performance. Its impact on economic development today arises mainly through its effect on institutions.

Below is a **simplified** version of the paper's strategy:

In the first stage, the authors estimate the effect of settler mortality on modern institutions. The regression equation is:
$$
R_i = \alpha + \beta \log(M_i) + X_i \gamma + \epsilon_i
$$
where $R_i$ is the modern institutions indicator, $M_i$ is settler mortality, $X_i$ is a set of control variables (such as geography and climate), and $\epsilon_i$ is the error term. The **modern institutions indicator** here is mainly a country's **protection against expropriation risk** index, which reflects the risk that private investment faces of being **expropriated by the government** or **nationalized**. The index was constructed between 1985 and 1995 by experts at PRS, who assessed a large amount of information; it ranges from 0 to 10, with higher scores indicating that private property faces a lower risk of expropriation, that is, better protection of property rights. The index is widely used by many international investors and is considered a good reflection of a country's institutional environment.

In the second stage, the authors use the estimates from the first stage to test the effect of institutions on modern economic performance. The regression equation is:

$$
\log(Y_i) = \delta + \theta \hat{R}_i + X_i \lambda + u_i
$$
where $\log(Y_i)$ is modern economic performance (the log of per capita GDP), $\hat{R}_i$ is the predicted value of the institutions indicator obtained from the first stage, $X_i$ is a set of control variables, and $u_i$ is the error term.

#### **Data Sources**

- Settler mortality data: mainly from Philip D. Curtin's research, in particular his data on the mortality of European settlers in different colonies, covering the mortality rates of soldiers, bishops, and sailors stationed in the colonies from the 17th to the 19th centuries. Hector Gutierrez (1986) used Vatican records to construct mortality data for bishops in Latin America, filling gaps in Curtin's data;
- Institutional quality data: the authors mainly use the "expropriation risk" indicator provided by Political Risk Services (PRS), which reflects the risk that private investment faced of government expropriation or nationalization between 1985 and 1995. Supplementary data include the World Bank's Worldwide Governance Indicators, such as rule of law and government effectiveness, as well as the "institutional constraints" indicator from Polity III.
- Other data: per capita GDP data come mainly from the World Bank's World Development Indicators database; the authors also use a series of geographic and climatic variables, historical population data, and so on.

## Results

- The OLS results show that **institutional quality** has a significantly positive correlation with per capita GDP, which remains significant across a variety of settings: the full sample, subsamples, controlling for continent dummies, and more: ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240611142350244.png)
- ⬇️ The graphical results show the same pattern: for example, the United States, Canada, and New Zealand are among the countries with the highest protection against expropriation indices, and also among the countries with the highest per capita GDP: ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240611142636000.png)
- Settler mortality is negatively correlated with protection against expropriation risk ⬇️: ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240611143113866.png)
- Early institutions are correlated with modern institutions: the higher the quality of early institutions, the higher the quality of modern institutions. Meanwhile, settler mortality has a significantly negative effect on the quality of early institutions: ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240611142838181.png)
- The two-stage least squares results show that the effect of institutions on economic performance is indeed significant: ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240611143329839.png)
- The paper also conducts a series of robustness checks, including excluding the **neo-European countries** (the US, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand) from the sample; controlling for geographic and climatic variables (temperature, humidity, latitude, natural resources, soil quality, and whether a country is landlocked); demographic data for malaria-prevalent regions; controlling for historical and cultural factors (legal origin, religion, and the degree of linguistic fragmentation); alternative measurements of the variables; and tests of the instrument's validity and consistency. The original results remain robust!

## Concluding Remarks

- Many economists and social scientists firmly believe that differences in institutions and national policies are the **root cause** of cross-country gaps in per capita income. However, there is no consensus on what determines institutions and "the attitude of governments toward economic processes." This makes it very difficult to identify exogenous factors behind institutional differences in order to estimate their effect on economic performance. In this paper, the authors establish that differences in colonial experience can serve as one source of exogenous variation in institutions;
- The mortality of European settlers significantly influenced the early institutional arrangements of the colonies. High-mortality regions tended to establish **extractive institutions**, while low-mortality regions tended to establish Europe-like institutions that **protected private property**, and these early institutions still influence countries' economic performance today;
- The findings of this paper do not claim that present-day institutions were predetermined by colonial policies and cannot be changed. What the paper emphasizes is that the colonial experience is one of many factors that influence institutions. Because settler mortality is arguably exogenous, it is very useful as an instrumental variable for isolating the effect of institutions on economic performance. In fact, what the paper's conclusions suggest is that, in poor countries, substantial income growth will come from **continuous improvement of institutions**;
- Future work needs to open the black box of institutions, that is, to determine what effects extractive institutions or property-rights-protecting institutions actually produce.

## Extra

- This paper has now been cited 18,381 times, the most cited of all of Acemoglu's publications;

- In response to this paper, David Y. Albouy, then a junior faculty member at the University of Michigan, wrote an 18-page comment, published in the AER in 2012. This comment has now been cited 500 times, making it his most cited work to date. In the comment, Albouy questioned the validity of the paper's settler mortality data and found that, after adjustments, the instrumental variable estimates became unreliable;

- In response to Albouy's comment, Acemoglu and his two co-authors wrote a 34-page reply, also published in the same issue of the AER. It opens by quoting a line from the historical literature, originally from the Book of Job in the Old Testament of the Bible: *Hither thou shalt come, but no further*. This is an archaic English expression meaning "**this far and no further**." (This line is also the title of the NBER working paper version of the reply.)

  > In this verse, God is speaking to Job, describing His control over the sea and His power to set its boundaries:
  >
  > "And said, Hitherto shalt thou come, but no further: and here shall thy proud waves be stayed."
  >
  > The usual Chinese translation reads:
  >
  > "又说：你只可到这里，不可越过；你狂傲的浪要到此止住。"
  >
  > In this passage, God uses these words to demonstrate His authority and His command over the natural world.

- In the concluding remarks, the authors call for opening the black box of institutions. Accordingly, in 2005 they published the paper "Unbundling Institutions" in the JPE, assessing the effects of property rights institutions and contracting institutions on economic performance.
