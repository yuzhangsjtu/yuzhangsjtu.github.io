> What determines differences in levels of regional development? Much of the literature holds that the impact of institutions on economic performance is critically important; however, whether this relationship is **causal** still faces numerous **endogeneity** challenges.
>
> This paper innovatively analyzes the profound influence of colonial policies on modern institutions and economic performance by introducing European **settler mortality** during the colonial era as an instrumental variable. The study finds that high-mortality regions typically established **extractive** institutions that lacked protection for private property and constraints on government, leading to poorer modern economic performance; whereas low-mortality regions established **inclusive** institutions similar to those in Europe, fostering economic development. Institutional differences among former colonies explain about three-quarters of today's per capita income gap. After controlling for institutional factors, geography factors cease to be important.

**Title:** The Colonial Origins of Comparative Development: An Empirical Investigation

**Journal:** *American Economic Review*

## Institutions as the Fundamental Cause of Cross-Country Per Capita Income Differences – Theory and Challenges

#### **The Importance of Institutions**

- Why do per capita incomes differ so greatly across countries? While there is no consensus on the answer, differences in **institutions** and **property rights** have received widespread attention in recent years;
- Countries with better institutions—such as more secure property rights and fewer **policy distortions**—invest more in physical and human capital, and utilize these factors more efficiently, thereby achieving higher income levels. This view is supported by some empirical studies that find a positive correlation between property rights protection and economic development;
- To some extent, the importance of institutions is obvious. In reality, the starkly divergent development paths of **South Korea and North Korea**, and **East Germany and West Germany**, demonstrate how some regions of a country stagnate under planned economies and collective ownership, while others develop rapidly under systems of private property and market economies.

#### **Empirical Challenges**

- Reliable evidence that institutional differences play a **decisive** role in cross-country per capita incomes is currently still lacking;
- **Reverse causality**: it is quite plausible that wealthy economies opt for good institutions, or are able to afford the costs of establishing them;
- Perhaps more importantly, economic development is a **composite outcome** of many factors, which simultaneously influence both institutions and per capita income levels.

## Core Hypothesis and Historical Background

#### **The Core Hypothesis of this Paper**

- To estimate the impact of institutions on economic development, it is necessary to find sources of **exogenous** institutional variation. This paper attempts to utilize the mortality rates of European settlers in the colonies during the Age of Exploration as a source of exogenous variation, studying countries that were colonized. This yields a sample of economies that had similar per capita income levels 400 years ago but exhibit enormous per capita income disparities today;

- The figure below illustrates the negative correlation between colonial settler mortality and per capita GDP across different countries in 1995: ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240611134907740.png)

- The detailed explanation is as follows:

  - Institutional differences across countries trace back to different types of colonial policies during the European colonization era. In some areas where Europeans could not settle, they established extractive institutions — lacking protection for private property and checks on governmental power — aiming to transfer resources from the colony to the colonizers. Such institutions were more common in high-mortality areas (such as Africa and tropical regions);
  - Conversely, in colonies where large numbers of Europeans could settle—like North America, Australia, and New Zealand—colonists created institutions similar to Europe’s, emphasizing private property protection and constraints on governmental power. These institutions promoted economic development;
  - These institutional differences have persisted to the present day and influence the economic performance of modern nations (the complete logical chain is shown below ⬇️);

#### **Historical Background**

Mortality affected settlement activity; settlement activity affected early institutional forms; early institutions persisted and shaped current institutions.

**Theory 1: Mortality Affected Settlement Activity**

- The mortality rate among European settlers was a crucial determinant of their ability to settle in colonies. High mortality meant settlement difficulties; conversely, low mortality facilitated settlement. The research of Philip D. Curtin meticulously documents European settler mortality rates across different colonial regions; this data is vital for understanding settlement patterns;
- Early British settlement plans in West Africa failed due to extremely high mortality rates. Curtin records numerous examples, including a 46% death rate among Europeans in their first year in the "Province of Freedom" (Sierra Leone), and a 61% death rate for Europeans on Bulama Island (April 1792 – April 1793). In the first year of the Sierra Leone Company (1792-1793), 72 percent of the European settlers died;
- The Pilgrim Fathers chose to migrate to America rather than Guyana, partly because of Guyana's high death rate;
- In 1795, the Beauchamp Committee decided to ship British convicts to Australia rather than the Gambia River islands, where mortality was high.

**Theory 2: Types of Colonies and Settlement Activities**

Disparities in settler mortality shaped distinct colonial types, primarily extractive colonies and settler colonies.

- **Extractive Colonies**: colonies whose primary purpose was resource extraction, with no European intention of large-scale permanent settlement; colonists concentrated on extracting as many resources as possible from the local area. These places typically lacked the protection of private property and restraints on government;
  - **Congo**: Belgium implemented an extremely extractive policy in the Congo, implementing very extractive policies to obtain rubber, ivory, and other resources. The colonial government set up a strict control system subjecting locals to high-intensity forced labor;
  - **Latin America**: Spanish and Portuguese colonial policies also focused on resource extraction, especially in regions rich with gold and silver deposits. In these areas, the Spanish Crown imposed strict monopolies and trade controls mainly to transfer wealth from the colonies.
- **Settler Colonies**: Colonies where European metropolises attracted a large influx of European immigrants tried to replicate European-style institutions. These colonies' institutions generally emphasized private property protection and restraints on government and state power, resembling European institutions.
  - **Australia**: Most early immigrants to Australia were convicts of the jammed gaols, yet they managed to obtain legal protections and political rights similar to Britain's getting who established systems emphasizing private property and checks on governmental power;
  - **New Zealand, Canada, and the United States:** Countries that attracted large numbers of European immigrants, established European-like governments and institutions, and emphasized the protection of private property and checks against government power.

> Manning (1988) summarized: “in the European metropoli, Westminister models won the day over those of Versailles… But outside Europe, Africa, Latin America, much of Asia notwithstanding certain bids for liberty, it was France's despotic and absolutist monarch Louis XIV, rather than the limited monarchy produced for Britain, installed in a structure by early colonial administrators almost as if fraternité plus liberty and plantation slavery coexisted very comfortably without égalité.”
