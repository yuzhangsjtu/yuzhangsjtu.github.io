### 1. The Impact of the Pandemic on the Real Estate Market

![image-20200525185713659](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/bMuS5HqiNY8T9Vh.png)

Prof. Deng’s assessment of the Wuhan housing market: significant pressure in the short term; remains promising in the medium to long term.

I have consulted friends in the real estate sector, and quite a few voices maintain an optimistic outlook on Wuhan’s housing market expectations.

**Property management**: The Wuhan real estate authority published a list of property companies providing satisfactory services, and later also released a list of those that drew public dissatisfaction. The quality of property management will become a key indicator for residents when buying a home (how long attention to this indicator will last, though?).

**Transportation**: Forecast — properties outside the city center with good transport links will appeal more to buyers (and carry lower pandemic risk).

- Risk of falling home prices and rents

This risk is mainly concentrated in weaker third- and fourth-tier cities. In these places, incomes have fallen and investment sentiment is shot through with caution; at the same time, weak population mobility hits the rental market especially hard.

Root causes: Third- and fourth-tier cities are population outflow areas. Under the influence of housing reforms and the renovation of old urban areas, prices rose; but after the pandemic there is no enduring driver to keep them rising.

Prof. Deng believes there is a fairly high probability of salary reductions (by around 20%) faced by the civil servant crowd who draw on the public purse this year.

- Developer debt risks (high levels of developers’ equity shares pledged)
- High net-gearing ratios among developers (a very large volume of maturing debt — over one trillion yuan coming due in the next two years) and large-scale bond issuance in Q1. As of April 8, several dozen developers have already been acquired or liquidated.
- The risk of a downward spiral in developer investment spending (payment collection is falling, the land market remains lackluster, credit is suffering, and new loans are barred from flowing into segments related to real estate development).
- Impact on repayment of individual loans, and problems with rent‑based loans.

### 2. Main Issues in the Real Estate Market

#### 2.1 High home prices vs. inadequate household housing affordability

![image-20200525191524000](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/quvaixeZGVr1729.png)

Dean Deng considers the officially released figure for Wuhan’s average new-home price during 2019 to be on the low side.

- High rent-to-income ratios (a normal ratio should not exceed 25% of income; 30% is the alarm threshold)

![image-20200525191835823](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/XzwgbYvKCr9dFox.png)

All cities surpass 25%.

![image-20200525191941925](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/EkLRMVZTqJGI1w8.png)

The vicious circle in which macro-control drives home prices higher and higher.

Fiscal reliance on land revenue.

![image-20200525192157347](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/fZ8OQKH1zcCaALw.png)

If one considers just **cities** (setting the rural sector aside), revenue from land transfers accounts for an even larger share of fiscal revenue. In 2019, this reached 1.1 times in Wuhan.

This is entangled with our nation’s fiscal set‑up (tax‑sharing between central and local government) and the cadre-evaluation system that emphasizes GDP.

![image-20200525192522092](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/t1d8nZQYU7x6Kza.png)

“Real estate that has the national economy in a chokehold” vs. “a national economy that requires real estate as oxygen” – a rivalry.

Investment-led growth that is already top-heavy.

![image-20200525193003927](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/D7GrIwEoYi6tfJg.png)

**1998**: Incubate the housing industry as a new economic growth engine and consumer hot spot in the national economy (in 1998, the monetization and socialization of housing allocation). This took place against the backdrop of the Southeast Asian financial crisis.

**2003**: The real estate sector becomes a pillar of the national economy (in relation to the SARS outbreak).

**2008**: In confronting the global financial crisis, the sweeping 4-trillion-yuan package demanded local governments vigorously shore up the real estate sector.

**2015**: A share-market crash spurs extreme market liquidity — a “tsunami” that rushes into the housing arena.

**Expectations**: To secure steady growth, targeted (as in strategically reenergized) infrastructure development investment will remain the primary means.



- Monetary accommodation with exceedingly superfluous liquidity (the “gargantuan liquidity tsunami” post‑2008)
