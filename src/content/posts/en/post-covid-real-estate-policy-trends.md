### 1. The Impact of the Pandemic on the Real Estate Market

![image-20200525185713659](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/bMuS5HqiNY8T9Vh.png)

Prof. Deng's assessment of the Wuhan housing market: significant pressure in the short term, but promising over the medium to long term.

Having consulted some friends in the real estate industry, quite a few voices hold an optimistic outlook on Wuhan's housing market prospects.

**Property management**: The Wuhan real estate authority published a list of property companies with satisfactory service, and later also released a list of property services the public was dissatisfied with. The quality of property management will become an important consideration for residents when buying a home in the future (but how long will attention to this indicator last?).

**Transportation**: Forecast — developments outside the central urban area with convenient transport will be more popular (lower pandemic risk).

- Risk of falling home prices and rents

This is mainly concentrated in weaker third- and fourth-tier cities. In these places, affected by the pandemic, incomes have fallen and investment sentiment is heavily wait-and-see; at the same time, population mobility is weak, and the rental market is significantly affected.

Root cause: third- and fourth-tier cities are population-outflow areas. Driven by housing reform, old-city renovation, and similar measures, prices rose; but after the pandemic, there is no enduring driver to keep them rising.

Prof. Deng believes that this year the group of civil servants who rely on public finances will, with fairly high probability, face pay cuts (on the order of 20%).

- Developer debt risk (large scale of developer equity pledges)

- High net-gearing ratios among developers (a large volume of maturing debt — over one trillion yuan coming due over the next two years) and large-scale bond issuance in Q1. As of April 8, several dozen developers had already been acquired or liquidated.
- Risk of declining developer investment growth (falling payment collection, a lackluster land market, credit setbacks, and new loans barred from flowing into real estate development).
- Impact on repayment of individual loans, and the problem of rent loans.

This is related to our country's tax system (the tax-sharing system) and the cadre-evaluation system (GDP).

Real estate holding the national economy hostage vs. the national economy holding real estate hostage.

Investment-led growth that is too heavy.

![image-20200525193003927](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/D7GrIwEoYi6tfJg.png)

**1998**: Cultivate the housing industry into a new economic growth point and consumption hotspot for the national economy (in 1998, the monetization and socialization of housing allocation). The Southeast Asian financial crisis.

**2003**: The real estate sector becomes a pillar industry of the national economy (SARS).

**2008**: To respond to the economic crisis, the four-trillion-yuan package required local governments to vigorously support real estate.

**2015**: The stock market crashed, and liquidity (a flood of money) poured into the housing market.

**Expectation**: To maintain growth, (new) infrastructure investment will remain the primary means.



- Monetary easing and excess liquidity (after 2008, releasing a flood of money)

![image-20200525193327336](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/cHzO2Joa4duNwlF.png)



- Unstable real estate control policies, which have altered home-buying expectations

![image-20200525193452514](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/iuoBMjmVqACI3Xh.png)

The control objectives are always wavering among maintaining growth, stabilizing home prices, and expanding employment; when growth momentum is insufficient, home prices are deregulated.

After purchase and price restrictions were lifted in 2015, home prices surged in a retaliatory rebound (first-tier cities, with a short inventory-clearing cycle and insufficient supply).

In 2016, price restrictions began again, along with divorce restrictions and resale restrictions.



#### 2.2 Uneven development among cities

![image-20200525194130343](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/Ud7ePDWhVpLRHmQ.png)



In the short term, look at policy; in the medium term, look at industry; in the long term, look at population.

#### 2.3 Irrational structure of housing demand (heavy on ownership, light on rental)

After the pandemic, **the emphasis on ownership may intensify**. The homeownership rate in developed countries is around 50%, while ours exceeds 90%, with a mismatch between where people work and where they live.

Over 70% of urban households' total wealth is allocated to housing.

![image-20200525194713393](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/OXEp4j9eV1Tvoa7.png)

![image-20200525194925364](https://i.loli.net/2020/05/25/wTCMUkzKxQislh1.png)

After the pandemic, the rental market needs more intervention!

The return on renting is too low — it takes 50-plus years to recoup the investment.

Companies that entered the rental market, such as Vanke and Sino-Ocean, are gradually withdrawing!

Social capital doesn't care.

The government should grant rental entities tax reductions, tax cuts, and subsidies (rental subsidies lack channels for distribution).

Q&A:

1. Some students believe home prices in third- and fourth-tier cities will rise (less affected by the pandemic?)

Answer: There's no problem for third- and fourth-tier cities around regional central cities, but other cities lack population support.

2. The reasons for the rise in Shenzhen home prices

Answer: Shenzhen has more than 20 million people, but housing in Shenzhen's urban districts accounts for only 48% of the total, while urban villages outside the city limits account for 52% (15 million people). After the easing of policy, demand was released.

3. Will the LPR get locked in at a loss?

Answer: No. The impact of the pandemic is long-lasting.

4. The trend of Wuhan home prices

Answer: Wuhan's average price is currently not high (compared with cities of the same tier), around 20,000 yuan, and there is still some room for increase within the Third Ring Road.

5. How to stabilize rental commercial shops

Answer: Government tax reductions, and two-way [price restrictions].

6. Will the real estate bubble burst?

Answer: No. With monetary easing on a global scale, people will choose to allocate assets into physical housing.

7. The effect of collective construction land entering the market

Answer: Remains to be seen.

8. The problem of small-property-rights housing

Answer: The total amount is over 2 billion square meters; it will be legalized by category and gradually. Housing on non-permanent basic farmland, and within adjustable plans, will be legalized.
