> Is history driven by grand **structural forces**, or by the decisions of **key individuals**? Focusing on European monarchs from the late medieval to early modern period, this paper attempts to answer this classic historical debate. The researchers dug deep into historical documents, constructing quantitative indicators for **monarchs’ cognitive ability** and **state performance**, and innovatively deployed the royal **coefficient of inbreeding** as an instrumental variable, ingeniously resolving the endogeneity problem. The study finds that monarchs’ cognitive ability is significantly and positively correlated with national performance: more capable monarchs led their countries to greater prosperity. Notably, the effect of a monarch’s ability was more pronounced in settings where **royal power was barely restrained by institutions such as parliaments**. This research not only provides empirical evidence for the view that “**heroes make history**” but also offers a fresh perspective for understanding how leaders shape the course of history.

## Research Background and Question

The relationship between **national leaders** and the **destiny of nations** has long been one of the core debates in the social sciences. In the 19th century, historian Thomas Carlyle famously advanced the “**great man theory** of history,” asserting that “the history of the world is but the biography of great men.” This view holds that the historical process is primarily driven by outstanding individuals, and that a nation’s rise and fall depends on the caliber and deeds of its rulers. During the same period, an opposing perspective, typified by **Marxism**, stressed the determining role of structural factors, arguing that deep-seated forces such as **population and the economy** shape state performance, and that the emergence of leaders is itself a product of these structural forces. In this view, leaders are seen as “**servants of history**,” as expressed by Fernand Braudel: “Man does not create history; on the contrary, it is initially history that creates man.” In short, scholarship has long oscillated between the notions that “heroes make history” and “history makes the man.”

In recent years, economists have attempted to provide evidence for this historical controversy using quantitative methods. Jones and Olken (2005) exploited the unanticipated leadership transitions caused by the **natural or accidental deaths of national leaders** in the post–World War II period, finding significant changes to countries’ economic growth paths following a leader change, thereby demonstrating that leaders can indeed affect national economic performance. A subsequent study by Besley et al. (2011) further found that if a deceased leader was highly educated, the negative impact of their death on economic growth was even more pronounced. Yet these contemporary studies left some issues unresolved:

- First, leader “**quality**” is difficult to observe directly—the studies mentioned above often used changes in economic growth around a leader’s tenure as a proxy for leadership, which is confounded by other influences;
- Second, even if a leader’s exit time is exogenous (e.g., an accidental death), the selection of a successor may still be **endogenous** (for instance, chosen by the ruling party or the elite);
- Third, and the central concern of this paper, systematic **causal evidence** testing the impact of “**great individuals**” on national rise and fall in historical settings has been lacking. Put differently, we have remained uncertain whether, across the long sweep of state-building, individual monarchs’ talent truly shaped nations’ destinies or whether broad structural forces were the determinant factor.

Addressing this gap, Ottinger and Voigtländer turn their gaze to the epoch of European monarchy from the late Middle Ages to the early modern period (roughly CE 1000–1800). This was a critical phase in the foundation of the modern nation-state, traditionally considered an important testing ground for the debate between the “great man theory” and structural explanations. The core question this paper seeks to answer is: **Did the personal ability of European monarchs exert a significant *causal* impact on the performance of the states they ruled?**  Through a rigorous empirical strategy, the authors aim to determine the extent to which monarchs’ ability, historically, actually “**made history**,” and to what extent a nation’s rise and fall were driven by established structural conditions. In short, this study investigates whether, in medieval and early modern Europe, “**capable rulers**” bestowed stronger performance, territorial expansion, and developmental momentum on their states, and probes how this influence varied with or without institutional constraints.

## Data and Methodology

#### **Dataset**

To study the question outlined above, the authors constructed a new **reign-level** dataset encompassing European monarchs from the 10th to the 18th centuries and the performance of their states. Specifically, the data include 339 monarchs from 13 major European countries, with each treated as a single observation. These countries represent the leading kingdoms of Europe of the day (e.g., England, France, Spain, etc., the exact list being based on the research scope of the historian **Woods (1913)**). For each monarch, the following core variables were recorded:

- **Monarch Ability (Ruler’s Capability):** The authors adopt the historian Frederick Adams Woods’s assessments of the abilities of members of European royal houses from his works (1906, 1913) as their measure of a monarch’s “cognitive ability.” Woods scored hundreds of royal figures on their qualifications regarding **Intellect and Moral Traits**, providing short comments and supporting sources for each ruler. Building on these historical documents, Woods **classified** monarchs’ intellectual talents on a scale from low to high ability (e.g., “–” denoting low ability, “+” high ability, with intermediate steps such as “±”). The authors treat Woods’s evaluations as proxies for a monarch’s “cognitive ability” and quantify them for analysis as a continuous variable (for example via reasonable standardization–the raw categories stand from – to + by a  as including degrees).
