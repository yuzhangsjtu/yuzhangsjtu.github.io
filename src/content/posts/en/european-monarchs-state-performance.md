> Is the course of history driven by grand **structural forces**, or shaped by the decisions of **key individuals**? Taking as its subject the monarchs of late-medieval to early-modern Europe, this paper attempts to answer this classic historical debate. The researchers dug deep into the historical literature, constructed quantitative indicators of **monarchs' cognitive ability** and **state performance**, and innovatively used the royal "**coefficient of inbreeding**" as an instrumental variable, ingeniously resolving the problem of endogeneity. The study finds that a monarch's cognitive ability is significantly and positively correlated with national performance: more capable monarchs were better able to lead their countries toward prosperity. The monarch's ability mattered especially in settings where **royal power was not constrained by institutions such as parliaments**. This research not only provides empirical evidence for the view that "**heroes make history**," but also offers us a new perspective for understanding the role of leaders in the historical process.

## Research Background and Question

The relationship between **national leaders** and the **fate of nations** has long been one of the central controversies in the social sciences. In the 19th century, the historian Thomas Carlyle proposed the famous "**great man theory of history**," namely that "the history of the world is but the biography of great men." This view holds that the historical process is driven primarily by outstanding individuals, and that a nation's rise and fall depends on the talents and deeds of its rulers. During the same period, another view, represented by **Marxism**, emphasized the determining role of structural factors, arguing that deep forces such as **population and the economy** shape state performance, and that the very emergence of leaders is itself a product of these structural forces. Under this view, leaders are regarded as "**servants of history**," as Braudel put it: "Man does not create history; on the contrary, it is first of all history that creates man." In short, scholars have long debated between the two notions of "heroes make history" and "history makes the man."

In recent years, economists have tried to provide evidence for this historical controversy using quantitative methods. Jones and Olken (2005) exploited the unexpected leadership turnover brought about by the **natural deaths or accidental deaths of national leaders after World War II**, and found that the path of a country's economic growth changed significantly after a change of leader, demonstrating that leaders do indeed affect a country's economic performance. The study by Besley et al. (2011) further found that if the deceased leader had received a good education, the negative impact of his death on economic growth was even more pronounced. However, these modern studies still leave unresolved problems:

- First, leader "**quality**" is difficult to observe directly; the studies above often use the change in economic growth before and after a leader's tenure as a proxy variable for leadership, but this is simultaneously affected by other factors;
- Second, even if a leader's departure time is exogenous (such as an accidental death), the selection of a successor may still be **endogenous** (for example, chosen by the ruling party or the elite);
- Finally, and the focus of this paper, in historical settings there is a lack of systematic **causal evidence** to test the impact of "**great men**" on the rise and fall of nations. In other words, we still do not know whether, over the long historical process of state-building, the talents of individual monarchs truly shaped the fate of nations, or whether macro-structural factors were the decisive force.

To address the above research gap, Ottinger and Voigtländer turn their attention to the period of European monarchies from the late Middle Ages to the early modern era (roughly 1000 to 1800 CE). This period was a crucial stage in the founding of the modern nation-state, and has always been regarded as an important setting for testing the dispute between the "great man theory of history" and structuralism. The core question the paper seeks to answer is: **Did the personal ability of European monarchs have a significant causal effect on the performance of the states they ruled?** The authors hope, through a rigorous empirical strategy, to determine the extent to which the high or low ability of monarchs in history "**made history**," or whether the rise and fall of nations was driven mainly by given structural conditions. In short, what this study sets out to resolve is whether, in medieval and early modern Europe, an "**enlightened ruler**" could confer stronger performance, territorial expansion, and developmental momentum upon a state, and how this effect differed in the presence or absence of institutional constraints.

## Data and Methods

#### **Dataset**

To study the above question, the authors constructed a new **dynasty-reign** (reign-level) dataset covering the monarchs of major European countries from the 10th to the 18th centuries and the performance of the states they ruled. Specifically, the data contain 339 monarchs from 13 major European countries, with each monarch treated as one observation. These countries include the major kingdoms of Europe at the time (for example England, France, Spain, etc., the specific list being based on the research scope of the historian **Woods (1913)**). For each monarch, the data record the following core variables:

- **Monarch ability (ruler's ability):** The authors adopt the historian Frederick Adams Woods's assessment of the abilities of members of European royal families in his works (1906, 1913) as a measure of a monarch's "cognitive ability." Woods rated the **psychological and moral qualities** of hundreds of royal figures, and provided a brief commentary and reference sources for each ruler. On the basis of these historical documents, Woods **coded** monarchs' intellectual talents into a classification from low to high (for example, "–" indicating low ability, "+" indicating high ability, with intermediate grades such as "±"). The authors treat Woods's assessments as indicators of a monarch's "cognitive ability," and in the analysis quantify them for use as a continuous variable (for example through standardization). It should be noted that, although Woods strove to assess individual ability independently of state performance when scoring, because this was, after all, a subjective assessment based on the historical record, there may be endogeneity bias—for example, if state performance influenced the historian's impression of a monarch's ability, this would create reverse causality. The authors deal with this specifically in their model (see the causal-identification strategy below).
- **State performance:** To measure the performance of the state during each monarch's reign, the authors use three different indicators. The first is the **subjective evaluation of the state's overall performance** provided by Woods (1913), a composite indicator that, based on several sub-indicators, makes an overall judgment of the state's rise and fall during that reign. The authors call it the "State Performance" indicator. This subjective indicator may incorporate a composite assessment of economic prosperity, social stability, territorial change, and so on. Given that the subjective scoring of a single historian may be biased, the authors introduce two objective indicators as supplements: the second is the **percentage change in the state's territorial area** during the monarch's reign, computed from the data on national borders every five years from 1100 to 1790 in Europe provided by Abramson (2017), giving the net change in territorial area between the start and end years of each monarch's reign; the third is the **change in urban population**, which combines the above changes in territorial borders with the historical urban-population data of Bairoch et al. (1988) to compute the increase or decrease in the total urban population within the state's territory during each monarch's reign. Through these three indicators, the authors characterize the state's performance under a given monarch from both subjective and objective angles.
- **Coefficient of inbreeding:** From a detailed database of European royal genealogies, the authors collected a measure of the blood relationship between each monarch's parents, namely the "**coefficient of inbreeding**." This coefficient reflects how probable it is that a monarch's parents inherited the same ancestral genes; the higher its value, the closer the parents' blood relationship and the higher the degree of inbreeding. A high inbreeding coefficient often leads to the risk that offspring exhibit "inbreeding depression," including declines in cognitive ability, health problems, and so on. Therefore, this coefficient can be regarded as a potential factor affecting a monarch's ability. The authors successfully computed this value for the majority of monarchs with complete family-genealogy records.

**Research design and causal identification:** The paper uses the **instrumental-variable (IV) method** to identify the causal effect of monarch ability on state performance. The core idea is to exploit the "natural-experiment" conditions provided by European succession institutions and royal-marriage patterns to obtain observations of approximately random variation in rulers' ability:

1. **The hereditary succession system provides an exogenous mechanism for leader turnover:** Medieval and early-modern European dynasties generally practiced the hereditary system of **succession from father to son**. That is to say, when a monarch died, he was usually succeeded by his direct descendant (the eldest son or a designated heir), without regard to the heir's level of ability. This succession rule to a large extent **decouples a monarch's ability from the order of succession**. In short, a prince of mediocre ability might ascend the throne solely by virtue of bloodline, while an extremely talented distant relative who was not in the direct line of succession had no claim to the throne. This feature gives the allocation of monarch ability a certain **randomness**—rulers did not obtain the throne through a talent-based selection, but mainly through order of birth. The authors' data also show that in most cases succession to the throne did indeed follow a predetermined bloodline rather than considerations of performance. Of course, in history there were also a few abnormal successions (such as coups, elected monarchs, etc.), which the authors mark and control for in their data processing (for example by adding a "hereditary succession" dummy variable, in order to control for the bias that irregular succession might introduce).

2. **Royal consanguineous intermarriage produced quasi-random fluctuations in monarch ability:** To consolidate power and maintain their bloodline, the various European royal houses frequently intermarried among close relatives. Because people at the time did not understand the genetic disadvantages of inbreeding, but on the contrary believed that "pure royal blood" was an advantage. In fact, it was not until the **early 20th century** that the biological sciences recognized the adverse effect of inbreeding on the health and ability of offspring, and began to measure the inbreeding coefficient. More importantly, because royal genealogies are extremely complex, many consanguineous relationships are "hidden" across generations, and people at the time found it very difficult to trace all the blood connections. All of this means that a monarch's **degree of inbreeding was quite random at the time**: the royal houses did not deliberately cultivate "low-ability" heirs; quite the opposite, they may have believed that consanguineous marriage was conducive to preserving an excellent bloodline. Therefore, the inbreeding coefficient of monarchs of different periods and different families exhibits a considerable degree of chance variation, providing us with a natural-experiment variable. The authors use a monarch's coefficient of inbreeding as an instrumental variable to explain variation in a monarch's cognitive ability. As long as the relevance and exogeneity conditions of an instrumental variable are satisfied, variation in this coefficient can be used to identify the causal effect of monarch ability on state performance.

   - **Relevance (first stage)**: It is necessary to prove that the degree of inbreeding did significantly affect a monarch's cognitive ability. That is to say, monarchs with a higher inbreeding coefficient tended to have lower ability. The authors verify this in the data, finding that the inbreeding coefficient has a strong negative predictive effect on monarchs' ability scores. This relationship also accords with genetic theory and historical cases (for example, **Charles II** of the Spanish Habsburg dynasty, who was intellectually impaired owing to a high degree of inbreeding, is a typical example mentioned by the authors).
   - **Exogeneity (exclusion restriction)**: It requires that the coefficient of inbreeding can affect state performance only through its effect on monarch ability, and that there is no other independent channel. In other words, if a high inbreeding coefficient directly caused a decline in state performance, rather than doing so by lowering the monarch's talent, then the IV strategy would be invalid. The authors conducted extensive investigations to ensure exogeneity. On the one hand, as noted above, people at the time did not recognize the harms of inbreeding, so there is no sign that **consanguineous marriage was consciously linked to strategies of state governance**—this point is important, because if a king's parents chose consanguineous or distant-relative marriages for political reasons and thereby affected the state, for example for the sake of alliances or territory, this might introduce bias. The authors address this potential problem by exploiting the "**hidden**" inbreeding coefficient: they decompose the inbreeding coefficient into the part due to direct close-relative marriage of the parents (such as marriage between cousins), and the part accumulated from hidden blood relationships in more distant generations of the genealogy. Even after removing the component in which the parents are close relatives (which may be the result of a conscious choice), using only the degree of hidden inbreeding accumulated across generations as the instrumental variable, the results remain robust. This indicates that the inbreeding coefficient affects state performance mainly through its effect on monarch ability, rather than through other factors. In addition, the authors also control for other variables that may affect state performance in order to strengthen the exclusion restriction, such as the frequency of war (the proportion of years of war during a monarch's reign), strategies of gaining allies or territory through marriage, and the previous generation's state performance (the performance during the preceding monarch's reign). A series of robustness checks show that the estimation results are almost unchanged after introducing these controls. In sum, the coefficient of inbreeding as an instrumental variable satisfies, both theoretically and empirically, the requirements of relevance and exogeneity reasonably well, providing support for identifying the causal effect of monarch ability.

#### **Model and Estimation**

Combining the above elements, the authors mainly use two-stage least squares (2SLS) for estimation. In the first-stage regression, monarch ability (the standardized value of Woods's cognitive scores) is predicted by the coefficient of inbreeding; in the second stage, the monarch ability predicted in the first stage is used to estimate the effect on state performance (examining the three indicators of subjective performance score, percentage change in territory, and percentage change in urban population, respectively). The model adds country fixed effects (controlling for the constant institutional or geographic differences across countries) and time-trend controls (although, as the authors note, because the monarchs of different countries reigned for varying lengths of time and overlapped with one another, linear era fixed effects cannot simply be added; the authors purge era factors by comparing monarchs of different countries who reigned during the same period). To test robustness, the authors also conduct a **difference-in-differences-style paired regression**: they compare, by differencing, two monarchs who reigned in different countries during the same period, which can simultaneously eliminate the common effects of country and time. In addition, the authors re-checked and expanded Woods's historical scores, including independently re-scoring monarch ability and performance themselves and with assistants on the basis of encyclopedia materials, with results highly consistent with Woods's; they also incorporated more countries into the sample (such as Poland, Hungary, etc.) and extended the time horizon to 1914, finding the main conclusions unchanged. Through the above methods, the paper does its utmost to ensure the credibility of the estimates, reducing the endogeneity problem to a minimum.

## Key Findings

Through historical data and instrumental-variable analysis, this paper reaches the following key conclusions:

- **Monarch ability is positively correlated with state performance, with a causal effect:** The higher a ruler's cognitive ability, the better his state's performance during his reign. A simple correlation analysis first shows that the phenomenon of "the state flourishes under an enlightened ruler, and the state declines under a benighted ruler" was widespread in history. More importantly, the instrumental-variable estimates support the causal nature of this relationship. The authors find that monarchs with a higher degree of inbreeding tended to have worse state performance (this is a "reduced-form estimate" test): each one-standard-deviation increase in the inbreeding coefficient leads to a decline of about 0.25 standard deviations in the state's overall performance index, a shrinkage of the state's territory by about 5%, and a decline in urban population by about 4%. At the same time, the coefficient of inbreeding is significantly negatively correlated with monarch ability, indicating that the decline in ability brought about by consanguineous marriage is an important cause of the decline in state performance. Through IV estimation, the authors quantify the causal effect of monarch ability: when a monarch's cognitive ability increases by one standard deviation, the state's overall performance index rises by about 0.8 standard deviations, the territorial area expands by about 16%, and the urban population grows by about 14%. This result indicates that in medieval and early modern Europe, differences in the personal talent of leaders could bring about significant differences in the fate of nations, and the magnitude of this effect was quite considerable. ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250224152812414.png)
- **The main channel of influence is cognitive ability (intelligence), not other traits:** The authors further explore the specific mechanism by which inbreeding affects the states ruled by monarchs. Through exploratory mediation analysis, they find that **about 75% of inbreeding's adverse effect on state performance operates through lowering the monarch's cognitive ability**. By contrast, other aspects that inbreeding may affect (such as non-cognitive traits like emotional stability, or physical qualities such as health, height, number of offspring, etc.) did not significantly transmit to state performance. In other words, the **intellectual factor** within the "talent for governing a state" is the key that determines a nation's rise and fall. A monarch with outstanding cognitive ability is better at governing the state, whereas emotional temperament or physical strength has a relatively limited effect on the effectiveness of governance. This confirms to some extent the emphasis in political psychology on the importance of leaders' cognitive ability.
- **Talented monarchs strengthen the state through pragmatic governance and prudent use of arms:** To reveal the specific actions by which "enlightened rulers" promote national development, the authors examine the effect of monarch ability on fiscal affairs, law, trade, war, and other domains. The results show that **high-ability monarchs tended to improve state finances, promote commerce and trade, strengthen the rule of law and social order, and raise the living conditions of the people**. Militarily, they reduced unnecessary involvement in foreign wars, but when they chose to go to war they were often more likely to win. The statistics show that higher-ability monarchs went to war slightly less often, but won a higher proportion of battles, and thus expanded their territory by carefully selecting opportunities for war, with a tendency to acquire high-value urbanized regions. This means that outstanding monarchs were not blindly bellicose, but were able to "make wise decisions about conflict," achieving territorial expansion and national prosperity while reducing the costs of war. These mechanistic findings provide concrete corroboration for why monarch ability affects state performance: **a wise leader is better at governing the state and managing its finances and at strategic decision-making, thereby bringing about national progress on many fronts**.
- **Institutional constraints weaken the effect of monarch ability:** It is worth noting that the paper finds that the magnitude of a monarch's role depends on whether his power is subject to institutional checks. The authors construct an indicator measuring the **degree of constraint** on a monarch's power (based on the Polity IV modern-state institutional scores combined with the historical record, such as the development of parliamentary power), used to distinguish two situations: "royal power essentially unrestrained" and "royal power subject to substantive limits." The empirical results show: only when a monarch's power was not subject to much constraint did the level of his personal ability significantly affect state performance. For those monarchs strongly constrained by parliaments or nobles (such as monarchs of constitutionally limited or powerful-parliament eras), whether wise or benighted, there was no significant difference in indicators such as territorial expansion and urban development under their rule. In other words, **when power was highly concentrated in an individual**, the "talent for governing a state" could be fully brought to bear; whereas when power was subject to institutional constraints, the operation of the state relied more on institutions and collective decision-making, and the influence of the individual monarch was greatly diluted. This finding fits the theoretical expectations of political economy regarding the relationship between institutions and the role of leaders: **strong constraining institutions (such as representative bodies and rule of law) can prevent a nation's fate from depending excessively on an individual, thereby to some extent achieving the outcome that "even a mediocre ruler will not ruin the country."**

In sum, the paper's findings provide strong evidence supporting the following historical picture: in medieval and early modern Europe, **individual monarchs did indeed, to a considerable degree, "shape history."** Talented monarchs made the state stronger through good governance and prudent territorial expansion through war; whereas monarchs of insufficient ability often led to national decline and loss of territory. This effect was especially pronounced in an environment of autocratic centralization, directly reflecting the influence of "heroes making the times." And as institutions such as parliaments developed and grew stronger, the importance of the individual relatively declined, and the rise and fall of the nation was determined more by the institutional framework, reflecting the factor of "the times making the hero." When the two sets of conditions intertwine, the development of history bears both the imprint of "great men" and the constraint of the institutional environment.

## Contributions and Significance

This research makes important contributions at both the literature and the practical levels.

First, the **innovations in data and method** open a new perspective for studying European history. The authors built a **reign-by-reign** dataset covering more than a dozen major European countries over several centuries, recording in detail monarchs' ability and the state's performance, and taking into account the dynamic changes in national borders. By contrast, past quantitative studies of differences in historical development often took modern national borders and long cycles (such as 50 years, per-capita GDP, or urbanization rate, etc.) as their units. The fine-grained data of this paper allow researchers to analyze the historical process at the **ruler-dynasty** level, bridging the gap between micro-level leadership traits and macro-level national development. Second, the authors introduce the "**coefficient of inbreeding**" as an instrumental variable, ingeniously exploiting the quasi-random variation provided by royal-marriage genealogies to achieve causal identification of the role of leaders in historical settings. This methodological innovation provides a new exemplar for research in political and economic history—demonstrating that even in the distant past, where experiments cannot be conducted and no modern natural experiment can be found, factors of a "natural experiment" can still be mined to identify causal relationships.

In terms of theoretical contribution, the paper is the first study to provide systematic evidence for **medieval and early modern Europe**, demonstrating the **causal effect of individual leaders on the rise and fall of nations**. This finding provides an empirical answer to the enduring "great men vs. structure" debate: differences in leaders' ability did indeed produce significant differences in state performance, thereby supporting the view that "great men make history." However, the paper also emphasizes the importance of context—the role of leaders can be brought fully to bear only when institutions place almost no limits on the individual; conversely, under strong constraints the influence of the individual is weakened. Therefore, this research does not simply take the side of one traditional view, but reveals the **complementary relationship between leaders and institutions**: in pre-modern Europe, individual monarchs played a large role, but as institutions arose, their role exhibited diminishing returns. This insight enriches our understanding of the dynamics of state formation and long-run development. For example, it echoes the recognition in modern institutional economics of the value of **checks-and-balances mechanisms**—strong institutions can mitigate the impact of uncertain individual factors on a nation, thereby improving the stability and predictability of governance.

In addition, the paper's findings have implications for real-world policy and subsequent research. First, in the modern context, although democratic polities and institutional constraints are already relatively mature, there are still many countries and organizations that depend heavily on individual leadership. This research reminds us: **when power is excessively concentrated, the quality of leaders will profoundly affect organizational performance**. Therefore, in such situations, selecting high-ability leaders and preventing decision-making power from falling into the hands of the mediocre is extremely crucial for a nation's rise and fall. Second, this research emphasizes the importance of **establishing and improving checks-and-balances mechanisms**: as history shows, strong institutions can prevent catastrophic consequences caused by individual incompetence. By the same token, contemporary states should strive to perfect the rule of law, parliaments, and checks and balances on power, so as to reduce dependence on a single leader, so that regardless of a leader's talent, the state can maintain basic operation and development. In sum, the paper provides a lesson for modern governance from historical experience: **one must both attend to selecting able and virtuous leaders, and perfect institutions to guard against individual mistakes**.

Academically, this paper opens a new direction for the quantitative study of the influence of leaders in history. It demonstrates that the tools of modern econometrics can be applied to historical materials to distill reliable causal inferences. This has a demonstrative effect for fields such as political and economic history, the history of economic growth, and the economics of leadership. Future researchers can, building on the data and methods provided by the authors, further explore the role of leaders in other regions of history (such as the Asian imperial systems) or in different periods (such as antiquity, or the transition to the modern era), thereby constructing a more comprehensive theoretical framework of "**leaders and long-run development**."

## Potential Limitations

Although the paper's contributions are outstanding and its research design is meticulous, some limitations and points requiring caution still remain:

- **The subjectivity and accuracy of the historical assessments:** The paper's measures of monarch ability and performance rely mainly on the historical record of Woods (1913). Such indicators, based on a historian's evaluation, inevitably carry subjectivity. For example, a historian, in judging a monarch as "able" or "incapable," may be influenced by the outcomes of the state under his rule, thereby creating an ex post judgment bias. However, the authors have already had several research assistants independently re-score part of the sample and compare it with Woods's results, finding that the consistency correlation coefficient between different scorers reaches 0.73–0.82, indicating that Woods's data are quite reliable. Moreover, it is worth mentioning that Woods himself held a "**social Darwinist**" view, assuming that consanguineous marriage would produce superior rulers. This means that if any bias existed, it would, on the contrary, tend to overestimate the ability of inbred monarchs and underestimate their adverse effect on the state, thereby tending to underestimate rather than exaggerate the effect found in the paper. Even so, the coarseness of the historical scores may still mask some subtle differences—for example, the different effects on performance of a monarch's different types of talent (military vs. domestic administration)—which exceeds the discriminating power of Woods's simple positive/negative evaluation.
- **The exogeneity assumption of the instrumental variable may be limited:** The instrumental-variable strategy requires that the coefficient of inbreeding affect state performance through no channel other than the monarch's talent. The authors have done their best to argue this point, but there are still potential factors worth discussing. On the one hand, if certain dynasties **consciously** pursued political interests through marriage (for example, a "strong-with-strong" marriage to consolidate an alliance or expand territory), then consanguineous or distant-relative marriage may not be random, but rather related to national strategy. Such a situation would violate the exogeneity assumption of the IV—fortunately the authors, by using the "**hidden coefficient of inbreeding**," exclude the influence of direct close-relative marriage of the parents, reducing the risk of such endogeneity. On the other hand, besides lowering cognitive ability, inbreeding may directly bring about problems such as poor health of the monarch or scarcity of offspring, thereby affecting national stability (for example, the early death of a monarch or the absence of an heir triggering a succession crisis). The authors control for these **physical factors** in the appendix (such as lifespan, number of children, etc.) and find that they are not the main channel. But we must still acknowledge that the causal effect obtained from the IV analysis is a local average treatment effect (LATE), applicable mainly to explaining the impact on the state of ability differences caused by inbreeding. This estimate does not necessarily represent the magnitude of the effect of ability differences from all sources. Therefore, if certain changes in a monarch's ability did not stem from bloodline (for example, talent improved through education or personal experience), its effect may differ from the value estimated in this paper.
- **Extrapolation across time and region:** This research focuses on European monarchs from 1000 to 1800, with subjects mainly the several major powers of Western and Central Europe. Although these cases are very crucial for understanding the origins of the modern state, whether their conclusions can be generalized to other temporal and spatial contexts requires caution. First, the challenges of governing a state and the standards of evaluation may differ across periods. For example, medieval evaluations of "state performance" emphasized **territory** and **order**, whereas modern society may pay more attention to the economy and people's livelihoods. Second, the political cultures and succession systems outside Europe (such as Asian empires and African kingdoms) differ, so the role of monarch ability may differ from the European experience. If future research can collect similar data from other regions, it will help test the universality of this paper's conclusions. Third, the authors are unable to add explicit era fixed effects to the regression, which means it is difficult to fully remove the influence of cross-century macro trends. Although the matched-difference method partly alleviates this problem, there may still be certain slowly evolving **structural trends** (such as technological progress, climate change, population pressure, etc.) that simultaneously affect the performance of multiple countries. If these long-run trends happen to be correlated with the degree of family inbreeding (although there is no clear reason, coincidence cannot be ruled out), it may affect the purity of the causal inference. However, given that the authors have already added fixed effects at the country level and controlled for most era effects through the contemporaneous-comparison method, the impact of this problem should be limited.
- **Sample selection and scope:** The dataset contains only the 13 major countries assessed by Woods, and some small states or short-lived regimes are not covered. This means that the results of this paper are more representative of the situation of the major powers of medieval and modern Europe. For those countries not included, whether monarch ability is equally important remains uncertain. In addition, the authors cut off the sample period at around the time each country entered the 19th century; with the rise of the Industrial Revolution and the wave of democratization, the relationship between leaders and national development may have undergone a qualitative change. For example, in the democracies that appeared in the 19th and 20th centuries, the mechanism for producing leaders differs (election rather than heredity), leaders turn over frequently and are subject to term limits, and whether a similar effect can still be observed is worth exploring. In the appendix, the authors extend the sample to 1914 and incorporate more monarchical states, finding that the results remain robust. However, once one goes beyond the framework of monarchy (such as a modern presidential state), the nature of the leader's influence needs to be reassessed. Therefore, this paper's conclusions should be understood mainly as a description of the causal mechanism within **pre-modern monarchical states**, and can offer only indirect implications for modern political systems.

Overall, despite the above limitations, the authors, through various robustness checks and supplementary analyses, have reduced the impact of the limitations as much as possible. In reading the paper, we need to recognize these potential problems, but at the same time we should also note the efforts the authors made to address each problem. Therefore, these limitations do not substantively weaken the paper's central conclusion; on the contrary, they point the direction for further improvement and extension in subsequent research.

## AI's Personal Commentary

**Evaluation of paper quality:** Reading through the full text, one can sense that this is a rigorous and creative piece of research. The paper's publication in the top economics journal *Econometrica* also confirms its academic standard. In their research design, the authors ingeniously transform historical institutional features into an identification strategy, and the innovation of using the coefficient of inbreeding as an instrumental variable is especially praiseworthy. In terms of data work, they hand-built a monarch-state panel dataset spanning 800 years; this involved an enormous amount of work and required meticulous verification of historical sources, reflecting the authors' solid command of historiography and their data-processing ability. In the econometric analysis, the authors consider multiple robustness checks and analyze and control, one by one, the possible sources of endogeneity, making the causal inference convincing. The paper's exposition is logically clear: starting from the theoretical controversy, it gradually introduces the empirical strategy and findings, both responding to a grand academic question and providing concrete data support. The only point open to discussion is that the discussion of certain complex mechanisms is somewhat brief—for example, although the measurement of parliamentary constraints is explained, it still feels summary, and readers might expect more historical detail as illustration. However, this does not detract from the paper's overall persuasiveness and readability.

**Outlook for future research:** This research opens the door to "the influence of leaders in history," and there are many interesting directions worth exploring afterward:

- **Cross-cultural and cross-regional comparison:** This paper focuses on Europe; similar research could be conducted on the role of historical rulers in other civilizations. For example, do the emperors of successive Chinese dynasties, the sultans of the Ottoman Empire, and so on, exhibit a similar pattern in which "ability affects the rise and fall of the nation"? These regions have different bureaucratic systems and succession rules, and so could test the universality of this paper's conclusions. In addition, one could compare the similarities and differences between Western European monarchy and the monarchies of Eastern Europe, the Middle East, and other regions.
- **Different types of talent and leadership styles:** Woods's scores mainly measure overall intelligence/talent, but leadership may be multidimensional. Future research could attempt to distinguish the roles in governing a state of **cognitive ability** (such as ingenuity, learning ability) and **non-cognitive ability** (such as daring, diplomatic skill, personal charisma). For example, some leaders excel at military conquest, others at internal governance, and these strengths may have differing effects on state performance under different environments. Through finer historical assessment or case studies, one might enrich the understanding of "which talents matter most."
- **Policy choices and specific governance behaviors:** Although this paper analyzes the mechanism through battle win rates, war frequency, and so on, there are still more details worth mining. For example, are high-ability monarchs more inclined to carry out certain reforms (such as tax reform, legislative innovation)? Were their responses during famines or crises more skillful? Combined with qualitative historical research, one could link leaders' ability to specific policy events, deepening our understanding of the mechanism.
- **The interaction between leader turnover and institutional evolution:** This paper suggests the importance of institutional constraints; in the future one could further study **how leader turnover in turn affects institutional development**. For instance, do **several consecutive enlightened monarchs** actively push to establish stronger institutions (because they may have the foresight to set up rules to constrain their successors)? Conversely, does **a succession of benighted rulers** stimulate society's demand for institutional reform (such as a demand to establish a parliament to limit royal power)? This dynamic interaction between leaders and institutions is the key to understanding long-cycle national evolution.
- **Modern implications and quantitative verification:** Although modern states differ greatly from the pre-modern, there are still some comparable points. For example, one could study the relationship between the educational background, health, age, etc. of leaders in contemporary authoritarian states and national economic performance, or exploit random factors in elections (such as the randomness of a candidate's narrow win or narrow loss) to test the role of leaders' ability in democratic polities. This would bring historical insights into the present and provide a basis for policy-making.

In sum, this *History's Masters* by Sebastian Ottinger and Nico Voigtländer hands in a convincing answer to the long-standing question of "**who writes history**." With detailed data and rigorous inference, the paper shows how, under special historical conditions, leaders influence the fate of nations, while also modestly acknowledging the role of context and institutions. It is both an important supplement to the existing literature and a stimulus for further reflection on related topics. After reading this paper, I have gained a deeper understanding of the dialectical relationship between individual agency and institutional factors in history, and I am also full of anticipation for related future research. As the paper's title "**History's Masters**" suggests, those monarchs standing at the summit of power—some did indeed become the masters of history, but how great a role they could play ultimately depended on the institutional soil and the era's backdrop of the time. This conclusion is both firm and dialectical, and is thought-provoking.
