> This paper proposes a novel and promising method to reduce belief in conspiracy theories, showing that through personalized dialogues with AI, even staunch believers may change their minds. This approach departs from earlier pessimistic views that consider individuals deeply entrenched in conspiracy theories as virtually unchangeable. The research demonstrates that **well-designed AI systems can play a significant role in improving public discourse and countering misinformation**, provided they are used responsibly.

## Introduction

#### **The Prevalence of Conspiracy Theories**

The widespread existence of conspiracy beliefs is a serious public problem. These beliefs often involve secret and malevolent plans executed by powerful hidden forces. Although many conspiracy theories lack evidence or are even irrational, large numbers of people still believe in them. Taking the United States as an example, previous surveys have shown that **as many as 50%** of Americans believe in some form of conspiracy theory. This prevalence is alarming, as conspiracy beliefs are considered classic examples of beliefs highly resistant to evidence.

- **Traditional Psychological Explanations**: Conspiracy beliefs arise through socio-psychological processes that undermine rational decision-making and the acceptance of evidence. For example, people may believe in conspiracy theories to satisfy psychological needs such as a **desire for control over their environment**, a **quest for certainty and predictability**, to **safeguard their sense of safety and stability**, and to seek a **sense of uniqueness**. These psychological needs cause conspiracy theories to function as more than mere personal opinions—they become a means of maintaining psychological stability, making these beliefs very difficult to change through evidence.
- **The Conspiracy Doom Loop**: Many theories hypothesize that once conspiracy beliefs form, especially when fused with **personal identity** or **group affiliation**, these beliefs become extremely entrenched. Individuals may process information through **motivated reasoning**, selectively ignoring evidence that contradicts their beliefs. Consequently, fact-based interventions are often considered almost futile in changing the minds of conspiracy theorists.

#### **The Persuasive Potential of LLMs**

- **The Problem with Traditional Interventions**: If sufficiently powerful and persuasive evidence tailored to the individual is provided, could conspiracy theorists change their beliefs? The authors argue that previous attempts at intervention might have failed because these interventions lacked adequate depth and specificity. Conspiracy theorists often possess a wealth of "evidence" that they can use to argue with doubters. Thus, broad and non-specific counterarguments might fail to be persuasive.
- **AI as a Solution**: Large Language Models (LLMs), like this, offer two major advantages. Firstly, they can access a **vast scale of information** covering diverse topics. Secondly, they can craft **personalized counterarguments** targeting an individual's specific conspiracy beliefs and supporting reasons. Leveraging this capability, LLMs can directly respond to and refute the specific evidence supporting an individual’s conspiracy belief, thereby overcoming the difficulties in previous persuasion attempts.

## Experimental Procedure

#### **Study Participants**

**Target Sample**: The study set a definitive target sample size. Participants were sampled through CloudResearch's Connect participant pool, aiming for a qualifying sample of **1000 individuals** quota-matched to US Census data on age, gender, race, and ethnicity, ensuring some representativeness.

**Sample Filters**: A total of 1214 individuals entered the study flow, with some participants excluded for not meeting specific criteria. Key exclusion criteria included:

- **Writing Quality Check**: The study introduced a pre-treatment writing quality screener to ensure participants had good reading and writing abilities and could provide serious answers to open-ended questions. This check was conducted at the beginning of the study, primarily to exclude individuals using automated programs, unable to understand questions, or unwilling to engage thoroughly.
- **Attention Check**: Participants also needed to pass an attention check question; those failing were removed.
- **Insufficient Conpsiracy Belief**: Participants providing personal conspiracy beliefs that did not fit the criteria (i.e., not typical conspiracy theories) or who had low belief conviction (less than 50% belief strength) in their provided conspiracy narrative were also excluded.
