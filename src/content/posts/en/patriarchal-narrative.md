When certain dregs evolve over millennia and become so deeply embedded, through class power, in a society's informal and formal institutions, they become as pervasive as water, sunlight, and air — so pervasive that most people immersed in them regard their existence as unquestionably natural, a matter of course.

Before I started primary school, there was a playmate living not far from home, a slightly built, slender child. My grandmother told me that when his mother gave birth to him she was only 15 or 16, her tone laced with the lightness one reserves for recounting curious anecdotes.

During primary school, little boys made fun of girls' bodies, as if the knack for body shaming were inscribed in their genes, and then drew delight from the girls' shyly evasive silhouettes.

In secondary school, teachers, using methods we would today call emotional manipulation and gaslighting, disparaged humanities classes and students who had switched from science to humanities, constructing gender prejudice. From the lips of close family, distant relatives, and irrelevant strangers alike, I heard countless times the fallacies that "girls do well when they're young, but once they are in senior high school or university they can't compete with boys," or that "girls are bad at maths and suited to the humanities, while boys are bright-minded and suited to the sciences." In break time, when a sanitary pad accidentally slipped from a girl's schoolbag onto the floor, she would snatch it up in flustered panic to evade her male classmates' gaze.

All through growing up, I've been a bystander, a living experiencer, and an executioner of the patriarchal narrative.

Bodies, glances, language, inanimate objects — everything can be made into a disciplinary tool. Across a vast white wasteland, the patriarchal narrative assiduously carves ugliness after ugliness. And that's why giving birth too young is something you're allowed to mock; that's why women's physiological needs are made shameful; that's why a proliferation of fallacies turns into a ready-­made endorsement.

Last year, at its start, a friend complained on Weibo about why her mother married a man like her father, and I realized with shock that I had had exactly the same feeling. The family is the first strategic front of the patriarchal narrative. My grandmother endured decades of exploitation and had no real means to resist: yet she, a victim herself, further disciplined her daughter­-in-­law for "managing too much." The disastrous marriages I have seen were mostly caused by men's faults — addiction to alcohol, gambling, whoring, laziness, domestic violence, and the like. The preferential treatment and lowered expectations accorded to men give rise to widespread domestic tragedy; if becoming a parent takes time and a process, then, so it seems, that process has been decidedly longer for the fathers.

Yesterday, a friend noted in her social media feed that "the gifts people take along when visiting relatives during Spring Festival — predominantly cigarettes and alcohol — have conspicuously male-­centred consumption characteristics, and the joy of the holiday is built upon the misery of women in the family." She was immediately branded a radical feminist. (P.S. Some scholars, exploring the connection between consumption of male-­characteristic goods like cigarettes and alcohol and the naming on property deeds to uncover family bargaining dynamics, got it published in the *JDE*.)

The patriarchal narrative also fuels linguistic corruption. I once saw, on a Bilibili comment thread, "Fuming, shaking, chilled — so when will men finally be able to rise?" The jokester was spontaneously dismantling and reassembling feminist discourse. Barrage messages such as the "kenfu alert," "Ningbo is so big," and "driving up pro box's price" paint an obviously unfettered image of the men's­ fisting movement  —  Yes: discourse is power.

There are times I feel that the world is a giant unconsummated credit structure: on one hand, we have created blood‑stirring epics; on the other, we contain the darkest cruelty, the blackest evil.

<br/>

Finally, a few words on gender equality; here my position is:

You must straighten something by bending it further;

Equality is meaningful for an individual, or for a given family  — but in any forseeable future, advocating gender equality, across groups, can be a kind of mere  mirrored-impossible moon-blue flower, in a  suspended  idyll .
