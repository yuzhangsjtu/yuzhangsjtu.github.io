When certain dregs evolve over millennia and become embedded, by the force of class, in society's informal and formal institutions, they become as pervasive as water, sunlight, and air — so much so that most of the people immersed in them regard their existence as natural and self-evident, a matter of course.

Before I had even started primary school, there was a playmate living not far from home, slight and slender of build. My grandmother told me that when his mother gave birth to him she was only 15 or 16, her words full of the breeziness one uses when recounting curious anecdotes.

During primary school, little boys made jokes about girls' bodies, as if the knack for body shaming were carved into their genes, and then drew pleasure from the girls' dodging, evasive figures.

In secondary school, teachers — in ways that, looking back now, amount to PUA — denigrated the humanities-track classes and the students who had switched from the science track to the humanities track, constructing gender prejudice. From the mouths of close family, of relatives, and of inconsequential people alike, I heard countless times the fallacies that "girls get good grades when they are young, but once in senior high and university they cannot compete with the boys," and that "girls are bad at maths and suited to the humanities, while boys are clever-minded and suited to the sciences." When, during a break, a female classmate accidentally dropped a sanitary pad on the floor while taking it from her schoolbag, she would scramble to pick it up in a fluster, fleeing the gaze of the male classmates.

Growing up all along the way, I became a bystander, an experiencer, and an executioner of the patriarchal narrative.

The body, the gaze, language, still objects — anything can become a tool of discipline. On the vast white wasteland, the patriarchal narrative meticulously carves all too much ugliness. And so, giving birth too young becomes something that can be mocked; and so, women's physiological needs are made shameful; and so, widespread fallacies become reasonable endorsements.

Early last year, a friend complained on Weibo about why her mother would marry someone like her father, and I exclaimed that I had exactly the same experience. The family is the first battlefield of the patriarchal narrative. My grandmother endured decades of exploitation and had no way to truly resist; yet she, a victim herself, went on to discipline her daughter-in-law for "meddling too much." The unhappy marriages I have witnessed were, for the most part, caused by men's faults: alcoholism, gambling, whoring, laziness, domestic violence, and so on. The favoritism toward men and the low expectations placed on them have led to widespread family tragedy. If becoming a parent requires a process, then evidently that process is somewhat longer for fathers.

Yesterday a friend pointed out in her social-media feed that "the gifts given when visiting relatives during Spring Festival are mainly tobacco and alcohol, which carry distinctly male consumption characteristics; the joy of the holiday is built upon the suffering of the female members of the family" — and was immediately labeled a man-hating feminist. (PS: Some scholars, taking as their entry point the share of household consumption made up of male-characteristic consumer goods such as tobacco and alcohol, investigated the relationship between the names on housing property titles and family status, and published it in the *JDE*.)

The patriarchal narrative also stimulates the corruption of language. On Bilibili I have seen the comment "shivering with rage and cold — when will men ever be able to stand up?", with the wisecrackers spontaneously dissolving and reconstructing feminist discourse. Bullet-comment phrases such as "Jinfu warning," "Ningbo is really big," and "driving up the price of the pussy" reveal the unbridled brazenness of the men's-fists movement. Yes — discourse is power.

I often feel the world is a giant unfinished building: on the one hand we have created astonishing epics, and on the other, it harbors the very blackest, very darkest of evils.

Finally, a few words on the question of equal rights. My view is:

To right a wrong, one must overcorrect;

Equal rights are meaningful for the individual or the family, but in the foreseeable future, advocating equal rights between men and women at the level of the group can only be flowers in a mirror and the moon in water, a castle in the air.
