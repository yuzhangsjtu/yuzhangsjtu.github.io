## Modernity

> It designates the period from the mid‑18th‑century European Enlightenment to the mid‑1980s, characterized by secularization, **rationalization**, democratization, individualization and the rise of **science**.

### 1 Origins and history

“Modern” refers to anything that is of the contemporary era. Distinctions between the ancient and the modern were already remarkably clear as early as late‑16th‑century Europe. At the outset, the concept of modernization — making things more modern — was seen as a regression; it was only by the 19th century that modernization increasingly came to be viewed in a positive, affirmative light. Three‑quarters of the way through the 20th century, the modernization of transportation, housing, social attitudes, fashion and all manner of other things had come to be regarded as both inevitable and progressive. In social theory, however, “modernity” carries a far broader meaning and spans a much longer arc, stretching from the mid‑18th century to the 1980s. Enlightenment philosophers assailed tradition, religious authority and arbitrarily given beliefs, championing instead the idea that human progress could be achieved only through rational thought, scientific method and the pursuit of freedom and equality. Sociology itself is a product of modernity; its disciplinary mission is to obtain reliable knowledge about the social world by scientific means, so as to intervene in society appropriately, reshape it, and benefit all humanity.

### 2 Meaning and interpretation

**Modern society refers to the post‑feudal era in Europe, a collective term for post‑feudal societies that encompasses industrialization, capitalism, urbanization and urbanism as a way of life, secularization, the establishment and diffusion of democratic institutions, the application of science and technology in the production process, and a thoroughgoing movement toward the equalization of every domain of human life.** In stark contrast to the emotionally charged, religiously oriented values of traditional society, modernity specifically denotes a rational mode of thought and an impersonal, matter‑of‑fact “business‑like” attitude. Weber described this process as the “disenchantment of the world”, meaning that secularization gradually spreads across the entire globe along with the expansion of legal‑rational capitalism.

As a social model, modernity has triumphed in several domains: it has revolutionarily expanded the limits of material‑goods production, created immense wealth for a subset of relatively affluent nations, and made many aspects of human society increasingly equal. In the 20th century, many sociologists firmly believed that modernity was a proven social blueprint and that, whether they actively pursued it or were swept along involuntarily, every country would eventually move in that direction. This proposition gradually evolved into what became known as modernization theory, perhaps the best‑known version of which is Rostow’s (1961). Rostow identified the following developmental stages in the modernization process:

- First, a society begins to “catch up” with those that have already successfully modernized, so that its economy starts to grow. Building upon a traditional agrarian society, latecomer countries must break free from long‑entrenched traditional values and institutions and achieve modernization by investing in infrastructure and new industries.
- Next, sustained investment in emerging high technology raises levels of production, which in turn fuels the rise of mass consumption, establishing a self‑sustaining economic model.

Although economies such as Hong Kong SAR, Taiwan (China), the Republic of Korea and Singapore have followed a broadly similar path, from today’s vantage point Rostow’s theoretical model seems unduly optimistic, for many countries — especially in Africa — have failed to modernize along these lines.

> Looking retrospectively from our current moment this is indeed empirically observable phenomena; certain regions truly constitute backward localities — some hijacked by religion, others ensnared by power. Geographical determinism still retains discursive currency.

For a number of social theorists, not least Bauman (1987), **the key to understanding modernity lies in grasping the distinctive culture and mentality it embodies.** An analogy with gardening might help. The modern mentality values order and dislikes chaos and randomness. Hence, if society is likened to a natural garden, then weeds that have taken hold unrestrained should be corrected, and the wild environment tamed and transformed. As the power of the nation‑state gardener swells, the means and the tools for taming nature likewise multiply. The metaphor extends beyond the nation state, however, for orderliness and neatness is a pervasive requirement of modern life.
