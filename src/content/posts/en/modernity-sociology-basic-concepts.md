## Modernity

> It refers to the period from the mid-18th-century European Enlightenment to the mid-1980s, characterized by secularization, **rationalization**, democratization, individualization, and the rise of **science**.

### 1 Origins and history

"Modern" refers to anything having to do with the contemporary. The distinction between the ancient and the modern was already quite pronounced in Europe as early as the late 16th century. At first, the concept of modernization, that is, making things more modern, was regarded as a kind of regression; it was not until the 19th century that modernization increasingly came to be seen as carrying positive, affirmative overtones. After three-quarters of the 20th century had passed, the modernization of transportation, housing, social attitudes, fashion, and all sorts of other things had come to be regarded as both inevitable and progressive. In the field of social theory, however, the meaning of "modernity" is far broader, spanning a stretch of time that reaches from the mid-18th century all the way to the 1980s. The Enlightenment philosophers attacked tradition, religious authority, and arbitrarily imposed beliefs, advocating instead the idea that human progress can be achieved only through rational thought, scientific method, and the pursuit of freedom and equality. Sociology itself is a product of modernity; the mission of the discipline is to obtain reliable knowledge about the social world through scientific methods, and thereby to intervene in and transform society appropriately, for the benefit of all humanity.

### 2 Meaning and interpretation

**Modern society refers to the era after European feudalism, a collective term for post-feudal society that encompasses industrialization, capitalism, urbanization and urbanism as a way of life, secularization, the establishment and diffusion of democratic institutions, the application of science and technology in the production process, and an all-around movement toward the equalization of human life.** In stark contrast to the emotion-laden, religiously oriented values of traditional society, modernity refers specifically to a rational mode of thinking and a dispassionate, "matter-of-fact" attitude. Weber called this process the "disenchantment of the world," meaning that, as legal-rational capitalism expanded, secularization gradually spread across the entire globe.

As a social model, modernity has achieved great success in several domains: it has revolutionarily raised the limits of the production of material goods, created an enormous amount of wealth for a portion of relatively affluent countries, and made many aspects of human society increasingly equal. In the 20th century, many sociologists firmly believed that modernity was a successful social model, and that whether they actively pursued it or were forced to be swept up in it, every country would ultimately develop in this direction. This proposition gradually evolved into what is widely known as modernization theory, the most famous version of which is undoubtedly Rostow's (1961) view. Rostow pointed out that the process of modernization comprises the following developmental stages:

- First, a society begins to "catch up" with the countries that have already successfully modernized earlier, so that its economy begins to grow in scale. On the foundation of a traditional agrarian society, latecomer countries must shake off the traditional values and institutions they have long adhered to, and achieve modernization through investment in infrastructure and emerging industries.
- Next, sustained investment in emerging high technology promotes a rise in the level of production, which in turn drives the rise of mass consumption, and a sustainable economic model is thereby established.

Although economies such as Hong Kong (China), Taiwan (China), South Korea, and Singapore have followed a similar path, from the present-day vantage point Rostow's theoretical model is still overly optimistic, because many countries, especially African countries, have not achieved modernization along this path.

> Looking at it now, this is indeed the case; some regions are truly civilizational backwaters, some held hostage by religion, others coerced by power. Geographical determinism still has its market.

For some social theorists, and especially for Bauman (1987), **the key to understanding modernity is to grasp the distinctive culture and mode of thinking it contains.** We can use gardening as an analogy. The modern mode of thinking prizes order and rejects chaos and randomness. Therefore, if society is likened to a natural garden, then the rampant weeds should be corrected, and the wild environment tamed and transformed. As the power of the nation-state gardener grows ever greater, the means and tools for taming nature also become more and more numerous. This metaphor applies not only to the nation-state, because order and tidiness are a universal requirement of modern life.

### 3 Critique and discussion

The biggest problem with modernity is that it is overgeneralized. In the view of many critics, it is merely an after-the-fact description of some (though not all) of the features of modern society, and it offers no explanation whatsoever for the causes of modernization. This theory is also unable to explain the vast inequality that still exists worldwide, as many developing countries have not achieved economic takeoff as predicted. This concept mentions several key social processes, but it is often vague, and most of it is descriptive rather than analytical. As a result, we cannot tell which of its many constituent elements is the main driving force of the modernization process. Is the main driver of modernization the capitalist economic system, or industrialization? What role does democratization play? Where does urbanization fit in: is it one of the causes, or a result? Neo-Marxist scholars attack modernization theory for implying an inevitable logic of this sort: that underdeveloped countries will be incentivized to learn from developed countries, and thereby enter a channel of rapid economic growth and prosperity. The reality is that, looking across the entire world, backward countries are forced to accept a prolonged state of dependence and subordination, reduced to suppliers of raw materials for the wealthy countries, with the populations of these countries becoming cheap labor for Western multinational capitalist corporations. Therefore, the concept of modernity is itself ambiguous and muddled, and the modernization thesis also has deep-seated fatal flaws.

### 4 Significance and value

With the emergence of postmodern social theory, modernity seemed to have reached its end, and the academic community began to reflect on and reevaluate this concept. Some sociologists insist that we have not yet entered postmodernity, and that at most it is "late" or "reflexive" (Giddens 1990). This is not the death knell of modernity, but a reminder that we must face squarely the negative dimensions of modernity, such as environmental destruction. Moreover, as the public begins to question the reliability of science as a means of revealing the truth, and as various authorities come under increasing challenge, the uncertainty of social life is also growing by the day. Habermas (1983) likewise pointed out that postmodern social theorists prematurely abandoned the enterprise he called the grand project of modernity. The basic features of modernity have been only partly realized, and we ought to continue building it in depth rather than hastily give it up. There is still much left for us to do, such as advancing democratic participation, fighting for equal life chances for the various social classes, achieving gender equality, and so on. In short, modernity is an unfinished enterprise, and we ought to keep striving rather than give up at this point.

Based on the idea of "multiple modernities," a more recent study criticizes the practice of equating modernization with Westernization (Eisenstadt 2002). This study runs counter to the earlier view, opposing the notion that there is only a single route to modernity, and likewise disagreeing with the practice of copying wholesale the standardized, uniform model of Western society. Empirical research on modernity worldwide tells us that this view is mistaken. In fact, there are many paths to modernity; as the saying goes, all roads lead to Rome. Japan's modernization and America's are very different. Moreover, as things currently stand, what China is developing is yet another brand-new path. The modernization of some countries, even the United States, has not been accompanied by drastic secularization as some of the earlier scholars predicted; on the contrary, religious belief has been well preserved and upheld, even as industrialism and technological progress proceed in parallel. Other countries, such as the Saudi Arabian model, not only bear a strong religious coloring but also selectively learn from the Western model and then add their own distinctive elements. From this perspective, the multiple-modernities agenda is very likely to inspire more practically oriented assessments, and thereby breathe new life into the concept of modernity in the near future.
