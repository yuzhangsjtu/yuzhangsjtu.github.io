> The paper reviews archaeological evidence from western Eurasia spanning the Neolithic to the Bronze Age (approximately 11,700 to 5,300 years ago), noting that although pronounced wealth inequality occasionally appeared, these instances of inequality were for the most part fleeting and rare, and did not begin to persist until the late Neolithic. The authors argue that an early **fiercely egalitarian culture** suppressed the emergence of durable wealth gaps, until changes in farming **technology** (such as the ox-drawn plough) raised the value of material wealth relative to labor, and that, combined with the **concentration of elite power** in proto-states and the emergence of **slave labor**, created the conditions for sustained economic inequality.

## Detailed Summary

*The Origins of Enduring Economic Inequality* reviews the archaeological evidence for economic inequality in prehistory and explores the **origins** of **enduring wealth inequality**. By analyzing data from hunter-gatherer societies and Neolithic farming societies, the authors find that although relatively high wealth inequality did appear in human societies more than five thousand years ago, these instances of inequality were typically fleeting and rare, in sharp contrast to the great inequality that has persisted over the past five millennia. To explain this phenomenon, the paper focuses on **why** significant and durable wealth gaps emerged only thousands of years after the Agricultural Revolution (not until the very end of the Neolithic), and **through what mechanisms** these wealth gaps were able to be maintained over the long term. Employing an interdisciplinary approach, the authors gather a large amount of archaeological data and build models, arguing that before the end of the Neolithic there existed a "**radical egalitarian**" culture that prevented the emergence of durable inequality; it was only in the late Neolithic that new farming **technologies** raised the value of material wealth relative to labor, and that, together with the concentration of elite power in **early proto-states** (and the subsequent exploitation of slave labor), provided the political-economic conditions for the long-term persistence of high wealth inequality. The paper concludes that the origins of enduring economic inequality in human society did not stem from an inequality "creation event" at the dawn of agriculture, but rather from the qualitative change brought about by the **interaction** of the aforementioned **technological**, **institutional**, and **cultural** factors at the end of the Neolithic.

## Introduction

With the advance of archaeology, a great deal of evidence indicates that ancient human societies were on the whole relatively **egalitarian**, even though glimmers of wealth inequality occasionally appeared in the late Upper Paleolithic and afterward. These early instances of inequality were exceedingly rare and difficult to sustain. For example, the Sungir site on the Russian Plain (ca. 24,000 years ago) yielded extraordinarily lavish grave goods, yet among the 29 excavated Upper Paleolithic burial sites, no other find compares to it. In France, 46 cemeteries have been discovered just within the final 5,000 years of the Paleolithic, but likewise none has produced burials as rich as those at Sungir. This fact shows that **although inequality did exist in early human societies, it was very scarce and fleeting**.

Entering the early Neolithic, even though humans began to **settle down** and experiment with agriculture, the archaeological record still shows that society on the whole remained relatively egalitarian, lacking durable, fixed class divisions. For example, the Natufian culture of the Levant (ca. 12,000–10,000 BCE) was once regarded as an early "**proto-farming**" society, but burial evidence does not show clear social stratification: although early Natufian burials contain personal ornaments as grave goods, with variation from site to site, the most recent research indicates that these differences do not represent social class. On the contrary, in the late Natufian, grave goods almost completely disappeared and burials across sites became more uniform, indicating that differences in material wealth were not culturally emphasized. The large settlements of this period (such as the Abu Hureyra site on the banks of the Euphrates), although they saw the appearance of private houses and incipient agriculture, did not exhibit significant differences in house size or burials; households were of similar status, suggesting that society remained egalitarian and "had not yet developed the **social classes that we associate with the towns** of historical times." It can thus be seen that **from the late Paleolithic to the early Neolithic, although modes of production and patterns of settlement underwent enormous changes, the archaeological evidence does not show enduring, significant wealth inequality**.

Against the above background, the authors propose the **core motivation** of this study: why did pronounced, long-lasting wealth inequality not arise immediately after the appearance of agriculture, but instead become widespread only as late as the end of the Neolithic? In other words, **what prevented early inequality from becoming durable, and what conditions ultimately allowed inequality to persist**? This question carries important scholarly significance, because it bears on our understanding of the origins of economic inequality in human society: if the Agricultural Revolution did not immediately lead to enduring inequality, then traditional theories about how private property and sedentism cause class differentiation may need to be reexamined. This study aims to fill this knowledge gap, drawing together the perspectives of archaeology, anthropology, and evolutionary game theory to clarify the timing and causes of the emergence of **"enduring wealth inequality"** in human society.

## Research Question

The core question the paper seeks to answer is: **In the thousands of years after the appearance of agriculture, why did human societies maintain a relatively egalitarian wealth distribution, while significant and durable wealth inequality did not emerge until the end of the Neolithic (about five thousand years ago)?** The authors stress that their concern is not with the "**one-off creation**" of inequality, but with the process of **how wealth gaps were able to persist over the long term**, and with why this enduring inequality occurred only about four thousand years after the Agricultural Revolution. This question carries important practical and theoretical significance:

- **Practical significance**: The gap between rich and poor in today's society is rooted deep in the course of human history. By probing the mechanisms by which wealth inequality emerged in ancient times, we can gain a deeper understanding of the structural causes of economic inequality. For example, if early societies were able to maintain equality over the long term, then the inequality of modern society may not be an inevitable product of human society, but rather the result of specific environmental and institutional conditions. This recognition offers a historical reference for today's social efforts to narrow the wealth gap.
- **Theoretical significance**: Traditional theories often regard the **Agricultural Revolution** as the starting point of inequality, holding that **sedentism** and **private property** naturally give rise to class differentiation. However, the way this paper frames its question challenges this linear view, shifting attention instead to **why inequality is able to persist** rather than **how it arises**. This shift enriches the theoretical framework of research on social inequality: the authors introduce the perspectives of cultural norms and institutional evolution, positing that early humans may have actively suppressed the growth of inequality. Answering this question therefore helps clarify the conditions required for economic inequality to evolve from a sporadic phenomenon into the norm in human society, an important contribution to both economic history and the theory of institutional evolution.

In short, the question of this study aims to reveal the **"origins of enduring economic inequality"**: what allowed wealth inequality to transform from a sporadic, highly unstable phenomenon into a social feature that could be maintained across generations. Answering this question not only deepens our understanding of prehistoric economic organization, but also offers new ways of thinking about economic inequality in the millennia that followed, and even in modern times.

## Research Methodology

To answer the above question, the authors adopted a comprehensive research approach, including the collection and organization of archaeological data, comparative case analysis, and the construction of theoretical models.

#### **Data Collection and Indicator Estimation**

The authors first constructed a database covering the degree of wealth inequality in western Eurasia over the past ten thousand years and more. This database compiles estimates of wealth inequality for 185 different prehistoric societies, ranging in time from the late Paleolithic (about twelve millennia ago, i.e., around 10000 BCE) all the way to the Roman Empire period (several centuries CE). Since there are no direct records of income or property, the authors used **archaeological proxy indicators** to estimate the degree of inequality in wealth distribution, including **dwelling area, the size of storage facilities, land tenure, and the value of grave goods**. Based on these archaeological measures, the authors calculated the wealth Gini coefficient for each society, and through a series of corrections made the degree of inequality estimated from different types of indicators comparable. For example, they adjusted for differences in the degree of inequality that house area versus grave goods reflect about wealth (grave goods are usually distributed more unequally than dwelling area), as well as for biases from sample size and from omitting those without property (such as the landless or slaves). These adjustments ensure that the inequality indicators for each period and region are as statistically consistent as possible.

#### **Case Analysis and Comparison**

On the basis of the data, the authors conducted in-depth analyses of several key archaeological cases in order to distinguish situations of **"fleeting inequality"** from those of **"enduring inequality."** They selected societies known to show early signs of inequality (such as the Sungir cemetery mentioned above, Neolithic villages in the Near East, and early Bronze Age villages in Southeast Asia), examined whether the inequality in these societies persisted or was short-lived, and analyzed the possible causes of its rise and fall. For example, by comparing changes in the location and size of grain storage pits at different stages of an archaeological site, one can judge whether unequal wealth holdings emerged and were subsequently dissolved. This qualitative analysis helped the authors identify which factors (such as resource monopolies, trade opportunities, or catastrophes) might cause a surge of inequality in certain periods, only for it to disappear afterward.

#### **Theoretical Model Construction**

In order to understand the mechanisms of enduring inequality more systematically, the authors built an evolutionary game theory model to simulate the dynamics of distribution within societies. The core idea of the model is to introduce people with different behavioral strategies in the group: some individuals follow the norm of **"equal sharing" (civic sharers)** and are willing to divide resources equally within the group; others are inclined to **"grab" (grabbers)** more resources without sharing equally. The authors assumed that in the prehistoric environment, subpopulations with different strategies would compete to survive: an overly unequal group, with reduced internal cooperation, might be no match for groups with high levels of internal egalitarian cooperation when facing environmental shocks or inter-group conflict, and would thus "fail" and be eliminated. This model used computational simulation to trace the evolution of wealth-distribution inequality across many generations from 15000 BCE to 6000 BCE. The simulation outputs include the Gini coefficient and its distribution for each generation of groups, for comparison with the archaeological estimates. The model helped the authors test whether, **in the absence of major external changes (such as technological or institutional innovation), a society that practices an equal-sharing norm would have its inequality kept at a low level over the long term, with occasional peaks of inequality difficult to sustain**.

#### **Supplementary Analysis**

In addition, in order to verify the generality and robustness of the hypothesis, the authors conducted corroborating research from multiple angles. They made horizontal comparisons, drawing on anthropological data, of the relationship between wealth inequality and the form of wealth in small-scale societies with different economic forms. For example, using ethnographic data, they compared economies in which labor is the main limiting factor (such as slash-and-burn horticultural societies) with economies in which land/capital is the main limiting factor (such as plough-based farming or pastoral societies that rely on cattle), quantifying the correlation between the importance of material wealth and the Gini coefficient. They also examined regions with no direct connection to western Eurasia, such as prehistoric societies in Southeast Asia, in order to test whether their theory holds in different cultural contexts. Through these methods, the authors sought to ensure that their conclusions were not an artifact peculiar to a particular region or sample.

In sum, the paper adopted a method combining **"data—cases—model"**: first using a newly constructed large archaeological database to depict the macro-level trend in the evolution of inequality, then probing the micro-level mechanisms through specific cases and cross-cultural comparison, and finally using a theoretical model to test the possibility that egalitarian norms suppress inequality. Given the uncertainty of archaeological data, the authors also stressed that their inferences are based on **"reasonable conjecture"** rather than conclusive evidence, and they maintained cautious wording throughout their account.

## Data and Results Analysis

#### **The Evolution of the Degree of Inequality in Prehistory**

Using the archaeological data they collected, the paper presents the broad trend of wealth inequality since the end of the Paleolithic (see Figure 1 in the paper). On the whole, over the long stretch from ten thousand to five thousand years ago, the wealth distribution of each society was relatively egalitarian, with Gini coefficients usually around **0.15–0.35**. For example, according to the authors' calculations, the early Near Eastern village of Jerf el Ahmar around 9000 BCE had a material wealth Gini coefficient of only about 0.158. **Before the Bronze Age** (before about 3300 BCE), only a very small number of societies reached a degree of inequality approaching that of modern society. These **exceptions** mainly appeared in the affluent centers of the late Neolithic to Chalcolithic period, such as the Mesopotamian sites: the wealth inequality at places like Tepe Gawra, Ur, and Tell Brak around the fifth millennium BCE was already quite high. The authors note that the wealth Gini coefficients of these societies were comparable to those of later traditional agrarian societies with state governance but without large-scale slavery—in other words, they had already reached an inequality **approaching "modern" levels**. However, these early instances of inequality were still **exceedingly rare** in the broader span of space and time, and did not immediately trigger widespread transformation. By and large, the wealth gaps of Neolithic western Eurasian societies remained at relatively low levels; the dashed line in the chart (the average of the observed values 4,000 years ago) shows that the average wealth Gini coefficient at that time was only about **0.36**. By contrast, after entering the Bronze Age and Iron Age, more and more societies saw their degree of wealth inequality rise, and by the Roman Empire period, inequality reached an unusually high level: the population's wealth distribution across the regions of the Roman Empire was extremely lopsided, far surpassing that of earlier agrarian societies. Modern scholars estimate that around the year 2000 the average wealth Gini across countries was about 0.695, while a classical society like the Roman Empire, built on the foundation of slave labor, had a degree of wealth inequality comparable to or even higher than that. Therefore, judging from the data trend, **sustained high-level wealth inequality did not appear immediately alongside the Agricultural Revolution, but rather formed and intensified gradually within roughly the past 5,000 years**. Before that, even where inequality appeared, its scale and durability were clearly limited.

#### **Instances of Fleeting Inequality and Their Dissolution**

The paper uses several archaeological examples to show that early instances of inequality often failed to persist and were ultimately re-leveled. These cases, which the authors call **"failed experiments,"** provide insight. A typical example comes from the mammoth-hunter settlements of the late Paleolithic on the Russian Plain. At the Radomyshl site (earlier in date), archaeologists found a large communal grain storage pit located in the center of the dwellings, evidently for the shared use of all residents. But at the somewhat later Dobranichevka site, each dwelling had its own storage pit, meaning that resource storage shifted from communal to private. Even so, the differences in size among these private storage pits were not great, indicating that the resources obtained by each household were still roughly comparable. Only at a few sites did clear signs of inequality appear: at the Mezin site, for example, 6 of the 8 storage pits were concentrated near a single dwelling, and that dwelling yielded a large quantity of fine artworks and ornaments obtained from afar (shells, etc.). By one estimate, if these pits represent the village's entire store, then that "wealthy" household may have controlled 96% of the village's grain reserves, and the Gini coefficient of its house area reached 0.509 (still 0.31 after adjustment), far higher than the overall level of 0.22 for the other households. This shows that the settlement at one point exhibited considerable wealth concentration. However, the archaeological evidence also reveals a **subsequent sharp equalization**: at sites dating to after about 18,000 years ago (including the location of Sungir), no more burials with rich grave goods are found; many large settlements broke apart, populations became smaller and migrated frequently, and the wealth monopolies that had previously appeared vanished without a trace. As Soffer put it, these changes were **"oscillatory,"** meaning that early wealth inequality did not develop into an enduring institution.

A similar situation also occurred at the transition between the Neolithic and the Bronze Age. The authors cite the most recent archaeological research on two neighboring villages in the upper Mun River valley of northeastern Thailand. At the village of Ban Non Wat about 4,000 years ago (when copper had just been introduced), the wealth Gini coefficient estimated from grave goods surged by more than 50% at the start of the Bronze Age. Archaeologists found that the village was located on an important long-distance trade route, and a small group of people were the first to monopolize the exchange of rare wealth (copper, carnelian, marine shells, etc.), thereby forming a prominent "aggrandizer" elite class. At that time, powerful families gained social status by controlling these novel and precious materials. However, this inequality was not maintained forever: the archaeological strata show that after only about eight generations (possibly less than two centuries), the wealth in burials abruptly declined, and the previously lavish elite disappeared. Researchers believe that as resources such as bronze became more widely available, those elites who had earlier gained an advantage through monopoly lost their special status, and the society's wealth levels returned to relative equality. This case vividly illustrates that **the flourishing of inequality brought about by resource monopoly may be only fleeting**: when the monopoly is broken or external conditions change, high inequality becomes difficult to sustain. This is similar to the cases of the Paleolithic mammoth-hunter settlements and the Sungir burials, suggesting that **the inequality of early societies lacked a firm institutional foundation and easily reverted to equality under internal or external pressure**.

#### **"Radical Egalitarianism" and Its Role**

Since early instances of inequality repeatedly "died young," the authors went on to explore the equalizing mechanisms that people of the time may have employed. The archaeological evidence suggests that many early Neolithic societies seem to have deliberately **suppressed visible differences in wealth and status**. Take the Levant as an example: at many sites of the late Natufian and Pre-Pottery Neolithic (PPNA/PPNB), burial customs underwent a marked change: **grave goods in individual graves were greatly reduced**, replaced by communal "secondary burial" rituals—the most typical being the practice of removing the skulls of the dead and reburying them together. The archaeologist Ian Kuijt has argued that this practice reflects a set of social norms aimed at limiting the accumulation of individual wealth and power and at emphasizing group identity over personal glory. Uniform and simple burials (lacking differentiated tomb structures and grave goods) conveyed the idea that **"everyone is equal and shares the same ancestors."** At the same time, house architecture also displayed a **high degree of homogeneity**—dwellings were almost indistinguishable in shape, size, and layout—further indicating that differences in material wealth or social status were deliberately downplayed. On this basis, Kuijt argued that during the period of rapid economic and social change between 11,000 and 9300 BCE (from the gathering of wild cereals to cultivation to animal husbandry, and the appearance of large villages), the people of the Levant **reinforced and standardized egalitarian social norms in order to uphold group ethics and curb the development of inequality**.

This practice of "**radical egalitarianism**" was not confined to the Levant. At the Near Eastern Neolithic site of Jerf el Ahmar, communal underground granaries were built in the early phase, and the storage, processing, and consumption of food clearly had a community-sharing character. Although over time the locale gradually shifted from communal toward household-based production and consumption, for a considerable period communal granaries and "communal kitchens" continued to function, emphasizing group sharing. It was not until the middle PPNB (around 9500 years ago), with the rise of truly **"household-centered"** farming communities, that private granaries finally replaced communal storage. The archaeologist Karen Wright described the general pattern of this region: from early open communal access (the outdoor shared granaries of the PPNA period), to semi-public kitchens at house porches in the middle transitional stage, to fully interior, concealed private storage in the late stage. This process shows that **Neolithic people once strove to maintain communal sharing in order to avoid the monopolization of wealth, but ultimately gave way in part to private accumulation**.

It is worth noting that it was precisely because early humans adopted these equalizing measures that early agricultural societies did not immediately give rise to great inequality. The authors point out that the relatively egalitarian state of the early Neolithic was not merely the passive result of continuing the classless tradition of the Paleolithic, but rather a product of people actively **"keeping vigilant watch over and severely sanctioning"** behavior that sought to make oneself stand out. This collective cultural counterbalance ensured that for a very long time households differed little in material status, and wealth inequality was both scarce and fleeting. As the anthropologist Christopher Boehm described of the North American Iroquois: "their egalitarianism (like that of hunter-gatherers) was maintained by **constant vigilance** and **sometimes very harsh sanctions**." It can thus be seen that, before the appearance of the state and formal law, prehistoric societies already had within them a powerful set of **social monitoring and balancing mechanisms** that prevented individual members from accumulating too much wealth or power.

#### **Simulation Model and Dynamic Verification**

Through the evolutionary model, the authors further verified the above view that "egalitarian norms suppress inequality" on a theoretical level. The simulation results show that, in the absence of major technological or external shocks, a society that adheres to the norm of **"universal equal sharing" (all-civic equilibrium)** does indeed have its wealth inequality maintained at a low level over the long term; although inequality may occasionally rise during this period, it is usually **fleeting**. The results in Figure 2 of the paper show that, while it is not the case that the Gini coefficient in the simulated population never exceeds 0.5, such cases are an extreme minority—and these may correspond in reality to precisely those sporadic lavish burials or wealth monopolies in the archaeological record. Most of the time, the wealth Gini of the simulated groups hovered at a relatively low level (averaging about 0.18), significantly lower than the average of the archaeological observations (about 0.35). Nonetheless, the shape of the simulated distribution is similar to that of the archaeological estimates, both exhibiting a **"long tail"** feature: in most cases inequality is very low, but in a small fraction of cases noticeably elevated Gini values appear. This shows that the model captures a key feature of the evolution of inequality in prehistoric societies—**that it occasionally deviates from the egalitarian state but cannot be sustained over the long term**.

The model further reveals why high-inequality states are difficult to sustain: in the simulation, when subpopulations within a group come to contain both "**sharers and grabbers**" and have lower average returns, such unequal groups are often at a disadvantage when competing with **fully equal-sharing** groups, and may be eliminated due to insufficient internal cooperation. In other words, a group that has become noticeably differentiated internally and lacks cohesion is more likely to fail and perish when it comes into conflict with groups that maintain egalitarian cooperation, or when it faces environmental pressure. This is consistent with the archaeological record: the societies that briefly exhibited high inequality (such as the aforementioned Ban Non Wat) all ultimately experienced decline and were replaced by a more egalitarian social state. The model also points out that **egalitarian social norms may themselves possess evolutionary stability**—they not only indirectly suppress inequality by raising the group's chances of survival, but may also directly, through cultural customs (such as equal division of inheritance and restrictions on conspicuous displays of wealth), make wealth gaps difficult to perpetuate across generations. In sum, the simulation analysis supports the authors' inference: in the early Neolithic, unless new exogenous factors intervened, inequality would have been difficult to "on its own" become enduring and severe under existing conditions alone.

#### **The Relationship Between Agricultural Technology and Wealth Inequality**

The study went on to explore how the introduction of **new agricultural technologies** altered the dynamic equilibrium described above. In particular, the authors distinguished the effects of two production paradigms on inequality: the **"labor-limited"** economy and the **"land-limited"** economy.

In a labor-limited economy, land and resources are relatively abundant, and labor is the main bottleneck; conversely, in a land-limited economy, the population is large but arable land is limited, and wealth accumulation depends more on the possession of material capital such as land and draft animals. Using data from contemporary small-scale anthropological societies, the authors showed that there is a systematic difference in wealth inequality between these two types of economy: the more material wealth is the limiting factor of production, the more unequal the society's wealth distribution. For example, in societies dominated by hoe horticulture (where labor is clearly the limiting factor and land is relatively abundant), the average wealth Gini is about 0.35, whereas in societies dominated by plough farming or pastoralism (where material wealth such as land and livestock becomes critical), the average Gini is about 0.58. This empirical regularity suggests that when technological progress (such as the application of the ox-drawn plough) makes owning more land and livestock significantly raise productivity, wealth inequality will **rise substantially**. The authors note that the adoption of ox plowing and animal traction in late Neolithic western Eurasia was precisely such a transformation. If one extrapolates using the above empirical coefficients, then in transitioning from a primitive "labor-intensive" slash-and-burn economy to a "land-intensive" ox-plough agro-pastoral economy, the Gini coefficient could in theory increase by about **0.24**, comparable to the difference actually observed in the archaeological data between the middle-to-late Neolithic and the early Neolithic (about 0.22). This quantitatively supports the paper's key claim: **changes in agricultural technology raised the importance of material wealth, thereby pushing up wealth inequality**.

#### **Elite Power, State Formation, and Inequality**

Finally, the paper analyzes the impact of changes in the **form of political organization** on the persistence of wealth inequality. In an environment without a formal state, the maintenance of inequality depends mainly on economic power itself. However, archaeologists have found that in some regions, significant class differentiation had already begun to develop before the appearance of complex social organization (so-called "**archaic proto-states**"). Henry Wright's research indicates that in Mesopotamia and Mesoamerica, class-organized social forms had already flourished for several centuries before the formation of early states. Using their data, the authors confirm that **in the absence of the coercive power of state machinery, inequality had already risen significantly in the land-limited economies of the Bronze Age**. They divided the sample into four situations: (1) labor-limited and without a state, (2) labor-limited and with a state, (3) land-limited and without a state, and (4) land-limited and with a state, and then compared the wealth Gini of each group. The results show that from situation (1) to situation (3) (i.e., technology shifting from labor-intensive to land-intensive, but still without a state), the average Gini rose from about 0.355 to 0.577, an increase of about 63%; and from situation (3) to situation (4) (the appearance of a proto-state regime), the average Gini rose further from 0.577 to 0.706. This means that **the increase in the importance of land/wealth alone was already enough to bring inequality to a fairly high level, and the appearance of early states added the finishing touch, raising inequality to yet another level**. Especially noteworthy is that once early states developed the use of slave labor, the degree of inequality became extreme: the authors estimate that in societies with a centralized polity and widespread use of slave labor (such as the Roman Empire), the average wealth Gini was more than 0.08 higher than that of proto-states without slaves (about 0.78 vs. 0.70), a highly significant difference. If slaves themselves are treated as wealth (with slave owners possessing the person and labor value of slaves), then the actual wealth Gini could be as high as 0.87. Therefore, **state power and coercive mechanisms allowed elites to consolidate the advantages they had previously gained economically, and pushed inequality toward a more enduring and more extreme state**.

In sum, the paper's data analysis and model results corroborate one another, painting a **picture** of the evolution of wealth inequality in human society:

> In the prehistoric stage, when the environment was permissive and groups prized equality, inequality was suppressed to a low level and was difficult to sustain over the long term; but when **material conditions** and **social scale** changed—farming **technology** raised the possibility of accumulation, population pressure made **land** a scarce resource, and **political organization** developed toward higher levels—wealth inequality broke through its earlier constraints and began to **persist and steadily intensify**.

## Discussion

#### **Main Findings and Their Significance**

The main finding of this study is that **enduring wealth inequality in human society was not a natural and inevitable result of the appearance of agriculture, but was able to emerge only under specific technological, institutional, and cultural conditions in the late Neolithic**. Throughout the long age of hunting-gathering and early farming, a cultural and institutional environment of "radical egalitarianism" generally prevailed, in which group members, through mechanisms of sharing and checks and balances, effectively prevented wealth gaps from becoming fixed across generations. This finding highlights the key role of **cultural-institutional factors** in the formation of inequality: rather than viewing inequality as a natural product of the development of productive forces, the authors demonstrate that social norms can delay or even block the widening of inequality. Only when new **technological/ecological conditions** appeared (such as ox-plough technology raising the importance of land wealth) and weakened the dominant position of labor in the economy, and when new **political structures** formed (such as elites beginning to concentrate power and establishing the rudiments of proto-states), was this equilibrium maintained by egalitarian norms broken. In other words, **the birth of enduring inequality stemmed from a joint transformation of technological, political, and cultural factors**. The significance of this integrated perspective is that it explains both why the level of inequality changed little for a considerable time after the Agricultural Revolution and why, once conditions ripened, inequality underwent a "leap." Theoretically, this challenges the simple causal chain of "**agriculture → private property → inequality**" and emphasizes the powerful equilibrating forces within society during the intervening millennia and the reasons for their eventual failure.

#### **Alternative or Competing Explanations**

The authors' conclusions stand in contrast to certain existing theories and also respond to debates among different viewpoints. Among these, the **theory of state formation** is one important competing explanation. Acemoglu and Robinson, for example, have argued that the appearance of the state and elite extraction are the main drivers of rising inequality. However, the authors raise a question: without other changes, why would an early egalitarian public allow a minority to seize a dominant position? In prehistoric societies that lacked vast disparities in force, an ordinary hunter could still use a ranged weapon to kill a would-be tyrant, and "for an organized elite to extort an unorganized commonality" was not easy to accomplish at the time. More importantly, the temporal order does not match either: empirical data show that large increases in inequality **preceded** the establishment of the formal state. For example, both the archaeologist Wright's research and Figure 1 of the paper indicate that **significant wealth inequality appeared in the Bronze Age**, whereas genuine state regimes and effective elite coercive power formed only afterward. Therefore, attributing the origins of inequality entirely to state power is insufficient. Another viewpoint emphasizes **elite force and technologies of expropriation** (such as the "**predatory technology**" proposed by Dow and Reed) as making inequality possible, but the authors likewise point out that this explanation overlooks a problem: without some degree of "**consent**" or cooperation within the group, how could elite force succeed? Prehistoric farmers were not unarmed, and to impose enduring oppression in an environment where everyone was a "radical egalitarian" was extremely difficult. In fact, the authors argue that **ordinary people's acceptance of new institutions** is one of the key factors in whether inequality can persist. They point out that when new technologies and forms of production organization brought benefits, even if in the long run these changes mainly benefited the elite, some ordinary people might support these changes because of short-term gains or social identification, thereby "**actively accepting**" a new order that would harm their own relative position. This perspective differs from a purely elite-coercion account, emphasizing the role of non-elites in the process of establishing an unequal order: **without a certain degree of cooperation or acquiescence, a stable class society is difficult to form**.

In addition, some scholars explain inequality from the angles of **population pressure**, **warfare**, and **geographical environment** (such as Carneiro's "circumscription and the origin of the state" theory, which holds that population growth and land scarcity drove competition and the emergence of the state/inequality). The authors' findings partly accord with these views—for example, land becoming scarce is indeed one of the key factors—but they also go beyond single-factor explanations, demonstrating a **complex interaction**: population-land pressure (an ecological factor) + new agricultural technology (an economic factor) + elite organization (a political factor) + the loosening of cultural norms (an ideational factor) acted jointly, and only then did enduring inequality ultimately arise. This multi-factor framework provides a richer context for understanding prehistoric inequality, and it is also accommodating toward other theories: no single alternative theory suffices to explain all the phenomena, but each can be incorporated into and discussed as one link in the authors' integrated model.

Overall, through data and models the authors respond to the various theories and propose that **the origins of enduring inequality are not the mechanical product of some single law (such as agriculture or the state), but the product of an integrated social-technological-political transformation**. This view is groundbreaking in scholarly terms, offering a new path to reconcile the debate surrounding the origins of inequality and providing empirical evidence for it.

## Conclusion

#### **The Final Conclusions of the Study**

The conclusion of the paper is clear: enduring economic inequality in human society did not exist from time immemorial, but truly emerged only roughly five thousand years ago, in the **late Neolithic**. Before that, even when wealth gaps occasionally widened in various societies, the conditions for such gaps to persist were lacking—on the contrary, egalitarian culture and practices continually "pulled inequality back." Only after certain new factors appeared did inequality break through this cycle and accumulate:

- First, new farming **technologies** (such as ox plowing and the use of animal traction) raised the productive role of material property, making diligence alone insufficient to secure one's livelihood status, and wealth began to depend more on **heritable property (fields, livestock)**;
- Second, as settlements expanded and resource competition arose, early **political structures** that concentrated chiefly power appeared, and elites were able to extract the public surplus and consolidate their own position;
- Finally, and perhaps as a consequence, the **social norms** that maintained equality gradually weakened or were breached, and members of society began to accept the reality of hierarchical differentiation.

This "three-pronged" combination ultimately led to the formation of true **"enduring wealth inequality."** In the history that followed (the Bronze Age, the Iron Age, and on into modern times), once inequality had technological and political support, it remained a basic feature of every civilized society, and in certain circumstances (such as slave societies) reached extremely high levels. The authors particularly stress that **the "enduring inequality" they seek to explain does not refer to certain families remaining wealthy, but to the class differentiation of society as a whole being stably present across generations**. In this sense, the paper successfully explains why such fixed class divisions were absent in the Paleolithic and early Neolithic, yet became the norm afterward.

#### **Research Contributions**

This study makes contributions in several respects:

- First, it constructs and uses a **large quantitative archaeological database** spanning multiple periods, which is rare in previous research in economic history and archaeology. By calculating the wealth Gini coefficients of different prehistoric societies, the authors extend the application of contemporary inequality measures to the prehistoric era, providing a quantitative basis for comparing economic inequality across different historical periods;
- Second, the paper **integrates archaeological evidence with economic theory**: it offers both in-depth analysis of archaeological details and anthropological cases and the introduction of evolutionary game models and quantitative comparison, building a bridge between the empirical and the theoretical. This interdisciplinary methodology demonstrates a new paradigm for studying ancient economic phenomena and represents a methodological innovation for the discipline of economics;
- Third, theoretically, the authors propose an **integrated framework** to explain the origins of inequality, emphasizing the interaction of three sets of factors: technology, institutions, and culture. This framework enriches our understanding of the mechanisms of the long-term evolution of economic inequality and, differing from single-factor explanations, is therefore illuminating for economic history, institutional economics, and research on social inequality.

#### **Shortcomings and Limitations**

Despite its significant contributions, the paper also has certain limitations:

- First, the sparseness and uncertainty of the archaeological data pose challenges to the conclusions. The authors candidly admit that many of their inferences are more accurately described as "**reasonable conjecture**," because direct evidence of prehistoric wealth distribution is very limited and can only be inferred with the help of proxy indicators. Issues such as the comparability of data between different sites and sample bias may all affect the precision of the Gini coefficient estimates. Although the authors try to improve reliability as much as possible through various adjustments and detailed appendices, this limitation remains unavoidable;
- Second, the study focuses mainly on **western Eurasia**, a limited scope from the perspective of human civilization as a whole. Although the authors examine the evidence from the upper Mun River valley in Thailand to verify the generality of their view, the situation in other regions where agriculture arose independently (such as Mesoamerica and East Asia) is not discussed in detail. Whether the timing of the emergence of enduring inequality in these regions is similar, and whether it likewise conforms to the conditions the authors propose, requires further research;
- Third, the model's assumptions are relatively **idealized**. In reality, factors such as inter-group conflict, cultural transmission, and dramatic environmental change may all act on the evolution of inequality simultaneously. Although the authors' model demonstrates that egalitarian norms can be stable over the long term, it can only offer an abstract account of complex historical processes and cannot encompass all the influencing factors. This means that when their conclusions are applied to the evolution of a particular civilization, they must still be weighed against the unique background of that civilization;
- Finally, regarding **why** egalitarian norms ultimately loosened and how ordinary people came to "**accept**" the new order of inequality, although the paper discusses this it remains an open question. This involves mechanisms at the more subtle levels of psychology, social networks, and the like, which this study was unable to explore in depth.

## AI Commentary

**Points of innovation**: The greatest innovation of this paper lies in its **combination of a macro perspective with micro-level mechanisms**. The authors examine the macro phenomenon of economic inequality on a time scale of more than ten thousand years, using quantitative archaeological indicators to fill a gap in historical statistical data. This "big history" perspective is very rare and bold, and refreshingly so. At the same time, they do not stop at describing trends, but dig deep into the mechanisms behind them, including the evolution of cultural customs and the rise of political structures. This integrated research approach spanning archaeology, anthropology, and economics is highly groundbreaking. In addition, the paper **redefines the angle of the question**: shifting from the previous focus on the "**one-step occurrence**" of the origins of inequality to a focus on how inequality "**persists**." This shift brings an entirely new problem consciousness and explanatory framework, and is a paradigmatic contribution to the field of inequality research. Finally, the introduction of the model provides a quantitative deductive tool for the traditionally largely qualitative study of prehistoric societies, demonstrating a possible mechanism by which egalitarian norms maintain social stability. This evolutionary-game perspective enriches the way we understand cooperation and competition in human society.

**Limitations**: First, the issue of **data reliability** always lingers. Although the authors have done their best to make adjustments and have repeatedly stressed the conjectural nature of their conclusions, archaeological data ultimately leave a large margin of error. Some key conclusions (such as that the average Gini of the early Neolithic was only about 0.17) may be revised as more discoveries are made. In addition, the authors focus mainly on agrarian societies and pay less attention to inequality in **pastoral societies**. For example, did prehistoric nomadic and pastoral societies also follow a similar pattern? Under this economic form, wealth is mainly embodied in livestock, which may be easier to accumulate and thus give rise to inequality, but the discussion in the paper is limited. Furthermore, although the model is persuasive, its parameters and structure are relatively simplified. In reality, unequal groups did not always fail because of internal discord; they might also conquer egalitarian groups by force (history also offers cases of affluent elite groups expanding). The model does not include dynamics such as **group amalgamation** and **imitation**, which may underestimate the possibility of inequality spreading. Therefore, the authors' conclusions still require caution when explaining the rise and fall of specific civilizations. Finally, although cultural factors are emphasized, the paper does not fully develop **how** "radical egalitarianism" originated or **what its essence is**. This may lie beyond the scope of economics, but it is very important for understanding why early humans chose the egalitarian path. Some hold that this is related to modes of hunting, religious concepts, and so on, and one hopes there will be more interdisciplinary discussion in the future.

**Inspiration and outlook**: The insights this paper brings are far-reaching. First, it reminds us that **inequality is not a linearly rising, inevitable course for humanity to pass through**. In human prehistory there was a long stage of "egalitarian society," which means that the degree of inequality in modern society is a product of specific conditions, not an unchangeable fate. This gives those seeking social fairness today a certain confidence: by adjusting institutions and culture, it may be possible to partly recreate the egalitarian mechanisms of prehistory on a human scale. Second, the paper's method demonstrates the enormous potential of the intersection of historical research and economics. Quantitative analysis need not be confined to periods with written records; archaeological and anthropological data can also be connected with economic models. This opens a road for "**long-durée**" economic research. Third, the authors' framework can be used to compare the evolution of inequality across different civilizations. For example, we can ask: why did strong inequality appear relatively early in ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia, while in certain regions (such as most of prehistoric Europe) marked stratification did not appear until the middle-to-late Bronze Age? Such comparisons could test whether the technology-land-state three-factor model proposed by the authors is universally applicable. Finally, this study prompts new reflection on the **power of cultural norms**. Modern economics often focuses on institutions and technology and pays insufficient attention to culture. Yet the prehistoric experience shows that cultural ideas (such as an egalitarian ethos) can shape economic outcomes over the long term. Therefore, in contemporary discussions of narrowing the wealth gap, perhaps we should also consider how to foster a social-cultural atmosphere conducive to equality. In short, this paper not only answers the riddle of prehistoric inequality but also offers a unique historical mirror for understanding and addressing the problem of inequality in today's world. As the authors show, only by jointly considering all three aspects—technological progress, institutional arrangements, and cultural ideas—can we more fully grasp the causes of inequality and its possible paths of evolution.
