The paper reviews archaeological evidence from western Eurasia spanning the Neolithic to the Bronze Age (approximately 11,700 to 5,300 years ago), noting that while occasional instances of pronounced wealth inequality did appear, these were mostly fleeting and rare, with persistent inequality only emerging in the late Neolithic. The authors argue that an early **culture of fierce egalitarianism** suppressed the emergence of durable wealth disparities, until changes in farming **technology** (such as ox-drawn ploughs) raised the value of material wealth relative to labor, and the **concentration of elite power** in proto-states, coupled with the emergence of **slave labor**, created the conditions for sustained economic inequality.

**Title:** The Origins of Enduring Economic Inequality

**Journal:** *Journal of Economic Literature*

**Authors:**

- <u>Samuel Bowles</u>: Research Professor and Director of the Behavioral Sciences Program, Santa Fe Institute. Sam’s scientific curiosity and passion has led him to explore three broad themes: The long-term evolution of societal institutions, including property rights and forms of cooperation, with attention to the effects on economic and political inequality. The coevolution of societal institutions and individual behaviors (and the values motivating them), focusing on the genetic and cultural evolution of ethical and other-regarding preferences. The creation and propagation of a new paradigm in economics to transform what students learn and how citizens think and talk about the economy and its evolving place in the biosphere.
- <u>Mattia Fochesato</u>: Assistant Professor at the Department of Social and Political Science, Bocconi University. His research focuses on the long run determinants of income and wealth distribution across different world regions.

## Detailed Summary

*The Origins of Enduring Economic Inequality* reviews archaeological evidence for economic inequality in prehistory and explores the **origins of persistent wealth inequality**. By analyzing data from hunter-gatherer and Neolithic farming societies, the authors find that while high wealth inequality did appear in human societies before 5,000 years ago, these episodes were typically transient and rare, contrasting sharply with the large-scale, persistent inequality that has characterized the past five millennia. To explain this phenomenon, the paper investigates **why** long-lasting, pronounced wealth gaps emerged only millennia after the Agricultural Revolution, at the very end of the Neolithic, and examines **the mechanisms** through which these gaps were maintained across generations. Employing a multidisciplinary approach, the authors gather extensive archaeological data and construct models, proposing that a form of cultural norm existed end-0 before the late Neolithic—an ethos of end-1 **radical egalitarianism** —that blocked durable inequality. It was only in the late Neolithic that new farming **technologies**enhanced the value of material wealth relative to labor. Combined with the concentration of elite power in early proto-states and consequently, the exploitation of slave labor, these changes furnished the necessary political-economic conditions for the persistence of such hierarchical stratification.

## Introduction

Advances in archaeology have provided considerable evidence that ancient human societies were largely **egalitarian**, albeit with periodic glimmers of wealth inequality beginning in the late Upper Paleolithic. These early instances were exceedingly rare and unsustainable. For example, the Sungir site in the Russian Plain (ca. 24,000 years ago) yielded extraordinarily lavish grave goods, yet among the 29 excavated Upper Paleolithic burial sites investigated, no counterpart comparable to that has been found anywhere. Across the 46 cemeteries discovered in France over the 5,000-year terminal stretch of the Paleolithic, there commences likewise appeared no evidence marking affluence as pronounced on par with the artifacts held from Sungir territory. This suggests that **inequality in early human societies, where it existed, was scarce and fleeting**.

Entering the early Neolithic—despite the onset of **sedentism** and incipient agriculture—the archaeological record still points to societies that remained relatively egalitarian on average side, lacking firmly entrenched, multi-generational class divisions. The Levantine Natufian culture (ca. 12,000–10,000 BCE), for example, was once viewed as an early near- to **quasi-farming** stock.
