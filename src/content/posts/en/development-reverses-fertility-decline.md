> The broad development experience of human societies in the twentieth century shows that as the level of economic and social development rises, the total fertility rate of a population trends downward. This negative relationship has become one of the most robust and widely accepted empirical regularities in the social sciences.
>
> In this 2009 Nature paper, however, the authors show that as human development rises to a very high level and increases further, the relationship between development and fertility turns from negative to positive. This means that the fertility rebound brought about by high levels of development could slow the pace of population aging and ease the social and economic problems associated with low fertility.
>
> But looking back from more than a decade later, did things really turn out the way the paper found?

**Title:** Advances in development reverse fertility declines

**Journal:** Nature

**Authors:**

- Mikko Myrskylä: Director of the Max Planck Institute for Demographic Research and Professor of Social Data Science at the University of Helsinki; his work focuses on population health, fertility dynamics and population forecasting, among other areas;
- Hans-Peter Kohler: Frederick J. Warren Professor of Demography at the University of Pennsylvania; his main research focus is fertility and health in developing and developed countries.
- Francesco C. Billari: Professor of Demography at Bocconi University; his main interests are fertility and family change, the transition to adulthood, life-course analysis, population forecasting, digitalization and demography, and comparative survey research.

**Publication date:** 6 August 2009

### Background

#### **Fertility-related data**

- **Total fertility rate**: Total Fertility Rate-**TFR**, the average number of children a woman bears over her reproductive lifetime; it is one of the most commonly used indices for measuring the fertility level of a region or country. It is typically used to project population growth rates, understand changes in population structure, and assess the effects of social policies and economic development on fertility behavior;
- **Replacement level**: A total fertility rate of around **2.1** is usually considered replacement-level fertility, meaning the population can naturally replace itself, neither growing nor shrinking. If the total fertility rate is below 2.1, the population may shrink; if it is above 2.1, the population will grow. (In other words, if each couple has 2.1 children on average, the society's total population remains stable over the long run.)
- **Fertility warning level**: A total fertility rate of **1.5** is considered the warning level. International experience shows that once a country's or region's total fertility rate falls below 1.5, it is very difficult for it to rebound;
- A set of current figures: Data from China's seventh national population census ("七普") show that China's total fertility rate in 2020 was 1.3, below the warning level; in 2023, China's total fertility rate fell to 1.0, while Japan's total fertility rate that same year was 1.3. In 2023, South Korea's total fertility rate was 0.72, the lowest in the world, and Shanghai's total fertility rate that same year was 0.7. In cross-national comparison, China's current total fertility rate is the third lowest in the world (or thereabouts). The current total fertility rates of the world's major developed countries lie between 1.3 and 1.8.

#### **Human Development Index**

Human Development Index-**HDI**, a composite indicator proposed by the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) to measure a country's level of social and economic development. It aims to go beyond the traditional use of Gross Domestic Product (GDP) as the single yardstick of economic development, providing a more comprehensive perspective on development by assessing the various aspects of human development more fully. The Human Development Index is based on the following three main dimensions:

- **Longevity indicator**: a long and healthy life, measured by life expectancy to gauge the level of health;
- **Education indicator**: being knowledgeable, measured by the adult literacy rate and years of schooling (including expected years of schooling and mean years of schooling) to gauge the level of education;
- **Standard-of-living indicator**: having a decent standard of living, measured by Gross National Income per capita (GNI per capita) to gauge a country's economic conditions and living standards.

These indicators are aggregated (geometric mean) and normalized to produce a value between 0 and 1, which serves as a measure of a country's level of human development. The closer the HDI value is to 1, the higher the country's level of human development.

HDI does not reflect inequality, poverty, human security, empowerment and the like; these are reported in the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (Sustainable Development Goals-**SDGs**).

The countries and regions currently at the top of the HDI ranking include Switzerland, Norway, Finland, Hong Kong, Denmark, Sweden, Germany and Iceland, all with an HDI greater than or equal to 0.95; China scores 0.79, ranking 75th (out of 195).

### The "J-shaped" relationship between the Human Development Index and the total fertility rate

#### **Cross-sectional analysis**

- In the figure below, the blue squares show the distribution of HDI and TFR in 1975, when HDI ranged from 0.25 to 0.887 and TFR from 1.45 to 8.5. Overall, the fitted curve declines throughout, with a correlation between the two (Spearman's rank correlation) of **-0.85** (P<0.01);
- The red triangles show the relationship between HDI and TFR in 2005, with HDI ranging from 0.3 to 0.966 and TFR from 1.08 to 7.7. When HDI<0.85, the correlation between HDI and TFR is -0.84 (P<0.01), while when HDI≥0.9, the correlation between the two turns from negative to positive, at **0.51** (P<0.01);
- In 2005, the average TFR of countries with an HDI between 0.9 and 0.92 was 1.24, while among countries with an HDI above 0.95 the average TFR reached 1.89. In these highest-HDI countries, the population can be maintained at replacement level with a relatively small need for immigration.

#### **Longitudinal analysis**

- Figure 3 shows the time paths of the HDI-TFR relationship for all countries with an HDI≥0.9 in 2005;
- Definition of the **reference year**: for a country or region, the year with the lowest TFR within the window in which its HDI lies between 0.85 and 0.9 is selected as the reference year;
- The second and fourth quadrants show TFR declining as HDI increases; the first quadrant shows TFR rebounding as HDI increases;
- In 2005, 18 countries had entered the **first quadrant**: Norway, the Netherlands, the United States, Denmark (1), Germany (2), Spain (3), Belgium (4), Luxembourg (5), Finland (6), Israel (7), Italy (8), Sweden (9), France (10), Iceland (11), the United Kingdom (12), New Zealand (13), Greece (14) and Ireland (15). That is, as their level of development rose further and the HDI of these 18 countries reached 0.9 or higher, the earlier downward trend in their total fertility rates had been reversed. For example, the fertility reversal in the United States occurred in 1976 (the reference year), when its HDI was 0.881; Norway's reversal took place in 1983, when its HDI was 0.892; in Israel, the reversal of the TFR decline occurred in 1992, when its HDI was 0.880;
- The countries in the **fourth quadrant** in 2005 were Japan, Austria (16), Australia (17), Switzerland (18), Canada (19) and South Korea (20); in these countries, further increases in HDI did not bring a reversal of low fertility.

#### **Regression analysis and the critical level**

- Methods including a difference-in-differences model with time fixed effects were used to estimate the effect of changes in HDI on TFR, using panel data on 37 countries over 1975-2005 whose HDI had reached 0.85 by 2005;
- The critical level at which the HDI-TFR relationship turns from negative to positive is **HDI=0.86**; when HDI<0.86, the effect of a one-unit increase in HDI on TFR is -1.59 (P<0.05), while when HDI≥0.85, the marginal effect of its change is 4.07 (P<0.001);
- In practical terms, when HDI≥0.85, every 0.05 increase in HDI raises the average number of children born per woman by 0.2.

### Research implications

- Digging into the mechanisms: Why, in most developed countries, do increases in HDI at higher levels cause fertility to rebound? Given the heterogeneity of institutional, cultural and policy environments across developed countries, further research is needed to investigate the different mechanisms behind this reversal, especially in view of exceptions such as **Japan**, Canada and **South Korea**. Specifically, we need a better understanding of how improvements in labor market flexibility, social security and individual welfare, gender and economic equality, human capital, and social/family policies can promote a relative rise in fertility in developed societies;
- The combination of high HDI and low TFR in the wealthy East Asian countries (Japan, South Korea) can perhaps be explained by the fact that these countries did not, over the course of their development, build institutions that promote work-family balance and gender equality;
- As long as the most developed countries focus their efforts on the well-being of their citizens and build sound institutions, further advances at high levels of development have the potential to reverse the trend of fertility decline; these countries may then be able to maintain stable population sizes and slow the negative effects of population aging.

### Looking back more than a decade later, has the fertility decline really been reversed?

#### **Doubts from peers**

This Nature paper was published in 2009 and attracted widespread attention; its citation count has now exceeded one thousand.

<img src="https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240319202615463.png" alt="image-20240319202615463" style="zoom:67%;" />

Figure 5: The paper's citation count as of March 2024, as shown on Google Scholar

Some scholars have also questioned the paper's key finding. In a paper published in 2021, Hampton Gray Gaddy used updated data and found that the positive relationship between higher levels of development and fertility seems to have broken down over the past 10 years. For details, see the paper: [A decade of TFR declines suggests no relationship between development and sub-replacement fertility rebounds](https://doi.org/10.4054/DemRes.2021.44.5).

![image-20240322152039030](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240322152039030.png)

#### **Analysis and projections from the latest *Lancet* paper**

On March 20, 2024, *The Lancet* published online a paper that systematically analyzed changes in fertility across 204 countries and territories worldwide from 1950 to 2021, and projected fertility scenarios out to the year 2100.

![Figure thumbnail gr1](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/gr1.jpg)

⬆️ The *Lancet* paper's recorded and projected TFRs for each region of the world

Note the trajectory of the purple High income line: its TFR shows no clear reversal between 2000 and 2021, and displays a marked decline after 2010.

The paper finds that from 1950 to 2021, the global TFR fell by more than half, from 4.84 to 2.23, with TFRs declining in all countries and territories. In 2021, 94 countries and territories still had TFRs above the replacement level (2.1); by 2100, that number will fall to 6. The global TFR is projected to drop to 1.83 by 2050 and to 1.59 by 2100.

**Title**: Global fertility in 204 countries and territories, 1950–2021, with forecasts to 2100: a comprehensive demographic analysis for the Global Burden of Disease Study 2021

**Authors**: GBD 2021 Fertility and Forecasting Collaborators

**Link**: [https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(24)00550-6](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(24)00550-6)
