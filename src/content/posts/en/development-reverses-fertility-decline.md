> Extensive development experience across human societies in the twentieth century shows that as socio‑economic development rises, the total fertility rate tends to fall. This negative association has become one of the most robust and universally accepted empirical regularities in the social sciences.
>
> Yet in this 2009 *Nature* paper, the authors show that when human development advances to a very high level and then increases further, the development–fertility relationship turns from negative to positive. That implies the fertility rebound brought about by high development could slow the pace of population ageing and improve the social and economic problems linked to low fertility.
>
> But, looking back from more than a decade later, does the evidence really pan out as the paper found?

**Title:** Advances in development reverse fertility declines

**Journal:** Nature

**Authors:**

- Mikko Myrskylä: Director of the Max Planck Institute for Demographic Research and Professor of Social Data Science at the University of Helsinki; his work centers on population health, fertility dynamics and population forecasting.
- Hans-Peter Kohler: Frederick J. Warren Professor of Demography at the University of Pennsylvania; his main research focuses on fertility and health in both developing and developed countries.
- Francesco C. Billari: Professor of Demography at Bocconi University; his main interests are fertility and family change, transitions to adulthood, life‑course analysis, demographic foresight, digitalisation and demography, and comparative survey research.

**Publication date:** 6 August 2009

### Background

#### **Fertility‑related indicators**

- **Total Fertility Rate (TFR)**: The average number of children a woman would bear over her reproductive lifetime; one of the commonly used indices to measure the fertility level of a region or country. It is often used to project population growth, understand changes in population structure, and evaluate how social policies and economic development shape fertility behaviour.
- **Replacement‑level fertility**: A total fertility rate of about **2.1** is generally regarded as replacement‑level fertility, meaning the population can naturally replace itself, neither growing nor shrinking. When TFR falls below 2.1, the population may gradually decline; when it exceeds 2.1, the population tends to grow. (That is, a couple needs to have, on average, 2.1 children for the long‑term total population size to remain stable.)
- **Warning‑level fertility**: A total fertility rate of **1.5** is deemed a warning level. International experience suggests that once a country’s or region’s TFR drops below 1.5, it is very hard for it to recover.
- A set of current figures: China’s seventh national population census recorded a TFR of 1.3 in 2020, below the warning level; in 2023 China’s TFR fell to 1.0, while in the same year Japan’s TFR stood at 1.3. In 2023, South Korea’s TFR was 0.72, the lowest in the world, and Shanghai’s TFR in the same year was 0.7. Viewed cross‑nationally, China’s current TFR ranks around third‑last (more or less). Current TFRs in the world’s major developed countries lie roughly between 1.3 and 1.8.

#### **Human Development Index**

The Human Development Index – **HDI** – is a composite measure of a country’s level of socio‑economic development, proposed by the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP). It aims to go beyond the traditional reliance on Gross Domestic Product (GDP) as the sole economic measure and instead offers a more holistic perspective on development by assessing different dimensions of human well‑being more comprehensively. HDI is based on three principal dimensions:

- **Health dimension**: a long and healthy life, measured by life expectancy to gauge health levels;
- **Education dimension**: being knowledgeable, measured by the adult literacy rate and length of schooling (comprising both expected years of schooling and mean years of schooling) to gauge educational attainment;
- **Standard‑of‑living dimension**: having a decent standard of living, measured by Gross National Income per capita to gauge a country’s economic conditions and living standards.

These indicators are aggregated (via the geometric mean) and normalised to a value between 0 and 1, serving as a measure of a country’s human development level. The closer HDI is to 1, the higher the country’s human development.

HDI does not capture dimensions such as inequality, poverty, human security or empowerment; these are reported separately in relation to the United Nations’ Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

The highest‑ranked countries and regions measured by current HDI include Switzerland, Norway, Finland, Hong Kong, Denmark, Sweden, Germany and Iceland, all of which score ≥ 0.95. China scores 0.79 and ranks 75th (out of 195).

### The “J‑shaped” relationship between the Human Development Index and the total fertility rate

#### **Cross‑sectional analysis**

- In the figure below, blue squares show the distribution of HDI and TFR in 1975: HDI then ranged between 0.25 and 0.887, TFR between 1.45 and 8.5, and overall the fitted curve is downward‑sloping, with a Spearman’s rank correlation of **–0.85** (P < 0.01).
- Red triangles show the HDI–TFR relationship for 2005, with HDI ranging from 0.3 to 0.966 and TFR ranging from 1.08 to 7.7. Where HDI < 0.85, the HDI‑TFR correlation is –0.84 (P < 0.01); where HDI ≥ 0.9, the correlation turns from negative to positive, reaching **0.51** (P < 0.01).
- In 2005, the mean TFR of countries with HDI between 0.9 and 0.92 was 1.24, whereas among countries with HDI greater than 0.95 the mean TFR reached 1.89. In the highest‑HDI countries the population could sustain replacement‑level fertility with only modest reliance on immigration.

#### **Longitudinal analysis**

- Figure 3 shows the time paths of the HDI–TFR relationship for all countries with an HDI ≥ 0.9 in 2005.
- **Definition of the reference year**: For a country or region, the year within the HDI window of 0.85–0.9 that recorded its lowest TFR is taken as the reference year.
- The second and fourth quadrants show cases where TFR falls as HDI increases; the first quadrant shows cases where TFR rebounds as HDI increases.
- In 2005, 18 countries had entered the **first quadrant** : Norway, Netherlands, United States, Denmark (1), Germany (2), Spain (3), Belgium (4), Luxembourg (5), Finland (6), Israel (7), Italy (8), Sweden (9), France (10), Iceland (11), United Kingdom (12), New Zealand (13), Greece (14) and Ireland (15). In other words, with further advances in development these 18 countries reached an HDI of 0.9 or higher, and their earlier TFR decline has reversed. For example, the reversal in the United States occurred in 1976 (the reference year), when HDI stood at 0.881; Norway’s reversal happened in 1983, when HDI was 0.892; in Israel the reversal of TFR decline occurred in 1992, when HDI was 0.880.
- The countries that stayed in the **fourth quadrant** in 2005 were Japan, Austria (16), Australia (17), Switzerland (18), Canada (19) and South Korea (20); here, further increases in HDI did not bring about a reversal of low fertility.

#### **Regression analysis and threshold level**

- Models including difference‑in‑differences with time fixed effects were employed to estimate the effect of HDI variation on TFR; the panel data span 37 countries observed between 1975 and 2005 whose HDI reached 0.85 by 2005.
- The threshold at which the HDI–TFR association turns from negative to positive is **HDI = 0.86**. When HDI < 0.86, a one‑unit increase in HDI is associated with a change in TFR of –1.59 (P < 0.05); when HDI ≥ 0.85, the marginal effect is 4.07 (P < 0.001).
- In practical terms, above the HDI threshold of 0.85, each increase of 0.05 in HDI adds an average of 0.2 children per woman.

### Insights

- Exploring mechanisms: Why has a higher HDI led to a fertility rebound in most developed countries? Given the heterogeneity of developed nations’ institutional, cultural and policy settings, further research is needed to unpack the different mechanisms underlying this reversal – particularly keeping in mind outlying cases such as **Japan**, Canada and **South Korea**. More specifically, there is a need to understand better how improved labour‑market flexibility, social security and personal welfare, gender and economic equality, human capital and social/family policies might each foster a relative rise in fertility within developed societies.
- The association between high HDI and low TFR among East Asia’s prosperous countries (Japan, South Korea) may be interpretable through their lack of institution‑building to promote work–family balance and gender equity along their development path.
- As long as the most developed countries focus on raising citizen well‑being and building sound institutions, further advances at high levels of development may yet reverse fertility decline; such countries could in turn sustain a stable population size and mitigate the negative effects of population ageing.

### A decade on: has the TFR decline really been reversed?

#### **Scepticism from peers**

This *Nature* paper was published in 2009, attracted widespread attention, and has now been cited more than a thousand times.

<img src="https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240319202615463.png" alt="image-20240319202615463" style="zoom:67%;" />

Figure 5: Google Scholar citation count as at March 2024

Some scholars have cast doubt on the paper’s key finding. In a 2021 publication, Hampton Gray Gaddy used updated data and found that the positive association between further increases in high development and fertility appears to have broken down in the most recent decade. For details see the paper: [A decade of TFR declines suggests no relationship between development and sub‑replacement fertility rebounds](https://doi.org/10.4054/DemRes.2021.44.5).

![image-20240322152039030](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240322152039030.png)

#### **Analysis and projections from a recent *Lancet* paper**

On 20 March 2024, a paper in *The Lancet* systematically analysed fertility trends for 204 countries and territories worldwide between 1950 and 2021, and projected fertility scenarios out to 2100.

![Figure thumbnail gr1](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/gr1.jpg)

⬆ Recorded and projected TFRs by global region from the *Lancet* paper

Look at the trajectory shown in purple – “High income”: its TFR between 2000 and 2021 reveals no clear rebound, and it shows again a pronounced decline after 2010.

The paper finds that, between 1950 and 2021, global TFR more than halved, dropping from 4.84 to 2.23, with TFRs declining in every country and territory. In 2021, TFR still remained above the replacement level of 2.1 in 94 countries and territories; by 2100 that number is projected to fall to 6. By 2050, global TFR is forecast to drop to 1.83, and by 2100 to 1.59.
