> Significant psychological differences exist between northern and southern Chinese people. For example, southerners are often thought to favor **holistic thinking** and **collectivism**, while northerners lean more toward **analytic thinking** and **individualism**. This difference is also frequently regarded as an East-West difference. What is the origin of this group-level personality difference?
>
> Talhelm proposes the **rice culture theory**, arguing that growing rice requires collective cooperation in irrigation, which differs considerably from the individual labor form of wheat, and that it is precisely this difference that gives rise to the personality differences between northern and southern populations. Using field experiments based in rice regions and wheat regions (psychological experiments concerning holistic thinking and individualism) and statistical data (divorce rates, invention patents), Talhelm confirmed that the explanatory power of the rice culture theory is causal.

**Title:** Large-Scale Psychological Differences Within China Explained by Rice Versus Wheat Agriculture

**Journal:** Science

**First Author:**

- Thomas Talhelm, Associate Professor at the University of Chicago Booth School of Business, a behavioral scientist with a strong interest in cultural research, paying particular attention to the cultural psychology of Chinese people. He has made outstanding contributions to systematizing and promoting the rice culture theory.

**Publication date:** 9 May 2014. This paper stems from work the first author did while on an exchange in China during his PhD studies.

<br/>

**Background:**

- Personality differences between Eastern and Western populations are widespread. Psychology holds that Western culture places more emphasis on individualism and analytic thinking, whereas East Asian culture places more emphasis on collectivism and holistic thinking.
- **Analytic thinking** refers to a mode of thought that emphasizes abstract concepts and formal reasoning, such as the "law of non-contradiction": if A is true, then not-A is false. **Holistic thinking** approaches problems in an intuitive way: if A is true, not-A may also be true.
- This difference in thinking is reflected not only between East and West but also within East Asia, such as between northern and southern China.

**Competing explanations:**

- **The subsistence style theory**: Certain forms of subsistence (such as agriculture) require more mutual interdependence than others. For example, the independence and mobility of herding make pastoralists more individualistic, whereas the stability and high labor demands of settled agriculture make agricultural cultures more collectivist.
  - This theory has some explanatory power, but it lumps together different forms of settled agriculture; even within cropping, the cultivation of rice and wheat differs greatly.
  - The subsistence style theory has major limitations in explaining East-West differences, because many regions in the West also practice settled agriculture - for instance, wheat (or barley) is widely grown in Europe as well.

- **The modernization hypothesis**: As society grows wealthier, the population becomes more educated, and capitalism deepens, people become more individualistic and more analytic in their thinking.
  - This theory is supported by the World Values Survey as well as by research on the transition of indigenous Maya peoples to a market economy.
  - But the theory struggles to explain why Japan, South Korea, and Hong Kong still adhere to collectivism even though their GDP per capita already exceeds that of the European Union.

- **The pathogen prevalence theory**: A high prevalence of infectious diseases makes dealing with strangers dangerous, so these regions become more conservative and place more emphasis on collectivism.
  - Pathogen prevalence is related to heat, and rice also grows in tropical and subtropical regions, so pathogen theory may mask the underlying factor of rice cultivation.

**The rice theory:**

- The establishment of complex irrigation networks requires collective cooperation.
- Because of the networked nature of irrigation, participants' water-use behavior has externalities, so different households need to coordinate their water use.
- The dredging and maintenance of irrigation networks is often carried out by the village rather than falling on isolated individuals.
- Evidence shows that the labor time required to grow rice is at least twice that required for wheat.
- Rice cultivation involves a labor exchange during a window period: farmers coordinate their sowing dates so that different households harvest at different times, leaving a window for mutual help. ("**Double Rush**" - rushing to harvest and rushing to plant, haha.)

> By comparison, wheat cultivation requires little labor time and relies more on natural rainfall, with a lower need for cooperation.
>
> An earlier EJ post compared the cultivation differences between rice and wheat in more detail, and added the important mechanism that enforceable punishment makes collective cooperation effective. See: [EJ - Rice cultivation and the origin of cooperative behavior](https://yuzhang.net/2024/02/14/20240212-EJ-%E6%B0%B4%E7%A8%BB%E7%A7%8D%E6%A4%8D%E4%B8%8E%E5%90%88%E4%BD%9C%E8%A1%8C%E4%B8%BA%E7%9A%84%E8%B5%B7%E6%BA%90/).

**A natural experiment based on China:**

Northern and southern China make excellent subjects for an experiment. Bounded by the Yangtze River, the north has long grown more wheat and the south more rice.

- Northern Anhui consists mostly of wheat counties, while southern Anhui is rice-dominated.
- The controlled comparison between northern and southern China keeps the two groups within the same institutional background.

**Experiments and data:**

- **The triad task**: also called the triad task, in which participants are shown three items, such as a train, a bus, and a track, and asked to choose two to pair together. The existing literature finds that groups from Western individualist cultures prefer analytic matching, i.e., train and bus; whereas East Asians prefer to choose the holistic relationship, i.e., train and track.
- **The sociogram task**: participants are asked to draw a diagram of their own social network. The nodes are circles representing oneself and one's friends, and the circle representing oneself is often a different size from those representing others. Some studies have found that Americans draw themselves about 6 millimeters larger than others. Europeans draw themselves about 3.5 millimeters larger, while Japanese draw themselves slightly smaller.
- **The loyalty and nepotism task**: this measures whether people make a clear distinction in how they treat friends versus strangers. Collectivist cultures are characterized by people drawing a clear line between friends and strangers. Participants are asked to imagine conducting a business transaction with (a) an honest friend, (b) a dishonest friend, (c) an honest stranger, and (d) a dishonest stranger; honesty would cause the participant to make money, while dishonesty would cause them to lose money. Across different scenarios, how much would the participant spend to punish or reward them? Past experiments found that Singaporeans reward friends far more than they punish them, which can be seen as a form of loyalty. Americans are more likely than Singaporeans to punish friends for bad behavior. This study predicts that, compared with people from wheat regions, people from rice regions are less likely to punish their friends.

- Other data: provincial divorce rates (the idea being that regions where individualism prevails have higher divorce rates), the number of invention patents (the idea being that regions dominated by individualism and analytic thinking have more invention patents), and other data.

**Results:**

- The rice theory successfully explains the formation of holistic thinking and individualism in the north and south. People from provinces with a higher proportion of rice paddies are more holistic in their thinking; the results remain significant even when narrowed down to counties in Sichuan, Chongqing, Hubei, Anhui, and Jiangsu.
- The competing explanations of the modernization hypothesis and the pathogen prevalence theory are ruled out: the modernization hypothesis holds that people in less developed regions are more holistic in their thinking, but in the regression results the coefficient on GDP per capita is positive, with people in developed regions being more holistic; the pathogen prevalence theory holds that provinces with higher disease incidence are more holistic, but the regression results show that high-incidence regions actually have less holistic thinking (the regression coefficient is negative, and the result remains robust when historical incidence is narrowed down to the county where the participant lives).
- In the results of the sociogram task, people from rice regions are more likely to draw the circle representing themselves smaller than the circle representing their friends, while people from wheat regions draw the circle representing themselves 1.5 millimeters larger than that of their friends. (This analysis also rules out the modernization hypothesis and the pathogen prevalence theory.)
- In the loyalty and nepotism task, people from rice regions reward their friends more (and punish friends less); there is no difference between people from rice regions and wheat regions in how they treat strangers.
- Rice regions have lower divorce rates and fewer invention patents (providing evidence beyond the experiments).
- Using the climatic suitability for rice cultivation as an instrumental variable, the results remain robust (mitigating the reverse causality problem, i.e., that it is not because of holistic thinking that people prefer to grow rice).

**Discussion:**

- The rice theory also has explanatory power for the phenomenon whereby regions such as Japan and South Korea, which have already entered the ranks of the developed world, still exhibit deeply rooted holistic thinking.
- Rice can also be grown on dry land, which has lower water and cooperation requirements; thus, the role of irrigation in the formation of north-south differences needs further investigation.
- What is the mechanism? The rice theory provides a starting point but not a process; is the mechanism values, institutions, or something else? This needs further clarification. (In [EJ - Rice cultivation and the origin of cooperative behavior](https://yuzhang.net/2024/02/14/20240212-EJ-%E6%B0%B4%E7%A8%BB%E7%A7%8D%E6%A4%8D%E4%B8%8E%E5%90%88%E4%BD%9C%E8%A1%8C%E4%B8%BA%E7%9A%84%E8%B5%B7%E6%BA%90/), the author proposes one approach to testing the mechanism, namely mining the text of folktales and songs to find how value norms are transmitted ([Folklore](https://doi.org/10.1093/qje/qjab003), QJE 2021).)
- How long can the rice effect last? In an industrial age in which most people do not engage in agricultural practice, how much longer will the rice effect (holistic thinking and collectivism) persist?
- In the commentary in the same issue of Science, the commentator Professor Henrich mentions that the rice theory may help explain why the sparks of China's eleventh-century industrial revolution were extinguished: because northern (Mongol) invasions and climate change drove people south into the rice paddy regions, where clans held an ecological advantage, and the emergence of state-level political and legal institutions strengthened the power of clans ([AER](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.100.2.135), 2010).
