> Significant psychological differences exist between northern and southern Chinese people. It is often assumed that southerners prefer **holistic thinking** and **collectivism**, while northerners lean toward **analytic thinking** and **individualism**. These differences are frequently treated as being synonymous with East–West differences. So what are the origins of these group-level personality differences?
>
> Talhelm proposes the **rice culture theory**, arguing that because growing rice requires cooperative irrigation networks whereas wheat relies more on individual labor, this fundamental difference has shaped the personality differences between northern and southern populations. Using field experiments on holistic thinking and individualism conducted in rice and wheat regions, as well as statistical data (divorce rates and invention patents), Talhelm confirmed that the explanatory power of the rice culture theory is causal.

**Title:** Large-Scale Psychological Differences Within China Explained by Rice Versus Wheat Agriculture

**Journal:** Science

**First Author:**

 – Thomas Talhelm, Associate Professor of Behavioral Science at the University of Chicago Booth School of Business. As a behavioral scientist with a keen interest in cultural research, he pays particular attention to the cultural psychology of the Chinese people. He has made outstanding contributions to systematizing and popularizing the rice culture theory.

**Publication date:** 9 May 2014. This paper is the result of work the first author undertook while he was on an exchange in China during his PhD studies.

<br/>

**Background:** 

- Personality differences between Eastern and Western populations are widespread. In psychology it is held that Western cultures lay greater stress on individualism and analytic thinking, whereas East Asian cultures lay greater stress on collectivism and holistic thinking.
- **Analytic thinking** refers to a mode of thought that focuses on abstract concepts and formal reasoning—the "logical law of non-contradiction," such that if A is true, not-A must be false. **Holistic thinking** approaches problems in a more intuitive manner; if A is true, not-A may also simultaneously be true.
- This thinking-style difference appears not only between East and West but also within East Asia, for instance between northern and southern China.

**Competing explanations:**

- **The subsistence style theory**: Certain forms of subsistence (e.g., herding) require more interdependence than other forms. For example, the independence and mobility of pastoralism make herders more individualistic, whereas the stability and high labor demands of settled agriculture make agrarian cultures more collectivist.
  - This theory has some explanatory power, but it lumps different forms of settled agriculture together; yet within cropping, rice and wheat cultivation differ enormously.
  - The subsistence style theory faces major limitations in explaining East–West differences, because many areas in the West also practice settled agriculture—for example, wheat (or barley) is commonly grown throughout Europe.

- **The modernization hypothesis**: As societies become wealthier, educational attainment rises, and capitalism deepens, people become more individualistic and display more analytic thought.
  - This theory has been supported in the World Values Survey and in research on indigenous Maya communities as they transition to a market economy.
  - However, the hypothesis struggles to explain why Japan, South Korea, and Hong Kong persist with collectivism when their GDP per capita already exceeds that of the European Union.

- **The pathogen prevalence theory**: High prevalence of infectious diseases makes interacting with strangers hazardous, so those regions become more conservative and stress collectivism.
  - Pathogen prevalence is linked to temperature, and rice is also cultivated in tropical and subtropical regions, which means pathogen theory may mask the underlying role of rice farming.

**The rice theory:**

- Building complex irrigation networks requires collective cooperation.
- Because networks of irrigation generate externalities in water-use behavior among participants, different households need to coordinate their water usage.
- Dredging and maintaining these networks is normally carried out collectively by the village, rather than falling upon isolated individuals.
- Evidence shows that the labor time needed to grow rice is at least twice that required for wheat.
- An exchange of labor during limited "planting/harvest windows" happens in rice cultivation: farmers coordinate sowing dates so that different households harvest at different times, leaving open a window for mutual help. (Think "Double Rush—rush to harvest and rush to plant", haha.)

> In comparison, cultivating wheat requires far less labor time, relies much more on natural rainfall, and has a demonstrably far lower need for coordination.

**A natural experiment based on China:**

Northern and southern China make excellent subjects for natural experiments. Longitudinally divided clearly by the Yangtze River, the north has traditionally grown far more wheat and the south far more rice, passing on varying personality styles under vastly different agricultural regimes within the *same* institutional boundaries.

**Experiments and Data:**  

- **The triad task**: Participants are shown three items, e.g., a train, a bus, and a track, and asked to pick two and pair them up.. Existing literature finds that participants from Western individualist cultures prefer analytical matching, pairing train and bus together; East Asians tend to favor holistic links and so pair train and track together.
- **The sociogram task**: Participants draw representations of their social networks. Nodes are circles representing the self and friends; the “self” circle is often rendered in a different size from the others. One study reported that Americans drew a self circle approximately 6 mm bigger than the friend circle, Europeans about 3.5 mm larger, and Japanese smaller than friends.
- **The loyalty and nepotism task**: This measures whether people draw clear distinctions in how they treat friends versus strangers. Collectivist cultures are characterized by people drawing a sharp line between friends and strangers. Participants imagine conducting a business transaction with (a) an honest friend, (b) a dishonest friend, (c) an honest stranger, and (d) a dishonest stranger. Honesty would lead to participants earning money, dishonesty to losing money; across different scenarios they allocate money to reward or punish these characters. Earlier work found Singaporeans were markedly more rewarded friend was than punishing, showing strong loyalty. Compared with them, Americans were more likely to punish a friend who behaved poorly. The present study predicted those from rice regions are less prone to punishing friends than participants from the wheat heartlands, while this will precisely equal out in treatment of a meriting stranger.
