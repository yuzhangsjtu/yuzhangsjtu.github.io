> This article uses China’s anti-corruption campaign as an example to illustrate the logic behind an authoritarian regime’s transition from **tolerating corruption** to **severely punishing corruption**: the advantage of **economic performance** no longer dominates promotions, while **political loyalty** and integrity become more critical. High-performing officials are more susceptible to investigation during an anti-corruption campaign, and local policy agendas tend to align more closely with the central government. The transition is not self-enforcing, and the long-term equilibrium remains to be observed.

## Introduction

**Corruption as a Tool of Rule**

Under authoritarian systems, corruption is a complex phenomenon, linked not only to **institutional deficiencies** but also closely connected to the distribution of political power and strategies of rule. Traditional corruption research often simplistically attributes corruption to "weak institutions" or "insufficient oversight." However, recent studies have found that leaders of authoritarian regimes might choose to strategically tolerate corruption for **strategic considerations**.

Specifically, corruption plays a cross-cutting role in authoritarian systems:

- **Positive Effects** (Instrumental Value): Corruption can provide additional **economic rents** to the bureaucratic system. Given the scarcity of economic resources, these rents can be used to sustain the stability of the ruling coalition. For instance, by allowing officials to share the proceeds of corruption, leaders can incentivize them to promote local economic development while maintaining support for the regime.
- **Negative Effects** (Governance Risks): The spread of corruption triggers a cascade of problems, including **social dissatisfaction**, **resource misallocation**, and a **crisis of ruling legitimacy**. Once corruption spirals out of control, public trust in the regime plummets, threatening its stability.

In practice, there is significant variation in the tolerance for corruption among authoritarian regimes. Some regimes permit the existence of institutionalized corruption (Chang and Golden 2010; Montinola and Jackman 2002), while others rigorously strike against corruption, leading to substantial political purges within the party, bureaucracy, and military (Gillespie and Okruhlik 1991).

The above evidence implies that leaders of authoritarian regimes grapple with a fundamental **strategic dilemma**: they must strike a balance between **economic performance** and **political loyalty**. Using China's recent anti-corruption drive as a starting point, this paper proposes a **theoretical framework** for corruption governance.
