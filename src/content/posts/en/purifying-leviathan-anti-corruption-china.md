> Using China's anti-corruption campaign as an example, this article illustrates the logic of an authoritarian regime's transition from **tolerating corruption** to **severely punishing corruption**: the advantage of **economic performance** no longer dominates promotions, while **political loyalty** and integrity become more critical. High-performing officials are more susceptible to investigation during the anti-corruption campaign, and local policy agendas trend more closely toward alignment with the central government. The transition is not self-enforcing, and the long-term equilibrium remains to be observed.

## Introduction

**Corruption as a Tool of Rule**

Under authoritarian systems, corruption is a complex phenomenon, related both to **institutional deficiencies** and closely connected to the distribution of political power and strategies of rule. Traditional research on corruption often simply attributes it to "weak institutions" or "insufficient oversight," while research in recent years has found that the leaders of authoritarian regimes may, out of **strategic considerations**, choose to deliberately tolerate corruption.

Specifically, corruption has a **dual effect** in authoritarian systems:

- **Positive effect** (instrumental value): Corruption can provide additional **economic rents** to the bureaucratic system. Against a backdrop of scarce economic resources, these rents can be used to sustain the stability of the ruling coalition. For example, by allowing officials to share in the proceeds of corruption, leaders can incentivize them to promote local economic development while maintaining their support for the regime.
- **Negative effect** (governance risk): The spread of corruption triggers a series of problems, including **social dissatisfaction**, **inequitable resource distribution**, and a **crisis of ruling legitimacy**. Once corruption spirals out of control, public trust in the regime declines sharply, threatening regime stability.

In fact, there is significant variation in tolerance for corruption among authoritarian regimes. Some regimes permit the existence of institutionalized corruption (Chang and Golden 2010; Montinola and Jackman 2002), while others rigorously crack down on corruption, leading to large-scale political reshuffles within the party, the bureaucracy, and the military (Gillespie and Okruhlik 1991).

The realities above show that the leaders of authoritarian regimes face a core **strategic dilemma**: they need to find a balance point between **economic performance** and **political loyalty**. Taking China's recent anti-corruption campaign as the entry point, this article proposes a **theoretical framework** for the governance of corruption: namely, in order to promote economic development, leaders may choose to adopt a tolerant attitude toward corruption (i.e., the **permissive model**); but when corruption threatens regime stability, leaders may launch a large-scale anti-corruption campaign and turn toward stricter political control and anti-corruption measures (i.e., the **punitive model**).

Based on this theoretical framework, this article raises several core questions, the answers to which will help in understanding the **governance logic** of one-party authoritarian states:

- Why does corruption emerge under a one-party regime and become "tolerated" for a period of time?
- What are the implications of corruption for economic performance and officials' ideological positions?
- Under a one-party system, how does corruption interact with the political selection system, thereby affecting the relationship between leaders and officials?
- When corruption is viewed as a serious threat to regime stability and ideological integrity, why and how do leaders complete the transition from the permissive model to the punitive model through an anti-corruption campaign?

To answer these questions, this article takes as its objects of study China's recent anti-corruption campaign and the changes in the political selection landscape. Through empirical analysis of promotion and investigation data for a large number of leaders of Chinese prefecture-level cities, and with the aid of machine learning methods to analyze changes in the policy topics of local governments' annual work reports, the study finds:

- **Changes in promotion criteria**: Before and after the anti-corruption campaign, the criteria for promoting officials shifted from economic performance to political loyalty.
- **Targets of corruption investigations**: Officials with high economic performance but lacking political connections were more likely to become anti-corruption targets. (Consistent with the prediction of the **permissive model**.)
- **Consistency of policy implementation**: After the anti-corruption campaign, local governments' policy implementation aligned more closely with the central government's policy agenda. (Consistent with the prediction of the **punitive model**.)

#### **Contributions of this article**

- **Supplements the literature on the tradeoff between the economic benefits of corruption and legitimacy**: Corruption is thought to bring benefits to political leaders, including increasing fiscal revenue (Guardado, 2018), manipulating elections (Klašnja et al., 2018), and sustaining popular support (Manzetti and Wilson, 2007). Some studies argue that corruption has a "greasing effect" that promotes economic activity (Leff, 1964; Li and Wu, 2010; Shi et al., 2021). Others point out that corruption suppresses private investment and harms economic growth (Chen and Kung, 2019; Earle and Gehlbach, 2015; Wang, 2021; Zhu and Shi, 2019). This article deepens this discussion, focusing on the tradeoff between the **economic benefits** brought by corruption and the **costs** it imposes on regime legitimacy, and analyzing the impact of this tradeoff on **political selection**. This perspective is consistent with recent theoretical research on the governance of corruption (Hollyer and Wantchekon, 2015; Lu and Lorentzen, 2016), but this study places special emphasis on the role of corruption and anti-corruption in the **political selection mechanism**.
- **Comparison with existing anti-corruption research**: Li et al. (2022a, b) explore the impact of corruption and anti-corruption on regime survival, arguing that a leader's decision to control corruption depends on the magnitude of **crisis risk**. This article, by contrast, emphasizes the **triggering factors of an anti-corruption campaign**: primarily the **decline in public support** and the **erosion of officials' loyalty**, rather than crisis risk alone. This article further studies the **forms of ruling-coalition construction** under the **two governance models** (the permissive model and the punitive model), and proposes that the anti-corruption campaign is a **trigger for the transformation of governance models**.
-  **Extends the literature on party survival and the importance of ideology**: A party's, and especially an authoritarian party's, **reliance on ideology** is crucial to its survival (Bizzarro et al., 2018; Gehlbach and Keefer, 2011). Corruption scandals weaken popular support for politicians (Ferraz and Finan, 2008; Klašnja, 2015), and for a one-party regime, **public trust in the party** is the key to political order (Desposato et al., 2021; Wang, 2019). This article proposes that when corruption threatens **the party's ideology and public trust**, the ruling party switches from the **permissive model** to the **punitive model** through an anti-corruption campaign. This article shows that this process is accompanied by a **large-scale political rectification** to rebuild the party's political discipline and institutional integrity and to maintain regime stability. China's anti-corruption experience resonates to a certain degree with other countries (such as **India** (Vadlamannati, 2015), **Vietnam** (Bai et al., 2019), and **Rwanda** (Jones, 2022)), illustrating the general political role of anti-corruption campaigns in authoritarian systems.
- **Supplements the literature on political selection in authoritarian regimes**: In China, the criteria for political selection have **mixed characteristics**: the degree of emphasis placed on competence versus loyalty differs across levels and periods (Landry et al., 2018; Shih et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2022). This article finds that after the launch of the anti-corruption campaign, the importance of **political loyalty** in the selection of officials increased significantly, manifested concretely in local officials more actively **promoting the party's policy line**. This finding accords with recent research on the political selection of lower-level officials (Gans-Morse et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2022; Yang, 2021), and supports the view that "an **integrity-based regime** and **loyalty criteria** may be complementary."

## The Permissive Model and the Punitive Model

Unlike personal dictatorships or military governments, the ruling coalition of a one-party regime is built around the party organization as its core (Svolik 2012). This means that the appointment and removal and management of local officials (i.e., the **power over personnel**) is a key tool by which the party controls local elites and maintains the stability of the coalition. Local officials bear the dual responsibilities of **promoting economic growth** and **upholding the party line** (loyalty). In actual operation, in order to overcome institutional obstacles, local officials often rely on informal networks and regional preferences to attract investment and develop the economy, thereby giving rise to corrupt behavior. This forms the characteristics of "**crony capitalism**" that combines widespread corruption with high-speed growth.

Whether a leader decides to punish corrupt officials depends on the degree to which corruption damages **regime legitimacy**. When leaders believe that the erosion of belief in the party program by corruption is still mild, and that economic growth is crucial to the regime's survival, they tend to adopt a tolerant attitude toward corruption (the permissive model). Under this model, economic performance is the main criterion for evaluating officials, and corruption is tolerated to a certain extent because it can generate the necessary economic rents and strengthen the ruling coalition. At the same time, although loyalty is important, it is often maintained through personal relationships and the distribution of benefits. Therefore, the core characteristics of the permissive model are an emphasis on performance and leniency toward corruption.

However, the spread of corruption under the permissive model poses a **dual threat** to regime legitimacy:

- **Decline in popular support**: An authoritarian system that lacks institutionalized oversight is often unable to sustain a transparent and efficient governance system (Hollyer et al. 2015). The violations of rights and inequitable distribution of resources brought about by corruption trigger social dissatisfaction (Cai 2008; Lorentzen 2013; Mattingly 2016).
- **Rising centrifugal forces within the ruling coalition**: In an environment of "tolerating corruption," local officials rely on their personal abilities to extract more rents, and may develop a "sense of ownership" ideologically, thereby deviating from the party line.

When such threats are sufficient to seriously damage ideological integrity, leaders may choose to turn toward a stricter punitive model. This shift takes the **anti-corruption campaign** as its occasion: in the punitive model, the role of economic performance is weakened, and loyalty and integrity become the key considerations of political selection.

A leader's decision can be expressed through a diagram of a **strategic trilemma** (⬆️ Figure1), in which the allocation of weights among three main factors—**economic performance**, **political loyalty**, and **governance integrity**—determines the choice of governance model.

- Under the **permissive model**, leaders are more inclined to emphasize economic growth and political loyalty, which helps local officials obtain incentives through corruption and thereby promote local economic development.
- Under the **punitive model**, leaders place more emphasis on **political loyalty** and **governance integrity**, in order to strengthen central control over local governments and rebuild the regime's legitimacy.

According to this model, the **permissive model** and the **punitive model** are switched according to the relative emphasis the leader places on economic growth versus political stability, while the launch of an anti-corruption campaign is usually the **inevitable choice** after **institutional loopholes** have accumulated to a certain degree. Once corruption poses a direct threat to regime stability, the leader will turn toward the punitive model, further strengthening political loyalty and governance integrity.

This transformation process has empirical significance. When the leader, through an anti-corruption campaign, clears out corrupt officials who previously benefited under the "permissive model," while at the same time weakening the role of economic performance in promotions, the governance transition from a "tolerate corruption" model to a "severely punish corruption" model is achieved. The leader weighs the political and social risks triggered by corruption against the economic costs of anti-corruption and the difficulties of institution-building, and ultimately chooses to use campaign-style anti-corruption to rapidly reshape the loyalty and integrity of the ruling coalition. This kind of large-scale anti-corruption action is **not an isolated case** in history; the governing experiences of leaders such as Park Chung-hee, Chun Doo-hwan, Chiang Ching-kuo, Paul Kagame, and Muhammadu Buhari also embody a similar logic.

## China's Permissive Period and Punitive Period

#### The Permissive Period (1990-2012)

The period before 2002 was characterized by the **rapid expansion of the private economy** and **economic liberalization**, and the party's governing thought was influenced by a pragmatic ideology, typically expressed in Deng Xiaoping's "white cat, black cat" dictum: "It doesn't matter whether a cat is white or black; as long as it catches mice, it is a good cat." (Tsai 2006). In this political atmosphere, corruption accelerated and spread at every level, from infrastructure construction to development projects. High-profile corruption cases represented by the Xiamen smuggling case fully illustrated the prevalence of government-business collusion (Shieh 2005).

The subsequent period (from 2002) basically continued the predecessor's governance approach of emphasizing economic growth. Although several Politburo members were also investigated for corruption during their second terms in this period, on the whole personnel selection remained oriented toward **economic performance**—political selection was influenced both by factional patronage politics and by officials' economic performance (Jiang 2018). However, owing to the fragmentation of power within the party, power became dispersed at the upper levels in the latter part of this period (Shih and Lee 2020).

During the permissive period, although economic inequality intensified, the economic interests of the elite group and the public still largely overlapped. At the policy level, considerable weight was given to economic growth, while the "Three Represents" thought (representing advanced productive forces, advanced culture, and the interests of the broad masses of the people) provided an ideological basis for the party (Tsai 2006). In such an atmosphere, although corruption violated the law and the party program, actual punishment was not severe. Some senior officials involved in local economic development gradually formed a "**sense of entitlement**" to their own political rents, which strengthened the structural presence of corruption.

However, the corruption-tolerating model gave rise to two major threats:

- **Social dissatisfaction and the weakening of legitimacy**: Human-rights violations, workplace disasters, and environmental deterioration triggered by corruption accumulated into serious social grievances (Mattingly 2016; O'brien and Li 2006). Owing to limited administrative capacity, the central government rarely directly intervened in such social unrest (Cai 2008; Lorentzen 2013). As a result, popular support for the regime declined, threatening the foundations of its legitimacy.
- **The hidden danger of internal political fragmentation**: As "tolerating corruption" incentivized some high-performing local officials to accumulate power and resources, they might deviate ideologically. High-performing local officials might, after accumulating sufficient economic resources, develop "confidence in their political achievements," form individualism or localism, and move away from the party line, thereby harming the cohesion of the ruling coalition and the party's ideological integrity (Chen and Hong 2021).

Taken together, the "permissive model" of this period, while leading with economic growth, adopted a relatively lenient attitude toward corruption, but its negative effects gradually accumulated, laying the groundwork for the later turn toward a stricter governance model.

#### **The Punitive Period (2012-present)**

The anti-corruption campaign of this period is an important step toward the punitive model. During the period from the 18th to the 20th Party Congress, the anti-corruption campaign investigated 553 provincial- and ministerial-level officials, more than 25,000 prefecture- and city-level officials, and 182,000 county-level departmental officials. In particular, in 2015, the number of high-ranking officials investigated for corruption reached a historical peak (⬇️ Figure2).

<img src="https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241217202357762.png" alt="image-20241217202357762" style="zoom:67%;" />

Alongside the large-scale investigations, he emphasized "staying true to the original aspiration and keeping the mission firmly in mind" in order to strengthen the governing image of serving the people. To rationalize this campaign, he no longer confined himself to the single-dimensional goal of economic growth, but advocated inclusive development and "giving the people a sense of gain" as the criteria for evaluating the success or failure of reform. Correspondingly, the central government invested substantial resources in policy areas such as **poverty alleviation**, **environmental protection**, **income redistribution**, **antitrust** actions, and **common prosperity**. These policies responded to a certain degree to public concerns about corruption and social inequality (⬇️ Figure3, Baidu Index data: the topics of corruption and inequality reached historical peaks around the time he took office).

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241217202521803.png)

Some scholars argue that the anti-corruption campaign strengthened his political authority (Gore 2016), but at a deeper level, this move was intended to pave the way for establishing a political order with a stronger ideological appeal. In his discourse, the goal of this campaign was, through the party's self-revolution, to "escape the historical cycle of rise and fall" and to achieve the legitimacy of the party's long-term rule (Zhao 2016). Against a backdrop of intensifying inequality and declining social cohesion, the legitimacy challenges facing the party became increasingly prominent. Xi Jinping's agenda was aimed at addressing these problems, and because the regime had been relatively lenient toward corruption before this, the corruption problems of a large number of officials were exposed in the spotlight, making a large-scale, severe anti-corruption purge unavoidable.

## Formulation of the Hypotheses

This article proposes three hypotheses that, from the dimensions of **economic performance**, **political loyalty**, and **changes in policy topics**, make predictions about the relationships among the appointment and investigation of officials and their policy orientations.

#### **Hypothesis 1**

**"Before the anti-corruption campaign, high-performing (high-capability) officials were more likely to be promoted; after the anti-corruption campaign, this positive association weakened or even disappeared. At the same time, regardless of whether before or after the anti-corruption campaign, loyalty was always an important factor in promotion."**

In the paper's theoretical model, under the "permissive model" the leader places a high value on economic performance, because at this time economic growth is regarded as the key to sustaining the ruling coalition and obtaining economic rents. Corruption is tolerated under this model because it provides the ruling elite with the necessary political and economic resources. Thus, before the anti-corruption campaign is launched (i.e., during the permissive-model period), high-performing officials are more favored because they can deliver better economic performance, and are therefore more likely to be promoted. However, political loyalty (such as an official's political connection with superior leaders) also has a certain influence during this period, because appointments and removals of party personnel are not entirely determined by economic indicators, and loyalty remains a necessary condition for sustaining the ruling coalition.

Once the leader decides to shift from the "permissive model" to the "punitive model" and to clear out corrupt officials through a large-scale anti-corruption campaign, the political environment will change. At this time, the importance of economic performance declines, replaced by higher requirements for political loyalty and integrity. Under the "punitive model," promotion is no longer mainly driven by performance, and high-performing officials do not necessarily continue to enjoy a promotion advantage. However, loyalty remains important, and even becomes relatively more prominent, because the leader hopes to rebuild the ruling coalition and ideological legitimacy by selecting officials loyal to the party line.

#### **Hypothesis 2**

**"When corruption opportunities are proportional to economic performance, high-performing officials are more easily investigated during the anti-corruption campaign."**

Under the "permissive model," high-performing officials not only bring economic growth but may also exploit their advantages in the economic sphere to obtain more corruption rents. This means that high performance often coexists with a high opportunity for corruption. When the leader decides to launch an anti-corruption campaign and transition to the "punitive model," past tolerated corrupt behavior will be investigated. Because high-performing officials have more opportunity to accumulate resources through corrupt means, they are more likely to become key targets of investigation in the anti-corruption campaign.

In addition, under this logic, it is also necessary to consider the moderating effect of political connections (loyalty) on the probability of being investigated. If a high-performing official simultaneously possesses solid political connections and shows clear loyalty to superior leaders, this loyalty may to a certain extent provide protection and reduce the probability of being investigated.

#### **Hypothesis 3**

**"After the anti-corruption campaign, local executives display greater consistency with central leaders in policymaking."**

When the regime shifts from the "permissive model" to the "punitive model," not only do the rules of promotion and investigation change, but officials' orientations on policy topics adjust accordingly. The permissive model emphasizes economic performance, and policy priorities related to the "old agenda"—such as economic growth, industrial development, and urbanization—appear more frequently in local government work reports.

After entering the punitive model, the regime places greater emphasis on political loyalty and ideological purity. To demonstrate loyalty and cater to the will of central leaders, local officials align their policy topics more closely with the central line, highlighting the "new agenda"—such as poverty alleviation, environmental protection, political loyalty, and party discipline and the rule of law—and downplaying the emphasis on traditional economic growth and industrial-zone development. By changing their policy discourse, local officials send a signal of staying consistent with the political signals of superior leaders, thereby achieving political self-protection and adaptation to the punitive model.

## Data and Methods

#### **Data**

The data sources include the anti-corruption investigation reports published by the Central Commission for Discipline Inspection (CCDI), as well as the Chinese Official Dataset (COD) compiled by the China Center for Economic Research (CCER) (Yao et al. 2020). The objects of study are the mayors and municipal party secretaries serving in major prefecture-level cities from 2013 to 2021 (hereinafter referred to as "city-level leaders"), a total of 1,708 officials, of whom 151 (about 8.84%) were investigated for corruption problems during this period.

- **Anti-corruption investigation data**: Official reports of anti-corruption investigations were obtained from the official website of the CCDI. These reports contain the personal information of the investigated officials and the timing of the investigation. By matching this information with the COD data, it is possible to identify which city-level leaders were investigated for corruption between 2013 and 2021 (Yao et al. 2020).
- **Official and economic performance data**: The COD data covers the career and personal information of mayors and municipal party secretaries of prefecture-level cities from 1994 to 2021, such as age, gender, ethnicity, and educational background, as well as the GDP growth rate of the city in the corresponding year (Yao and Zhang 2015). Because city-level leaders may rotate through multiple locations during their tenure, this provides the conditions for using a fixed-effects model to separate an official's individual contribution to economic performance (capability) from city-specific effects.

This study mainly focuses on mayors and municipal party secretaries, rather than provincial- and ministerial-level or higher-level officials, and does not include deputy mayors or other heads of city-level party and government departments. The reasons are:

- City-level leaders are a key reserve group for the selection of provincial-level leaders, and their appointment, removal, and investigation have a profound impact on the form of the future ruling coalition.
- The economic performance of deputy or lower-level officials is difficult to measure clearly, while the model and hypotheses proposed in this article need to be based on clearly measurable performance indicators.

#### **Core Variables**

**Official Capability (Capability)**:
To measure an official's long-term contribution to economic performance, this article adopts the method of Yao and Zhang (2015), exploiting the feature of officials serving across cities to decompose the GDP growth rate, through a **fixed-effects model**, into an official's individual effect, a city effect, and a year effect, thereby obtaining the official's individual effect $δ_i$ as the metric for capability:
$$
y_{i(jt)} = \pmb{X}_{i(jt)} + \delta_i + \psi_j + \gamma_t + \epsilon_{i(jt)}
$$
In the equation above, $y_{i(jt)}$ is the GDP growth rate of official $i$ in city $j$ in year $t$, $\pmb{X}_{i(jt)}$ are control variables, $\delta_i$ is the official's individual effect (the **capability indicator**), $\psi_j$ is the city fixed effect, $\gamma_t$ is the year fixed effect, and $\epsilon_{i(jt)}$ is the error term.

For officials investigated during the anti-corruption campaign, the capability value is estimated based on the economic performance during their tenure.

**Political Connection (Connection)**

The literature points out that an official's personal relationship with superior leaders can measure their political loyalty (Shih et al. 2012; Jia et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2022). This article defines political connection in terms of the **shared work experience** between city-level leaders and provincial party secretaries: when a city-level leader and the incumbent provincial party secretary had a superior-subordinate working relationship in a previous provincial or municipal government (and the official's rank at the time was no lower than two levels below the secretary), this is regarded as the existence of a political connection. In addition to the binary connection indicator, this article also constructs a continuous indicator of connection strength based on the **number of years worked together**. The personal-characteristic control variables include educational background, ethnicity, gender, and age together with its squared term.

#### **Empirical Strategy**

To test H1 and H2, this article uses a Probit model to analyze the probabilities of promotion and investigation. To test H3, it uses the Structural Topic Model (STM) to analyze textual data.

**Promotion Model (Test of H1)**

Hypothesis 1 predicts that economic performance is significantly positively correlated with promotion before the anti-corruption campaign, while this association weakens after the campaign. This article takes the promotion event (1 for promotion, 0 for not) as the dependent variable and uses a **Probit model**:
$$
Promotion_{ijt} = \Phi[\alpha + \beta \cdot {Capability}_i + \gamma \cdot {Connection}i +  \pmb{X}_{ij}b + u_j + T]
$$
where $Promotion_{ijt}$ is the promotion-event indicator for official $i$ in city $j$ in year $t$, $Capability_i$ is capability, $Connection_i$ is political connection, $\pmb{X}_{it}$ are personal-characteristic control variables, $u_j$ and $T$ are city and year fixed effects respectively, and $\Phi$ is the standard normal cumulative distribution function. Comparing the estimation results for the two periods, **before the anti-corruption campaign** (before 2013) and **after the anti-corruption campaign** (after 2013), allows H1 to be tested.

**Investigation Model (Test of H2)**

H2 holds that high-performing officials are more easily investigated during the anti-corruption campaign, especially when they lack political connections. To this end, this article takes the investigation event (1 for being investigated, 0 for not) as the dependent variable and builds a Probit model:
$$
\text{Pr}(Investigation_{ij}) = \Phi[a + b \cdot {Capability}_i +  c \cdot {Connection}_i + d \cdot ({Capability}_i \times {Connection}_i) + \pmb{X}_ib + + \phi_i + u_j + T_j]
$$
where $investigation_{ij}$ is the investigation event, $Capability_i$ and $Connection_i$ are defined as above, $Capability_i \times Connection_i$ is the interaction term of the two, used to test the moderating effect of political connections on the probability that high-performing officials are investigated, $X_i$ are control variables, $\phi_i$ is the set of dummy variables for whether the official served as a city-level leader in each year from 2013 to 2021, $u_j$ is the set of dummy variables indicating the region of the official's last post, and $T_j$ is the set of dummy variables indicating the year inspected by the CCDI's inspection team. Through this model, one can analyze the investigation risk faced by high-performing officials during the anti-corruption campaign and the buffering effect of political connections (here, $u_j$ and $T$ are in fact just city fixed effects and year fixed effects, right?).

**Policy Topic Analysis (Test of H3)**

To test H3 (that after the anti-corruption campaign, local policy topics trend more toward the ideological direction required by the center), this article uses the **Structural Topic Model (STM)**, an advanced text-analysis method, to analyze local governments' work reports. It collected over 1,200 prefecture-level city government work reports from 2013 to 2018 and conducted text topic analysis. Among 50 topics, it identified topics highly related to the centrally advocated agenda (such as **poverty alleviation**, **environmental protection**, **political loyalty**, and **party discipline and state law**) as well as traditional economic topics (such as industrial-zone development, macroeconomic policy, urbanization, and economic growth), comparing the changes in the proportions of these topics before and after the anti-corruption campaign, thereby testing H3.

To ensure the robustness of the results, this article conducted multiple tests, including replacing the capability indicator, excluding specific official samples (such as officials connected to purged provincial leaders), and segmenting the analysis according to the tenure of central leaders.


## Results

Through analysis of data on mayors and municipal party secretaries of Chinese prefecture-level cities from 1994 to 2021, as well as text analysis of anti-corruption investigation reports and local government work reports from 2013 to 2021, this article tests the three proposed hypotheses (H1, H2, H3), and provides multidimensional empirical support for the governance logic of the transition from "tolerating corruption" (the permissive model) to "severely punishing corruption" (the punitive model).

- **Changes in the promotion pattern**: H1 proposes that before the anti-corruption campaign, economic performance (an official's capability) significantly influenced the official's promotion—the higher the official's capability, the greater the probability of promotion; while after the anti-corruption campaign, this positive association weakens, and political loyalty becomes a more critical promotion factor. The analysis of the 1994-2021 data shows that **before the launch of the anti-corruption campaign in 2013**, an official's capability was significantly positively correlated with promotion (officials with high economic performance had a promotion advantage). After **the start of the anti-corruption campaign**, high-performing officials no longer significantly enjoyed a promotion advantage. At the same time, regardless of whether before or after the anti-corruption campaign, officials with a colleague relationship with provincial leaders (reflecting **political loyalty**) always held an advantage in promotion. This shows that after the anti-corruption campaign the weight of economic performance declined, and loyalty became more prominent in political selection. ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241217213544586.png)

- **Changes in the pattern of corruption investigations**: H2 expects that when corruption opportunities are positively correlated with economic performance, high-performing officials are more likely to become targets of investigation during the anti-corruption campaign, especially those lacking the protection of political connections. The empirical results show that high-performing officials (who had more corruption opportunities under the permissive model) were more likely to be investigated during the anti-corruption campaign. More importantly, political connections have a **moderating effect** on investigation risk: if a high-performing official had a close relationship with superior leaders, this relationship provided a certain degree of protection, thereby reducing the probability of being investigated; without such a relationship, high-performing officials were more easily purged. ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241217213645758.png)

  ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241217213710440.png)

- **Shift in policy topics**: H3 proposes that after the anti-corruption campaign, local governments embody greater **consistency** with the central leaders' agenda in policymaking, with the topics they focus on shifting from the traditional "old agenda" of economic growth and industrial-zone development to the "new agenda" of poverty alleviation, environmental protection, political loyalty, and party discipline and state law. Using the text data of more than 1,200 prefecture-level city government work reports from 2013 to 2018, and employing the Structural Topic Model (STM) for analysis, the results show that after the anti-corruption campaign, the proportion of emphasis in local reports on "**new agenda**" topics (such as poverty alleviation, environmental governance, demonstrations of loyalty, and party discipline and the rule of law) rose significantly, while attention to "**old agenda**" topics such as traditional economic growth, macroeconomic policy, industrial-zone development, and urbanization declined significantly. The test of H3 shows that the shift in governance model is reflected not only in the rules for promoting and investigating officials, but also in **policy discourse** and **agendas**. By reshaping their policy priorities, local governments proactively stay aligned with the ideology and policy priorities of central leaders, thereby reflecting the stricter political and ideological control under the punitive model. ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241217213735965.png)![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241217213800696.png)

## Conclusion and Discussion

- **Contribution**: The comparative politics literature generally holds that the stability of authoritarian regimes derives in part from rent-sharing and power distribution carried out through institutionalized means (Gandhi and Przeworski 2007; Wright et al. 2015). However, existing research has not yet adequately explained the drivers of institutional change within one-party states. Taking China's anti-corruption campaign as an example, this article shows how political leaders slow institutional erosion by changing their governance model. After the governance model shifts from the corruption-permitting "permissive model" to the "punitive model" characterized by severe anti-corruption, political loyalty and integrity replace economic performance as the priority considerations of the ruling party.
- **Empirical evidence support**:
  - **Changes in the patterns of official investigation and promotion**: Officials who performed relatively well (high performers) during the anti-corruption campaign were more easily subjected to corruption investigations, and economic performance no longer significantly influenced promotion at this time; conversely, the political connections established with superior leaders remained an important factor determining promotion.
  - **Reconstruction of policy topics**: A structural topic analysis (STM) of local governments' annual work reports finds that after the anti-corruption campaign local officials tended more to stay aligned with the central leaders' policy agenda.

- **The limits of institutional commitment and transformation**: This article's research shows that one-party states have limitations of institutional commitment in changing their governance model. This commitment problem stems from the tension between sustaining popular support and consolidating the ruling coalition. When the leader tries to turn the situation around by punishing corruption, although this can clear out some corrupt officials and promote the transformation of the governance model, it inevitably has to pay the price of reducing the attention paid to economic performance.
- **Doubts about the sustainability of the punitive model**:
  - **The absence of self-enforcement of the model**: If the transformation relies on campaign-style strategic maneuvers, this new model is difficult to sustain on its own, and there is a potential risk of a swing back or distortion.
  - **The problem of single-minded reliance on loyalty and integrity**: A one-sided emphasis on loyalty will trigger pointless competition within the bureaucratic system, thereby harming the cohesion of the ruling coalition. Ultimately, the regime may have to reintroduce performance considerations in order to strike a balance between loyalty and capability.
