## I

Have you ever heard the sound of rain falling on a tin roof?

Autumn in the south arrives late and entirely unapologetic. By the end of September the air still holds the steaming damp of late summer, like a letter written long ago but never sent — the paper yellowed, the handwriting stubbornly refusing to fade.

I am in the habit of going out at dusk, with no destination at all. I walk down the old street outside the university's south gate, lined with plane trees. The streetlamps are not yet lit; the sky is a gray-blue that has no name. Leaves spiral down from somewhere very high, brushing past the unlit lamp covers, and finally press themselves against the rain-soaked asphalt like postmarks no one had time to read. When I step on them, they crumble. Crumble without a sound.

Later I thought: those leaves were probably not waiting for anyone to read them. They simply happened to fall there, and I simply happened to walk by. Most encounters in this world amount to no more than that.

## II

You always used to say you wanted to live in a small town with sloping streets.

Not too lively — but in the early morning there should be an old flower seller pushing a wooden cart past your door, piled with droopy daisies and white roses still wet with dew. You said you would get up early every day, walk barefoot across the wooden floor to the window, hair a mess, squinting, and shout down, "Save me two bunches of the white ones." The old man would look up and smile with his missing front tooth, then set the two prettiest bunches on your doorstep. When you went down to fetch them, the stone steps would be cold; the soles of your feet would jolt, and you would be fully awake.

You said the little town should be near the sea — but the sea should not be visible. You should have to cross a low hill to reach the beach. You said that was what gave it a sense of ceremony: every time you saw the sea, it would be like the first time. When a wave rushed up and soaked the hem of your skirt, you would pretend to be annoyed and step back, then fail to resist taking two steps forward again. You said you were greedy; you wanted to be close to everything.

Back then we sat by the window on the second floor of the dining hall, you poking at the long-cold shredded potatoes on your plate with your chopsticks, your eyes bright as if you carried a whole sky of stars. I listened to you sketch that little town — the flagstone streets and the secondhand bookshop on the corner, the café with a tabby cat at its door, the way the whole street turned amber at dusk. I couldn't get a word in. I just kept nodding.

Then you suddenly stopped, tilted your head, and asked me: "What about you? Where do you want to live?"

I thought for a long time and said: "The place you described sounds pretty good."

You laughed and said I had no ambition. But when you laughed, the tips of your ears were red.

The dining-hall light that day was a warm yellow, falling on your face like a thin layer of honey. I memorized that image, the way you memorize a melody you have heard only once and can never forget. On countless sleepless nights afterward, whenever I closed my eyes, there it was — you with your head tilted, the tips of your ears red, the shredded potatoes on your plate gone cold.

## III

When September walked to its end, the osmanthus bloomed all at once.

That sweetness was a muffled thing. It seeped, without announcing itself, into the fibers of your clothes, into strands of hair, into the open pages of books. Outside the south-facing window on the third floor of the library stood an extremely old osmanthus tree, its branches reaching in, almost touching the books stacked on the sill. I always sat in that seat, not really reading, just watching those tiny golden flowers fall from the branches bit by bit, landing on the open page.

Once, you came around from behind the shelves and found me staring blankly at an open copy of *One Hundred Years of Solitude*, seven or eight osmanthus blossoms scattered on the page. You said nothing — only reached out and, with great care, picked the flowers up one by one and put them into the little tin box you always carried. The box had originally held mints; on its lid was the blurred picture of a windmill.

"For bookmarks," you said softly.

I never asked which book you meant to press them into. Some questions, left unasked, allow the answer to remain forever the one you wanted.

The day deep autumn truly arrived, there was no warning. It was just that, opening the window in the morning, the air had turned suddenly dry and bracing, like biting into an apple not yet ripe — the sourness spreading from the tip of the tongue all the way to the chest. Overnight, most of the plane-tree leaves had gone yellow. When the wind blew, they rattled and roared, like countless hands applauding, or countless people whispering.

I pulled my coat tight and stood on the balcony, looking at the sky above the opposite roof. Autumn clouds are high and thin, as if someone had gone over them with a scraper, thin enough to show the deep blue underneath. Beautiful, yes — but look too long and you feel lonely. Things that are too clean always make a person lonely: a lake frozen over in winter, a wall freshly painted white, the letters you wrote me — handwriting neat and orderly, even the punctuation meticulous.

Tidiness that deliberate is usually hiding something.

## IV

Later, you really did move to a city by the sea — though not the small town with sloping streets you had described.

You sent photos: a wide road lined with coconut palms, the sky blue to the point of looking unreal. You said winter there wasn't very cold; a light jacket was enough. You said the apartment you rented had a big balcony where you could hang a lot of laundry. You said there was a rice-noodle-roll shop downstairs, and the owner's wife always added an extra egg for you. You said many, many ordinary, trivial, mild things, like someone carefully proving to someone else: look, I'm doing fine.

I looked at those photos, at that sun-bleached road and those listless coconut palms, and a very strange feeling rose in me. Not sadness, not envy, not reluctance. Something more hidden, more deeply recessed — like standing in a room that ought to be familiar and suddenly noticing that the picture on one wall has been replaced. You can't say what exactly is wrong. You simply know that something is no longer the same.

I replied: That's wonderful.

Then I turned off my phone and went out to the balcony to bring in the dried laundry.

## V

Before winter came, I bought a pot of asparagus fern at the flower shop by the school's back gate.

The shopkeeper said it was easy to keep: indoors, no direct sun, water it every few days. I put it in the corner of my desk and looked at it for a while every day. Its leaves are fine as mist, green as a rumor about spring. Sometimes I think plants may be the most honest creatures in the world. Treat one well and it grows beautiful; forget it and it slowly withers. It doesn't get angry, doesn't demand answers, doesn't disappear one day without warning and leave you struggling through every possible explanation. It simply, quietly, responds to exactly what you give it — no more, no less.

In November, at a flea market, I saw a tin box. Not yours — but very like it. The picture on the lid was a lighthouse, rusted by sea wind down to a blurred outline. I picked it up and opened it. Inside it was empty, with the faint remnant of some unnameable sweetness — maybe candy, maybe time.

I didn't buy it. I just stood there holding that empty box, stood there for a long while.

The stall keeper thought I was hesitating over the price and said: "Five kuai. Take it."

I shook my head, put the box back, and turned and walked into the November wind. Behind me the stall keeper muttered something, which the wind tore to pieces — not one word of it reached me.

## VI

Deep autumn is a slow farewell.

It doesn't end with the spectacular finality of summer, where the downpours and the cicadas stop on the same beat. It is one leaf falling after another, one degree dropping after another, the daylight shortened one day at a time — patient to the point of cruelty. You know perfectly well winter is coming, yet every day looks no different from the day before. Until one morning you step outside and find the ginkgo leaves have carpeted the entire road, rustling underfoot, and only then does it occur to you, far too late: so autumn has already gone this far.

It is the same with the way some people leave your life — not slamming the door, but retreating bit by bit into a blurred background. First the replies come slower; then you stop liking each other's posts; then the shared topics thin out; then even finding their name requires scrolling back through a very long chat history. Each step is trivial. Put together, they are the full distance of an autumn.

The last message I received from you was in the first week of December. You said it was raining there — that wet southern cold, different from when we were at school. You said you suddenly missed the hot-and-sour noodles from the shop at the back gate, the kind with far too much vinegar. You said—

And then there was no more.

That message stopped right after "you said," like a piece of music breaking off at its sweetest bar, like a heavy snow falling thick and soundless all night, so that when you open the door in the morning the whole world is white, clean enough to make you want to cry.

I never asked what you had meant to say.

Some sentences, left unfinished, are already at their best ending.

The asparagus fern in the corner of the desk has grown taller again. The new shoots reach out like extended fingers, toward the window. There is no osmanthus tree outside the window, only a small patch of gray-white sky. But those shoots keep growing stubbornly toward the light, as if they know something I don't.

I watered it, then sat down and opened a new notebook.

On the first page, I wrote one line:

**Some people are your deep autumn. You walk a long way before you dare to look back and give them a name — and by then the season has passed, and the only name left to give is "faraway."**
