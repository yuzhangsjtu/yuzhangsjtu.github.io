> **State capacity** is crucial for development and growth, but there is a lack of evaluation of whether reforms aimed at enhancing state capacity are effective.
>
> By analyzing the implementation of the Pendleton Act of 1883 in the U.S. postal system, this paper explores the impact of a civil service reform that **separated politics from administration** on the quality of public services. The study finds that the reform significantly reduced postal delivery errors, increased productivity, and improved service quality by stabilizing civil servants' careers and reducing political interference. In addition, the reform weakened the influence of local politics and promoted the development of independent newspapers. The study provides empirical evidence demonstrating the effectiveness of civil service reform in strengthening state capacity and improving the quality of public services.

## Introduction

**State Capacity**

State capacity refers to a state's ability to implement policies, maintain order, provide public services, and so on; it is a key factor in economic development and growth. Efficient state capacity can drive social progress and economic prosperity.

Generally speaking, state capacity is considered to include **administrative capacity**, **fiscal capacity**, **infrastructure capacity**, military capacity, and so on. The fragility of state capacity explains state failure to a large extent. **Fukuyama** even published a book in 2014, ***State-Building**: Governance and World Order in the 21st Century*, exploring the building of state capacity.

**Civil Service Reform**

In many countries, civil service reform is regarded as a core measure for improving state capacity. These reforms aim to build a professional, **non-partisan**, and efficient civil service in order to improve the quality of public services. The idea behind these reforms can be traced back to scholars such as Weber (1922), who argued that establishing a non-partisan, professionalized bureaucracy is a necessary condition for efficient **state governance**.

**The American Context**

Before the Pendleton Act of 1883, the appointment of officials in the U.S. federal government relied mainly on the so-called "**spoils system**," under which appointments depended on political connections rather than professional competence. This system led to corruption and inefficiency.

The Pendleton Act, passed in 1883, marked the beginning of civil service reform in the United States. The act aimed to reduce political interference in civil service appointments, establish a rule-based recruitment system, and protect civil servants from political interference.

Taking the implementation of the Pendleton Act as its point of entry, this paper evaluates the impact of civil service reform on administrative capacity.

## The Pendleton Act

#### **The Gilded Age**

The Gilded Age, i.e., the **Gilded Age** in the paper's title, refers to a period in American history roughly from the 1870s to the early 20th century. This period was known for rapid economic growth, accelerating industrialization, intensifying social stratification, and **political corruption**.

The term "Gilded Age" was coined by historians in the 1920s after the novel of the same name published by Mark Twain in 1873 (*The Gilded Age: A Tale of Today*). The novel satirized the greed and political corruption of post-Civil War America.

Corruption such as government-business collusion, bribery, and electoral fraud was extremely widespread in this period. Political machines such as Tammany Hall controlled a great deal of political power in places like New York City. In response to corruption and social injustice, various reform movements arose, such as the Progressive Movement, which promoted political, economic, and social reforms, including antitrust laws, labor laws, and electoral reform.

#### **The Spoils System**

The spoils system was a widespread practice in 19th-century American politics, referring to the system whereby the winning party distributed government positions and other benefits to its supporters, friends, and party members. This system originated from the concept of "**spoils**," that is, the victor distributes the spoils. In the United States, this system reached its peak during the presidency of Andrew Jackson (1829-1837) and was gradually abolished after the passage of the Pendleton Act in 1883.

Its characteristics are as follows:

- **Political connections first**: Appointments to government positions were based mainly on loyalty and contributions to the ruling party, rather than on candidates' professional competence or qualifications;
- **Rewarding supporters**: The winning party would hand out government positions as rewards to its supporters, consolidating its power base;
- **Frequent turnover of officials**: After each election, the new ruling party's coming to power would lead to large-scale turnover of officials, with most of the new officials being supporters of the new party;
- **Political machines**: Political organizations in some localities, such as Tammany Hall in New York City, controlled voters and maintained their political power by distributing positions;
- **Bribery and corruption**: To obtain or keep their positions, officials often had to pay bribes to the party or political machine, resulting in serious government corruption.

The spoils system led to a series of **consequences**: because appointments were based on political connections rather than professional competence, many government departments lacked effective management and expertise; officials' tenures were unstable, lacking long-term planning and policy continuity; officials had to donate to political parties to keep their positions, making the channeling of benefits and corruption pervasive in the policymaking process; government decisions and resource allocation were based more on political interests than on the public interest, harming social fairness and public welfare; government corruption and inefficiency aroused public discontent and increased society's distrust of the political system.

#### **Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act**

The Pendleton Act is a U.S. federal law, passed by the 47th United States Congress and signed into law by President Chester A. Arthur on January 16, 1883. The act is named after its chief proponent, George Hunt Pendleton, then a senator from Ohio.

The act's main measures are as follows:

- **Selection by examination**: The act stipulated that candidates for government positions should be selected through open **competitive examinations**, rather than appointed through political connections;
- **Preventing political interference**: The act protected incumbent civil servants from being dismissed **for political reasons**, reducing the impact of political turnover on the civil service;
- **Prohibiting political contributions**: Civil servants were prohibited from donating to political parties to keep their positions, thereby reducing political corruption;

In the initial phase, the act applied to only a portion of federal government positions, especially key departments such as the **postal system** and customs offices. By the late 19th and early 20th centuries, most federal government positions had been brought into the civil service system.

## Data and Methods

#### **Main Dependent Variable - Postal Delivery**

The postal system has played an important role in nation-building in many countries. The U.S. postal system, created by the Postal Act of 1792, is considered one of the key institutional drivers of national growth and social progress. In its early days, the postal system facilitated communication and the spread of information across regions, helping to cultivate an **informed citizenry**.

The expansion of postal services not only facilitated long-distance economic transactions but also allowed merchants to sell through mail orders and catalog advertising. The postal system also provided banking services, helping investors find opportunities. Business-related mail accounted for a large share of 19th-century correspondence, and some have argued that America's commitment to postal services formed the foundation of nationwide growth.

By the end of the 19th century, the coverage of the U.S. postal system already extended across the entire country. The locations of all post offices opened between 1860 and 1905 illustrate this, highlighting the ubiquity of the postal system as a form of American state capacity. This extensive coverage underscores the importance of the postal system in the 19th century; **some views hold that the postal system was in effect the core of the central government at the time**.

The postal system back then was probably the equivalent of the modern internet; on this basis, the authors argue that the capacity and efficiency of the postal system are an important indicator of state capacity.

**Data sources**: The main data come from administrative data compiled by the U.S. postal system in the 19th century. Specifically, these include The Annual Report of the General Superintendent of the Railway Mail Service and The First Assistant Postmaster General.

**Specific indicator**: Postal delivery errors. These data come from annual statistics recording errors in the distribution and forwarding of mail (*Statement of errors in the distribution and forwarding of mail*). When distributing intercity mail, clerks of the Railway Mail Service (RMS) would check all items in mail pouches to detect misdirected mail. These errors were recorded and submitted to supervising postal employees to track employee performance. The historical statistical reports provide two city-year level indicators of delivery errors: **the number of slips containing errors** and **the total number of errors on returned slips**. The study focuses mainly on the latter, so as to comprehensively reflect a post office's errors.

#### **Main Explanatory Variable - Expansion of the Act**

The initial reform covered 23 districts; 30 more districts were added between 1884 and 1892, and by 1893 the reform had extended to all post offices offering free delivery service.

#### Identification Strategy

The paper uses a "stacked" event study design, estimating the impact of civil service reform on postal service performance by constructing a Difference-in-Differences model. The specification is as follows:
$$
y_{ijk} = \beta treat_{ij} \times post_k + \theta_{ij} + \tau_{jk} + \gamma\pmb{X}_{ijk} + \epsilon_{ijk}
$$
$\beta$ is the key estimated parameter, capturing the effect of undergoing civil service reform on postal service performance.

## Results and Mechanism Analysis

#### **The Act's Effect on Postal Performance**

- **Reduction in postal delivery errors**: The reform significantly reduced postal delivery errors. The study finds that delivery errors fell by 19-22%, a result indicating a marked improvement in the reliability of postal services. This reduction was consistent across the two major reform waves (1883 and 1893), with no evident pre-reform trends, supporting the external and internal validity of the findings.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240802213259356.png)![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240802213404969.png)
- **Improved cost efficiency**: An analysis of productivity data for the postal free delivery service finds that the act also significantly increased the volume of mail handled per carrier and the cost efficiency per unit of mail.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240802213554712.png)

#### **Mechanisms of Performance Improvement**

- **Reducing the influence of personnel turnover and political cycles**: After the reform, officials' tenures increased significantly and job stability improved. This was because the act's implementation reduced the frequent personnel turnover caused by political cycles.
- **Performance in election years**: This effect was especially pronounced in presidential election years. This indicates that civil service reform improved service quality mainly by weakening political influence over officials.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240802214148622.png)
- Ruling out other explanations: An analysis of postal personnel records rules out the possibility that the act improved performance by increasing the number of employees. The study shows that the reform did not significantly change personnel selection criteria, and the overall quality of employees showed no significant difference before and after the reform. Meanwhile, an analysis of aggregate mail volume data rules out the possibility that the performance improvement was driven by a reduced workload.

#### **Impact on Local Parties**

- By reducing the federal positions available for political support, the reform weakened local parties' control over government positions;
- An analysis of the number and circulation of **partisan newspapers** and **independent newspapers** finds that civil service reform led to a decrease in the number of partisan newspapers and an increase in the circulation of independent newspapers, consistent with the hypothesis that local parties' influence weakened.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240802214401428.png)

## Conclusion

- Civil service reform not only improved public sector performance by separating politics from administration, but also helped weaken the role of local parties.

## Asides

- This paper is very simple, but its significance is quite weighty; as the one-sentence conclusion above states, separating politics from administration is extremely important;
- In the 1980s, China also carried out a degree of reform to separate the party and the government;
- **Woodrow Wilson**'s idea of the **politics-administration dichotomy**:
  - Woodrow Wilson (Thomas Woodrow Wilson) was a president with a doctoral degree and had served as president of Princeton University. In 1887, he published the article The Study of Administration in *Political Science Quarterly*, emphasizing the politics-administration dichotomy;
  - **Politics**: Wilson held that politics concerns the formulation of national policy and is mainly the responsibility of elected officials and legislatures. This part of the work requires weighing public opinion, conflicting interests, and policy choices;
  - **Administration**: Administration, in turn, is the process of executing policy and should be the responsibility of professional, impartial administrators. The duty of administration is to implement and execute the policies already formulated, ensuring that government operates efficiently and fairly.
  - Wilson advocated the professionalization of administration, stressing the establishment of a civil service system based on ability and performance to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of government operations. He also emphasized that administrative personnel should remain politically neutral and should not be subject to the influence of party politics or electoral cycles. The neutrality and stability of administrative agencies are key to ensuring the quality of public services and the credibility of government.
- On **state capacity**, Fukuyama, in his book *Political Order and Political Decay: From the Industrial Revolution to the Globalisation of Democracy*, proposed a ternary "state-rule of law-democracy" framework, arguing that the balance of the three is the path to state success. This view is in fact consistent with Acemoglu's view in *The Narrow Corridor*.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240802220316734.png)
