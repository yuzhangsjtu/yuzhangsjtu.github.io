> **State capacity** is essential for development and growth, yet there is little evaluation of whether reforms meant to enhance state capacity are effective.
>
> This paper examines the impact of civil service reform—specifically the separation of **politics from administration**—on public service quality by analyzing the implementation of the Pendleton Act of 1883 within the U.S. postal system. The study finds that the reform significantly reduced mail delivery errors, raised productivity, and improved service quality by stabilizing civil servants' careers and reducing political interference. Furthermore, the reform weakened the influence of local political machines and spurred the growth of independent newspapers. The research provides empirical evidence that civil service reform is effective in strengthening state capacity and raising the quality of public services.

**Title:** Strengthening State Capacity: Civil Service Reform and Public Sector Performance during the Gilded Age

**Journal:** *American Economic Review*

## Introduction

**State Capacity**

State capacity refers to a state's ability to implement policies, maintain order, provide public services, and so on. It is a crucial factor for economic development and growth. Effective state capacity can drive social progress and economic prosperity.

Generally, state capacity is considered to include **administrative capacity**, **fiscal capacity**, **infrastructure capacity**, military capacity, and others. The fragility of state capacity largely explains the failure of states. **Fukuyama** even published a book in 2014, ***State-Building**: Governance and World Order in the 21st Century*, exploring the construction of state capacity.

**Civil Service Reform**

In many countries, civil service reform is viewed as a core measure for improving state capacity. These reforms aim to build a professional, **non-partisan**, and efficient civil service to raise the quality of public services. The idea behind the reforms can be traced back to scholars such as Weber (1922) and others, who argued that establishing a non-partisan, professionalized bureaucracy is a necessary condition for effective **state governance**.

**The American Context**

Before the Pendleton Act of 1883, appointments in the U.S. federal government mainly relied on the so-called “**spoils system**,” where official positions depended on political connections rather than professional competence. This system led to corruption and inefficiency.

The Pendleton Act, passed in 1883, marked the beginning of civil service reform in the United States. The act aimed to reduce political interference in the appointment of civil servants, establish a rule-based recruitment system, and protect civil servants from political meddling.

This paper uses the implementation of the Pendleton Act as a starting point to evaluate the impact of civil service reform on administrative capacity.

## The Pendleton Act

#### **The Gilded Age**

The Gilded Age, the term in the paper’s title, refers to a period in American history roughly from the 1870s to the early 1900s. This era was known for rapid economic growth, accelerating industrialization, increasing social stratification, and **political corruption**.

The moniker “Gilded Age” was given by historians in the 1920s, based on Mark Twain’s 1873 novel of the same name (*The Gilded Age: A Tale of Today*). The novel satirized the greed and political corruption of post-Civil War America.

Corruption phenomena such as collusion between business and politics, bribery, and election fraud were extremely common during this period. Political machines like Tammany Hall controlled substantial political power in cities such as New York. In response to corruption and social injustice, various reform movements arose, such as the Progressive Movement, which promoted political, economic, and social reforms, including antitrust laws, labor laws, and electoral reforms.

#### **The Spoils System**

The Spoils System was a prevalent practice in 19th-century American politics, referring to the system where the winning political party distributed government positions and other benefits to its supporters, friends, and party members. The system derives from the concept of spoils belonging to the victor, meaning the winner distributes the booty. In the United States, this system reached its peak up to the presidency of Andrew Jackson (1829-1837) and was gradually dismantled after the passage of the Pendleton Act in 1883.

Its characteristics are as follows:

- **Political connections first**: Appointments to government offices were chiefly based on loyalty and contribution to the ruling party, rather than on candidates' professional competence or qualifications;
- **Rewarding supporters**: The winning party would hand out government jobs as rewards to supporters, consolidating its power base;
- **Frequent official turnover**: After each election, a new party taking power would cause large-scale turnover of officials, most of the new officials being supporters of the new party;
- **Political machines**: Local political organizations in some areas, such as New York’s Tammany Hall, controlled voters and maintained political authority by dispensing patronage;
- **Bribery and corruption**: To obtain or keep their positions, officials often had to provide bribes to a party or political machine, resulting in rampant government corruption.

The spoils system led to a series of **consequences**: because appointments were based on political ties rather than professional competence, many government departments lacked effective management and expertise; officials’ tenures were unstable, lacking long-term planning and policy continuity; officials needed to donate money to political parties to retain their jobs, leading to pervasive conflicts of interest and corruption in policymaking; government decisions and resource allocation were driven more by political interests than the public good, harming social equity and public welfare; government corruption and inefficiency provoked public discontent and increased distrust of the political system in society.

#### **The Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act**

The Pendleton Act is a United States federal law, passed by the 47th United States Congress and signed into law by President Chester A. Arthur on January 16, 1883. The act is named after its principal proponent, then-Senator George Hunt Pendleton of Ohio.

The main provisions of the act include:

- **Examination-based selection**: The act stipulated that government positions should be filled through open **competitive examinations** selecting candidates, rather than through political connections;
- **Protection against political interference**: The act protected incumbent civil servants from being dismissed **for political reasons**, reducing the impact of political turnover on the civil service corps;
- **Prohibition of political contributions**: It forbade civil servants from making financial contributions to political parties in order to keep their jobs, thereby reducing political corruption;

Initially, the act applied to only a portion of federal government positions, especially key departments like the **postal system** and customs offices. By the late 19th and early 20th centuries, most federal government jobs had been brought under the civil service system.

## Data and Methods

#### **Primary Explained Variable — Postal Delivery**

Postal systems have played a significant role in nation-building across many countries. The U.S. postal system, created by the Postal Act of 1792, is considered one of the crucial institutional drivers promoting national growth and social progress. In its early years, the postal system facilitated communication and the dissemination of information between regions, helping to cultivate **an informed citizenry**.

The expansion of postal services not only promoted long-distance economic transactions but also allowed merchants to sell goods via mail order and catalog advertising. The postal system also provided banking services that helped investors find opportunities. Business-related mail comprised a large share of 19th-century correspondence, and some have argued that the American commitment to postal services formed the base of nationwide growth.

By the late 19th century, the U.S. postal system’s reach extended nationwide. This is underlined by the locations of all post offices opened between 1860 and 1905, highlighting the ubiquity of the postal system as an emblem of U.S. state capacity. The postal system’s wide coverage emphasizes its 19th-century importance: **some views hold that the postal system essentially was the core of the central government at that time**.

The postal system of that era was perhaps comparable to today’s internet, and based on this, the authors argue that the capacity and efficiency of the postal system are significant indicators of state capacity.

**Data Source:** The main data come from administrative records compiled by the U.S. postal system in the 19th century. Specifically included are *The Annual Report of the General Superintendent of the Railway Mail Service* and *The First Assistant Postmaster General*.

**Specific Indicator:** Postal delivery errors. This data comes from annual statistics recording errors in the distribution and forwarding of mail (*Statement of errors in the distribution and forwarding of mail*). When clerks of the Railway Mail Service (RMS) distributed mail between cities, they checked all items in mail pouches to detect misdirected mail. These errors were recorded and submitted to supervisory postal officials for tracking employee performance. The historical statistical reports provide two city-year-level measures of delivery errors: the **number of pieces that contain errors** and the **aggregate number of errors found across returned pieces**. The study primarily focuses on the latter to comprehensively reflect errors within a post office’s scope.

#### **Primary Explanatory Variable — Expansion of the Act**

The initial reform covered 23 districts; between 1884 and 1892, 30 additional districts were added, and by 1893 the reform extended to all post offices with free delivery services.

#### Identification Strategy

The paper employs a “stacked” event study design, constructing causal estimates using a Difference-in-Differences model to gauge the effect of civil service reform on postal service performance. The specification is as follows:
$$
y_{ijk} = \beta\,treat_{ij} \times post_k + \theta_{ij} + \tau_{jk} + \gamma\pmb{X}_{ijk} + \epsilon_{ijk}
$$
$\beta$ is the key parameter of interest, and it captures the effect of experiencing civil service reform on postal service performance.

## Results and Mechanism Analysis

#### **The Effect of the Act on Postal Performance**

- **Reduction in Postal Delivery Errors:** The reform significantly reduced postal delivery errors. The study finds that delivery errors decreased by 19–22%, a result that indicates substantially improved reliability for postal services. This reduction was consistent across the two major reform waves (1883 and 1893) and showed no significant pre-reform trends, lending support to both the external and internal validity of the findings. ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240802213259356.png)![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240802213404969.png)
- **Improved Cost Efficiency:** By analyzing productivity data on free delivery postal services, it was found that the act also significantly raised the volume of mail handled per carrier and improved the cost efficiency per unit of mail. ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240802213554712.png)

#### **Mechanisms for Performance Improvement**

- **Reduction in Personnel Turnover and Mitigation of Political Cycles:** After the reform, officials’ tenures lengthened significantly and job stability rose. This was because the act’s implementation reduced frequent personnel turnover caused by political cycles.
- **Performance in Election Years:** This effect was notably pronounced during presidential election years. This suggests that the civil service reform primarily improved service quality by weakening political influence on officials.  ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240802214148622.png)
- **Ruling Out Alternative Explanations:** Through analysis of postal personnel records, the possibility that the act improved performance by hiring more employees can be excluded. The study indicates the reform did not noticeably change selection standards, and the overall caliber of the workforce showed no significant difference before and after the reform. At the same time, an analysis of aggregate mail volume data rules out the possibility that performance improved because of a falling workload.

#### **Impact on Local Political Parties**

- By reducing career federal positions available for political patronage, the reform weakened the grip of local political parties over government jobs.
- **Partisan and Independent Newspapers:** By analyzing the number and circulation of **partisan newspapers** vs. **independent newspapers**, it was found that civil service reform led to a decline in the number of partisan newspapers and an increase in the circulation of independent newspapers, a result aligned with the hypothesis that the influence of local political parties diminished. ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240802214401428.png)

## Conclusion

- Civil service reform not only improved the public sector’s performance by separating politics from administration but also helped weaken the role of local parties.

## Asides

- This is a plain paper in form, but its meaning is extraordinarily weighty—as the one-sentence conclusion above says, separating politics from administration is deeply significant;
- In the 1980s, China also carried out some degree of reform toward separating the party from government administration;
- **Woodrow Wilson’s** Politics–Administration Dichotomy:
  - Woodrow Wilson was a president with a doctoral degree and had served as the president of Princeton University. In 1887, he published an article titled “*The Study of Administration*” in *Political Science Quarterly* that stressed the politics–administration dichotomy;
  - **Politics**: Wilson argued that politics deals with the formulation of national policy, primarily conducted by elected officials and the legislature. This part of the work involved contending with public opinion, interest conflict, and policy choices;
  - **Administration**: Administration, by contrast, is the execution of policy and should be performed by professional, impartial administrators. The duty of administration is to carry out and implement already formulated policies, ensuring the effectiveness and equitability of government operations.
  - Wilson advocated for the professionalization of administration, highlighting a merit- and competence-based civil service system to increase the efficiency and performance of government’s work. At the same time, he emphasized that administrative personnel ought to remain politically neutral, not subject to partisan politics or election cycles. The neutrality and stability of administrative agencies represent the key to the quality of public services and government credibility.
