**Q&A:**

*Q1: Hello Professor Guan! While using the CEADs database, I noticed that in the 47-sector data for Chinese provinces, the data for sub-sectors within industry are largely estimated. Could you explain what this estimation process and methodology involve? It seems the energy data for industrial sub-sectors in the 47-sector dataset are also estimated.*

A1: China does not officially release carbon emission data; CEADs calculations are based on inventory methods. At the provincial level, this is done using energy balance sheets and industrial final-consumption figures; emission factors vary from one province to another. The methodology is introduced in an article on our website. As for the 47-sector energy data — do Jiangsu or Zhejiang, for example, not publish their energy balance sheets openly? For some years, we managed to obtain energy balance sheets through specific channels; for others, we estimated based on other years’ datasets. We were able to acquire those internally circulated balance sheets. Please see the paper for details.

*Q2: Hello Professor Guan. Earlier, the World Bank estimated the total loss of global GDP; China’s level of recovery turned out swifter than expected. Now that some countries are imposing lockdowns yet again, will China’s already restored production capacity lead to permanent changes in global supply chains and, consequently, will some countries ramp up trade barriers against China?*

A2: Not every country has suffered a sustained blow — China's economy, for instance, had already returned to positive growth in the third quarter. Most of Asia has kept the pandemic under fairly strict control. Vietnam, for example, saw its industrial chains benefit during the pandemic: large amounts of investment poured in, production capacity recovered, and that is generating momentum for future exports. As for China, a great deal of hot money has flowed in seeking a safe haven; from the exchange-rate perspective, outside investors' confidence in China remains strong. Trade barriers are closely tied to international politics — the World Bank's current projects foresee two possible extremes: barriers being tightened, or trade being opened up completely.

*Q3: The Nature paper in April estimated that this year's carbon emissions would fall by 4–7%, depending on how the pandemic developed. Now that the second wave has become severe, what is your estimate of the total reduction in carbon emissions this year?*

A3: Countries with strong economic growth may see somewhat larger declines. Future emission reductions will depend on the structure of investment. The pandemic may turn out to be no more than a minor cold — its impact on overall future emissions is limited.

*Q4: Professor Guan, the figures in your papers are beautifully made, including those flow diagrams of embodied resource and environmental transfers. As a student, may I ask which software they are made with?*

A4: Not a single figure was made by me — they were all made by my students. Most are done in AI (Adobe Illustrator); the raw plots come from R and Origin. In any case, everything gets polished up in Illustrator.

*Q5: Electric vehicles are becoming more and more widespread. In the transport data, how do you distinguish electric vehicles from fuel-powered ones?*

A5: We estimate by proportions, under a number of prior assumptions — using past data to estimate the recent period.

*Q6: On the emissions of small countries?*

A6: At the current rate of growth, their emissions will surpass China's within 5–10 years. Controlling them will not be easy either, and the situation is very different from that of EU countries. The small countries also differ greatly among themselves — in political systems, economic structures, and so on — so there is no unified policy instrument; the problem can only be approached bottom-up.

*Q7: Many of the sixty small countries are key partners in China's Belt and Road Initiative. Against the backdrop of global emission reduction, the coal-power projects in China's Belt and Road investments have long been criticized by foreign media, and much of that coal power may end up as sunk costs. Do you have any specific suggestions?*

A7: *The New York Times* and *The Guardian* have interviewed me about this. It is not that China wants to export coal power — it is that these countries need the electricity to develop their industries, and China brings cleaner technology with it. Some industries cannot run on clean energy; steelmaking, for example, can only use coal power. China's role is to support Belt and Road countries with cleaner energy, and that should count as a carbon sink for China.

*Q8: Are there forecasts for carbon emissions and carbon absorption?*

A8: Our team does not yet have that capability — at present we mainly work on carbon sources and do little on sinks. Globally, not much carbon-sink forecasting is being done either. The Global Carbon Project has some short-term forecasts.

*Q9: Can the 1.5°C target still be achieved?*

A9: China aims to reach carbon neutrality by mid-century, and the major developed countries' pledges also point to mid-century. It mainly depends on future policy.
