### 1 Background

China is the largest emitter, facing international pressure to reduce emissions and domestic demand for emission reduction.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/11/22/lEZcjDBKCnYwbQ4.png)

China aims to achieve carbon neutrality by 2060.

The IPCC sets carbon neutrality for 2050, to keep global warming within a specific range.

Socioeconomic activity needs energy to support it, and the use of fossil energy releases carbon dioxide, so carbon emissions are closely tied to economic growth. Under the New Normal, the domestic production structure and China's role in global trade are changing. How will China's carbon emissions change in this new phase?

### 3 Results

After 2013, China's overall carbon emissions entered a stable phase.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/11/22/pMJ73aD2oK6BLFl.png)

Fossil energy emissions + industrial production processes (cement production)

Around 2006, China's total carbon emissions surpassed those of the United States; from 2001 to 2012, they grew by 10% per year on average.

In 2020, carbon emissions fell in the first half of the year, but by October the January-October total had already exceeded that of the same period last year, indicating a strong recovery of China's economy. The United States has still not recovered.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/11/22/x9Sun3zp7sUtQPV.png)

China's carbon emissions are growing, but carbon intensity (carbon emissions per unit of GDP) is declining.

Carbon emissions are accounted for, not measured.

Carbon emissions = energy consumption * emission factor

There is uncertainty on both sides.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/11/22/2sqebB1UH9pdNjr.png)

This figure is really clever: the gradient color shows total carbon emissions, the four colors show the contribution of each factor, and the yellow part shows that energy efficiency has improved, slowing down the growth of China's carbon emissions.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/11/22/Uj7xE5ehdvLRq4J.png)

The situation in the three years 2007, 2010, and 2012

Jiangsu, Zhejiang, and Shanghai are the region with the largest net inflow. The overall direction of the flows has not changed, but their volume has dropped considerably.

Life and consumption along the eastern coast are built, to a large extent, on the carbon emissions and other emissions of the central and western regions.

Q&A

Q1: What is behind the trend of rising carbon emissions in recent years? Can carbon be replaced with nitrogen?

A1: New construction after the economic transformation. Emissions should keep fluctuating (rising) up to 2030, peaking in 2030. Yes, it can be replaced, and structural decomposition is also possible, but the data would require nitrogen dioxide emissions for every single sector.

<br>

Q2: Haha, Professor Mi, besides PhD students do you also recruit postdocs? What requirements are there in general? And what are the requirements for the PhD?

A2: You must have a master's degree and English test scores. It mainly depends on the mutual fit of our backgrounds.

<br>

Q3: Professor Mi, can other factors be introduced into the factor decomposition, for example finance?

A3: Macro factors can be introduced; finance does not really work. SDA is essentially an identity (the Kaya identity?).

<br>

Q4: What are the current hot topics or frontier questions in environmental IO? Could you give some examples? Thank you.

A4: Hard to answer.

<br>

Q5: Professor Mi, why are per capita (household) carbon emissions highest in Inner Mongolia? Could you explain that in more detail?

A5: It is related to Inner Mongolia's energy structure. Inner Mongolia is one of China's energy bases; for example, producing the same bottle of water leaves a higher carbon footprint in Inner Mongolia. In addition, per capita consumption in Inner Mongolia is high.

Q8: Hello Professor Mi. I have noticed the targets mentioned by the top leadership: 2030 (peak carbon emissions) and carbon neutrality by 2060. I would like to ask what you think about the impact that future carbon neutrality may have on the economy. Can the economic and industrial growth brought by new energy compensate for this impact? A8: It is probably the biggest piece of international news on tackling climate change. China's carbon emissions now account for more than a quarter of global emissions every year, so this will have a major impact on industry. I believe the impact can be compensated.

Q10: What is the significance of the convergence of carbon emissions across China's income groups?

A10: When formulating emission reduction policies, the impact on different social strata needs to be considered: emission reduction by group and by region. Existing policies target regions but rarely target income groups. A carbon tax, for example, places different burdens on different household groups.
