### 1 Background

China is the world’s largest emitter, facing both international pressure to cut emissions and domestic demand for abatement.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/11/22/lEZcjDBKCnYwbQ4.png)

By 2060, China aims to reach carbon neutrality.

The IPCC sets a 2050 timeline for carbon neutrality to keep global warming within a specified range.

Socioeconomic activity depends on energy; fossil fuel use emits CO₂ during consumption, so carbon emissions are closely tied to economic growth. Under the New Normal, with shifts in domestic production structure and China’s role in global trade, how will China’s carbon emissions evolve in the new phase?

### 3 Results

After 2013, China’s total carbon emissions entered a plateau.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/11/22/pMJ73aD2oK6BLFl.png)

Emissions from fossil energy + industrial processes (cement production).

Around 2006 China’s total carbon emissions surpassed those of the US; from 2001 to 2012 the annual average growth was 10 %.

In 2020, emissions fell in the first‑half year, yet by October the January – October total already exceeded the same period last year, showing strong recovery in China’s economy. The US has still not recovered.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/11/22/x9Sun3zp7sUtQPV.png)

China’s carbon emissions are still growing, but carbon intensity (carbon emissions per unit GDP) is declining.

Carbon emissions are estimated, not measured.

Carbon emission = Energy consumption × emission factor. So there are sources of uncertainty on both sides.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/11/22/2sqebB1UH9pdNjr.png)

A very clever figure here: the gradient fill shows total carbon emissions, four colours show the contributions of individual factors. The yellow–coloured portion – energy efficiency improvements – pulled China’s carbon emission growth rate downwards.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/11/22/Uj7xE5ehdvLRq4J.png)

2007 → 2010 → 2012.

Jiangsu, Zhejiang and Shanghai are the largest net‑inflow region. The overall direction of flows hasn’t changed, but the volume of the flows has fallen significantly.

The living standards and consumption along the eastern seaboard are built to a large extent on the carbon and other emissions generated in central and western China.

Embodied carbon in China’s exports.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/11/22/mfpdAtv54JO2XnD.png)

Embodied carbon in China’s exports peaked in 2008, then these from other countries’ demand for Chinese goods dropped after that.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/11/22/vR18nJsjLgptrX9.png)

‘Energy efficiency’ is still the most important factor slowing or reducing China’s carbon emission growth, followed by economic structural Transition → Production structure. Exports are not the main driver. These plots are superb too!
