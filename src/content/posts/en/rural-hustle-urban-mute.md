On Youth Day last year, the actor He Bing said in the speech *Rising Waves*:

> Looking at you, I feel nothing but envy. The riches amassed by humanity over thousands of years — all knowledge, insight, wisdom, and art — seem to be gifts carefully prepared for you. Science and technology thriving, culture flourishing, and cities shimmering — the fruits of modern civilization, unwrapped layer by layer, are laid out for your full enjoyment.

On screen, young people threaded through cities, traveled abroad, cheered in esports arenas, and danced *otaku* routines on rooftops.

These rising waves surge through cities; the countryside has lost its voice.

The city is naturally where modern civilization takes root and grows. The concentration of capital, power, and knowledge stacks goods and services at such density that a person merely needs to walk downstairs or tap a phone to touch everything millennia of human endeavor has amassed.

Express transit, neon billboards, storefronts flecked with foreign alphabets, streams of mall-goers and ambient soundtracks — these shape the imagery of the city. Voices, electronic sounds, machine hum create an ordinariness that is comparatively higher in decibels than the countryside’s baseline noise, proclaiming the city’s pride and pushiness. Noise seems to be a tonic city counterpart.

Except — behind all the bustling traffic lies a muted aloneness — bare, disconnected.

My friend KY finds Beijing’s streets, Guangzhou’s, and Nanjing’s feel about the same — the same-shaped pervious bricks, the same plane trees, the same blank-faced people hurrying by, the same blue-sky-mirroring glass curtain walls that try to soften the buildings’ crushing weight. So instead, he chose to sit in his hotel room and binge-watch mukbang live streams.
