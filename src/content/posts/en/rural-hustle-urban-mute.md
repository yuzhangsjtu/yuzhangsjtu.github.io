On Youth Day last year, the actor He Bing said in his speech "The Next Wave" (后浪):

> I look at you, filled with envy. The wealth that humankind has accumulated over thousands of years — all the knowledge, the insight, the wisdom and the art — seems like a gift prepared just for you. Flourishing technology, luxuriant culture, prosperous **cities** — the fruits of modern civilization are unwrapped layer by layer, there for you to enjoy to your heart's content.

In the video, young people weave through cities, travel in foreign lands, cheer in esports arenas, and dance otaku routines on rooftops.

The next wave surges through the cities, while the countryside falls into aphasia.

The city is, as a matter of course, the place where modern civilization grows. The concentration of capital, power, and knowledge spreads goods and services at an extremely high density, so that people need only go downstairs or tap on their phones to touch the fruits humankind has accumulated over thousands of years.

Fast transportation, neon billboards, storefronts covered in foreign symbols, the hurried flow of people in shopping malls and the background music — all shape the imagery of the city. Human voices, electronic sounds, and mechanical noises constitute a baseline din higher in decibels than that of the countryside, proclaiming the city's pride and overbearing presence. It seems that clamor is the city's counterpart.

And yet, behind all the bustle lies a profound thinness and aphasia.

On the streets of Beijing, Guangzhou, and Nanjing, my friend KY experiences cities that are much the same. The same-shaped permeable bricks, the same plane trees, the same complexioned pedestrians, the same glass curtain walls setting off the blue sky to ease the buildings' oppressive imposition on space. And so, he chooses to stay in his hotel room and watch mukbang. Sometimes there comes a feeling that, for the scenes of particular districts of a city, there is a swapping and interweaving of memories — the tourists seen on the streets of Beijing appear in Nanjing as well. And so it is just as the joke describes:

> In China, almost every city has a street, packed with crowds, full of tourists, going by the name of an "old street," in the name of being artsy, monotonously selling handicrafts wholesaled from Yiwu, selling stinky tofu, grilled squid, and milk tea. This street is called Nanluoguxiang in Beijing, Tianzifang in Shanghai, Hefang Street in Hangzhou, Tunxi Old Street in Huangshan, the Confucius Temple in Nanjing, Dongguan Street in Yangzhou, Kuanzhai Alley in Chengdu, Gulangyu in Xiamen, Hubu Alley in Wuhan, the Muslim Quarter in Xi'an, Ciqikou in Chongqing, West Street in Guilin, Pichaiyuan in Qingdao, the Ancient Culture Street in Tianjin, the Fire Palace in Changsha, Barkhor Street in Lhasa, the Old Town of Lijiang in Lijiang...

City designers cater to the vulgar tastes of the masses; they project their ambitions about success onto the city, constructing a vast forest of steel and concrete and homogenized works. Walking down Kuanzhai Alley, looking into the exquisite little shops hung all over with panda logos, the clerks shout slogans to solicit customers whenever someone passes by, and once the customer has walked on they start playing on their phones, then shout the slogan again when the next customer passes — like NPCs in a game called "Earth Online," repeating fixed actions, waiting for the player to step forward and trigger further dialogue. This, one has to say, is a kind of aphasia.

Then I see aphasia happening in more places. The chilling fake electronic laughter blaring from Douyin in subway cars and the wooden expressions of the executioners, the shoulder-to-shoulder and arm-in-arm formed in the manner of meal companions, the silence of couples each facing their own phones in restaurants. The wasteland grows in places unseen; the city's clamor constructs a bubble-like spiritual prosperity, and so the petty bourgeoisie begin to build up their own reserves of meaning in livehouses, bookstore meet-and-greets, and exhibitions. This reminds me of Žižek's sardonic remarks about the middle class.



There is also another group of successful people who have extended their sense of "groundedness" into the countryside.

The traditional countryside is natural, and it is also clamorous. The frogs and cicadas of summer, these beautiful images, coexist with the cursing of unhappy families and the buzzing of mosquitoes. The clamor of the countryside has more to do with living beings, possessing its own independence and complexity.

But its mouth is gradually being covered.

I see the decision-makers and the planners and designers, in their plans and imaginings about the countryside, depersonalizing the countryside of its local inhabitants. It first happens in the violation of the rural landscape. In the rural tourism attractions of the Jiangnan region and in academic planning and design exhibitions, the same horse-head walls appear, the same wells, the same bodies of water and pink muhly grass built to cater to tourists; the poverty of architectural symbols spreads into the countryside. In planning texts at every level, the authors do not shy away from using the word "create" (打造) as the productive verb for rural tourism. As the renowned scholar Professor Ge put it, "history serves reality" — the countryside should serve the city. Shanghai's countryside has already become a second city (or a city-plus), a place that serves the city and bears the external functions of the city's core area. In the coordinated development of urban and rural areas, for the most part it is the city that wants to coordinate the countryside, not the countryside that wants to coordinate the city. Middle-aged city dwellers step out of their bizarre world and into the countryside, searching for the nostalgia in their memories, and then reap the pleasures of consumption amid a carefully embellished rusticity.

Next, in the areas close to the city, a confrontation between intellectuals and decision-makers has taken place. Rural tourism and modern agriculture are the two pillars of rural revitalization's requirement for thriving industry; as rural revitalization gradually develops into village-by-village revitalization, many villages have been injected with the expectations of rural tourism and modern agriculture. Publicity runs ahead of practice — research reports show that the market for rural tourism is on the order of trillions, with annual visitor flows reaching billions. After the top-down decomposition of targets, infrastructure construction has been rolled out on a large scale, and so some scholars warn that **at most less than 5% of the country's villages have any possibility of earning city dwellers' "nostalgia" money.**

Finally, what is strange is that they have made cities "all look alike," yet in turn demand that the countryside preserve its "rurality." As my friend said, "Urban civilization is more like picking flowers." Having grown tired of the easily obtained clusters of blossoms, people begin to seek a kind of transcendence; when the pulse-like ecstasy of short videos causes the system of attention allocation to collapse, they turn their gaze toward the soil that supports the growth of the flowers. They say the countryside should look like the countryside, calculating its "rurality content" along dimensions such as population, industry, and landscape; they say the preciousness of the countryside lies in its authenticity, that mountains and waters, roads and woods, and winding paths leading to secluded places are what make a village. They invade the countryside with their loudspeakers, proclaiming their masterly imagining and arrangement of this space. Yet they forget that the countryside should first of all be the villagers' countryside.

For a moment I truly cannot tell whether this is intellectuals being responsible or irresponsible toward the countryside.
