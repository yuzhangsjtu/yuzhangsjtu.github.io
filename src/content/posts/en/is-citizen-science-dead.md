Is Citizen Science Dead?

Christopher S. Lowry and Kristine F. Stepenuck

Our world is polarized by social, economic, political, and environmental events. It is impossible to discuss scientific discoveries or recommendations without causing a negative reaction and opposing views—either from informal interactions at the grocery store or during the review process of high-impact journals. As professional scientists, we are often viewed with distrust by members of the public. This stems partly from our failure to communicate our work effectively as well as our commitment to cultivating the industry of scientific research that requires us to maintain control in directing our research and authority over it. Hence, we typically push scientific discovery onto the public without engaging them in generation of new knowledge. This creates questions about how citizen science fits into the scientific continuum. Fundamentally, citizen science allows professional scientists to combine and engage with lay persons to produce scientific outcomes. This is a great opportunity for bridging the chasm to promote collaboration and build trust between and with the greater scientific community. To achieve this, we must evaluate the issues of inclusivity, bias, exploitation, and utility that amplify the chasm and restrict the implementation and engagement of citizen science in the scientific process. Without such action, it begs the question, “Is citizen science dead?”

Citizen science lacks inclusivity due to nomenclature, recruitment strategy, mechanistic approach to conducting the science, and even with respect to the scientific problems being addressed. The term citizen science may be a polarizing agent, which separates those who feel included from those who feel excluded. Those who participate in citizen science are often highly educated and affluent, lacking representation of the entire population, even with the field’s depiction as “public participation in scientific research” and other less divisive terms. Using any terminology that separates professional scientists from lay-persons in discourse about scientific endeavors or outcomes can create an immediate chasm, within which the authority of the lay-persons is in question. The inclusivity of citizen science is also affected by requirements specific to the project outcomes, including requirements related to equipment, accessibility, time, ability, and classification of participants. The scientific questions being addressed can also preclude some peoples, such as those disproportionally affected by contamination, from gaining exposure to desired benefits. However, collaborative and cocreated citizen science models, in which scientists and lay-persons jointly develop and implement research, are examples of successful inclusive approaches. Take for instance the thousands of University Extension offices within the United States which expand a university’s knowledge and resources to communities to engage the public in research. In this process, university academics foster relationships with communities, advance research topic implementation, and gain local benefits through solving local challenges.

The professionalization of science can lead to bias against citizen science. For example, **scientists may distrust lay data collectors: even though they often heavily rely on students with limited training to collect “valid” data for use in their own studies.** Issues of quality control relative to data collection and analysis of citizen scientists as well as of ethics, such as how much effort scientists can request of volunteers, are two of the most common arguments against use of the data to support their science type. Quality control issues can grow exponentially in large-scale programs where spatial and temporal inconsistencies in data collection and variability in training, and individual biases can all impact results. Nevertheless, this can also be true for professional sciences that use disparate data sets. Indeed, professional scientists are experts at quantifying uncertainty. With this basis, we must set standards for error analyses for citizen science and help implement a plan to identify possible known sources of error. Models exist that resemble the quality assurance and documentation handbooks of the EPA to offer guidance.

Lack of feedback from professionals and failure to acknowledge the public as valid contributors to science in regards to decision-making and the peer-review process all contribute to feelings of exploitation. Furthermore, the public may be viewed only as a vehicle for data acquisition, rather than as research collaborators. This adds to the devaluing of their contributions to knowledge generated. This exploitive nature can result in high turnover of volunteers and short project half-lives. While we know the loop from citizen to professional science cannot be closed unless
there is bidirectional transference of information, moving thinking beyond citizens as instruments is difficult and requires a significant time investment to accomplish. One resolution is to engage citizen scientists during the inception of the study and to include them throughout the scientific process. An active partnership is a means to not only close the loop but also serve as a mechanism to build relationships that can ultimately accord participatory citizen scientists equal footing to develop and distribute information to satisfy local needs.

Finally, citizen science is not a one-size-fits-all model that has some practical limitations distinct from traditional scientific research that demand different knowledge, skills, and actions of professional scientists. Some research questions require expensive and/or highly specialized equipment, limiting participation. Partnering effectively with community members also takes time. to recruit, train, support, and provide feedback to participants And, perhaps most importantly, time is required to foster relationships to promote trust, that may be the highest barriers for professionals to enter. Diverging trajectories of research may also occur, with professional scientists can focus on generating peer-reviewed products over longer timelines (though long shelf lives), whereas citizen scientists value the short-term feedback offered through a presentation or an externally facing website. Acknowledging that citizen science is not perfect is not a complete solution; We must also amplify opportunities for scientific discovery which is already apparent within the peer-reviewed literature. Research through Zooniverse EyeWire, eBird, the Large-Hadron Collider, and CoCoRaHS demonstrate impact and scalability in practice.

Without addressing the issues of inclusivity, bias, exploitation, and utility, citizen- access science could die, losing its place in the spectrum of scientific processes.
