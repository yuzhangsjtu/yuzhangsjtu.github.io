In my view, the turning point from a social *subject* to a social *science* hinges above all on the use of causal inference. Owing to my own limited abilities and narrow-minded interests, my command of causal inference techniques remains at a very superficial level—but that is also a necessary step toward transforming my research into what I can genuinely discuss with peers as “scientific work.” Meanwhile, I am indolent by nature, an advanced‑stage procrastinator. In my past learning (struggles) I have collected a few materials on causal inference, for your reference!

#### 1 Textbooks

- [**Causal Inference** *The Mixtape*](https://mixtape.scunning.com/)

  My personal favorite, and it has a web version! The book strikes the right balance between depth and breadth. In the preface the author explains why he wrote it: he lacked a textbook that integrated theory with (reproducible) empirics equally well. The book supplies classical paper examples and ships with code in Stata, R and Python — practically a hand‑holding guide.

- [**Causal Inference**: What If](https://www.hsph.harvard.edu/miguel-hernan/causal-inference-book/)

   Another excellent book; it feels more specialist and somewhat more rigorous than the previous one. The authors also provide companion data and code. A pity is that it is available only as a PDF, not online. Incidentally, there is a crowdsourced [Chinese translation](https://jiajunluo.mystrikingly.com/), and as I searched today, it seemed that someone has also prepared a [document](https://causalai.github.io/what-if/).

- [**The Effect**: An Introduction to Research Design and Causality](https://theeffectbook.net/)

  Compared to the first two, this one’s biggest feature is “practically no math.” The author tries to walk readers through the key aspects of causal inference solely using prose and causal diagrams. It also provides a web version, data, and code in multiple languages!

- Basically Econometrics; Advanced Econometrics with Stata Applications. I am sure everyone knows about these two. I find both useful, the kind you keep handy to look up. The fly in the ointment is that the supplied examples are few or modest in scope.

</br>

#### 2 Research tools

The mainstream econometric software boils down to Stata, R and Python; students working on equilibrium models and structural estimates may also be using Matlab. In the era of large language models, the learning curve around code is arguably the smallest obstacle in learning econometrics.

A few resources for basic syntax, well might include:

- [Python-Fundamentals](https://github.com/dlab-berkeley/Python-Fundamentals)
- [R-Fundamentals](https://github.com/dlab-berkeley/R-Fundamentals)
- [Stata-Fundamentals](https://github.com/dlab-berkeley/Stata-Fundamentals)

⬆ All are repos from Berkeley’s D-Lab. The content is squarely beginner-friendly and works nicely for getting started; the deliver it in Jupyter‑type notebooks. What a remarkably conscientious labour.

</br>
