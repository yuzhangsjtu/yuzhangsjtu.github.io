This paper investigates the long-term impact of the 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act on the economic development of the Western United States. Using a difference-in-differences design, the authors find that the Act significantly reduced the supply of Chinese labor, leading to a decline in manufacturing output and slowing economic growth in the Western region, with effects persisting at least until 1940. Although the Act's original intent was to enhance employment opportunities for white workers, the study shows that white workers did not significantly benefit from it. Instead, they were negatively affected by the decline in production efficiency. This reveals the extensive negative consequences that restrictive immigration policies can have on the economy.

## Introduction

####  **Background**

In 1882, the United States passed the Chinese Exclusion Act, which prohibited Chinese laborers from entering the country and denied those already in the U.S. the right to re-entry or citizenship. The Act garnered widespread public support, especially in the American West, home to a large number of Chinese immigrants. Despite widespread political backing, the primary motivation was **economic concerns**.

**Reasons for Supporting the Act:**

- The large presence of Chinese laborers was seen as crowding out employment opportunities for white workers and depressing wages, particularly in low-skilled jobs;
- By 1880, Chinese laborers constituted 12% of the working-age male population in the West and 21% of the immigrant population. Their presence was believed to undermine the economic opportunities of white workers, particularly those born in the United States.

**Reasons for Opposing the Act:**

- Some business owners opposed the Act, arguing that Chinese laborers were highly efficient and could not be easily replaced. Business owners feared that a complete ban on Chinese labor entering the U.S. could cause severe economic damage, as Chinese laborers contributed immense productivity in many sectors.

While some literature explores the Act's impact on Chinese communities, with many studies finding that it prompted a significant departure of Chinese people from the U.S., research on its effects on **non-Chinese labor** (the supposed beneficiaries of the Chinese Exclusion Act) and **overall economic activity** is relatively scarce. This provides a clear **research gap** for this study.

#### **Research Questions**

Before the passage of the Chinese Exclusion Act, Chinese workers in the Western U.S. were heavily concentrated in key sectors like railroads, mining, manufacturing, and services, playing a vital role in economic activity. What, then, would be the consequences of **reducing the Chinese workforce**?

- **Potential Positive Effects**: Theoretically, reducing the Chinese labor supply could **lessen competition among white workers**, thereby increasing white workers' wages and employment rates (aligned with the theory of economist George Borjas that a decrease in immigration pushes up native labor wages);

- **Potential Negative Effects**: Losing these skilled workers could lower productivity and even reduce job opportunities for white workers, particularly in industries requiring coordinated work between Chinese and white laborers. This effect might manifest through **economies of scale in production** or labor complementarities.

The core goal of the authors is to use rigorous **empirical research** to analyze the impact of the Chinese Exclusion Act on white workers and the broader economic development of the American West. Specifically, their research focuses on two key questions:

- Did the Chinese Exclusion Act positively impact the employment and wages of **non-Chinese labor** (i.e., white workers)?
- Did the Act negatively impact **overall economic activity** (such as manufacturing production)?

To answer these questions, the authors plan to conduct an empirical analysis using county-level data. This data covers eight states in the Western U.S. from 1850 to 1940. Analyzing the economic and labor market conditions across these states before and after the Act’s passage allows the authors to quantitatively assess its real impact.

## History of the Chinese Exclusion Act

#### **Chinese Labor Immigration**

**History of Chinese Immigration to the West**: By 1880, the Chinese constituted the largest immigrant group in the American West. Unlike European immigrants who entered the U.S. via the Atlantic Ocean, the Chinese arrived through the Pacific, landing in San Francisco and gradually dispersing to other regions of the American West. In 1880, Chinese made up about a quarter of the workforce in the West. Although occupying a significant place in the Western labor market, Chinese immigrants were a negligible proportion of the total U.S. population, as 85% of Americans at the time resided east of Illinois.
