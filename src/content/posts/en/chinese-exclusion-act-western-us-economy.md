> This paper studies the long-term impact of the 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act on the economic development of the Western United States. Using a difference-in-differences design, the authors find that the Act significantly reduced the supply of Chinese labor, leading to a decline in manufacturing output and slowing economic growth in the West, with effects persisting at least until 1940. Although the Act was originally intended to improve employment opportunities for white workers, the study shows that white workers did not benefit significantly from it; instead, they were hurt by the resulting decline in production efficiency. This reveals the broad negative consequences that restrictive immigration policies can have on the economy.

## Introduction

####  **Background**

In 1882, the United States passed the Chinese Exclusion Act, which barred Chinese laborers from entering the country and stripped Chinese laborers already in the U.S. of the right to re-enter or obtain citizenship. The Act enjoyed broad social support at the time, especially in the American West, which was home to a large Chinese immigrant population. Although the Act received widespread political backing, its primary motivation was **economic concerns**.

**Reasons for supporting the Act:**

- Large numbers of Chinese laborers were seen as taking jobs from white workers and depressing wages, particularly in low-skilled positions;
- By 1880, the Chinese accounted for 12% of the male working-age population in the West and 21% of its immigrant population, and their presence was believed to undermine the economic opportunities of white workers, especially native-born white American workers.

**Reasons for opposing the Act:**

- Some business owners opposed the Act, arguing that Chinese laborers were extremely efficient and could not easily be replaced. They worried that a blanket ban on Chinese laborers entering the U.S. could cause serious economic losses, because Chinese workers contributed enormous productivity in many fields.

While some literature has explored the Act's impact on the Chinese community, and many studies find that it prompted large numbers of Chinese to leave the United States, research on its effects on **non-Chinese workers** (the Act's intended beneficiaries) and on **overall economic activity** remains relatively scarce. This provides a clear **research gap** for the study.

#### **Research Questions**

Before the Chinese Exclusion Act was enacted, Chinese workers in the West were highly concentrated in several key sectors, such as railroads, mining, manufacturing, and services. They played an important role in economic activity. What consequences, then, would **reducing the Chinese workforce** bring?

- **Potential positive effects**: In theory, reducing the Chinese labor force could **lessen the competition among white workers**, thereby raising white workers' wages and employment rates (following economist George Borjas's theory that a reduction in immigration pushes up the wages of native labor);

- **Potential negative effects**: Losing these skilled workers could lower productivity and even reduce employment opportunities for white workers, especially in industries that required Chinese and white workers to work in tandem. This effect could operate through **economies of scale in production** or labor complementarities.

The authors' core goal is to use rigorous **empirical research** to analyze the Chinese Exclusion Act's impact on white workers and on the overall economic development of the American West. Specifically, their research focuses on the following two key questions:

- Did the Chinese Exclusion Act have a positive effect on the employment and wages of **non-Chinese workers** (i.e., white workers)?
- Did the Act have a negative effect on **overall economic activity** (such as manufacturing production)?

To answer these questions, the authors plan to conduct an empirical analysis based on county-level data. The data cover eight states of the Western U.S. from 1850 to 1940. By analyzing economic and labor conditions in these states before and after the Act's enactment, the authors can quantitatively assess the actual impact of the Chinese Exclusion Act.

#### **Research Contributions**

- **Contribution to the literature on the economic effects of immigration**: Related research falls broadly into two camps. The first holds that an increase in labor supply lowers native workers' wages and employment opportunities, a view supported by the immigration research of Borjas (2003, 2005) and Dustmann et al. (2017). The second holds that an increase in immigrant labor raises productivity and wages through complementarities in production and economies of scale. Evidence supporting this view shows that immigration promoted innovation and economic growth in the United States (both historically and in modern times), in Denmark, and in post-WWII Germany (e.g., Burchardi et al., 2019, 2020; Sequeira et al., 2020; Ottaviano and Peri, 2012; Foged and Peri, 2016; Peters, 2023). This view is also consistent with recent research on the economic effects of immigration restrictions, which shows that native-born U.S. workers did not benefit from the Immigration Acts of the 1920s (Abramitzky et al., 2022), and that the end of the Bracero Program in 1964 brought negative effects from immigration restrictions (Clemens et al., 2018). Meanwhile, the immigration quotas of the 1920s also reduced American innovation capacity (Moser and San, 2019). Since the Chinese Exclusion Act reduced the Chinese population living in the United States, our study also accords with findings that the expulsion of Jews from seventeenth-century Spain (Chaney and Hornbeck, 2016) and from the Soviet Union during World War II (Acemoglu et al., 2011) had long-lasting negative effects on the economy;
- **Advances the dialogue with the literature on the long-run development of the American western frontier**: Studies have found that the geographic isolation of the frontier fostered long-lasting individualistic preferences (Bazzi et al., 2020), while highly male-skewed sex ratios led to persistent gender norms (Bazzi et al., 2023). This paper shows that the geography of the western frontier and its male-biased sex ratios, through their interaction with the Chinese Exclusion Act, shaped the region's long-run development;
- **Complements existing research on the Chinese Exclusion Act**: The existing literature mainly analyzes the economic and social assimilation of the Chinese who remained in the United States and their descendants (Chen and Xie, 2020; Chen, 2015), but does not consider the Act's effects on non-Chinese workers or on aggregate production. Since the first version of this paper was released (January 2022), a working paper by Hoi (2023), using a sample of native-born American men, has found that low-skilled workers' wages rose after the Chinese left, which is consistent with this paper's finding that if native miners enter the sample, their earnings may have benefited from the Chinese Exclusion Act. Together, the two studies' results indicate that the Act did benefit some native-born American workers, but had negative effects on most workers and on the economy as a whole.

## History of the Chinese Exclusion Act

#### **Chinese Labor Immigration**

**History of Chinese immigration to the American West**: By 1880, the Chinese had become the largest immigrant group in the American West. Unlike European immigrants, who entered the U.S. across the Atlantic, the Chinese arrived via the Pacific at San Francisco and then gradually spread to other parts of the American West. In 1880, the Chinese made up about a quarter of the Western labor force; yet although Chinese immigrants held an important position in the Western labor market, their share of the national U.S. population was negligible, because 85% of the American population at the time still lived east of Illinois.

**Immigration organizations and labor management**: The arrival of Chinese immigrants was facilitated by The Six Companies, organizations that assisted with legal procedures and matched workers to employers in the United States. Chinese laborers were typically organized into "gangs". This mode of labor organization made Chinese workers attractive to employers, because an employer only needed to deal with the gang's leader (usually a Chinese merchant) and did not have to provide housing or logistical services for these laborers. This self-sufficient labor model effectively lowered employers' costs, especially in sparsely populated remote areas, where these Chinese workers also often supplied food and services to the local community.

**Origins and occupations of Chinese immigrants**: The first Chinese immigrants came to the United States during the California Gold Rush of the 1850s, and large numbers of Chinese laborers subsequently arrived to help build the transcontinental railroad. Most Chinese immigrants worked as short-term contract laborers; once a project was completed, they were often left behind in remote areas, which pushed them to turn to work such as logging, cooking, and laundry, and to provide services to local communities.

**Chinese immigrants' influence across industries**: By 1880, Chinese immigrants had penetrated many industries, especially manufacturing, services, and mining. For example, the Chinese held substantial shares of the shoe, hat, and cigar manufacturing industries. In addition, they also had a notable presence in service fields such as construction, dining, and cleaning.

**The dominance of male immigrants**: At the time, most Chinese immigrants were men. This was mainly driven by economic considerations: many Chinese men would return home after earning money in the United States, or bring their families over to America. This phenomenon reinforced the implementation of the Page Act of 1875, which restricted the immigration of Chinese women and left the sex ratio in Chinese American communities severely imbalanced.

#### **The Passage of the Chinese Exclusion Act**

**Motivations behind the enactment of the Chinese Exclusion Act**: Economic competition was the main motive driving the passage of the Chinese Exclusion Act. Both American-born white workers and European immigrants believed that the presence of Chinese laborers threatened their economic opportunities. Although Chinese laborers were very important to employers, the Act's proponents claimed that restricting Chinese labor would raise white workers' employment and wage levels. As a result, the Chinese Exclusion Act passed in 1882 barred Chinese laborers from entering the United States and blocked the path to citizenship for Chinese already residing there.

**Consequences of the Chinese Exclusion Act**: The Act's enactment led to a sharp decline in the Chinese labor force, especially in industries that relied on Chinese workers, such as railroad construction and mining. The paper also points out that the Act not only stopped new Chinese immigrants from entering the United States, but also had profound effects on the Chinese already in the country and their descendants. Its enactment disrupted entire production chains, particularly in industries dependent on Chinese labor, such as railroads and mining.

## Data and Identification Strategy

#### **Data**

The paper's main data come from multiple historical and economic datasets, forming county-level panel data covering eight Western U.S. states from 1850 to 1940. These states are Arizona, California, Idaho, Montana, Nevada, Oregon, Washington, and Wyoming. The data are drawn primarily from the U.S. Census along with other historical, social, and economic datasets, which record local labor forces, industries, and economic output, and specifically include the following:

- **Census data**: The U.S. Census provides the study with detailed information on demographic structure and labor markets, in particular each county's racial composition, types of labor (such as Chinese laborers and white workers), and occupational distribution;
- **Manufacturing output data**: Manufacturing production value data obtained from county-level economic activity datasets, recording each county's economic output;
- **County-level socioeconomic variables**: Including the level of urbanization, the share of non-Chinese immigrants, and the share of native-born white workers, which are used to control for heterogeneity across counties.

#### **Identification Strategy**

The authors adopt a difference-in-differences (DID) design, which exploits variation over time (the Act's enactment in 1882) and across the cross-section (differences in the Chinese population share in 1880), under the assumption that counties with a higher Chinese share in 1880 would be more strongly affected after the Act was enacted. The paper defines counties whose 1880 Chinese share was above the median as "high Chinese share counties", while counties below the median serve as the control group. The estimating equation is:
$$
Y_{ijt} = \alpha + \beta(HighChineseShare_{i,1880} \times 1_{t>1882}) + \Gamma X_{ijt} + \phi_i + \xi_{jt} + \nu_{ijt}
$$
where:

- $Y_{ijt}$ is the outcome variable of interest (such as labor supply or manufacturing output);

- $HighChineseShare_{i,1880}$ is a dummy variable identifying counties whose Chinese share in 1880 was above the median;

- $1_{t>1882}$ is an indicator variable marking the period after the Act's passage in 1882;

- $\Gamma X_{ijt}$ denotes control variables, such as years of railroad connection and the number of mines;

- $\phi_i$ and $\xi_{jt}$ are county and state-by-year fixed effects, respectively.

The key coefficient of the estimation is $\beta$, which captures the economic impact of the 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act on high Chinese share counties.

## Results

- **Effects on labor supply**: The Chinese Exclusion Act significantly reduced the supply of Chinese labor, by as much as 64% (the **counterfactual** based on Figure 2 shows that if the Chinese population had continued to grow at the same rate as between 1860 and 1880, it would have reached about 300,000 by 1940. In reality, there were only 100,000 Chinese in 1940. This rough comparison suggests that the Chinese Exclusion Act reduced the Chinese population by about 66%, which is quite close to the paper's estimate! **Amazing, who knew you could showcase plausibility this way**!). This impact was not limited to low-skilled workers but also extended to some high-skilled Chinese laborers. Contrary to expectations, the Act did not leave white workers better off; instead, it caused the supply of white labor to fall by roughly 28%. These results indicate that counties that lost Chinese labor did not become significantly better at attracting new white workers;
- **Effects on manufacturing output**: The Chinese Exclusion Act's negative impact on manufacturing was especially pronounced. The authors find that total manufacturing output fell by 62%, and the number of manufacturing establishments fell by 54% to 69%. These effects indicate that the reduction in Chinese labor directly led to a contraction of manufacturing activity, especially in the West, where production was predominantly labor-intensive. Although the productivity estimates trend negative, the statistical uncertainty is high, so the effect on productivity remains rather ambiguous;
- **Effects on skilled workers**: With respect to skilled labor, the authors find that the Act also had large negative effects on high-skilled white workers. Analyzing worker groups at different skill levels, the study shows that the Chinese Exclusion Act reduced the total number of white workers, with the effect especially pronounced in manufacturing. Moreover, in counties more heavily affected by the Act, the complementary relationship between high-skilled Chinese laborers and high-skilled white workers was broken, leading to losses in both groups of workers;
- **Dynamic effects**: The authors further analyze the long-run impact of the Chinese Exclusion Act on economic activity. The study shows that the negative effects were not confined to the short term after the Act's enactment but persisted into the 1940s. The blow that losing Chinese labor dealt to Western economic growth was persistent, especially in manufacturing and services.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241014163651019.png)

## Mechanisms

#### **Migration Costs**

The Chinese Exclusion Act's main purpose was to increase employment opportunities for white workers. However, the authors propose that white workers did not significantly fill the labor vacancies left by departing Chinese laborers, possibly because **migration costs** were high. These migration costs include information costs and travel costs, especially for labor migration across regions. The study shows:

- **Information and geographic distance**: White workers, especially those born outside the West, faced higher migration costs because they lacked sufficient knowledge of the West's climate, living conditions, and the like. In addition, geographic distance also raised the cost of relocating;
- **White workers and mining**: One exception is mining, where the authors find that Western-born white workers filled the vacancies left by departing Chinese laborers after the Chinese Exclusion Act passed, especially in the mining sector. By contrast, other white workers from the Eastern U.S. or Europe did not move into the West in large numbers.

By comparing white workers from different birthplaces, the study shows that migration costs had an important influence on labor mobility: especially in more distant regions, higher information and transportation costs made it harder for these workers to fill the gap left by the departing Chinese. The end result was that **local Western white workers benefited, but white workers from elsewhere did not move into the West in large numbers**.

#### **Labor Complementarity**

The study finds that **complementarity between Chinese and white workers** was an important factor shaping economic outcomes. Specifically, in many industries Chinese and white workers complemented one another rather than competing. The study shows:

- **Skill complementarity**: After the loss of Chinese labor, the number of white workers in the West, especially skilled workers, also declined, indicating that Chinese and white workers worked in tandem in certain industries and thereby raised productivity. Consequently, when Chinese laborers left, this affected not only their own positions but also reduced production opportunities for white workers, particularly high-skilled white workers;
- **The role of complementarity**: In manufacturing and mining in particular, the authors find that the reduction in Chinese labor hurt overall production efficiency, leading white workers to gradually leave these industries as well. The working relationship between the two groups was reflected not only in direct collaboration but also in mutual influence through industry chains and supply chains.

#### **Ruling Out the Local Consumption Channel**

Although the mass departure of Chinese laborers reduced local consumer demand, the authors argue that this was not the main driver of the Chinese Exclusion Act's negative economic effects. By comparing industries that produce widely traded goods (such as minerals and manufactured products), the study finds that the negative effects on manufacturing and mining were pervasive regardless of whether local consumer demand was present. The authors therefore conclude that the Act's shock to economic activity operated mainly through the reduction in labor supply rather than a decline in consumer demand.

## Conclusion and Discussion

#### **Conclusion**

The Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 had long-lasting negative effects on the economic development of the American West, and these effects persisted until 1940. The Act drove away large numbers of Chinese laborers, deterred many potential migrants from moving West, and failed to benefit native-born white American workers as expected. On the contrary, its implementation did not significantly increase white workers' employment opportunities or wages, but instead weakened the positive spillover effects that Chinese labor generated for economic activity.

#### **Competition and Spillover Effects**

The Act's original justification rested on the **economic competition** between native-born American workers and Chinese laborers, namely that the Chinese workers' low wages and high efficiency might harm white workers' economic opportunities. However, through empirical research the authors find that this competition effect was relatively small. Instead, the **positive spillover effects** of Chinese labor on the Western U.S. economy, such as raising productivity and working in tandem with white workers, proved more important. The departure of Chinese laborers weakened the labor supply and overall production efficiency of many industries, especially in sectors such as manufacturing and mining.

#### **Directions for Future Research**

- **Deeper investigation of mechanisms**: Although the paper uncovers some mechanisms (such as labor complementarity and migration costs) behind the negative economic effects, the authors believe that more fine-grained production data would help further reveal exactly how these mechanisms operate.
- **Effects of the Chinese laborers' departure on their descendants**: The authors note that, much like the forced relocation of Polish workers in post-WWII Europe, the departure of Chinese laborers in the nineteenth century may also have had profound effects on their descendants. Future research could explore whether these laborers' departure changed their descendants' economic behavior or human capital investment.
- **The Chinese Exclusion Act's impact on other countries in the Americas**: The authors also suggest studying the Act's economic effects on other countries in the Americas. Many Chinese moved to other countries in the Americas after 1882, such as Canada and Mexico; studying how these migrations affected local economic development would provide important historical context for the economics of immigration.

## Extra

- **The emergence of Chinatowns** was a survival strategy through which Chinese laborers coped with social exclusion. After the Act took effect, many Chinese chose to stay in the United States but faced severe discrimination and threats of violence. They gradually formed Chinese enclaves in some major cities (such as San Francisco, Los Angeles, and New York), and these places became community centers where the Chinese supported one another and survived in an exclusionary environment. Chinatowns not only helped the Chinese sustain themselves economically but also became important sites for preserving cultural identity;
- The paper mentions that "the records of the 1890 U.S. Census were destroyed in a fire", and only a few states' records were spared;
- The paper seems to carry an unstated policy implication, namely a reflection on the current American hostility toward China.
