### 1 Starting with the disputes over efficiency

Neoclassical definition of efficiency:

> Any equivalent substitution at the margin in value terms between two or more decisions (on resource use, production inputs, sale of goods, etc.). MR = MC?

The assumptions neoclassical economics imposes upon the real world limit its explanatory power for numerous problems — externalities, public goods, free‑rider issues, and so on. Later economists introduced transaction costs and bounded rationality to further strengthen economic explanation.

The real world is a world full of **friction**.

### 2 Core concepts

Neoclassical assumptions:

- Perfect information;
- Perfect information is possessed by perfectly rational decision‑makers;
- Perfect information possessed by perfectly rational decision‑makers can be processed, handled and turned into decisions at zero cost.

Built on the above, certain core concepts of efficiency in the neoclassical tradition take shape:

- Pareto efficiency
- Kaldor–Hicks efficiency: a reallocation of resources is efficient if the gains of the winners exceed the losses of losers, regardless of whether the winners compensate the losers (Kaldor, 1939; Hicks, 1939). ~Does this broadly resemble Bentham’s utilitarianism? *Justice*: a group of people stranded on a desert island find some fish — shared equally, no one survives; if some go without (and starve) the rest can live? Allocation?~

- Allocative efficiency: users of a resource maximise the net benefit of resource use according to their own cost and benefit functions under a given level of technology. The basic principle is that the marginal value of a resource must be equal across different uses, different points in time and different locations; hence this category of efficiency may also be called “marginal‑principle” efficiency.
- Technical efficiency: under constant production technology and market prices, and for a given factor‑input mix, the percentage of the minimum cost of producing a given quantity of output relative to the actual cost. A pair of input and output vectors is technically efficient if it is technically impossible to increase any output (or reduce any input) without reducing some other output (or increasing some other input) (Farrell, 1957; Yao Yang & Zhang Qi, 2001).

The real world is filled with **friction**: uncertainty, information asymmetry, incomplete contracts, opportunism and other factors are the sources of that friction. In the real world there exist **transaction costs**, **imperfect information** and **bounded rationality**.

**Friction** arises in essence because transaction costs in the real world cannot be measured precisely, and because decision‑makers are boundedly rational. Transaction costs that resist precise measurement inevitably introduce inescapable errors (or even outright mistakes) into marginal‑cost and marginal‑benefit calculations, while bounded rationality invalidates the assumption that decision‑makers always act according to the marginal optimum.

Efficiency under constraints:

- Pareto second‑best
- X‑efficiency: a state in which economic activity, for internal reasons, fails make full use of its existing resources or opportunities — a kind of organisational or motivational (low) efficiency. The X stands for all those factors that give rise to an allocative‑inefficiency condition (Leibenstein, 1966). The concept of technical efficiency is also related to [X‑inefficiency](https://www.economicshelp.org/blog/glossary/x/x-inefficiency.html). X‑inefficiency is said to occur when a firm fails to be technically efficient because of an absence of competitive pressures. e.g. a monopoly employs inefficient working practices because it has no incentive to cut costs.
- Remediable economic efficiency: an existing mode of organisational operation, resource allocation or economic system shall be called efficient if no superior alternative is available that promises greater net benefit (Williamson, 1996).

Notions of efficiency under constraints are cognisant both of the impossibility of measuring transaction costs precisely and of the limits of bounded rationality. Yet in the pursuit of efficiency they simultaneously require that decision‑makers be able to observe all constraints without cost, master every conceivable decision alternative, and reach an efficient decision instantaneously and costlessly. This harbours an internal contradiction in logic: as soon as the limited cognitive capacity of decision‑makers is acknowledged, they are simultaneously endowed with a costless ability to process information.

Tan Rong argues:

> The essence of research into New Institutional Economics lies in using institutional arrangements (governance structures included) to lower transaction costs wherever possible. That is at stake is that within a given resource‑use process, efficiency in resource use is highest when “rent dissipation is minimised” (Cheung, 1974).

### 3 A perspective that ties the three levels together

Measurement vs. practice

Something xx can be precisely measured yet cannot be practiced; something xx can be practiced yet cannot be precisely measured.

This prompts the notion of **dynamic convergence** toward efficiency (my term).

Levels:

- Societal foundations. This layer encompasses informal rules — culture, customs, morals, traditions, religion, and so on. It is generally presumed that informal institutions take shape spontaneously — “calculative choice aimed at purposive design is virtually absent” (Williamson, 1998); consequently this layer exerts little human‑driven influence on the active pursuit of efficiency. (Or is it perhaps — part of this very layer is itself the natural outcome of pursuing efficiency? For instance, custom xx or culture xx, might already encode an aspiration toward efficient outcomes?)

- Institutional environment. This layer encompasses formal rules — constitutional law, statutes, property rights, and so on. Although institutions at this level may, just as informal ones can, “come into being by evolutionary processes”, they “are more often subject to design” (Williamson, 2000). Thus the design of formal institutions provides humankind with the *first* opportunity to exercise active choice over efficiency.
- Governance structure. This layer may be understood as the various competitive arrangements which players in the economy spontaneously choose when operating under formal institutions. The purpose of governance structures is, for any given set of formal institutional constraints, to confer efficiency on governance processes applied to particular transactions — that is, to provide humankind with the *second* opportunity actively to strive for efficiency.
- Resource allocation. Here governance structures and formal institutions are taken as given; efficiencies associated with a marginal optimum can by approached through continual iterative adjustments to relative prices and quantities — this is humankind’s *third* opportunity actively to pursue efficiency.

In other words, naturally emerging → larger (big‑chunk) institutions → smaller (finer‑meshed) governance arrangements → micro‑tuning?

Traced in this way one layer at a time feels neither mathematically natural nor quite parsimonious.

> When Ostrom (2005) dissects institutional actions across levels, he likewise distinguishes four analytic tiers: a meta‑constitutional situation, a constitutional situation, a collective situation, and an operational situation.

Ostrom’s labelling seems the one capable of really bringing clarity to the mind in one stroke.

### 4 A definition of governance efficiency

- For resource allocation, governance efficiency fundamentally means maximising the net benefit flowing from an economic activity; in theory this outcome may be measured by the equality of marginal (social) cost and marginal (social) benefit, yet practical constraints on the accuracy of measurement compel us to substitute alternative measures when simultaneously reducing the constraints posed by factors such as transaction costs and bounded rationality.
- For governance structure level analysis, governance efficiency points to the *chosen* governance structure being one that can — in a comparative sense — minimise transaction costs more than alternatives already.
- For the institutional environment level, governance efficiency suggests nothing other than tracing back to which compare‑first **introduced** institutional surroundings lead to smaller transaction costs.

The actual **measurement** of transaction costs now possesses a suite of well developed approaches (Yvrande‑Billon & Saussier, 2004).
