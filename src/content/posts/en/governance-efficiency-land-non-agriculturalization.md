Key: Provides a definition of efficiency that interconnects three levels — resource allocation, governance structure, and institutional environment.



### 1 Starting from the controversy over efficiency

The neoclassical definition of efficiency:

> Any equivalent substitution in marginal value between two or more decisions (resource use, production inputs, sale of goods, etc.). MR = MC?

Neoclassical economics' assumptions about the real world limit its explanatory power for many problems, such as externalities, public goods, and the free-rider problem. Later economists introduced transaction costs and bounded rationality to further strengthen economic explanation.

The real world is a world full of **friction**.

### 2 Key concepts

Neoclassical assumptions:

- Perfect information;
- Perfect information is held by perfectly rational decision-makers;
- The perfect information held by perfectly rational decision-makers can be processed, handled, and turned into decisions at zero cost.

On the basis of the above, some key concepts of efficiency under the neoclassical framework were born:

- Pareto efficiency
- Kaldor-Hicks efficiency: after a resource is reallocated, if the increase in the winners' welfare exceeds the decrease in the losers' welfare, then this reallocation of the resource is efficient regardless of whether the winners compensate the losers (Kaldor, 1939; Hicks, 1939). ~Is this somewhat similar to Bentham's utilitarianism? In *Justice*, a group of people stranded on a desert island find some fish; split equally, no one can survive, but if some go without (and starve to death), can the rest survive? Allocation?~

- Allocative efficiency: a resource user, under a given level of technology, maximizes the net benefit of resource use according to their own cost and benefit functions. The basic principle is that the marginal value of the resource allocation is equal across different uses, different points in time, and different locations; therefore, this kind of efficiency can also be called the efficiency of the "marginal principle";
- Technical efficiency: under unchanged production technology and market prices, and following a given ratio of factor inputs, the percentage of the minimum cost required to produce a certain quantity of product relative to the actual cost. If it is technically impossible to increase any output (or reduce any input) without reducing other outputs (or increasing other inputs), then this input-output vector is technically efficient (Farrell, 1957; Yao Yang & Zhang Qi, 2001);

The real world is full of **friction**: uncertainty, information asymmetry, incomplete contracts, opportunism, and other factors are the causes of friction. In the real world there exist **transaction costs**, **imperfect information**, and **bounded rationality**.

**Friction** is, in essence, due to the fact that transaction costs in the real world cannot be measured accurately and that decision-makers are boundedly rational. Transaction costs that cannot be measured accurately cause unavoidable errors (or even mistakes) in the calculation of marginal cost and marginal benefit, while bounded rationality negates the assumption that decision-makers always make decisions according to the marginal optimum.

Efficiency under constraints:

- Pareto second-best
- X-efficiency: a state in which, for internal reasons, economic activity fails to fully utilize existing resources or opportunities — a kind of organizational or motivational (low) efficiency. This X represents all the factors that cause low allocative efficiency (Leibenstein, 1966). The concept of technical efficiency is also related to [X-inefficiency](https://www.economicshelp.org/blog/glossary/x/x-inefficiency.html). X-inefficiency is said to occur when a firm fails to be technically efficient because of an absence of competitive pressures. e.g. a monopoly employs inefficient working practices because it has no incentive to cut costs.
- Remediable economic efficiency: if no more suitable alternative exists that would be expected to yield greater net benefit, then the existing organizational operation, resource allocation, and economic system can be called efficient (Williamson, 1996).

Notions of efficiency under constraints recognize that transaction costs cannot be measured accurately and that effective rationality has its limits, yet in pursuing efficiency they require that decision-makers be able to observe all constraints at zero cost, master all possible decision alternatives, and at the same time make efficiency decisions costlessly and instantaneously. This leads to an internal logical contradiction: while acknowledging that decision-makers have limited cognitive capacity, it nevertheless reserves for them the ability to process information at zero cost.

Professor Tan Rong holds that:

> The essence of New Institutional Economics research is to reduce transaction costs as much as possible through institutional arrangements (including governance structures). That is, within a given resource-use process, when "rent dissipation is minimized" (Cheung, 1974), the efficiency of resource use is highest.

The theory of rent dissipation:

https://www.romeconomics.com/licensing-rent-dissipation/

> Rent Dissipation occurs when a valuable public good can be accessed with no restrictions under competition its value will dissipate at the margin after being consumed by an additional person, until all rent is completely dissipated. A good example of rent dissipation is the case of overfishing. Assume there is a sea that anyone can get access to. Fishermen will crowd at the sea to fish until all fish have been captured. Rent of the sea decreases to zero as it cannot further produce any output (fish). The sea has become a wasted capital. 
>

When valuable capital can be accessed with no restrictions, the first extractor may decide to destroy resources until an additional entry is worsen-off. For instance, a farmer, finding a farmland with rich soil, chooses to build (poor-quality) houses instead of plating crops on that land. It is to avoid competition because with no restrictive entry, anyone can step onto the farmland and plant its own crops. Hence it is better off for the farmer to destroy the rich resources so that further entrance is not profitable.
>
> After all, without clearly defining property rights rent dissipation will occur. To combat it, licensing is a major solution.

**Licensing** is one of the means of combating rent dissipation.

### 3 A perspective based on the linkage of three levels

Measurement VS practice

xx can be measured precisely but cannot be practiced; xx can be practiced but cannot be measured precisely.

Hence the notion of efficiency as **dynamic approximation** is proposed (my own term).

Levels:

- Societal foundations. This includes informal institutions (informal rules) such as culture, customs, morality, traditions, and religion. Informal institutions are considered to form spontaneously — "choice that follows purposive calculation is almost nonexistent" (Williamson, 1998); therefore, this level has little influence on humankind's active pursuit of efficiency. (Or, put another way, are these (in part) themselves natural behaviors in the pursuit of efficiency? For instance, does a certain custom or a certain culture already contain within it the meaning of pursuing efficiency?)

- Institutional environment. This includes formal rules such as constitutions, laws, and property rights. Although institutions at this level, like informal institutions, can "form through evolutionary processes," they are more often "deliberately designed" (Williamson, 2000). In this way, the design of formal institutions provides humankind with its first opportunity to actively choose efficiency;
- Governance structure. This can be regarded as the various competitive rules that agents spontaneously choose under formal institutions. The purpose of governance structures is, within the constraints of formal institutions, to achieve efficiency in the governance process for specific transactions — that is, to provide humankind with its second opportunity to actively pursue efficiency;
- Resource allocation. Taking governance structures and formal institutions as given, the marginal-optimum principle is realized through continual adjustment of prices and quantities, providing humankind with its third opportunity to actively pursue efficiency.

That is, nature → institutions (large) → rules (small) → fine-tuning?

Following this path through, it does not seem natural or concise.

> When Ostrom (2005) analyzes institutions at different levels, she also divides institutions into four levels, namely the meta-constitutional situation, the constitutional situation, the collective situation, and the operational situation.    

Ostrom's naming seems more capable of bringing a sense of sudden clarity.

### 4 A definition of governance efficiency

- At the resource-allocation level, governance efficiency essentially refers to the maximization of the net benefit of economic activity, which in theory can be measured by the equality of marginal (social) cost and marginal (social) benefit; but because in practice various factors limit the accuracy of measurement, alternative methods can be adopted to measure marginal cost and marginal benefit, so as to reduce the limitations that factors such as transaction costs and bounded rationality place on the measurement itself;
- At the governance-structure level, governance efficiency means that the **chosen** governance structure can, comparatively speaking, minimize transaction costs;
- At the institutional-environment level, governance efficiency means that the **established** institutional environment can, comparatively speaking, minimize transaction costs.

There are already very mature methods for the **measurement** of transaction costs (Yvrande-Billon and Saussier, 2004).

### 5 Judgment in reality: taking economic activity in the private domain and the public domain as examples

For the private domain, such as firms, generally it is only necessary to consider governance efficiency at the resource-allocation level.

This chapter is more concerned with economic activity in the public domain, that is, how humankind pursues governance efficiency when problems such as externalities, public goods, and "free-riding" arise.

Traditional resource economics holds that resource use often ignores non-market value, causing marginal cost to diverge into private cost and social cost; therefore, the government needs to regulate through quotas, taxes and fees, subsidies, and other means to achieve efficiency in resource use. (However, the problem is exactly how large the quotas, taxes and fees, and subsidies must be in order to accurately internalize the externality.)

Judgment of governance efficiency:

- If it is economic activity in the private domain, the market's supply-and-demand prices and quantities are the best structure; in this case, one can make decisions solely according to the efficiency standard at the resource-allocation level, without needing to consider the governance-structure and institutional-environment levels;
- If it is economic activity in the public domain, the market may be in a state of "failure"; one must first consider it from the standards of the governance-structure and institutional-environment levels, looking for a suitable governance structure and institutional arrangement to reduce transaction costs, and then realize efficiency at the resource-allocation level.

At the present stage, China's collective land is in a state of high value but low (or no) price.

> Market and government, in New Institutional Economics, are merely concepts of two different governance structures, rather than the relationship between market and government in Pigovian theory. New Institutional Economics holds that the government is an alternative to the market, and how to choose between them depends only on the size of their respective transaction costs. Pigovian theory holds that the government is omnipotent and is used to make up for the defects of the market.
