> This paper examines the incentives and consequences of elites expressing loyalty to the ruler through **sycophantic behaviors** (such as verbal praise or imitating policy themes) under authoritarian regimes. Analyzing the annual speeches of Russian regional leaders, the authors find that politically and economically vulnerable elites are more inclined to flatter the ruler in order to extend their time in office and secure political survival.
>
> The research has important implications for understanding how personality cults develop and how autocrats manage the trade-off between **loyalty** and **competence**.

**Title:** Playing the sycophant card: The logic and consequences of professing loyalty to the autocrat

**Journal:** *American Journal of Political Science*

## Introduction

#### **The Loyalty–Competence Trade-off**

- **Conflict between Loyalty and Competence**: Autocrats face a core problem: while they need competent elites to govern effectively, foster economic growth, and implement policies, they also fear those elites threatening their rule. Talented officials, owing to their independence and potential influence, may be less dependent on the ruler and thus more prone to defecting or challenging the autocrat’s power. Research by Tullock (1987) and Zakharov (2016) shows that precisely because of this potential threat, **autocrats often prefer loyal elites** over the most capable officials. Though competence is crucial for state functioning, loyalty better serves to consolidate an autocrat’s rule.
- **The Autocrat’s Dilemma**: When an autocrat surrounds themselves with sycophantic yes-men, they receive less information about the world, their advisors and officials become less likely to criticize and participate actively in policymaking, and as the ruler is showered with constant praise, they begin to overestimate their own abilities and the regime’s capacities. Consequently, the sycophantic behavior of authoritarian officials can lead to poorly prepared, ill-deliberated policy decisions with potentially catastrophic outcomes. Many observers identify exactly this yes-man syndrome as one reason behind Russian ruler Vladimir Putin’s decision to invade Ukraine in February 2022.

#### **Literature Review on the Loyalty–Competence Balance**

- **The Russian Case**: In studies of Russia, the economic performance of mayors and governors has no bearing on their political career prospects; rather, the key determinant of their careers is the ability to demonstrate allegiance to the regime in national elections (Reuter & Robertson, 2012).
- **The Chinese Case**: Some scholars find that local leaders are appointed based on economic performance (Li & Zhou, 2005), while others argue that only the most competent among the loyal candidates get promoted (Jia et al., 2015), or that loyalty matters more at higher administrative levels whereas the demand for skill dominates at lower tiers (Landry, 2008).

#### **Sycophancy as an Elite Strategy**

- **Elite Vulnerability under Authoritarianism**: Though the autocrat’s elites are crucial for regime survival, individual elites are not irreplaceable. Through **job reassignment** and **purges**, autocrats lock subordinates into an asymmetric power game. To survive in this environment of uncertainty and the threat of punishment, elites need to adopt various strategies to sustain their political careers. According to Wintrobe (1998), **sycophantic behavior** – i.e., **excessive flattery** and **deference toward the autocrat** – is a highly effective strategy. Especially in such power-imbalanced relationships, vulnerable elites try to avoid punishment or replacement by displaying unconditional allegiance to the ruler. This behavior not only helps soothe the ruler’s suspicions but also enables these elites to keep their posts.
- **The History and Practice of Sycophancy**: In 1925 within Mussolini’s Italy, then Party Secretary Farinacci proposed that Party members must make a pilgrimage to Mussolini’s birthplace; other Party members had little choice but to comply and craft their own obsequious expressions so as not to be outrivaled. Similarly, in the 1930s Soviet Union, Kaganovich – one of Stalin’s deputies – praised Stalin at every opportunity and proposed using “Long live Stalinism” instead of “Leninism” in Party slogans. The term soon mushroomed in the speeches of other officials, who likewise resorted to sycophancy as a survival strategy during Stalin’s **unpredictable** reign.
- **Sycophancy and the Cult of Personality**: When elites mention the autocrat in their utterances, defend their policies and actions, or pander to the autocrat, their behaviors collectively relate to a broader phenomenon – the **cult of personality**. Svolik (2012) notes that when an autocrat establishes personal rule and subordinates other elites, a large-scale cult of the leader is almost inevitable.

#### **Gaps in Existing Research**

- **Neglect of Verbal Sycophancy**: Although the loyalty–competence framework is central to the study of authoritarian politics, scholars have focused exclusively on how elites serve the autocrat materially. Non-material aspects – such as **verbal sycophancy toward the autocrat**, a common everyday practice under authoritarianism – have been overlooked.
- **Neglect of Heterogeneity**: Even if sycophancy is pervasive across autocracies, not all elites behave uniformly; how can we explain the variation among elites?
- **Effectiveness of Verbal Sycophancy**: Does verbal sycophancy actually work? This too has not received sufficient attention.

#### **This Paper’s Contributions**

- What the paper does: Through a textual analysis of nearly 1,000 annual speeches delivered by Russia’s regional leaders (such as governors), this paper examines the manifestations and effects of sycophantic behavior. It finds that sycophancy is not universal among all elites. Politically and economically more vulnerable governors are more inclined to demonstrate overt sycophancy in their public addresses (such as frequently mentioning Putin’s name or echoing Putin’s policy themes). Those governors who flatter the ruler remain in office for a longer period.
- **Extends the Loyalty–Competence Trade-off Theory**: The paper broadens the theory on the loyalty–competence balance, especially over non-material manifestations of loyalty (such as sycophantic behavior) under autocratic rule. Existing literature largely focuses on material ways through which elites signal loyalty (e.g., delivering votes, providing economic support), while omitting non-material modes such as sycophancy. By studying elites’ public displays of excessive adulation for the autocrat, the paper fills this gap and reveals the effects of such behavior on elite political careers. Through text analysis, it demonstrates that sycophany, especially among vulnerable elites, is as much an important marker of loyalty expression as material support.
- **Provides a New Lens on Elite Political Survival Strategies**: By analyzing open-spoken statements of Russian regional leaders (governors), the paper reveals that those who are politically and economically fragile are more prone to a sycophantic stance toward the leader so as to shield their incumbencies. This finding chimes with established scholarship: authoritarian rulers are generally disposed toward rewarding the loyal over merely the competent in matters of promotion. Drawing upon empirical evidence, the study establishes – in various forms of sycophancy – how expressive allegiants survive unreasonably longer in office than their non‑fawning counterparts.

**Distinguishing Elites: The Strong vs. The Weak**

Although all elites must be careful not to criticize the autocrat publicly, the paper argues that certain types of elites are more likely to exhibit the public signals of allegiance commonly labeled sycophancy. For the sake of simplicity, the authors divide elites into two categories: **the strong** and **the weak**. **Strong elites** are typically more independent economically, command ample resources and policy control, and possess greater competence and educational background, which makes them more qualified for their roles. While they are not irreplaceable, they face a lower risk of punishment as long as they perform satisfactorily. **Weak elites**, by contrast, tend to be less competent, less educated, and locked into a more asymmetric power relationship with the ruler. In performing their duties they rely more heavily on support from the autocrat and hence are more susceptible to purges or reassignment.

Social-psychological scholarship and organizational research into **ingratiation** find that sycophantic behavior is indeed more pronounced in highly asymmetrical relations, especially in non-Western, less merit-reliant environments. People deploy sycophancy to evoke in superiors a more positive impression of themselves and to signal that they are **subordinate** individuals who can only thrive under service-styled obedience to the predominant figure.
