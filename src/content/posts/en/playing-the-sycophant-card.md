> This paper examines the incentives and consequences of elites expressing loyalty to the ruler through **sycophantic behaviors** (such as verbal praise or imitating policy themes) under autocratic regimes. Through an analysis of the annual speeches of Russian regional leaders, the authors find that politically and economically vulnerable elites are more inclined to flatter the ruler in order to extend their time in office and secure their political survival.
>
> This research carries important implications for understanding how personality cults develop and how autocrats handle the trade-off between **loyalty** and **competence**.

**Title:** Playing the sycophant card: The logic and consequences of professing loyalty to the autocrat

**Journal:** *American Journal of Political Science*

**Authors:**

- <u>Alexander Baturo</u>: Associate Professor of Government, Dublin City University, Ireland. He works in comparative democratization, political leadership, and post-Soviet politics.
- <u>Nikita Khokhlov</u>: PhD candidate in Politics and International Relations at Dublin City University. He is working on comparative politics and subnational politics of authoritarian regimes. This paper is part of his doctoral dissertation.
- <u>Jakob Tolstrup</u>: Associate Professor, Aarhus University. He is a specialist on authoritarian politics and post-soviet politics (with a special emphasis on Russia, Ukraine, Belarus, Moldova, and Georgia).

## Introduction

#### **The Trade-off Between Loyalty and Competence**

- **The Conflict Between Loyalty and Competence**: Autocrats face a core problem: they need competent elites to govern the state effectively, promote economic growth, and implement policies, but at the same time they fear that these elites will pose a threat to their rule. Owing to their independence and potential influence, highly capable officials may be less dependent on the ruler and therefore more likely to defect or challenge the autocrat's power. Research by Tullock (1987) and Zakharov (2016) shows that it is precisely because of this potential threat that **autocrats often prefer to choose loyal elites** over the most capable officials. Although competence is vital to the functioning of the state, loyalty is more conducive to consolidating the autocrat's rule;
- **The Autocrat's Dilemma**: When an autocrat is surrounded by sycophantic yes-men, the information they receive about the world is reduced, their advisors and officials become less likely to criticize and actively participate in policymaking, and when the ruler is constantly praised, they overestimate their own abilities and the regime's capabilities. As a result, the sycophantic behavior of authoritarian officials leads to policy decisions that are poorly prepared and ill-considered, bringing potentially catastrophic consequences. Many observers point to this yes-man syndrome as one of the reasons behind Russian ruler Vladimir Putin's decision to invade Ukraine in February 2022.

#### **A Review of the Literature on the Loyalty–Competence Balance**

- **The Russian Case**: In studies of Russia, the economic performance of mayors and governors bears no relation to the prospects of their political careers; instead, the key determinant of their careers is whether they can demonstrate loyalty to the regime in national elections (Reuter & Robertson, 2012);
- **The Chinese Case**: Some scholars find that local leaders are appointed based on economic performance (Li & Zhou, 2005), while others argue that only the capable among the loyal candidates are promoted (Jia et al., 2015), or that loyalty matters more at higher administrative levels, whereas at lower levels the demand for competence dominates (Landry, 2008).

#### **Sycophancy as an Elite Strategy**

- **The Vulnerability of Elites under Autocratic Regimes**: Although the autocrat's elites are crucial to the survival of the autocratic regime, individual elites are not irreplaceable. Through **job reassignment** and **great purges**, autocrats can trap their subordinates in an asymmetric power game. To survive in this environment full of uncertainty and the threat of punishment, elites need to adopt different strategies to sustain their political careers. According to Wintrobe's (1998) theory, **sycophantic behavior** — that is, **excessive praise** of and **deference to the autocrat** — is a highly effective strategy. Especially in relationships of asymmetric power, vulnerable elites attempt to avoid punishment or replacement by displaying unconditional loyalty to the ruler. This behavior not only helps allay the ruler's suspicions but also helps these elites keep their posts;
- **The History and Practice of Sycophantic Behavior**: In 1925, in Mussolini's Italy, the then party secretary Farinacci proposed that party members must make a pilgrimage to Mussolini's birthplace; other party members had no choice but to do so and to devise their own forms of flattery so as not to be outdone. Similarly, in the Soviet Union of the 1930s, Kaganovich, one of Stalin's deputies, paid tribute to Stalin at every opportunity and proposed using "Long live Stalinism" instead of "Leninism" in the party's slogans. This phrase soon appeared with increasing frequency in the speeches of other officials, as they too used sycophancy as a survival strategy during Stalin's **unpredictable** reign;
- **Sycophantic Behavior and the Cult of Personality**: When elites mention the autocrat in their statements, defend their policies and actions, and cater to the autocrat, their behavior is, on the whole, related to a broader phenomenon — the **cult of personality**. Svolik (2012) points out that when an autocrat establishes personal rule and places other elites in a subordinate position, a large-scale cult of the leader almost inevitably emerges.

#### **The Gap in Existing Research**

- **The Neglect of Verbal Sycophancy**: Although the loyalty–competence framework occupies a central position in the study of authoritarian politics, scholars have focused only on the material aspects of elites serving the autocrat. Yet non-material aspects, such as **verbal sycophancy toward the autocrat** — a common everyday practice under authoritarianism — have been overlooked;
- **The Neglect of Heterogeneity**: Even though sycophancy is pervasive in autocratic states, not all elites behave alike. How can the variation among elites be explained?
- **The Effect of Verbal Sycophancy**: Does verbal sycophancy work? This too has not received adequate attention.

#### **This Paper's Research Contributions**

- This Paper's Work: Through a textual analysis of nearly 1,000 annual speeches delivered by Russia's regional leaders (such as governors), the paper explores the manifestations and effects of sycophantic behavior. The study finds that sycophancy is not universal among all elites. Politically and economically more vulnerable governors are more likely to display overt sycophancy in their public speeches (such as frequently mentioning Putin's name or imitating Putin's policy themes). Those governors who flatter the ruler remain in their posts for a longer time.
- **Extends the Loyalty–Competence Trade-off Theory**: The paper extends the theory of the loyalty–competence balance, particularly with respect to non-material expressions of loyalty (such as sycophantic behavior) under autocratic regimes. Most existing literature focuses on elites demonstrating loyalty through material means (such as delivering votes or providing economic support), while neglecting non-material modes such as sycophantic behavior. By studying elites' public expressions of excessive praise for the autocrat, this paper fills this gap and reveals the impact of such behavior on elites' political careers. Through textual analysis, the paper demonstrates that sycophantic behavior is just as important as the expression of loyalty, especially among vulnerable elites.
- **Provides a New Perspective on the Survival Strategies of Political Elites**: By analyzing the public speeches of Russian regional leaders (governors), the paper reveals that politically and economically vulnerable elites are more inclined to flatter the leader through their words in order to protect their posts. This finding is consistent with existing research, namely that autocrats are more inclined to reward loyal elites rather than to promote them on the basis of competence alone. Through empirical analysis, the paper shows that elites who express sycophancy actually remain in office longer than those who do not;
- **Advances Insight into How the Cult of Personality Forms**: Although some scholars interpret such cults as a quasi-religious phenomenon, or emphasize explanations rooted in leadership and psychology, the broad consensus is that such cults arise from the **personalization of power**. This paper shows that, while a certain degree of personalism may be an important condition for the formation of a cult of personality, what truly drives the cult is the relationship between the autocrat and those vulnerable elites who are especially susceptible to arbitrary punishment and purges. **When vulnerable groups begin to compete with one another in excessive flattery**, and the ruler systematically rewards them, the cult of personality reinforces itself.

## Research Hypotheses

**Distinguishing Elites: The Strong and the Weak**

While all elites must be careful not to criticize the autocrat publicly, this paper argues that certain types of elites are more likely to display the kind of overt loyalty signals commonly referred to as sycophancy. For the sake of simplicity, the authors divide elites into two types: **the strong** and **the weak**. **Strong elites** are typically more independent economically, possess abundant resources and control over policy, and have greater competence and educational background, so they are more capable of doing the job well. Although such elites are not irreplaceable, as long as they perform well they face a lower risk of punishment. By contrast, **weak elites** tend to be less competent and less educated, and their power relationship with the ruler is more unequal. They depend more on the support provided by the autocrat in carrying out their duties, and are therefore more vulnerable to the threat of purges or reassignment.

Research in **social psychology** and organizational studies on the phenomenon of **ingratiation** shows that sycophantic behavior is indeed more pronounced in highly asymmetric relationships, especially in non-Western environments with less meritocratic elite politics. People use sycophancy to evoke a more positive view of themselves and to show that they are **inferior**, that they can succeed only by playing the role of a submissive **servant**, thereby tying themselves more closely to their superiors.

The main hypotheses of this study are as follows:

**H1: As officials approach the end of their term, they will display stronger sycophancy**

When an official's term approaches its end, they face higher political vulnerability and are therefore more likely to demonstrate loyalty through sycophantic behavior. Because elites in autocratic regimes are easily replaced, especially as their term nears its end, officials become more dependent on the ruler's support to keep their posts. Thus, as their departure draws near, officials are more inclined to express loyalty to the autocrat through their words.

**H2: When an official's region depends on transfer payments from the central government, they will display stronger sycophancy**

Specifically, officials from poorer regions that depend on federal funding are more likely to demonstrate loyalty, because their regions cannot sustain development independently and must rely on the central government's fiscal support. This dependence drives them to win the ruler's trust and continued support through sycophantic behavior.

**H3: Officials with stronger outside options are less likely to flatter**

Sycophancy comes at a cost. Excessive flattery in public may be regarded as politically damaging to strong elites; insincere flattery is usually unpleasant for the individual engaging in it and is despised by observers. More powerful elites may also pay a heavy political price for playing the sycophant card. When they continually praise the incumbent, they become less able to present themselves as independent political actors. Perhaps even more damaging, the excessive use of flattery also cuts off the possibility of joining another elite coalition. On the other hand, personal traits may also affect the degree to which elites turn to flattery. Officials with a business background or other outside career options can survive outside the autocratic system through these channels and are therefore less dependent on flattering the ruler to sustain their political careers. By contrast, officials who have no outside options or who depend on the political system are more inclined to adopt a strategy of flattery in order to keep their positions.

**H4: Sycophantic behavior can extend an official's time in office**

Officials who demonstrate loyalty through flattery stay in office longer, or are more likely to be promoted. This hypothesis is based on the autocrat's tendency to retain officials who display loyalty, especially elites who actively flatter in public.

## Data and Methods

#### **Data Sources**

The authors chose the annual addresses delivered by Russian governors to regional legislatures between 2007 and 2021 as the primary data for analysis. These addresses are similar to the "State of the State" addresses given by U.S. governors, with their main content including a review of the past year's policy implementation, an analysis of current policy issues, and guidance on the direction of future policymaking. These addresses provide a sound data basis for studying how elites demonstrate loyalty through public statements.

The authors collected 924 governor address texts, covering numerous regions over the period 2007 to 2021. These addresses were mainly obtained through the websites of regional governments and legislatures, with some video speeches transcribed into text. In addition, the authors obtained, through local contacts, some addresses that were not accessible online from local newspapers or archives.

On average there were 62 addresses per year; annual addresses became more common especially after 2010, mainly because of a law requiring governors to report annually to regional legislatures. As a result, from 2010 onward the coverage of the dataset increased significantly, reaching 82% of the federal subjects of Russia.

#### **Sycophancy Indicators**

To measure governors' tendency to demonstrate loyalty in their addresses, the authors propose two main quantitative indicators:

- **Number of mentions of Putin**: This indicator assesses sycophantic behavior by counting the frequency with which a governor mentions "Putin" or his titles (such as "President" or "Prime Minister") in a speech. This method directly quantifies the number of times a governor publicly praises Putin, thereby measuring their demonstration of loyalty to the ruler;
- **Degree of imitation of Putin's policy themes**: To capture governors' verbal convergence of opinion, the authors construct an indicator to assess the degree of overlap between the themes in a governor's speech and the policy themes in Putin's speeches. The authors use a word-embedding-based technique called **Latent Semantic Scaling (LSS)** for this purpose; this method can classify texts according to predefined dimensions and compute thematic similarity through semantic similarity.

These two methods respectively measure the degree of a governor's direct verbal flattery and indirect flattery (expressing loyalty by imitating policy themes).

#### **Main Explanatory Variables**

- **Days to term-end**: that is, the number of days between the governor's speech and the end of their term;
- **Amount of federal transfer payments**: this indicator measures the economic dependence of the governor's region; the more a region depends on the central government's fiscal support, the more likely the governor is to display sycophantic behavior.
- **The governor's background**: governors with a business background, or governors who have served in security agencies (such as the KGB), may have more outside career options and are therefore less dependent on sycophantic behavior.

This part employs OLS regression.

#### **Analysis of the Consequences of Sycophantic Behavior**

To test whether sycophantic behavior helps extend a governor's time in office, the authors use a **survival analysis** model to analyze the relationship between sycophantic behavior and a governor's time in office.

## Results

#### **Factors Influencing Sycophantic Behavior**

- **Political Vulnerability**: By calculating the number of days between the governor's speech and the end of their term (i.e., "Days to term-end"), the results show that as a governor's term nears its end, the number of times they mention Putin in their speech increases significantly, indicating that they rely more on publicly expressing loyalty to seek the possibility of re-election or reappointment;
- **Economic Vulnerability**: By using the amount of federal transfer payments as an indicator of economic vulnerability, the authors find that governors from poorer regions that depend on the central government's fiscal support are indeed more inclined to mention Putin directly in their speeches, and their policy themes are more aligned with Putin's themes;
- **Personal Background**: Governors with a business background display less sycophantic behavior in their speeches (whether directly citing Putin or imitating policy themes), which confirms the hypothesis. By contrast, although governors with a security agency background have certain outside career options, their sycophantic behavior did not decrease significantly.

#### **Is Sycophantic Behavior Effective?**

The authors used a Cox proportional hazards model to analyze the relationship between governors' expression of loyalty to Putin (by mentioning Putin's name or imitating Putin's policy themes) and their time in office.

The results show that **frequently mentioning Putin's name alone does not significantly extend a governor's time in office**. Although politically weaker governors do tend to mention Putin frequently in their speeches, this strategy alone is not enough to significantly increase their chances of staying in office. However, **when a governor adopts a strategy in their speech that is highly aligned with Putin's policy themes, sycophantic behavior can bring positive political outcomes**. This means that if a governor expresses loyalty by imitating Putin's policy themes, rather than merely mentioning his name, sycophantic behavior can indeed help them maintain their political position.

## Conclusion and Discussion

- **The Importance of Sycophantic Behavior in Modern Autocratic Regimes**: Although in some modern autocratic regimes strong personality cults and displays of excessive loyalty are not as evident as in the past (for example, during the Stalin era), sycophantic behavior remains pervasive in Putin's Russia;
- **The Heterogeneity of Sycophantic Behavior**: There are significant differences in sycophantic behavior across different elite groups. Elites who are politically and economically vulnerable and who lack alternative career options are more inclined to adopt a strategy of flattery as a means of political survival. Meanwhile, elites who are less dependent on the ruler's favor and who have more outside options generally behave in a more dignified manner. This heterogeneity suggests that the formation of a cult of personality is very likely the result of vulnerable elites continually escalating their loyalty signals in order to outdo their rivals in political competition;
- **The Formation of the Cult of Personality**: The emergence and reinforcement of a cult of personality often result from vulnerable elites competing in flattery to survive. When the ruler continually rewards such behavior, the cult of personality reinforces itself. Especially against the backdrop of the Putin regime's increasing repression and its emphasis on regime unity, sycophantic behavior becomes a survival strategy for more and more elites;
- **Directions for Future Research**: The authors call for future research to further explore the costs and benefits of sycophantic behavior. For example, sycophantic behavior can not only help elites avoid negative sanctions but may also bring more aid and privileges; these benefits help elites control the situation locally, pay off local elites and repressive agents, and quell local public discontent. In addition, research could further explore how elites respond to the sycophantic behavior of their peers, and whether sycophantic behavior spreads in a cascade effect.

## Extra

- This paper does not seem to address the problem of reverse causality very well. For instance, more flattery may be feedback and gratitude in response to identification with Putin;
- The significance levels in the paper's baseline regressions are not high, and the results for many indicators are not robust;
