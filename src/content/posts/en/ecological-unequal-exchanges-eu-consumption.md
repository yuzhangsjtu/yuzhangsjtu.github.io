### Abstract

In a globalised economy, the consumption of goods and services drives economic growth while also generating environmental pressures and impacts across the world. Currently, the European Union (EU) is one of the wealthiest economies in the world, and all of its 27 member states maintain a high level of consumption. This study assesses the geographic distribution and changes of the environmental pressures and impacts generated by EU consumption between 1995 and 2019.

This paper mainly employs the environmentally extended multiregional input-output (EEMRIO) approach and selects ten consumption footprint indicators, including: <mark>biodiversity loss from land use</mark>, greenhouse gas emissions, blue water consumption, land use, material consumption, photochemical oxidation, freshwater aquatic ecotoxicity, terrestrial ecotoxicity, marine ecotoxicity, and particulate matter formation. In terms of data, this study uses the EXIOBASE v.3.8.1 database, which includes annual input-output tables of 200 product sectors across 49 countries and regions.

The findings show that a large share of the environmental pressures and impacts analysed in this paper are displaced to countries and regions outside the EU, while more than 85% of the economic benefits remain within the member states. Even within the EU, the distribution of costs and benefits is unequal. Over the analysis period, the environmental pressures and impacts driven by EU consumption largely decreased within the EU but increased beyond its borders - for example, the EU's Eastern European neighbours bear the highest environmental pressures and impacts per unit of GDP associated with EU consumption.

### Introduction

The consumption of goods and services drives global economic growth, but it also causes various environmental pressures. More importantly, the consumption of goods and services and its associated environmental pressures are **not evenly distributed** across the globe! Consumption takes place in affluent places, such as most of Europe, but the environmental burden is given to other places. Clarifying the environmental pressures that these affluent places impose on others is crucial!

Selection of environment-related indicators:

- Four indicators recommended by the European Commission: greenhouse gas emissions, material consumption, land use, and surface or groundwater (or blue water) consumption
- Important indicators in the EXIOBASE database (based on relevant literature): particulate matter formation, freshwater aquatic ecotoxicity, marine ecotoxicity, terrestrial ecotoxicity, photochemical oxidation, and damage to ecosystem quality from land occupation

Consumption creates GDP (or value added). **Depending on their position in the supply chain, some countries gain a great deal of GDP but bear almost none of the environmental pressures and impacts of consumption**, while others face exactly the opposite! We can compare which regions benefit and which suffer the greatest losses!

#### The "Environmental-to-Value-Added Ratios" Most Unfavourable to the EU's Neighbouring Countries

EV-ratios, that is, the ratio of environmental pressure to value added - the emissions-to-value-added ratio.

To further understand the impact of EU consumption on different countries and regions, **we quantify the economic benefits as the value added, or GDP, associated with EU consumption both within and outside the EU, and compare them with the associated environmental pressures and impacts**. Except for the 2008 financial crisis, the value added associated with production for EU consumption increased from EUR 6.2 trillion in 1995 to EUR 13.4 trillion in 2019. Over the analysis period, 86-91% of the value added stayed within the EU. Within the EU, consumption related to services and to shelter contributed more than 43% and 23% respectively to the value added of EU consumption. By contrast, service consumption, manufactured products and mobility are the main contributors to the value added of EU consumption outside the EU.

At the national and regional level, the most populous EU member states - Germany, France, Italy and Spain - also have the highest shares of value added. As shown in the figure below, together they captured more than 60% of the value added of EU consumption in the late 1990s, and still captured 56.3% in 2019. Meanwhile, outside the EU, the United States (1.9-3.3%), the United Kingdom (1.1-1.7%) and China (0.1-1.7%) were the countries with the highest shares of the value added of EU consumption.

**Figure 4: Relative distribution of value added associated with EU27 consumption in 2019**

![Fig. 4](https://media.springernature.com/full/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1038%2Fs41893-022-01055-8/MediaObjects/41893_2022_1055_Fig4_HTML.png)

To compare the shares of value added and of environmental pressures and impacts caused by EU consumption, we use the emissions-to-value-added ratio (EV-ratio) (this indicator references [another paper](https://academic.oup.com/sf/article/93/1/405/2337929) by Professor Feng Kuishuang), by **dividing the share of global pressure or impact by a region's or a country's share of global value added**. A value <1 indicates that the share of economic benefits is higher than the share of environmental pressure or impact that a country bears for its contribution to global value chains, whereas a value >1 indicates that a country receives a share of environmental damage greater than its share of economic benefits.

From 1995 to 2019, the combined EV-ratio across all 10 analysed indicators for the EU was <1 every year. Conversely, when all countries and regions outside the EU are aggregated into RoW, its EV-ratio is consistently >1.

### Discussion

EU consumption is associated with the displacement of large-scale environmental pressures and impacts beyond its borders. For seven of the analysed pressures and impacts (all ecotoxicity indicators, greenhouse gas emissions, particulate matter formation, photochemical oxidation and material consumption), regions outside the EU increased significantly, while inside the EU they decreased. The outsourcing of environmental pressures and impacts from wealthy regions to poorer regions is clearly visible on a global scale today, because the pressures and impacts embodied in trade are increasing.

At the same time, recent research has confirmed a large-scale net flow of resources embodied in trade from poorer countries to richer countries - the so-called ecologically unequal exchange. The analysis of the EV-ratios associated with EU consumption adds to the discussion of ecologically unequal exchange, because it highlights the **unfavourable situation of many non-EU countries**.

The results show that the EV-ratios associated with EU consumption are more favourable for most member states than for non-EU countries and regions. At the same time, EU consumption brings higher environmental pressures and impacts per value added to the RoW Europe region than to anywhere else in the world. This region includes existing EU candidate members such as Albania, Montenegro or Serbia, as well as the EU's new candidate countries such as Ukraine and Moldova (current EU candidate members).

To avoid further adverse effects on human health and ecosystems within and outside the EU, it is crucial to reduce the environmental pressures and impacts associated with EU consumption. This can be achieved through a variety of mechanisms. Consumption choices, such as the mode and frequency of travel or dietary choices, can greatly reduce environmental pressures and impacts. Because many super-rich consumers, who currently contribute disproportionately to global environmental destruction and resource use, live within the EU, mitigation efforts need to focus on affluence and overconsumption. In addition, **the EU's new trade policies need to take into account the environmental pressures and impacts embodied in products and services, in order to prevent additional spillover effects outside the EU**. Policies to reduce spillover effects, such as policies on greenhouse gas emissions, need to take into account inequality in the process, in order to deliver fair outcomes for all. ##### Analysis of Unequal Impacts

We use the environmental pressures and impacts calculated in the previous step to analyse the distribution of the pressures and impacts associated with EU consumption across countries and regions. To analyse the impacts and pressures associated with the consumption of different products and services, we use different final demand vectors $\mathbf f^{s,\tau}$. For all 27 EU member states, which correspond to regions in the EEMRIO model, we run the model with a set of seven different final demand categories τ, aggregated into total final demand and into the final demand of selected products and sectors, which are then aggregated into six previously used [38](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41893-022-01055-8#ref-CR38) consumption categories: food, shelter, clothing, services, manufactured products and mobility. Supplementary Table 2 shows the allocation of sectors to consumption categories.

![image-20231115173228390](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2022/image-20231115173228390.png)

In addition, we also compared the distribution of the environmental pressures and impacts associated with EU consumption with the distribution of the associated value added worldwide. First, we determine the country or regional share of each pressure or impact caused by EU consumption. Next, we calculate the modelled share of value added in the supply chain. Finally, we use the EV-ratio introduced by ref. [23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41893-022-01055-8#ref-CR23), which has also been used under different names in other studies [22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41893-022-01055-8#ref-CR22),[24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41893-022-01055-8#ref-CR24). This indicator can be used to determine whether EU consumption has a favourable or unfavourable effect on a country or region, by dividing the share of environmental pressure or impact experienced by that country by its share of value added. A value >1 indicates that a country or region bears a share of pressure or impact higher than its share of value added associated with EU consumption (unfavourable), whereas a value <1 indicates that the country or region holds a share of value added higher than that of environmental impact or pressure (favourable). Conversely, a value of 1 indicates that a country or region is experiencing the same share of pressure or impact and of value added associated with EU consumption.

#### Data

The EXIOBASE v.3.8.1 database, 1995-2019, includes annual input-output tables of 200 product sectors across 49 countries and regions, covering all 27 EU member states. In addition, the database contains more than 1,000 individual socioeconomic and environmental pressure indicators as well as 126 impact categories. We used all **seven final demand categories** in EXIOBASE (household final consumption expenditure; final consumption expenditure of non-profit institutions serving households; government final consumption expenditure; gross fixed capital formation; changes in inventories; changes in valuables; exports: total free on board value) to calculate the environmental pressures and impacts of the 27 EU member states. The specific pressure and impact coefficients we used for the analysis are summarised in Supplementary Table 1. To parse the raw EXIOBASE v.3.8.1 data, we followed the methodology of ref. 51. We chose EXIOBASE v.3.8.1 over other MRIOs (such as GTAP) because of its large number of individual product sectors, the number of available pressure and impact coefficients, and its complete coverage of the 27 EU member states. The large-scale aggregation of countries into RoW regions, especially in Africa, Asia and South America, as well as Eastern Europe, is an obvious limitation of using the EXIOBASE v.3.8.1 database to show where EU consumption causes environmental pressures and impacts and the generation of value added. Therefore, we can show which countries and regions benefit from EU consumption and which regions experience disproportionately large environmental pressures and impacts. However, we **cannot determine whether specific social groups, such as high-income groups, gain high profits from the value added associated with EU consumption while others gain only minimal benefits**. In addition, some of the nine environmental pressures considered may have effects beyond the country or regional boundaries set by EXIOBASE. Therefore, additional modelling steps are needed to track the effects of environmental pressures, such as the health effects of atmospheric emissions after transport, or the effects of water consumption in downstream ecosystems, which are necessary for a comprehensive analysis of the effects on human health and ecosystems associated with EU consumption.

The indicator used to quantify greenhouse gas emissions in the supply chain is an aggregated indicator from the EXIOBASE database v.3.8.1, which covers multiple greenhouse gases (CO2, CH4, N2O, SF6, HFC and PFC) and is expressed in kgCO2e (carbon dioxide equivalent), based on their global warming potential over a 100-year time horizon, as recommended by the IPCC reports. It is worth noting that the greenhouse gas emissions in the EXIOBASE dataset include emissions from combustion and non-combustion processes, agriculture and waste treatment, but exclude emissions from land use, land-use change and forestry. To calculate the material use associated with EU consumption, the material use coefficients of several categories (including domestic extraction and use of crops and crop residues, grazing and fodder, forestry and timber, etc.) were aggregated into an indicator of total material use (in kilotonnes). Land use was initially calculated for 20 different land occupation categories (cropland, forest land, infrastructure land and other land use), and subsequently summed to determine the total land use associated with EU consumption (in square kilometres). The water consumption caused by EU consumption is calculated by aggregating the total blue water consumption coefficients of different economic sectors, such as agriculture, manufacturing or electricity. Freshwater aquatic, marine aquatic and terrestrial ecotoxicity are quantified through EXIOBASE's aggregated indicators and a problem-oriented method, which weighs the freshwater aquatic ecotoxicity potential, marine aquatic ecotoxicity potential and terrestrial ecotoxicity potential arising from the emission of each toxic substance to air, water and/or soil, measured in kilograms of 1,4-dichlorobenzene equivalent. The choice of infinite impacts is based on the recommendation of the International Reference Life Cycle Data System. To account for the photochemical oxidation associated with EU consumption, which is caused by the emission of substances such as non-methane volatile organic compounds or carbon monoxide, we used an aggregated and weighted indicator measuring the kilograms of ozone formed. As recommended in ref. 16, we chose the indicator using the maximum incremental reactivity method. Finally, particulate matter emissions are determined through emission-weighted coefficients in kilograms of PM2.5 equivalent. The value added in the supply chain associated with EU consumption is determined through value-added coefficients (in millions of euros) obtained from EXIOBASE v.3.8.1. More details on the selected indicators can be found in Supplementary Table 1.

In addition, we used the **land-occupation-specific biodiversity loss coefficients** developed specifically for the 49 EXIOBASE regions in ref. 12 to calculate biodiversity loss by measuring the **Potential Disappearing Fraction (PDF) of species**. These coefficients are calculated using the LC-IMPACT life cycle impact assessment model, which provides land-occupation-specific PDF coefficients for 804 different ecoregions and is based on previous research. We chose the PDF in this study to quantify the global impact of land use associated with EU consumption, because it can identify potential global extinctions and takes into account species-specific habitat characteristics and threats. However, this indicator does not take into account changes in local relative species abundance, which could be quantified by using additional, complementary indicators. For the multivariate regression analysis, the average purchasing power parity 2017 USD GDPpc values for all 49 EXIOBASE regions were calculated using the World Development Indicators data bank. If a country had no data, data were added from the World Economic Outlook (October 2022). The population data used to calculate the average values of the aggregated RoW regions were taken from the UN World Population Prospects 2022. #### Limitation

This study has several limitations. First, since 2011, the results for some pressures and impacts (for example, water consumption) are based on a now-casting routine, which extrapolates the relationship between environmental use and economic activity forward. To verify these now-cast pressures and impacts, we examined the coefficients in EXIOBASE v.3.8.1, because the economic data are updated all the way to 2019. In the years with non-now-cast data (before 2011), the country-sector pair coefficients of the environmental indicators used in this study did not change significantly, reflecting the gradual improvement of efficiency over the years. Similar trends are projected in the now-casting routine for the following years, up to 2019. In addition, updating the indicator system for all indicators and countries would require a great deal of additional work, involving thousands of coefficients. Updating these global databases is indeed a common problem. Considering that the economic data are in fact up to date, and that the environmental coefficients change little over time, we consider it reasonable to use the now-cast values provided by EXIOBASE. (Indeed, haha, a lot of the data in EXIOBASE after 2011 are fitted values.)

Second, the selection of these indicators is based on the analysis of ref. 16, which only targets the base year 2011, rather than covering the entire time frame of this study. We do not expect large changes compared with the base year used in the analysis, because ref. 16 analyses the variability among environmental indicators, and this variability is not expected to change significantly over time. The dynamics we show are mainly due to structural changes in trade structure, economic structure and consumption patterns, rather than to environmental coefficients changing over time.

Third, according to EXIOBASE, the data are obtained through a problem-oriented method. The ecotoxicity indicators used in this paper reflect the freshwater aquatic ecotoxicity potential per unit of toxic substance emitted to air, water and/or soil (measured in 1,4-dichlorobenzene equivalent per kilogram emitted), the marine aquatic ecotoxicity potential per unit of toxic substance emitted to air, water and/or soil (measured in 1,4-dichlorobenzene equivalent per kilogram emitted), and the terrestrial ecotoxicity potential per unit of toxic substance emitted to air, water and/or soil (measured in 1,4-dichlorobenzene equivalent per kilogram emitted). Although in comparison **the ecotoxicity results of EXIOBASE may be far smaller than life cycle assessment results**, for the sake of consistency we use EXIOBASE's indicators throughout the study.
