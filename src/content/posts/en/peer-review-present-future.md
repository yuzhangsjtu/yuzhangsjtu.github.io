> This article is a comprehensive outlook on the peer review system in scientific publishing. The authors begin by posing a set of core questions: **What is wrong with the current peer review system? Is it sustainable, and is it useful? Do other viable models exist?** Centered on these questions, they critically discuss the drawbacks of the current review mechanisms and explore alternative models and possible paths for reform. The research aims to **delineate the principal controversies facing peer review and propose potential improvements, so as to stimulate a deeper conversation among research stakeholders (including both producers and users of research outputs)**. The authors emphasize that the problems they list and the solutions they discuss are by no means exhaustive, but they hope this perspective piece will prompt the academic community to earnestly re-examine the current state of peer review and the directions for its future improvement. Overall, the paper aims to provide a holistic analysis of peer review as a scientific evaluation mechanism, to clarify its core challenges and reform objectives, and thereby to lay a groundwork for enhancing the fairness and effectiveness of scientific research.

**Title:** The present and future of peer review: Ideas, interventions, and evidence

**Journal:** *PNAS*

- <u>Balazs Aczel</u>: Professor and vice-dean for science at ELTE University, Budapest, Hungary. Aczel is the first and corresponding author of this paper. The paper has many authors, who appear to be listed in alphabetical order by surname.

**Published online:** 27 January 2025

<br/>

## Major Problems and Challenges

The authors summarize the numerous problems and challenges in the current peer review system, which are seen as eroding its fairness, effectiveness, and credibility:

#### **Insufficient Review Quality and Rigor**

Peer review is supposed to safeguard scientific integrity and correct errors, but in reality its quality is widely questioned. Many reviews fail to adequately identify problems in papers, allowing erroneous results and even academic misconduct to go undetected. For instance, one study found that about 18% of the statistical results reported in psychology journals were incorrect, and one in every eight of these papers had an error that affected the conclusions. A large body of research also points out that the methods and results in papers are frequently reported inadequately, making the data and conclusions difficult to reproduce. These review quality problems directly contribute to a **research reliability crisis**: significant errors go undetected before publication, and the proportion of papers retracted after publication is rising. The authors note that as the volume of scientific publications surges and research grows ever more specialized, high-quality review becomes more difficult: it is hard to find, for each manuscript, a reviewer well-versed in every aspect of the content. At the same time, the academic community has no consensus on what functions a "high-quality review" serves and lacks uniform standards, which makes measuring and improving review quality complex. On the whole, **the failure of review to effectively gatekeep scientific rigor** is the foremost current problem.

#### **Bias and Injustice**

Ideally, peer review should be fair and objective, but the authors enumerate how **personal and systemic biases** threaten review fairness. Some reviewers may hold preferences or preconceptions toward certain authors, institutions, research topics, or conclusions, leading to review outcomes that are skewed by factors other than the quality of the research itself. For example, in reviewing abstracts for medical conferences, single-blind review, compared with double-blind review, favors submissions from prestigious institutions and from authors in English-speaking countries; research in computer science also found that reviewers who know an author's name and affiliation are more inclined to accept papers from elite institutions or well-known scholars. This **"celebrity effect,"** or biases based on geography or gender, can cause the research of certain groups to be systematically undervalued. Although different studies have reached differing conclusions about biases related to social identities such as gender (some find that women authors fare better under signed review, others find no significant gender bias), on the whole the fairness of peer review is easily eroded by bias. This is reflected not only in individual reviewers but can also accumulate at the systemic level — for example, homogeneity between reviewers and authors in gender, academic faction, or nationality may produce favorable evaluations. The problem of bias makes it hard for review to be truly objective and fair.

#### **Low Consistency and Reliability**

The authors emphasize that peer review outcomes carry a considerable degree of randomness. Different reviewers often give markedly different evaluations of the same manuscript, that is, **poor review consistency**. A meta-analysis covering 45 studies found that the correlation between the scores two reviewers assign to the same manuscript averages only 0.34. Similar findings exist in the review of research funding: different experts frequently disagree in their assessment of the same grant application. This **lack of reliability** means that a manuscript's fate carries an element of chance — major findings may be rejected by top journals only to later prove extremely valuable, while mediocre or even problematic papers sometimes slip through and get published. The authors further point out that disagreement among reviewers **is not always constructive.** If the review stage gets too caught up in such disagreements, it may not truly advance science but instead lead authors to over-revise their work in order to accommodate the opinions of a few reviewers, thus **catering to a niche rather than to the expectations of the field as a whole**. Therefore, the **low reliability** of current peer review casts doubt on its ability to ensure publication quality.

#### **Inefficiency and Lag**

In modern research, information spreads extremely fast, yet the traditional peer review process appears slow and drawn-out. A paper typically takes months to go from submission to an initial review result, and multiple rounds of revision and resubmission can stretch to years. Compared with the accelerating pace of scientific discovery and the possibility that preprints offer for making findings immediately available to readers, the lengthy review cycle is increasingly out of step with the times. The authors note that in commercial, policy, or clinical decision-making situations that require swift reliance on scientific progress, an overly long publication cycle appears all the more unable to meet the need. Beyond the time cost, the review process has also been described as **"slow, inefficient, costly, arbitrary, and full of bias."** As a result, **low review efficiency** has become a widely criticized problem in academia, delaying the dissemination and application of scientific knowledge.

#### **Reviewer Misconduct and Motivation Problems**

**Misconduct** by some reviewers further damages the fairness and credibility of review. The paper points out that some reviewers exploit the power of anonymity for personal gain, for example by **obstructing the publication of papers that conflict with their own research**, or by **using the opportunity of reviewing to steal original ideas from unpublished manuscripts** for their own research. Such behavior violates academic ethics and undermines the integrity that peer review is supposed to uphold. In addition, reviewers commonly face the problem of **insufficient motivation**: review work often lacks clear rewards, has little to do with career advancement, and relies entirely on scholars' sense of academic responsibility. As the volume of manuscripts requiring review surges while the number of qualified reviewers is relatively shrinking, many reviewers feel overburdened or even experience **"reviewer fatigue."** Inexperienced reviewers may be unable to offer valuable comments, while true experts often lack the time to devote to careful review. The **shortage of reviewers and the absence of incentives** lead to declining review quality and worsening delays, forming a vicious cycle.

#### **The Impact of Predatory Journals**

The phenomenon of **predatory journals** in the academic ecosystem adds insult to injury for the review problem. These journals charge publication fees and publish papers with virtually no rigorous peer review, placing commercial interests above academic quality. It is estimated that by 2014 there were about 8,000 predatory journals, which together had published nearly 500,000 papers. Some authors, lacking awareness of journal reputation or driven by pressure for promotion, choose to publish their work in such journals. Even more strikingly, some scholars in less-developed countries worry that mainstream Western journals harbor geographic bias and therefore actively turn to these low-threshold journals instead. The prevalence of predatory journals **lowers the overall credibility of the literature**: because their profit is directly tied to publication volume, serious peer review would only reduce their revenue, so these journals have no incentive to improve review quality. They are flooded with papers that have not been rigorously vetted, **greatly lowering the signal-to-noise ratio of the academic literature**. The increase of such poor-quality publications makes it harder for researchers to distinguish high-quality research and erodes the value of legitimate peer review.

In sum, the main challenges currently facing peer review include: **poor review quality, widespread bias, low reliability of outcomes, inefficiency, insufficient reviewer motivation and capacity, and interference from external bad publishing practices**. These problems are intertwined and have severely shaken the research community's confidence in the peer review system. As the authors put it, the scientific community widely believes that the review system is in a state of "crisis" and needs earnest reflection and reform. Addressing these challenges to restore the fairness and effectiveness of review becomes the focus of the paper's subsequent discussion.

## Proposed Solutions

In response to the above problems, the authors propose a range of reform ideas and potential solutions in the text, assessing the strengths and weaknesses of each measure:

#### **Increasing the Openness and Transparency of the Review Process**

To strengthen the credibility of peer review, the authors advocate increasing the transparency of the review process. Specifically:

- The first is **transparent review**, that is, publishing all materials from the review process when the paper is published, such as the editor's decision letter, reviewers' comments, and the authors' responses. Some journals have already begun to try this: for example, *Meta-Psychology* makes the entire review process public from the moment of submission, while *Collabra: Psychology* makes the review record public after the paper is accepted. Open review lets readers understand the process by which a paper was revised and improved, and turns review comments into citable publications, thereby **granting reviewers recognition for their scholarly contribution**.
- The second is to promote **signed review**, that is, disclosing the reviewer's identity to the author. Signing can be mandated by the journal (as practiced by *F1000Research*) or done voluntarily by the reviewer. The authors argue that signed review may prompt reviewers to write comments more responsibly and avoid inappropriate remarks, and may help reveal potential conflicts of interest. When signed review is combined with open review records, the academic community can also use the public data to conduct meta-research, analyzing review networks and dynamics in depth (for example, whether early-career scholars tend to write more critical or more positive comments, and how the review burden is distributed across regions or seniority levels). However, the authors also point out the **pros and cons of signed review**: disclosing one's identity may make reviewers reluctant to raise sharp criticisms of well-known authors, or may make them decline review requests for fear of retaliation, thereby reducing the candor of review comments. Junior reviewers or those in vulnerable positions in particular are more likely, once their names are known, to be unwilling to openly criticize authorities out of concern for future interpersonal effects. Mandatory signing may also lead reviewers to only sign positive evaluations in order to please authors. Therefore, the authors take a cautiously optimistic attitude toward open review, holding that **improving transparency requires balancing review honesty against accountability**.

#### **Enhancing the Reliability and Rigor of Review**

To improve the consistency and effectiveness of review, the authors propose several measures:

- For example, **reviewer training** is considered one path to improving review quality. At present there are very few formal reviewer-training resources, and many new reviewers learn mainly through informal guidance from their mentors. Although a considerable share of scientists say they are willing to receive training, the limited research available suggests that training improves review quality very little. The authors mention an experiment finding that even explicitly informing reviewers of a journal's expectations only slightly improved review quality. Training is therefore no panacea, and large-scale implementation in particular poses challenges (unless concentrated training is conducted for a small group of specialized reviewers).
- Another measure is **increasing the number of reviewers per manuscript** to smooth out individual differences. If more reviewers are invited for a manuscript and their opinions are aggregated, overall reliability might improve. However, this is costly in practice, because editors already struggle to find enough reviewers to complete reviews on time. Moreover, involving more people may produce even more divergent opinions, making it harder rather than easier to reach consensus.
- Third, introducing standardized tools such as a **reproducibility checklist** has also been tried. Some journals and top AI conferences require authors to submit checklists on experimental design, evaluation of statistical models, and so on, in order to encourage reproducibility. Preliminary evidence suggests that checklists help promote the open sharing of code and data, but their effect on improving review quality remains inconclusive. Some computer science conferences (such as IEEE Supercomputing) even organize volunteers to verify the reproducibility of papers and award badges, but this practice does not necessarily work in other fields. A randomized experiment in medicine found that reminding reviewers to use reporting guidelines and checklists did not noticeably improve review quality. On the whole, measures to improve review reliability need more empirical testing, but the authors affirm reviewer training, expanding the sample, and using checklists as means worth exploring.

#### **Leveraging Preprint Platforms and Public Review**

To address the problems of slow review and delayed publication, the authors discuss the advantages of the **preprint model**. Preprint servers let authors make their research public immediately before formal publication; arXiv in the physical sciences has already collected more than 2 million papers, and **SSRN**, EconStor, and others in the social sciences and economics are also very active. In the life sciences, the COVID-19 pandemic drove the growth of medRxiv and bioRxiv. The greatest advantage of preprints is the **rapid dissemination of findings**: scholars can obtain information without waiting for lengthy journal review. For example, the economics community has long been accustomed to citing working papers not yet formally published, in order to bypass the long journal cycle. Preprints also help **reduce publication bias**, because research is no longer buried for having non-significant or unpopular results — at the very least it can be made public.

However, the authors point out that these benefits are not automatic: many preprints do not actually receive feedback or broad attention. The challenge therefore lies in how to **draw enough qualified reviewers to review preprints and offer meaningful suggestions for improvement**. The authors propose that AI technology might be used to help scale up: for example, developing automated tools to perform code checks, statistical tests, or grammar proofreading on preprints, thereby assisting human experts with the heavy work of screening. But they also warn that **the accuracy and usefulness of AI review need careful validation** and cannot do without the guidance of human experts. The rise of preprints also raises some concerns: because a preprint has not undergone formal peer review when it goes online, it may lack the necessary quality gatekeeping. In extreme cases, **error-ridden research or even malicious disinformation** could spread via preprints and mislead the public, especially in sensitive areas such as public health. Some therefore argue that preprint platforms should strengthen content moderation, but this in turn runs counter to the original purpose of preprints — rapid sharing — and also requires substantial resources. The authors believe that current judgments about the pros and cons of preprints may all be exaggerated: empirical research shows that the content of a paper usually differs very little between the preprint stage and after formal publication. This hints that **the influence of peer review on the quality of a paper's final version may be limited**. To combine the advantages of preprints with review quality, the article introduces innovative initiatives such as *Peer Community In* (PCI). PCI is organized by research communities outside the journal system to review and recommend preprints; more than 130 journals already recognize PCI reviews and accept papers it recommends, usually without further review. This model embodies the idea of review being led autonomously by the academic community, avoids the time spent shuttling manuscripts between different journals, and promises to save time and improve efficiency.

#### **Improving Reviewer Incentives and Recognition**

Given the current shortage of reviewers and their lack of motivation, the authors suggest adjusting the incentive mechanism to attract more high-quality reviewing.

- First, **material rewards** are one option. Some argue that large commercial publishers reap considerable profits yet rely on scholars' free labor to complete reviews, and ought to set aside part of their revenue to pay reviewers. However, many academic journals — especially non-profit ones or those with limited resources — have no budget to pay reviewers meaningful compensation, and even when they do it is only symbolic, hard to truly change reviewer behavior. Paying alone is therefore not a universal solution.
- Second, more realistic and effective are **reputational incentives and making review work visible**. A growing number of journals are beginning to **publish review comments**, turning reviewers' efforts into citable scholarly outputs. For example, journals such as *eLife* and *BMJ*, as well as conferences like NeurIPS and ICLR, have all implemented systems for making review comments public. Open review benefits not only readers but also gives reviewers the academic recognition they deserve. In addition, platforms like Publons can record an individual's review history and generate review records that employers can consult, so that reviewers can count review work as a scholarly contribution. Some services (such as Reviewer Credits) even offer concrete returns, such as statistical-consulting or language-editing vouchers, to reward active reviewers. Through these measures, **the academic value of review work is formally recognized**, thereby increasing scholars' willingness to participate in reviewing. The authors note that publishing review comments has an additional benefit: it helps various parties compare the review quality of different journals and identify more constructive review practices. Of course, open and non-anonymous review may make reviewers inclined to be milder and reduce sharp criticism, but there is as yet no sufficient empirical evidence for this. On the whole, **establishing a system of incentives and recognition for review work** is seen as an important step toward alleviating the reviewer shortage and improving review quality.

#### **Broadening the Reviewer Pool and Recruitment Methods**

To address the shortage of reviewer resources, the authors suggest expanding and diversifying the reviewer pool at the source. They point out that the **global review burden is currently distributed unevenly**: some countries (especially many low- and middle-income countries) account for a far higher share of submissions than of the reviewing they shoulder. This suggests that there are still many qualified but under-mobilized potential reviewers. What needs to improve is how to discover these candidates and motivate them to participate in review. Traditional review invitations are mainly made by editors contacting reviewers directly by email, but **more open recruitment models** have now emerged. For example, the "crowdsourcing"-style platform PREreview recruits volunteers to review publicly available preprint manuscripts, matching review demand with potential reviewers. Or again, the aforementioned journal *Meta-Psychology* will, when a paper enters review, issue a public announcement and invite any interested researcher to comment on the corresponding preprint. These initiatives are currently small in scale, but most are new things that have arisen only in the past decade and may well continue to grow in the future. The authors candidly admit that whether such reforms can succeed in the long run remains to be seen, but they consider them important attempts to improve the system. Besides human effort, **technological means can also assist in selecting reviewers**. Several manuscript-management systems now offer automatic reviewer-recommendation functions, using algorithms such as machine learning to screen suitable experts from a database. This holds promise for expanding the pool of review candidates beyond an editor's personal network and reducing the need for repeated invitations. Although some models are claimed to outperform traditional manual selection, the authors caution that **the effectiveness and relevance of algorithmic recommendation** still need to be validated.

Finally, the authors put forward an innovative idea: **refining the division of review labor**. As disciplines become highly specialized, requiring every reviewer to evaluate all aspects of a paper becomes impractical and burdensome. It may be better to let reviewers **each play to their strengths**, evaluating only the parts of a manuscript related to their expertise. This is especially applicable in fields with clear reporting standards or checklists, where experts from different fields can each check the compliance of different dimensions of the paper. Through **"multiple people cooperating, each reviewing their own part,"** one can both raise the review response rate and improve the professional depth of review comments.

#### **Reforming the Editorial Decision Process**

The authors argue that, beyond reviewers, **editors also play a key role in the review system**, and their decision-making methods likewise need to be improved and made transparent. They discuss the issue of **transparency in the editorial process**: for example, when deciding whether to accept or reject, should an editor consider only the technical reliability of a paper, or also its importance and the journal's positioning? At present the editorial decision process of most journals is a black box to outsiders, and only a few adopt open measures (such as *Frontiers* journals, which provide an online portal that lets authors and reviewers communicate directly during the review process; or the many machine learning conferences that use the OpenReview platform to publicly display submissions' review comments and final decisions). Even so, ordinary journals rarely make the editorial office's internal communications public, and the reasons most manuscripts are rejected (especially in a **"desk reject"**) remain opaque to outsiders.

The authors call for **standardizing and making public editorial decisions**, for example by setting clearer and more consistent rules for desk rejects to ensure fairness. A deeper reform concerns the way editors handle review comments. Many editors at present simply forward all review comments to the author unchanged, leaving the author to deal alone with possible contradictions and tedious details among them. This practice often leads to a long, repetitive revision process that may end in rejection after multiple rounds. To address this, the authors propose a framework of **"hierarchical logical review"**: editors should structure and organize review comments, distinguishing high, medium, and low levels of importance:

- The highest level concerns fundamental issues (such as whether the logic of the research design is sound); if such an issue has a fatal flaw, it should serve directly as grounds for rejection;
- The middle level, such as insufficient sample size, comprises major but fixable problems that should be addressed as priorities in a major revision;
- The lowest level is detail refinement (such as adding discussion of relevant literature), which has little effect on the conclusions and can be handled at the author's discretion during revision.

Such a **prioritization of problems** helps avoid putting the cart before the horse: if a high-level fatal problem cannot be solved, there is no point in discussing low-level issues. In reality, however, quite a few reviewers become absorbed in nitpicking minor problems while overlooking fundamental flaws such as research design. If an editor hands all comments to the author without guidance, it will only leave the author exhausted by dealing with trivial details while overlooking key problems, thereby needlessly adding rounds of back-and-forth revision. The authors argue that **providing training for editors and establishing such a logical review framework** can improve the objectivity of decisions. Specifically, editors should be required to **list the main problems and their priorities by severity** in the decision letter to the author. This way, when multiple reviewers' opinions conflict, there is a rule to follow: if a reviewer raises a high-priority problem, that problem must be solved even if only one reviewer raised it; while disagreements over low-priority issues can be handled at the editor's discretion or left to the author. This mechanism brings multiple benefits:

- (a) **Reconciling conflicting review comments**: a high-level important problem cannot be ignored even if only one reviewer points it out, while for minor issues with inconsistent opinions the editor can offer handling suggestions;
- (b) **Speeding up the review process**: if the first round of review already points out a fundamental flaw that cannot be easily remedied, the editor can reject the paper early on this basis, rather than wasting time waiting for subsequent revisions;
- (c) **Clarifying the direction of revision**: when the editor has already listed the problems to be solved by importance in the decision letter, the author can more clearly judge the probability of a successful revision. If the highest-level problem still remains unsolved after revision, the editor can state plainly that this will serve as grounds for rejection, which gives the author clear expectations;
- (d) **Improving the consistency of decisions**: this abstract but unified review framework provides a reference for editorial decisions across different manuscripts, reducing the arbitrariness caused by differing editorial styles.

Of course, the authors also candidly acknowledge the potential drawbacks of this method: (i) editors need to have sufficient ability to evaluate review comments by logical level, and in reality not every editor possesses this set of skills; (ii) it undoubtedly increases editors' workload, which may need to be addressed by **adding associate editors**, reasonable division of labor, or even securing compensation from publishers (many publishers are currently highly profitable and ought to invest in improving the treatment of their editorial teams in order to attract more excellent editors); (iii) not all decision factors can be incorporated into a logical-hierarchy evaluation — for example, a paper's impact and novelty carry different weights under different journal positionings and still require the editor's subjective judgment. On this the authors emphasize that introducing a framework is not meant to **constrain editorial autonomy** but to provide a semi-structured reference that increases transparency and consistency while preserving flexibility.

#### **Strengthening Empirical Research on Peer Review Reform**

The authors finally point out that to truly evaluate and optimize the various interventions above, **more rigorous empirical research and trials are needed**. They propose studying peer review itself the way one studies a scientific question: testing the effects of various review measures through **non-experimental studies** comparing the policies of different journals, **quasi-experimental designs** before and after policy changes, and even **randomized controlled trials**. Among these, randomized trials are the most persuasive but also the most challenging, possibly requiring a journal to agree to randomly assign manuscripts to different review processes, or multiple journals to collectively participate in randomly adopting different review policies across whole journals. Although randomization can reduce confounding bias, it is hard to carry out: obtaining the consent of authors and reviewers, ensuring the sample is representative, and the effect that "participating in a trial" itself has on behavior are all difficult problems. Some in the scientific community are also resistant to this kind of audit-style trial. Moreover, even if an intervention works in a trial at a few journals, it may have **unexpected indirect effects** on the entire publishing ecosystem. For instance, if a journal requires open data as part of an intervention trial, it may attract authors who are willing to share data, thereby raising that journal's data-sharing rate without improving overall sharing — because the papers with open data merely submitted to other journals decreased instead.

Despite these limitations, a fair number of review trials have been conducted so far:

- A recent systematic review and network meta-analysis pooled 24 randomized trials on peer review. The results show that interventions targeting reviewers (such as training and guidance) on the whole **slightly improved review quality** while (except for the "double-blind" measure) also **lengthening the time needed for review**, though these effects were small in magnitude and may apply only to specific settings. Interventions aimed at authors and editors were not statistically significant, perhaps because such trials are fewer and the data insufficient.
- Another review of 22 trials found that introducing a **statistical reviewer** is the most effective measure for improving manuscript quality (such as clarity of reporting); some interventions also affected the rejection rate — for example, **open review** significantly lowered the proportion of papers rejected.
- A third review showed that **double-blind review** to some extent mitigated gender bias.

In sum, the existing evidence provides some guidance for review reform, but it is far from enough. The authors call for more large-scale, diverse randomized trials to test more types of reform measures. One encouraging trend is that some journal alliances and groups have expressed willingness to participate in such "journalology" research, including jointly conducting controlled trials across multiple journals. Multi-center trials can improve the generalizability of results and reduce the influence of single-setting bias, and even when some reforms ultimately prove "ineffective" they can still offer valuable guidance to the academic community. By scientifically studying the review process of science itself, the academic community will have a stronger basis for deciding which reforms are worth promoting.

## The Authors' Views and Controversies

While proposing the above schemes, the authors maintain a prudent and balanced stance, displaying deep reflection on the value and prospects of peer review. On the whole, the authors acknowledge that the current system has serious flaws, but they do not deny the important role peer review plays in the scientific community. Their core view is: **peer review needs to be re-examined and improved, not simply abandoned**. This stance runs throughout the article; from the analysis of problems to the discussion of schemes, it reflects an attitude that dares to question the status quo while striving to find constructive ways forward. Notably, there are also differences within the author team on certain radical views, and they candidly admit in the text that **the author group holds divergent opinions on whether peer review should be abolished entirely**. This frankness shows that the article is not the view of a single voice but a fusion of the different perspectives of many scholars, aimed at sparking broader discussion.

The authors discuss in detail some **controversial views** surrounding peer review, including both arguments supporting the existing system and voices challenging or even subverting it. In discussing "whether peer review should be upheld," they raise two opposing arguments worth pondering:

- The first is the **"argument from history."** Historically, many major scientific breakthroughs (such as Watson and Crick's proposal of the DNA double helix structure in 1953, and Einstein's special theory of relativity in 1905) were published without undergoing peer review in the modern sense. It was not until the 1970s that scientific publishing fully introduced a rigorous system of external review. From this, some argue that had the current review mechanism existed back then, some epoch-making ideas might have been stifled by "**expert consensus**." The authors take Galileo as an example: in the 17th century he proposed subversive views such as heliocentrism, which neither conformed to authoritative opinion nor was backed by rigorous data, and might never have been published had peer review existed. This criticism — that peer review may **suppress innovation and cause academic uniformity** — holds that review tends to defend established "orthodoxy" and reject non-consensus ideas, yet many scientific paradigm shifts have been achieved precisely by **challenging consensus**. The text cites cases from the history of science of figures not recognized by the consensus of their time but ultimately of outstanding achievement, such as McClintock (a breakthrough in plant genetics), Karikó (a pioneer of the mRNA vaccine), and Krebs (the discovery of the biochemical cycle).
- The second argument against peer review is the **"argument from pluralism."** Scientific pluralism holds that research should encourage the coexistence of multiple models and methods; even if they contradict one another, they may jointly drive progress. The article uses the complex principles of the Global Positioning System (GPS) as an analogy: GPS simultaneously draws on Newtonian mechanics, quantum mechanics (atomic clocks), special and general relativity, and a map projection that approximates a "flat earth" when rendered on the ground — these theoretical frameworks contradict one another from a purely logical standpoint, yet combined they work effectively. This shows that **seemingly erroneous or incompatible models may also help approach the truth**, and that science often progresses through failure and error. If reviewers screen too strictly by a uniform standard, they may instead hinder methodological diversity and miss the chance to **find what is right through what is wrong**.

Facing the above doubts about peer review, the authors likewise present considerations in favor of retaining the review system. One major concern is science's **public credibility and influence**. Those who oppose abolishing review worry that if anyone could publish research without gatekeeping, a large amount of **misleading or shoddy research** would flood the literature and damage science's reputation. After all, science depends on the trust of the public and decision-makers, and abandoning review entirely might lead to "**publishing anarchy**." But the authors counter that even with current peer review, it is hard to prevent problematic research from being published — examples of **absurd or fabricated papers passing review and getting into print** are not rare. On the other hand, many researchers in practice already obtain the latest findings through preprints, without formal review, in order to keep up with developments in their field, while the general public often deeply trusts published papers simply because they see the "peer reviewed" label, which may instead create **a false sense of security**. Therefore, the effectiveness of defending the review system merely on the grounds of "avoiding the publication of fake papers" is debatable.

Another common worry is that the absence of review gatekeeping would **lower the quality threshold of research**. On this the authors point out that without competition to cater to high-impact journals, researchers might actually have less motivation to pursue "low-risk, high-hype" speculative research or even fraud, because a dazzling publication record would no longer be the sole standard of research evaluation. At the same time, abolishing review might help **break geographic inequality**: the high-threshold review currently dominated by the developed West marginalizes many scholars in low- and middle-income countries from "global science." Because publication is slow and recognition hard, scientists in these regions are often reluctant to release research data early, creating a vicious cycle. Without the cumbersome barriers of review, researchers could exchange ideas more freely, which might instead promote the integration of global research. The authors even boldly suggest that perhaps **the potential harm of an "unfiltered" research ecosystem without peer review is not necessarily greater than that of the current highly screened monoculture**. Of course, they acknowledge that this claim is too radical for many scholars. There are sharp differences on this point within the author group as well, but it is precisely such differences that may give rise to **different methods and ideas, and thereby help explore feasible alternatives**. The authors do not really intend to reject peer review wholesale; rather, they hold that even an extreme possibility like "abolishing review" is worth serious consideration, for only thus can one re-imagine what review should look like in an ideal state. This open-ended exploration helps break fixed mindsets and inject intellectual vitality into the reform of the review system.

In the conclusion of the article, the authors return to a constructive stance, emphasizing that whatever views clash, the ultimate purpose is to make peer review better serve science. They point out that **the value of peer review lies in improving the quality of research and its presentation**, and if it fails to do so, then we need to face up to and address the various challenges discussed in this article. Thought-provokingly, the authors propose that we must ask a more fundamental question: **"What, ultimately, is peer review for, and whom does it serve?"** This shows that beyond technical improvements there is also reflection at the philosophical level. The research community needs to clarify its positioning of and expectations for peer review, and let these visions guide reform in practice. Thus, on the one hand the authors call for driving evidence-based improvement through experimentation and data collection, and on the other they emphasize that reform must be supported by clear goals and ideas, combining empirical research with conceptual analysis. In short, the authors' stance is not simply to defend or negate some status quo, but to advocate facing controversy with an open and rational attitude, working simultaneously on both the practical and the conceptual fronts to gradually perfect peer review so that it matches the high standards that science bears in modern society. This dialectical and forward-looking view provides readers with a comprehensive perspective for understanding the complexity of peer review and its possible directions of evolution.

## Extra
