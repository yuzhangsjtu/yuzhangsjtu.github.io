> This paper studies the relationship between **Gaokao scores** and **entrepreneurial propensity** in China, finding that individuals with higher Gaokao scores (stronger cognitive ability) are more inclined to choose the **state sector** or **stable careers** rather than **entrepreneurship**. Although high-scoring individuals possess strong entrepreneurial ability, their willingness to start businesses is suppressed by higher **opportunity costs**. This "**entrepreneurial reluctance**" is especially pronounced in regions where the state sector has stronger influence. The study reveals the profound impact of institutions and reward structures on **talent allocation**, offering a new perspective for understanding how to optimize the allocation of talent through well-designed policy incentives.

## Introduction

#### **Motivation**

At the theoretical level, traditional **entrepreneurial choice models** (such as the Evans and Jovanovic model) predict a **positive correlation** between **ability** and **entrepreneurial propensity**. Other theories, however, point out that many high-ability individuals prefer non-entrepreneurial career paths, creating a "**misallocation**" of talent (as in the theories of Baumol, Murphy, and others). Examining how entrepreneurial choices actually play out from the perspective of individual ability therefore carries significant theoretical and empirical value.

Existing research on the relationship between individual ability and entrepreneurship faces several major empirical challenges:

- **The difficulty of defining entrepreneurship**: In many survey datasets, entrepreneurial activity is not clearly distinguished from self-employment, and self-employment is not equivalent to "opportunity-driven" entrepreneurship;
- **Limits on data scale**: Because entrepreneurship occurs at a low rate in the general population, a large sample is needed to obtain a sufficient number of entrepreneurs;
- **Separating ability from family background**: It is difficult to disentangle the effect of an individual's ability from the effects of their education and family background.

Against this backdrop, the paper proposes to study the relationship between individual ability and entrepreneurial behavior by analyzing entrepreneurship in China. China not only has more than **20 million** private firms but also a large state sector and many state-owned enterprises. This diverse environment of career choices can provide a broader perspective for studying the relationship between ability and entrepreneurship. Moreover, joining the state sector is an ideal career choice for many Chinese, and this may have an important influence on the career choices of high-ability talent.

#### **Research Objectives**

The core of this study is to reveal a phenomenon of "**entrepreneurial reluctance**": with comparable backgrounds, individuals with higher Gaokao scores are actually less inclined to start businesses. The study explores this phenomenon through the following questions:

- **Does cognitive ability influence entrepreneurial decisions?** After controlling for factors such as school and major, do high-scoring individuals still display a lower propensity for entrepreneurship?
- **Do high-scoring individuals have alternative career options?** The value of Gaokao scores is not confined to entrepreneurship; scores may also carry high opportunity costs in other occupations (especially the state sector), which may lead high scorers to choose other career paths.

By exploring these questions, the study hopes to explain why high-ability individuals show hesitation toward entrepreneurship, and it proposes an "opportunity cost" hypothesis. According to this hypothesis, although the ability represented by Gaokao scores benefits both entrepreneurship and employment, high-ability individuals may have more attractive opportunities in salaried sectors (especially the state sector), which lowers their willingness to start businesses.

#### **Research Contributions**

- **Adds to research on entrepreneurship**: The study finds that high Gaokao scorers in China are more inclined to choose the state sector rather than entrepreneurship, in contrast to studies such as Levine and Rubinstein (2017). The differing results may stem from institutional differences between the two countries: China's state sector is more dominant, which affects the opportunities high-ability individuals choose among;
- **Complements the economic development literature**: On the one hand, public-sector talent is an important source of state capacity. A large political economy literature has documented the importance of China's policymakers for economic growth (e.g., Li and Zhou, 2005). On the other hand, when large numbers of high-ability individuals choose to work in the public sector, talent may be drained away. Some research suggests that, in the Chinese context, the dominance of the state sector may hinder economic development (e.g., Hsieh and Song, 2015). Brandt, Kambourov, and Storesletten (2020) build a quantitative model that emphasizes the importance of entry barriers in explaining regional productivity differences, and they show that these barriers are related to the size of the state sector. Although this paper cannot fully quantify the impact of these factors on economic development, its findings suggest that in certain regions and industries, reducing state-sector involvement could bring about more successful firms;
- **Advances our understanding of talent allocation**: The findings highlight the state's prominent role in talent allocation, especially in economies where the public sector is the main career path for high-skilled talent. This perspective can enrich our understanding of talent allocation across countries and economies.

## Data and Methods

#### **Gaokao Scores as the Core Measure of Ability**

The Gaokao: everyone knows it. If you know, you know!

In the admissions process, Gaokao scores are the primary basis for admission for the vast majority of candidates; only a very small number can earn extra bonus points through channels such as winning awards in academic competitions. The Gaokao score has become an important measure of individual cognitive ability and is widely regarded as having a major impact on candidates' future careers and lives. Compared with years of schooling alone, the **Gaokao score** is considered a more accurate measure of cognitive ability, and the authors therefore adopt it as their **core measure of ability**.

#### **Gaokao Data and Firm Registration Data**

- **Gaokao data**: The data used in the paper include the college admission records of all candidates who took the Gaokao from 1999 to 2003, sourced from the Tsinghua University data center and covering more than 20 million candidates. Of these candidates, about 12.4 million were admitted to college. The data record in detail each candidate's **total Gaokao score**, subject scores, and the college and major to which they were admitted. They also contain personal information such as gender, hukou status (urban or rural), birth year, birthplace, high school name, and political status. The comprehensiveness of these data allows the study to control thoroughly for variables such as individual ability, educational background, and personal characteristics.
- **Firm registration data**: The study uses nationwide firm registration records as of February 2015, sourced from China's State Administration for Market Regulation and covering registration information on about 27 million firms. The data include each firm's basic information (such as industry, registered capital, and place of registration) and shareholder information (covering both natural-person and corporate shareholders). In the study, natural-person shareholders are defined as the firm's founders, that is, entrepreneurs. Using encrypted national ID numbers, the authors matched the college admission data with the firm registration data, obtaining a sample of more than 8 million individuals and about 150,000 firms.

(This data is simply unbeatable!)

The authors took several steps in data processing to ensure the validity of the data and the representativeness of the sample. First, the paper includes only students taking the Gaokao for the first time, to avoid the score biases introduced by retaking the exam. In addition, because some ID numbers were missing or incorrect, the match rate was not 100%. Finally, through random sampling the authors obtained a 20% sample containing 1.57 million individuals, while ensuring data anonymity and privacy protection.

#### **China College Student Survey Data (2010-2015)**

To further supplement the study, the authors also use the China College Student Survey (CCSS) data from 2010 to 2015, collected by Tsinghua University. The survey covers about 30,000 graduates and contains detailed information on their first jobs, such as wages, benefits, and employer type. With the survey data, the authors can analyze the relationship between Gaokao scores and employment choices. The data also cover performance and behavior during college, such as whether students held student-union positions and whether they actively participated in social activities. The authors use this information to explore how Gaokao scores relate to personality traits such as sociability and risk tolerance.

#### **Research Design**

The paper identifies the effect of Gaokao scores on entrepreneurial propensity by controlling for individual backgrounds and fixed effects. Specifically, the authors construct the following regression model:
$$
{Firm}_{i,pyt,c} = \beta{Score}_{i,pyt,c} + \alpha X_i + \lambda_{pyt} + \theta_c + \epsilon_{i,pyt,c}
$$
where:

- ${Firm}_{i,pyt,c}$ is a dummy variable indicating whether individual $i$ has founded a firm by middle age;

- ${Score}_{i,pyt,c}$ denotes the Gaokao score of individual $i$;

- $X_i$ includes personal characteristics such as gender, hukou status (urban or rural), age, political background, and high school fixed effects;

- $\lambda_{pyt}$ denotes province-year-track fixed effects, which control for differences across regions, years, and exam tracks;

- $\theta_c$ denotes college fixed effects, which control for the influence of college reputation and personal networks on entrepreneurship.

The coefficient $\beta$ is the object of interest: the effect of Gaokao scores on entrepreneurial activity.

## Results

- There is a significant **negative correlation** between **Gaokao scores** and **entrepreneurial activity**: without controlling for other factors, high-scoring individuals are less inclined to found firms. Each one-standard-deviation increase in Gaokao score lowers the probability of entrepreneurship by about 7%.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241031163636317.png)
- **College admissions and other traits**: To further verify that the negative correlation between Gaokao scores and entrepreneurial propensity does not stem from biases in the admissions process, the authors analyze whether the admissions process selects on entrepreneurial traits. In US college admissions, for example, the criteria often go beyond academic performance to consider traits such as leadership and social responsibility, which are highly correlated with entrepreneurial propensity. In China's admissions system, however, **admission is based primarily on Gaokao scores**, and other non-academic factors are rarely considered. Chinese college admissions policy is therefore unlikely to introduce an admissions bias that affects entrepreneurial propensity. The data also show that only about 8.7% of students received extra bonus points for competition awards and the like; this share is small, and its influence on the overall results is limited. Moreover, the authors' regression analysis finds that even when bonus points are included as a control variable, the negative correlation between scores and entrepreneurial propensity remains significant. The admissions system thus does not significantly affect the relationship between scores and entrepreneurial activity.
- **Heterogeneity across majors and college tiers**: To examine how the relationship between scores and entrepreneurial propensity varies across majors and college tiers, the authors divide majors into three categories: **science and engineering**, **economics-finance-law**, and **humanities**. The analysis shows a significant negative correlation between Gaokao scores and entrepreneurial propensity in all three categories, with fairly consistent strength. For example, in science and engineering majors, each one-standard-deviation increase in Gaokao score reduces the probability of entrepreneurship by about 13%; in economics, finance, and law, the probability falls by 11%; and in the humanities, the figure is 14%. The authors also divide colleges into three tiers: the top 10 universities, those ranked 11 to 100, and other ordinary universities. The negative effect of Gaokao scores on entrepreneurial propensity remains stable regardless of college tier. This indicates that college tier has little influence on the relationship between scores and entrepreneurship.

## Why High Scorers Are Less Willing to Start Businesses

The paper proposes two potential explanations for the negative correlation between Gaokao scores and entrepreneurial propensity: the "personal traits hypothesis" and the "opportunity cost hypothesis."

#### **The Personal Traits Hypothesis**

**Hypothesis**: The personal traits hypothesis holds that high-scoring individuals differ from low-scoring individuals in traits such as **risk tolerance** and **social interaction**, and that these differences may make them unsuited to entrepreneurship. Specifically, high-scoring individuals may lack the diverse skills suited to entrepreneurship, or they may display a stronger tendency toward risk aversion.

**Verification process**: To test the personal traits hypothesis, the authors use the China College Student Survey data to examine the relationship between Gaokao scores and social participation and risk attitudes. In the analysis, social participation is measured by variables such as whether a student held a **student-union position** in college, while risk attitudes are measured through questionnaire choices (such as whether one prefers stable **investment returns**).

**Results**: The analysis shows no significant correlation between Gaokao scores and social participation or risk preferences. Specifically, high-scoring students do not differ significantly from low-scoring students in holding student-union positions or joining student clubs; as for risk preferences, high- and low-scoring individuals also show no significant difference in their preference for risky versus conservative investments. Although data limitations make it impossible to fully rule out the influence of unobserved traits, the current results do **not support** the personal traits hypothesis.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241031164605259.png)

#### **The Opportunity Cost Hypothesis**

**Hypothesis**: The opportunity cost hypothesis holds that the abilities high-scoring individuals possess are valuable in both entrepreneurship and salaried occupations, but in salaried occupations (especially the **state sector**) these abilities may bring greater returns. This difference in returns makes the **opportunity cost** of choosing entrepreneurship higher for high-scoring individuals, so they are less inclined to start businesses.

**Verification process**:

**The relationship between Gaokao scores and firm success**: If high-scoring individuals are indeed highly capable but unwilling to start businesses because of opportunity costs, then once they do choose entrepreneurship, their success rate should be higher than that of low-scoring individuals. To this end, the authors analyze the relationship between Gaokao scores and entrepreneurial success, measuring firm success with indicators such as firm size, cross-province registration, firm expansion (such as investing in other companies), and going public.

- **Results**: High-scoring entrepreneurs perform better on every success indicator. For example, each one-standard-deviation increase in Gaokao score is associated with an increase of about 1.2% in a firm's **registered capital** (i.e., firm size); high-scoring entrepreneurs also stand out in **cross-province registration**, **firm expansion**, and **going public**. These results indicate that once high scorers choose entrepreneurship, their firms are more likely to succeed, supporting the conclusion that they possess high ability. Thus, high scorers' unwillingness to start businesses does not arise from a lack of the skills or abilities entrepreneurship requires, but more likely from the influence of high opportunity costs.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241031165040323.png)

**Gaokao scores and the returns to salaried occupations**: The authors further analyze the relationship between Gaokao scores and starting wages and other job benefits in salaried occupations. They use within-college fixed effects to control for the influence of college tier on wages, and they examine whether individuals with higher Gaokao scores obtain higher income or benefits (such as hukou) in salaried occupations.

- **Results**: There is a positive correlation between Gaokao scores and **starting wages**: high-scoring individuals earn more in salaried occupations. In addition, high-scoring individuals are more likely to obtain a **local hukou** with their first job, which further raises their overall benefits in salaried occupations. These data therefore support the opportunity cost hypothesis: the returns high scorers receive in salaried occupations are significantly higher than those of low scorers, raising the opportunity cost that leads them to choose salaried occupations rather than entrepreneurship.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241031165238547.png)

**The state sector's appeal to high-scoring individuals**: The authors also examine whether Gaokao scores make high-scoring individuals more inclined to enter the **state sector** rather than the **private sector**, lending further support to the opportunity cost hypothesis. In addition, they analyze the share of state-sector employment across regions to test whether high-scoring individuals display a lower propensity for entrepreneurship in regions where the state sector accounts for a larger share.

- **Results**: The data show that the state sector holds greater appeal for high-scoring individuals. In the China College Student Survey, 43.4% of graduates entered the state sector, and among respondents, **64% chose the state sector as their most desirable employment option**. This suggests that, compared with private firms, the stability and social status the state sector offers are highly attractive to high-scoring individuals. Furthermore, regional analysis finds that in regions with a higher share of state-sector employment, high-scoring individuals show a significantly lower propensity for entrepreneurship. This indicates that the presence of the state sector does indeed suppress high scorers' choice of entrepreneurship.

## Conclusions and Discussion

- Individuals with higher Gaokao scores are (perhaps surprisingly) **less likely** to found firms. This pattern, however, does not mean that high scorers lack entrepreneurial ability. On the contrary, firms founded by high-scoring individuals are more likely to succeed than those founded by low-scoring individuals. The contrast between entrepreneurial entry rates and entrepreneurial success rates reveals a key insight: although Gaokao scores may reflect high general ability, high-scoring individuals are more inclined to choose career paths outside entrepreneurship, indicating that this allocation of talent is shaped by **opportunity costs**.
- **The state sector shows a significant role in drawing talent away from entrepreneurial activity**. This interpretation is supported by the data, which show that high-scoring individuals are more inclined to join the state sector. Moreover, in regions and industries where state influence is stronger, this pattern of **entrepreneurial reluctance** is more pronounced. Although the paper does not claim that this mechanism is universally applicable, its findings offer a broader perspective: **the way a society configures its reward structures affects the allocation of talent**. This point is crucial to discussions of talent allocation, a fundamental theme in political economy and development studies. The paper therefore conjectures that **differences in reward systems across countries lead to vastly different distributions of talent across sectors, depending on each country's economic incentive structures.**
