# This paper investigates the relationship between China's **Gaokao scores** and **entrepreneurial propensity**. It finds that individuals with higher Gaokao scores (indicating stronger cognitive ability) are more inclined to choose **state-sector positions** or **stable careers** rather than **entrepreneurship**. Despite possessing strong entrepreneurial capabilities, their willingness to pursue entrepreneurship is suppressed by higher **opportunity costs**. This phenomenon of "**entrepreneurial reluctance**" is especially pronounced in regions where the state sector exerts stronger influence. The research unveils the profound impact of institutional structures and reward mechanisms on **talent allocation**, offering fresh perspectives on how rational policy incentives can optimize the distribution of talent.

## Introduction

#### **Motivation**

At the theoretical level, traditional **entrepreneurial choice models** (such as the model by Evans and Jovanovic) predict a **positive correlation** between **ability** and **entrepreneurial propensity**. However, other theories point out that many highly capable individuals tend to gravitate toward non-entrepreneurial career paths, creating a "**misallocation**" of talent (as in the theories advanced by Baumol and by Murphy et al.). Consequently, examining the reality of entrepreneurial choice through the lens of individual ability carries significant theoretical and empirical value.

Existing research examining the relationship between individual ability and entrepreneurship faces several key empirical challenges:

- **Difficulty Defining Entrepreneurial Activity**: In many survey datasets, entrepreneurship is not clearly distinguished from self-employment, yet self-employment is not equivalent to "opportunity-driven" entrepreneurship;
- **Data Scale Limitations**: Since the incidence of entrepreneurial activity in the general population is low, a large sample size is needed to obtain a sufficient number of entrepreneurs;
- **Separating the Influence of Ability from Family Background**: It is difficult to disentangle an individual's ability from the effects of their educational attainment and family background.

Against this backdrop, this paper proposes studying the relationship between individual ability and entrepreneurial activity through the lens of Chinese entrepreneurship. China not only hosts over **20 million** private firms but also features a vast state sector and numerous state-owned enterprises. This diverse array of occupational choice environments can provide a broader perspective for studying the ability–entrepreneurship nexus. Moreover, employment within the state sector represents a desirable career path for many Chinese, a factor that could profoundly affect the occupational choices of high-ability talent.

#### **Research Objectives**

The central aim of this study is to illuminate the phenomenon of "**entrepreneurial reluctance**" – the counterintuitive finding that, under comparable backgrounds, individuals with higher Gaokao scores are actually *less* inclined to start firms. It seeks to explore this phenomenon through several encompassing research questions:

- **Does cognitive ability influence entrepreneurial decision-making?** Do higher-scoring individuals still display a lower propensity for entrepreneurship after controlling for university and major fixed effects?
- **Do higher-scoring individuals possess alternative career options?** The value signaled by Gaokao scores extends beyond entrepreneurship to potentially large attendant opportunity costs in other occupations, most notably within the state sector. These opportunity costs could incline higher-scoring graduates toward alternative career paths alongside entrepreneurship.

Through exploring these questions, the study aims to explain why high-ability individuals exhibit pronounced hesitation concerning entrepreneurship and proposes an “opportunity-cost” hypothesis. Under this hypothesis, although the abilities attendant to one’s score benefit both entrepreneurship and regular employment, well-credentialed individuals face attractive salaried alternatives—especially within sprawling state sectors—which *per se* diminish the relative expected return from starting a new firm.

Through this investigation, the research aims to explain why high-ability individuals exhibit entrepreneurial reluctance, and proposes an "opportunity-cost" hypothesis. According to this hypothesis, while the capabilities proxied by Gaokao scores are beneficial for both entrepreneurship and employment, high-ability individuals may face more attractive opportunities in salaried occupations (especially in the state sector), thereby diminishing their willingness to start a firm.
