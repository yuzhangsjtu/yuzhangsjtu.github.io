### Exploring How Housing Policy Can Raise Fertility Intentions Requires First Clarifying the Roles and Functions of Housing for Women of Childbearing Age and Families During the Four Stages of 'Bearing, Raising, Educating, and Establishing' the Next Generation

---

#### I. The Ideal and the Reality of the Current Fertility Situation

The current narrative promoting fertility intentions still focuses on the family, the male perspective, or societal needs, and exhibits a certain degree of neglect of female agency. It is true that having children requires investment from both partners, but regarding the initial link – “bearing” – this process is necessarily dependent on women. In past family structures and cultural moulds, women’s ultimate decision-making power regarding fertility was often co-opted, but with rising female education levels, this decision-making power is slowly flowing back.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image//img/20210724162150.png)

Looking at data from the CFPS, the characteristics of changes in the ideal number of children among Chinese women are:

1.  The willingness to have three or four children has significantly declined;
2.  Regardless of the birth cohort, having two children remains the most ideal number;
3.  There is a gradually strengthening trend towards having only one child;
4.  Compared to other age groups, the percentage of the **Post-90s generation** choosing not to have children has significantly increased.

The mainstream explanation for these changes is:

> A confluence of a series of social and natural realities and policy interventions has led to this outcome. The main components of this confluence are rising female education levels, increasing costs of raising children, and updates in cultural conceptions.

This outcome is by no means unique. Within demographic transition theory, almost every country moving towards development has undergone the transition from a "demographic dividend" to a "demographic burden."

CFPS data indicates that the average ideal number of children is 1.9 for Post-80s women and 1.7 for Post-90s women. However, the results of the Seventh National Population Census show that China's Total Fertility Rate (TFR) for women of childbearing age is merely 1.3, falling below the 1.5 warning level and far lower than the ideal number of children held by Post-80s and Post-90s women. So, what gap has emerged between ideal and reality? And what measures should be taken to bridge this gap?

The current low fertility rate can be attributed to the following three aspects, which can be termed the "Three Problems of Childbearing/Birth/Upbringing":

1.  **Wanting none at all.** Post-90s women, compared with other age cohorts, possess higher levels of education and greater self-awareness. Having perceived a series of gender inequalities and witnessed numerous tragic marriages and fertility experiences, a significantly larger proportion of more idealistic, modern women are choosing to have no children. Among women I know, several have already chosen singlehood or the DINK (Double Income, No Kids) lifestyle;

2.  **Not wanting many.** Having one child signifies a years-long detachment from the workplace for women. Having multiple children largely implies women must abandon the prime window for their career advancement. For women's own development, this cost is far too high, even if, aspirationally, two children remains the most idealized number. For the family, having more children also brings a series of rising costs and a decline in the quality of life for both spouses;
3.  **Cannot afford to bear/raise the child.** The entire journey, from planning for a child to raising them to adulthood, involves immense expenditures on home purchases, caregiving, and educational training. An important reality is that, within the family lifecycle, there is a widespread mismatch between the window period for fertility intentions (capability) and the peak period for raising capacity (income). Depleting household savings across generations (often described as “emptying six pockets”) and having grandparents help care for grandchildren have become a common phenomenon.

With a clear understanding of the issues outlined above, the current focus of policy efforts should be:

1.  Promoting a transition from having no children to having at least one, i.e., alleviating the "**Wanting none at all**" problem;
2.  Encouraging those with one child to have a second, i.e., addressing the "**Not wanting many**" and "**Cannot afford to bear/raise the child**" dilemmas.

As for having three children, some voices suggest that **“families with three children are either in rural areas or are high-net-worth individuals.”** Essentially, families willing to have three children require no external stimulation, while for those unwilling, any policy stimulus is likewise expected to yield negligible effects.

#### II. The Role and Function of Housing in the Four Stages of the Next Generation: 'Bearing, Raising, Educating, and Establishing'

Boosting fertility intentions necessitates a suite of policies, among which housing is a critical link. The process of reproduction is not merely an act of birth itself. Against the background of modernity and Chinese culture, reproduction encompasses childbearing, raising, education, and assisting children in establishing their own families—these four phases. Exploring how housing policy can raise fertility intentions first requires clarifying the distinct roles and impacts of housing for women of childbearing age and families during these four stages of the next generation: 'Bearing, Raising, Educating, and Establishing' (生育即“生、养、育、成”).

**Bearing** — **Owner-occupied housing is increasingly becoming a prerequisite for reproductive behavior.** Housing serves as the primary material vehicle for starting a marriage and is the most vital site for daily family life. In societal perceptions, one's home is directly tied to personal prestige and socioeconomic standing ('face' or mianzi). China's society is fundamentally rooted in the soil; even moving from 'Earthbound China' to 'Urban-Rural China,' housing remains the anchoring point of life within this vernacular sociocultural context. In the long period following national reconstruction, marriage was nearly universal—those of modest means married in their own way, just as the wealthy did in theirs. However, with society’s Second Demographic Transition, marriage has shifted toward being based on socioeconomic status (Yu & Xie, *Demography*), resulting in de facto "hypergamy" becoming increasingly common. Among the factors in hypergamous selection, owner-occupied housing is a crucial determinant. Settling down in an adequate home has become almost a precondition for marriage. Given that non-marital childbearing is uncommon practice in China, this requirement for homeowner-occupied housing thereby also increasingly serves as a general prerequisite for reproductive behavior to occur.

**Raising** — **Residential space conflicts within the context of skipped-generation (grandparental) childcare, alongside inadequacies in community-provided care infrastructure.** Current studies showcase varying figures, but research indicates the proportion of intergenerational childcare in China ranges from 24.1% to as high as 88.9%. 2014 survey data from the China National Committee on Ageing revealed that 60%–70% of children aged 0–2 nationally were being raised partially by grandparents, with 30% definitively cared for solely by their grandparents-to-children.
