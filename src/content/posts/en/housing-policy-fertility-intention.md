### Exploring how housing policy can boost fertility intentions requires first clarifying the role and function of housing for women of childbearing age and for families across the four stages of bearing, raising, educating, and establishing the next generation

---

#### I. The Ideal and the Reality of the Current Fertility Situation

The current narrative on promoting fertility intentions still emphasizes the perspective of the family or the male, or the perspective of society's needs, and exhibits a certain neglect of female agency. It is true that childbearing requires investment from both partners, but as for the initial link of "bearing," women are the necessary dependency of production. In past family structures and cultural shaping, women's ultimate decision-making power over fertility was captured, but as women's level of education has risen, this decision-making power is slowly flowing back.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image//img/20210724162150.png)

Looking at data from the CFPS, the characteristics of changes in the ideal number of children among Chinese women are:

1. The willingness to have three or four children has declined significantly;
2. Regardless of the cohort, two children remains the most ideal number;
3. The tendency toward having only one child is gradually strengthening;
4. Compared with other age cohorts, the proportion of the **Post-90s generation** choosing not to have children has increased significantly.

The mainstream explanation for this change is:

> A confluence of a series of social and natural realities and policy interventions has led to this outcome. The main components of this confluence are rising female education levels, increasing costs of raising children, and the renewal of cultural conceptions.

This outcome is not unique. In demographic transition theory, almost every country advancing toward developed status has undergone the process of transitioning from a "demographic dividend" to a "demographic burden."

CFPS data shows that the average ideal number of children for Post-80s and Post-90s women is 1.9 and 1.7 respectively, but the results of the Seventh National Population Census show that the Total Fertility Rate for China's childbearing-age population is only 1.3, below the warning level of 1.5, and far below the ideal number of children for Post-80s and Post-90s women. So what gap exists between the ideal and the reality? And what measures should be taken to bridge this gap?

The current sluggish fertility rate can be attributed to the following three aspects, namely the "Three Problems of Childbearing":

1. **Wanting none at all.** Post-90s women, compared with women of other age groups, have higher levels of education and self-awareness. Because a series of gender inequalities have been perceived, and having witnessed too many tragedies of marriage and childbearing, the proportion of more idealistic women of the new era choosing to have none at all has increased substantially. Among my female friends, several have already chosen not to marry or to be DINK (Double Income, No Kids);

2. **Not wanting many.** Bearing one child for the next generation means a years-long detachment from the workplace for women; if a woman bears multiple children, then to a large extent she must abandon the ascending phase of her own career. For women's own development, this cost is too high, even though, in terms of intention, two children remains the most idealized number. For the family, having more children also brings a series of rising costs and a decline in the quality of life for both spouses;
3. **Cannot afford to bear and raise.** From planning to have a child to raising the child to adulthood, this process faces enormous expenditures on home purchase, care, and education and training. An important reality is that, within the family lifecycle, there is a widespread mismatch between the window period of fertility intention (capability) and the peak period of raising capacity (income). Emptying six pockets, and having grandparents on both sides help care for the grandchildren, has become a common phenomenon.

Against the background of clarifying the above problems, the current thrust of policy should be:

1. Promoting a transition from non-childbearing to having at least one child, i.e., alleviating the problem of **wanting none at all**;
2. Stimulating those with one child to have a second, i.e., easing the dilemmas of **not wanting many** and **cannot afford to bear and raise**.

As for three children, there is a saying that **families with three children are either in rural areas or are high-net-worth**, that is, for three children, families with the intention need no external stimulus, while for families without the intention, even policy stimulus is not expected to have an obvious effect.

#### II. The Role and Function of Housing in the Four Stages of the Next Generation: "Bearing, Raising, Educating, and Establishing"

Stimulating the boosting of fertility intentions requires a series of policies, of which housing is a crucial link. The childbearing process is not merely the process of production; against the background of modernity and China's cultural background, childbearing means the four links of production, raising, education, and helping children establish their own families. Exploring how housing policy can boost fertility intentions requires first clarifying the role and function of housing for women of childbearing age and for families across the four stages of "bearing, raising, educating, and establishing" the next generation.

**Bearing** — Owner-occupied housing is increasingly becoming a precondition for the act of production. Housing is the first material vehicle of marriage and the most important site where the family's daily life takes place; in social evaluation, the house is directly tied to face. Chinese society is earthbound; even moving from "Earthbound China" to "Urban-Rural China," housing still plays the role of an anchor of life within earthbound society. For a long period after the founding of New China, marriage was universal—the poor had the way of the poor, and the rich had the way of the rich. But with the Second Demographic Transition, marriage has shifted to being based on socioeconomic status (Yu & Xie, *Demography*), that is, de facto "hypergamy" is becoming increasingly common. Among the discriminating factors of "upward" marriage, owner-occupied housing is an important component. Settling into a home has become a necessary condition for marriage, and given that non-marital childbearing is uncommon in China, it naturally also constitutes a precondition for the act of production.

**Raising** — Living-space conflicts and insufficient community care under the context of intergenerational raising. Various current studies show that the proportion of intergenerational raising domestically ranges from 24.1% to 88.9%. Survey data from the China National Committee on Ageing in 2014 indicates that nationwide, the proportion of children aged 0–2 raised by grandparents reaches 60%–70%, of which 30% of children are cared for entirely by grandparents. Among children attending kindergarten after age 3, 40% are also directly raised by grandparents. Intergenerational raising has become an important form of childcare for young children in China. In urban areas, intergenerational raising entails higher housing requirements, such as a larger housing area or living separately from grandparents (requiring multiple housing units, whether rented or purchased). At the same time, conflicts between the middle-aged and young generation and the elderly generation over living habits, family beliefs, and the raising of offspring readily occur in crowded living quarters. This will undoubtedly dampen fertility intentions. Compared with developed countries, China also has a certain problem of insufficient community care. Childcare for young children in China is still very much a matter of the private domain. In Europe and the United States, the **public care services** provided by social workers and community support have lightened the raising burden on young parents. Domestically, reliance on nurseries and family raising means that the childbearing-age population has the intention to have children but is constrained by raising capacity, and does not readily put childbearing into practice.

**Educating** — The alliance of housing property rights and the right to educational development further solidifies social stratification. In research on the wealth effect of housing, an important conclusion is that housing prices, overall, have a crowding-out effect on childbearing. In the early stage when childbearing-age couples buy a home, high housing prices directly crowd out the childbearing practice of part of the population, and by the time owner-occupied housing turns into the main component of family wealth, the couple has generally passed the childbearing window, which directly suppresses the bearing of multiple children. The income divergence caused by the housing wealth effect lowers social class mobility; when housing property rights ally with the tool of class ascent—the right to educational development—social stratification will become more solidified. Education is regarded as the most important channel for breaking class solidification, but constrained by the uneven spatial distribution of educational resources, housing has become the yardstick for the allocation of educational resources. For a long time in the past, the children of families that owned housing had the possibility of enjoying better education, and at the same time, the housing wealth effect could provide better support for the children's education in the later stage. Inequality has become one of the most important determining factors of today's society; the alliance of housing property rights and the right to educational development expands the expectation of inequality, becoming an inhibitor of marriage and a contraceptive of childbearing.

**Establishing** — Housing is the next generation's dating capital and guarantee of starting a family. This point is similar to the first point in this section, but the narrative angle is different; here it places more emphasis on the parents' angle, thereby forming the cycle of "bearing → raising → educating → establishing."

#### III. How Housing Policy Should Change to Boost Fertility Intentions

Boosting fertility intentions is, in essence, about reducing the tendency of family members, especially female family members, to want none at all and to be unwilling to have many; policy should be devoted to improving the overall childbearing environment. Among them, possible housing policies are:

1. Help childbearing-age couples obtain decent housing through multiple means. Cohabitation can make do with rented housing, but marriage and childbearing must be supported by decent housing. For example, in applications for various types of rental affordable housing, give priority and rent concessions to families with the intention and practice of bearing one or more children. Expand the supply of shared-ownership housing to help bridge the gap between the window period of fertility intention (capability) and the peak of family income. At the same time, for families living in shared-ownership housing, when they bear multiple children, grant them concessions on the transfer of the government-held share, or allow families to gradually expand the share of the property they hold, to help families complete the accumulation of housing wealth;
2. Expand channels of community care, lighten women's burden, and prompt women to return to the workplace sooner. In China, family raising is the most important way of raising the next generation, and one of the causes of this phenomenon is the insufficiency of community care. At the planning and design stage of residential development, community childcare facilities should be built in a certain proportion and to a certain standard, supplemented by the participation of social workers, homeowners' committees, and parents' associations, so as to reduce the time and likelihood that women are excluded from the workplace during the stage of raising young children.
3. A series of school-district housing policies and equal-rights-for-renters-and-buyers policies that promote educational equity, to help decouple housing property rights from the right to educational development.
