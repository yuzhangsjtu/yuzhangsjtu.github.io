> Over the past 500 years, the global pattern of wealth (development) has been reversed. The Mughal Empire in India and the Aztec and Inca Empires in the Americas were the wealthiest civilizations in 1500, while the civilizations of North America, New Zealand, and Australia were at relatively low levels of development. Today, however, income levels in countries such as the United States, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand are an order of magnitude higher than those of present-day India, Mexico, and Peru. Is geography destiny for nations, or do institutions determine the rise and fall of peoples?
>
> Using the urbanization rate and population density in 1500 as proxy variables for economic prosperity, the paper finds that they are negatively correlated with per capita income today. This pattern of "**Reversal of Fortune**" is inconsistent with the simple geographic hypothesis. The paper argues that this reversal reflects **institutional change** brought about by European colonialism. In relatively poor, sparsely populated regions, European colonizers tended to settle on a large scale and establish institutions that encouraged investment; in originally prosperous, densely populated regions, **extractive institutions** were more profitable. This "institutional reversal" can explain the income reversal over 500 years.
>
> Through detailed data analysis and the instrumental variables method, the authors demonstrate the critical role of **institutions** in long-term economic development, and in particular that, during industrialization, good institutions can promote rapid economic growth and long-term development. The paper refutes the geographic hypothesis and emphasizes the important impact of institutions on modern economic performance.

## Introduction

#### **The reversal of the global wealth pattern over the past 500 years**

In 1500, regions such as the Mughal Empire, the Aztec Empire, and the Inca Empire all possessed highly developed civilizations and relatively prosperous economies. Meanwhile, certain regions of North America, New Zealand, and Australia were relatively undeveloped and economically backward. However, after several centuries of historical development, the economic conditions of these regions underwent enormous changes. Today, the income levels of countries such as the United States, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand are an order of magnitude higher than those of India, Mexico, and Peru. This phenomenon is called the "**Reversal of Fortune**," which is the *Reversal of fortune* in the paper's title.

#### **The urbanization rate and population density in 1500 are negatively correlated with today's development level**

Since the concept of per capita GDP did not yet exist in 1500, the authors use the urbanization rate and population density to measure the level of economic development in 1500.

- **Urbanization rate**: Reflects the proportion of a region's population living in cities. The authors cite the views of historians, arguing that the urbanization rate is closely related to economic development.
- **Population density**: The number of inhabitants per square kilometer. High population density is usually associated with regions of high agricultural productivity, which can support larger populations.

What caused the reversal of the global wealth pattern over the past 500 years? Is geography destiny for nations, or do institutions determine the rise and fall of peoples?

## Geographic Hypotheses for Regional Development Disparities

The geographic hypothesis holds that differences in economic prosperity between countries are mainly determined by differences in geography, climate, and ecological characteristics. Proponents of this hypothesis argue that the natural environment has a direct impact on labor productivity and economic development. These views can be traced back to numerous historians and economists, including Machiavelli, Montesquieu, Toynbee, Marshall, Huntington, and Myrdal. There are several versions of the geographic hypothesis.

#### **The simple geographic hypothesis**

The simple geographic hypothesis holds that geographic, climatic, and ecological characteristics directly affect a country's economic performance. The core view of this hypothesis is that geographic factors directly determine differences in economic prosperity by affecting labor productivity, the disease environment, agricultural productivity, and so on.

- **The direct impact of climate**: For example, regions with suitable climates are more favorable for agricultural production, and labor productivity is also higher. These factors combine such that regions with suitable climates tend to be wealthier;
- **The disease environment**: The high-temperature, high-humidity environment of tropical regions facilitates the spread of disease, especially infectious diseases. This disease environment is detrimental to population health, thereby affecting labor productivity and economic development;
- **Agricultural productivity**: Geographic factors determine agricultural productivity. For example, climate and soil conditions suitable for agriculture can increase crop yields, which in turn support higher population densities and economic activity.

The simple geographic hypothesis emphasizes the **time-invariant** effects of geographic variables such as climate and disease on productivity, and therefore predicts that countries and regions that were relatively wealthy in 1500 should also be relatively prosperous today. However, this is inconsistent with the facts.

#### **The sophisticated geographic hypothesis (the temperate drift hypothesis)**

The sophisticated geographic hypothesis holds that certain geographic characteristics were useless, or even harmful, for successful economic performance in 1500, but may have later become beneficial. That is, geographic factors do not influence economic performance in a consistently fixed manner; rather, their role varies across different historical stages. Geographic factors, in particular historical periods or in interaction with particular technological conditions, have a major impact on economic development. A representative example is the **temperate drift hypothesis**, whose main points of focus are as follows:

- **The diffusion of agricultural technology**: Because temperate regions are suitable for the application of certain high-yield crops (such as wheat and barley) and agricultural technologies (such as the heavy plow and crop rotation systems), they gradually became wealthier after the agricultural revolution. These agricultural technologies and high-yield crops are not applicable in tropical regions, giving temperate regions an advantage in agricultural productivity;
- **Population density and technology diffusion**: The higher population density of temperate regions promoted technology diffusion and economic development. Higher population density facilitates the spread of knowledge and technology, driving rapid economic development;
- **The long-term shift of the economic center of gravity**: The center of gravity of economic activity gradually shifted from the tropical regions near the equator toward the temperate regions, a process called "temperate drift." Over time, the economic performance of temperate regions gradually surpassed that of tropical regions.

Although it seems plausible, the temperate drift hypothesis cannot explain the reversal of development. First, the reversal in relative incomes appears to be related to population density and prosperity before the arrival of Europeans, rather than to any inherent geographic characteristic of the region. Moreover, according to the temperate drift hypothesis, this reversal should have occurred immediately when European agricultural technology spread to the colonies. However, although the introduction of European agricultural technology (at least in North America) occurred earlier, the **reversal occurred in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries** and was closely associated with **industrialization**.

Another version of the sophisticated geographic hypothesis might be that certain geographic characteristics, such as the existence of coal deposits or easy access to the ocean, promoted industrialization. Subsequent empirical work refutes this hypothesis.

#### **Other versions of the geographic hypothesis**

- **The Neolithic Revolution and long-term historical impact**: Diamond argues that the Neolithic Revolution (i.e., the agricultural revolution) first occurred in regions with favorable geographic conditions, such as the Fertile Crescent. The timing of the agricultural revolution determined the leading position of these regions in technology and social organization. Because of differences in the timing of the agricultural revolution, gaps emerged between regions in technological development and military power. These gaps influenced the **strategies of conquest and domination during the European colonial period**;
- **Jeffrey Sachs's modern version**: Emphasizes geographic factors in modern economic development, including the disease environment, agricultural productivity, and transportation costs. For example, geographic location affects transportation costs; regions near coastlines and navigable inland rivers have lower transportation costs, which is more favorable for economic activity and trade.

Although the multiple versions of the geographic hypothesis offer different perspectives for explaining differences in economic prosperity, in many cases the predictions of the geographic hypothesis conflict with actual development, and the paper's empirical analysis shows that the role of geographic factors in long-term economic performance is relatively limited.

## The Institutions Hypothesis

The institutions hypothesis holds that differences in economic performance between countries and regions are mainly determined by their social institutions and forms of organization. Specifically, societies that can provide investment incentives and opportunities are more likely to prosper, while those that fail to do so tend to fall into poverty. Simply put, institutions can be divided into two categories:

- **Institutions of Private Property**: This type of institution secures individuals' control over their property, thereby encouraging investment and economic activity. Under such an institution, individuals can expect that the returns on their investment will be safeguarded, and are therefore more willing to invest and innovate;
- **Extractive Institutions**: Under this type of institution, power is concentrated in the hands of a few, who use their power to plunder the wealth and resources of the majority of the population. Most of the population in society faces a high risk of expropriation and holdup by the government, the ruling elite, or other agents. Such institutions suppress investment and economic activity, because most people lack a sense of security and investment incentives.

As early as the 17th century, John Locke emphasized the importance of property rights protection for productive activity. He argued that, without property rights protection, productive activity cannot be carried out effectively. Adam Smith also pointed out that "peace, easy taxes, and a tolerable administration of justice" are keys to economic prosperity.

The institutions hypothesis holds that a society's mode of organization and institutional structure determine a country's economic performance. Good institutions can provide effective property rights protection, promote market competition, and encourage innovation. Institutions are persistent; once established, they tend to influence a country's economic performance over the long term. However, when institutions undergo major change, economic performance changes accordingly.

**European colonialism** led to a kind of "institutional reversal": institutions of private property were introduced in originally poor regions, while extractive institutions were introduced or maintained in originally prosperous regions.

## How European Colonizers Chose Institutions

Historical evidence supports the view that colonizers introduced relatively better institutions in formerly sparsely populated, less prosperous regions; for example, in some colonies such as the United States, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Hong Kong, and Singapore, Europeans established institutions of private property. In other cases, Europeans established or took over existing extractive institutions in order to directly exploit resources, develop plantation and mining networks, or collect taxes.

Two factors were important in determining what kind of institutions European colonizers chose:

- **The level of returns from the institution to be adopted**: When the returns from extractive institutions were higher, Europeans were more likely to choose them. In densely populated regions that already had abundant resources, such as Central and South America, colonizers found extractive institutions more advantageous. These regions often had complex **social structures** and **political organizations**, and colonizers frequently exploited or adapted the existing institutions to achieve their extractive aims. By imposing heavy taxes, forcing labor, and exploiting existing resources, colonizers could quickly acquire large amounts of wealth. For example, the Spanish implemented the "encomienda" system in the Aztec and Inca Empires, forcing the indigenous population into arduous labor and the payment of tribute to the Spanish colonizers;
- **Whether Europeans could settle**: In sparsely populated regions with fewer environmental diseases, such as North America and Australia, colonizers tended to establish settlements and develop agricultural and commercial activities. These regions had no ready-made resources to plunder, so colonizers were more inclined to create stable social structures, attract more immigrants, and drive economic development. For example, British colonizers in North America established institutions of private property and self-government, encouraging European immigrants to settle and engage in agricultural production and commercial activities. Moreover, when large numbers of Europeans settled, the lower-status members among the settlers demanded rights and protections similar to, or even better than, those of their home country, which made it more likely that effective property rights institutions would be developed for all strata of society.

Two points should be noted regarding this part:

- **Extractive institutions can also be equilibrium institutions** (equilibrium institutions). Under extractive institutions, rulers can extract large rents from economic activity through means such as monopolizing trade, levying high taxes, and forcing labor. These rents provide rulers with strong incentives, inclining them to maintain the existing extractive institutions rather than carry out reform. As long as the rents are high enough, rulers can tolerate the economic inefficiency and social discontent that extraction may bring, allowing extractive institutions to persist. Judging from the development experience of former colonies, the equilibrium institutions were very likely designed to maximize the rents of European colonizers, rather than to maximize long-term growth;
- What matters is not the "plunder" or direct resource extraction by the European powers, but rather the **long-term consequences of the institutions** they established to support extraction. The notable feature of these institutions is that political power was highly concentrated in the hands of a few, who extracted resources from the rest of the population. For example, the main goal of Spanish and Portuguese colonization was to acquire silver, gold, and other valuables from the Americas, and they monopolized military power throughout to extract these resources. The mining networks established for this purpose were built on the foundation of forced labor and the oppression of the indigenous population.

## Empirical Strategy and Basic Results

This paper adopts a variety of empirical strategies to argue that institutional change induced by European colonization, and especially the institutional reversal, is the key to explaining the "Reversal of Fortune." Specifically, the main empirical steps taken by the authors and their basic results are as follows:

**(1) Documenting the "Reversal of Fortune" phenomenon using historical data**

The authors collected and organized data on the urbanization rate and population density in 1500, as well as modern per capita GDP data, and used scatter plots to intuitively show the negative correlation between economic performance in 1500 and today, i.e., the "Reversal of Fortune"; they used simple regression analysis to further confirm the statistical significance and robustness of this negative correlation.

**(2) Systematically testing the explanatory power of the geographic hypothesis**

Adding various geographic variables, such as latitude, temperature, humidity, and land fertility, into the regressions, they find that the "Reversal of Fortune" still holds after controlling for these variables. This indicates that simple geographic determinism cannot fully explain the reversal phenomenon. They further test the role of geographic variables related to industrialization (such as coal reserves and distance to navigable water), finding that their explanatory power for the reversal is also limited.

**(3) Providing evidence for the institutions hypothesis**

Using multiple variables that reflect colonial strategies and early institutions (such as the proportion of European settlers and the prevalence of forced labor), they find that these are significantly correlated with today's economic performance. This supports the importance of colonization-induced institutional change.

Using variables that reflect institutional characteristics (such as legal origin and property rights protection), they find that these can explain the "Reversal of Fortune" well, and remain robust after adding geographic variables. This further supports the central role of institutions. To address the endogeneity problem, the paper selects **the mortality rate of settlers during the European colonial period** as an instrumental variable. In high-mortality regions, Europeans found it difficult to settle on a large scale and tended to establish extractive institutions; in low-mortality regions, Europeans were able to settle in large numbers and were more likely to introduce institutions that protected property rights.

By examining the timing of when the reversal occurred, they find that it coincides with the timing of the Industrial Revolution and the rise in the importance of institutions. The **Industrial Revolution**, as a key point in time, further drove the occurrence of the reversal phenomenon. Societies with good institutions were better able to take advantage of the opportunities of industrialization and achieve rapid economic growth, while societies with poorer institutional quality failed to seize these opportunities, resulting in relatively backward economic performance.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240603143213885.png)

**(4) Refuting other competing explanations**

The authors mainly consider the influence of the human capital of European immigrants and the diffusion patterns of technology and ideas.

- **Refutation regarding the direct influence of Europeans**: The authors examine the relationship between the proportion of European immigrants in the population and economic performance. If human capital were the key, then the proportion of European immigrants should be positively correlated with today's per capita GDP. However, the empirical results show that, after controlling for institutional variables, the correlation of the immigrant proportion is not significant. This indicates that institutions, rather than human capital, are the more important determinant. Several cases further support this point: **Argentina**, with a high proportion of European immigrants, has performed poorly economically, while **Hong Kong** and **Singapore**, with low immigrant proportions, have grown rapidly;
- **Refutation regarding technology diffusion**: The authors point out that if the reversal reflected the diffusion of technology from Europe to relatively backward regions, then such diffusion should lead to convergence rather than divergence in economic performance. However, the long-term data show a pattern of divergence rather than convergence. This indicates that technology diffusion alone is insufficient to explain the "Reversal of Fortune." The literature further shows that technological convergence depends on factors such as the recipient country's institutions, human capital, and trade openness, which also highlights the importance of institutions.

## Conclusion

- Over the past 500 years, regions that were relatively wealthy in 1500 are now relatively poor, while regions that were relatively backward at the time are now wealthier. This reversal phenomenon has been verified through data analysis of the urbanization rate and population density. This indicates that there is a significant negative correlation between economic prosperity in 1500 and modern economic performance;
- The research shows that **institutional quality** is the main cause of the reversal of economic prosperity. European colonizers introduced different types of institutions in the colonies, depending on factors such as local population density, economic conditions, and settler mortality. Through the instrumental variables method, using settler mortality as the instrumental variable, the paper demonstrates the significant impact of institutional quality on modern economic performance. High-mortality regions tended to establish extractive institutions, while low-mortality regions were more likely to introduce institutions that protected private property;
- The paper refutes the geographic hypothesis, which mainly attributes economic development to geographic factors. Although geographic factors may be favorable for economic development in certain cases, they cannot explain why once-prosperous regions are now relatively poor. The paper's empirical results show that the geographic advantages of 1500 did not translate into modern economic success; rather, it was institutional quality that determined long-term economic performance;
- The reversal phenomenon occurred mainly in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, closely associated with the Industrial Revolution. Societies with good institutions were better able to take advantage of the opportunities of industrialization and achieve rapid economic growth, while societies with poorer institutional quality failed to seize these opportunities, resulting in relatively backward economic performance. The Industrial Revolution, as a key point in time, intensified the occurrence of the reversal phenomenon.

## Extra

- In the paper's title, Reversal of fortune, the word *Fortune* is quite interesting: it refers both to wealth and to fate, future, and circumstance; so the so-called "reversal of fortune" in the title can also be understood as a transformation of developmental circumstance, or a turning of fate;
- The institutions hypothesis and the reversal of relative incomes do not rule out the important role that **geographic factors** played through institutions at certain early times; the authors merely argue that institutional differences are the main source of differences in per capita income today. In the original text, the authors write:

  > First, differences in economic prosperity in 1500 may reflect both geographic factors (for example, the tropics being more productive than the temperate regions) and differences in social organization caused by non-geographic influences. Second, and more important, as we emphasize in Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson [2001a], a major determinant of European settlement and institutional development was the mortality rate faced by Europeans, which is a geographic variable.

  The Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson [2001a] referred to here is *The Colonial Origins of Comparative Development: An Empirical Investigation*.
