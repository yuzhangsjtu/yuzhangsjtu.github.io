> Over the past 500 years, the global pattern of wealth (development) has reversed. The Mughal Empire in India and the Aztec and Inca Empires in the Americas were among the wealthiest civilizations in 1500, while North America, New Zealand, and Australia had relatively low levels of civilizational development. Today, however, income levels in the United States, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand are an order of magnitude higher than those in present-day India, Mexico, and Peru. Is geography destiny for nations, or do institutions determine the rise and fall of peoples?
>
> Using urbanization rates and population density in 1500 as proxies for economic prosperity, the paper finds a negative correlation between these measures and per capita income today. This pattern of "**Reversal of Fortune**" is inconsistent with simple geographic hypotheses. The paper argues that this reversal reflects **institutional changes** brought about by European colonialism. In relatively poor and sparsely populated areas, European colonizers tended to settle in large numbers and establish institutions that encouraged investment; in previously prosperous and densely populated areas, **extractive institutions** were more profitable. This "institutional reversal" can explain the income reversal over 500 years.
>
> Through detailed data analysis and an instrumental variables approach, the authors demonstrate the critical role of **institutions** in long-term economic development, particularly that good institutions foster rapid economic growth and long-term development during industrialization. The paper refutes geographic hypotheses and underscores the significant impact of institutions on modern economic performance.

## Introduction

#### **The Reversal of Global Wealth Patterns Over the Past 500 Years**

In 1500, regions like the Mughal Empire, the Aztec Empire, and the Inca Empire possessed highly developed civilizations and relatively prosperous economies. In contrast, certain areas of North America, New Zealand, and Australia were relatively undeveloped and economically backward. However, after centuries of historical development, the economic conditions of these regions have undergone monumental changes. Today, the income levels of countries like the United States, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand are an order of magnitude higher than those of India, Mexico, and Peru. This phenomenon is referred to as the "**Reversal of Fortune**."

#### **Negative Correlation Between 1500 Urbanization Rate/Population Density and Today's Development Level**

Since the concept of GDP per capita did not exist for the year 1500, the authors use urbanization rates and population density to measure economic development levels in 1500.

- **Urbanization Rate**: Reflects the proportion of a region's population living in cities. Citing historians, the authors argue that the urbanization rate is closely related to economic development.
- **Population Density**: The number of inhabitants per square kilometer. High population density is typically associated with areas of high agricultural productivity, capable of supporting larger populations.

What caused this reversal of global wealth patterns over the past 500 years? Is geography destiny for nations, or do institutions determine the rise and fall of peoples?

## Geographic Hypotheses for Regional Development Disparities

Geographic hypotheses assert that differences in economic prosperity between countries are primarily determined by differences in geography, climate, and ecological characteristics. Proponents of this view argue that the natural environment has a direct impact on labor productivity and economic development. These ideas trace back to numerous historians and economists, including Machiavelli, Montesquieu, Toynbee, Marshall, Huntington, and Myrdal. There are several versions of the geography hypothesis.

#### **Simple Geography Hypothesis**

The simple geography hypothesis posits that geographic, climatic, and ecological features directly affect a country's economic performance. The core of this hypothesis is that geographic factors directly determine differences in economic prosperity by affecting labor productivity, disease environment, and agricultural productivity.

- **Direct impact of climate**: For example, regions with temperate climates are more suitable for agriculture and have higher labor productivity. These factors combine such that climatically favorable regions tend to be wealthier;
- **Disease environment**: The high-temperature, high-humidity environment of tropical regions facilitates the spread of diseases, particularly infectious diseases. This disease environment is detrimental to population health, thereby affecting labor productivity and economic development;
- **Agricultural productivity**: Geographic factors determine agricultural productivity. For instance, climate and soil conditions suitable for agriculture can increase crop yields, supporting higher population densities and economic activity.

The simple geography hypothesis emphasizes the **time-invariant** effects of geographic variables like climate and disease on productivity, thus predicting that countries and regions that were relatively wealthy in 1500 should still be prosperous today. However, this contradicts the facts.

#### **Sophisticated Geography Hypothesis (Temperate Drift Hypothesis)**

The sophisticated geography hypothesis suggests that certain geographic features might have been useless or even detrimental for successful economic performance in 1500 but could become beneficial later. That is, geographic factors do not influence economic performance consistently over time, but rather their role changes during different historical periods. A prominent version is the **temperate drift hypothesis**, whose main points include:

- **Diffusion of agricultural technology**: Temperate regions, suitable for certain high-yield crops (like wheat and barley) and agricultural technologies (like the heavy plow and crop rotation systems), gradually became wealthier after the agricultural revolution. These technologies and crops are not applicable in tropical regions, giving temperate zones an advantage in agricultural productivity;
- **Population density and technology diffusion**: Higher population densities in temperate regions foster technology diffusion and economic development. Higher population density facilitates the spread of knowledge and technology, spurring rapid economic growth;
- **Long-term shift in economic center of gravity**: Economic activity gradually shifts from equatorial tropical regions toward temperate regions, a process termed "temperate drift." Over time, the economic performance of temperate regions surpasses that of tropical regions.

Despite its seeming plausibility, the temperate drift hypothesis cannot explain the reversal of development. First, the reversal in relative incomes appears related to population density and prosperity before the arrival of Europeans, rather than any inherent geographic feature of a region. Furthermore, the temperate drift hypothesis would imply the reversal should occur immediately as European agricultural technology spreads to the colonies. However, although the introduction of European agricultural technology (at least in North America) happened earlier, the **reversal occurred in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries** and was closely associated with **industrialization**.

Another version of the sophisticated geography hypothesis might be that certain geographic features, such as the existence of coal deposits or ease of access to the ocean, facilitated industrialization. Subsequent empirical work refutes this hypothesis.

#### **Other Versions of the Geography Hypothesis**

- **Neolithic Revolution and Long-Term Historical Impact**: Jared Diamond argues that the Neolithic Revolution (i.e., agricultural revolution) first occurred in geographically advantaged regions, such as the Fertile Crescent. The timing of the agricultural revolution determined the early lead of these regions in technology and social organization. Differences in the onset of the agricultural revolution led to gaps in technological development and military power. These gaps influenced the strategies of **conquest and domination during the age of European colonialism**;
- **Jeffrey Sachs's Modern Version**: Emphasizes the role of geographic factors in modern economic development, including the disease environment, agricultural productivity, and transportation costs. For example, geographic position influences transport costs; regions closer to coasts and navigable inland rivers have lower transport costs and are more conducive to economic activity and trade.

Although multiple versions of the geography hypothesis offer different perspectives to explain differences in economic prosperity, their predictions often conflict with actual development. The paper's empirical analysis demonstrates that geographic factors play a relatively limited role in long-term economic performance.

## Institutions Hypothesis

The institutions hypothesis argues that disparities in economic performance between countries and regions are primarily determined by their social institutions and forms of social organization. Specifically, societies that provide incentives and opportunities for investment are likely to prosper, while those that fail to do so are destined for poverty. Simply put, institutions can be of two types:

- **Institutions of Private Property**: This type of institution secures individuals' control over their property, encouraging investment and economic activity. Under such a system, individuals can expect the returns on their investment to be safeguarded, making them more willing to invest and innovate;
- **Extractive Institutions**: Under these systems, power is concentrated in the hands of a few, who use their authority to expropriate the wealth and resources of the majority of the population. Much of society faces a high risk of expropriation and holdup by the government, ruling elites, or other agents. Such institutions stifle investment and economic activity because most people lack security and investment incentives.

As early as the 17th century, John Locke emphasized the importance of property rights protection for productive activity. He argued that without it, productive activities could not be effectively carried out. Adam Smith similarly noted that "peace, easy taxes, and a tolerable administration of justice" are keys to economic prosperity.

The institutions hypothesis contends that a society's mode of organizing and its institutional structure determine its economic performance. Good institutions can provide effective property rights protection, promote market competition, and encourage innovation. Institutions are persistent; once established, they often exert a long-term influence on a country's economic performance. However, when institutions undergo significant change, economic performance changes as well.

**European colonialism** led to an "institutional reversal": institutions of private property were introduced in previously poor areas, while extractive institutions were introduced or maintained in previously prosperous areas.

## How European Colonizers Chose Institutions

Historical evidence supports the view that colonizers introduced relatively better institutions in previously sparsely populated, less prosperous areas. In some colonies, such as the United States, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Hong Kong, and Singapore, Europeans set up institutions of private property. In other instances, Europeans established or took over existing extractive institutions to directly exploit resources, develop plantation and mining networks, or levy taxes.

Two factors were crucial in determining which type of institutions European colonizers chose:

- **The profitability level of the proposed institutions**: When extractive institutions were more profitable, Europeans were more likely to choose them. In densely populated areas with existing abundant resources, such as Central and South America, colonizers found extractive institutions more advantageous. These areas often had complex **social structures** and **political organizations**, which colonizers frequently exploited or adapted existing systems for extraction purposes. By imposing heavy taxes, compelling large-scale forced labor, and exploiting existing resources, colonizers could quickly amass significant wealth. For example, the Spanish implemented the "encomienda" system in the Aztec and Inca empires, forcing indigenous populations into arduous labor and tribute to Spanish colonizers;
- **Whether Europeans could settle**: In sparsely populated areas with fewer environmental diseases, such as North America and Australia, colonizers were inclined to establish settlements and develop agriculture and commerce. With no ready resources for extraction, colonizers preferred to create stable social structures, attract more immigrants, and foster economic development. For example, British colonizers in North America established institutions of private property and representative government, encouraging European settlers to immigrate and undertake agricultural production and commercial activities against a backdrop of political and legal environments supporting broad property rights. Furthermore, when large numbers of Europeans settled, the poorer segments of the settler population demanded rights and protections similar to, or even better than, those in the home country, which made the development of effective property rights institutions for a broad cross-section of society more likely.

Two points warrant attention here:

- **Extractive institutions can also be equilibrium institutions**. Under extractive institutions rulers can monopolize trade, levy high taxes, and force labor, thereby extracting substantial rents from economic activity. These rents provide rulers with a strong incentive to favor the maintenance of the existing extractive system over reform. So long as the rents are sufficiently high, a ruler can withstand the potential economic inefficiencies and social discontent extraction might cause, allowing extractive institutions to persist. Judging from the development experiences of former colonies, the equilibrium institutions were likely those designed to maximize rents to Europeans, not maximize long-term growth;
- What matter is not the "plunder" or direct resource extraction by European powers, but the **long-term consequences of the institutions** they set up to support extraction. The prominent feature of such institutions was a high concentration of political power in the hands of a few extracting resources from the rest of the population. For example, the main objectives of Spanish and Portuguese colonization were the acquisition of silver, gold, and other valuables from the Americas, accomplished throughout through the monopolization of military power to exploit these resources. The mining networks set up for this purpose were reliant on efficient forced labor and the oppression of indigenous peoples.

## Empirical Strategy and Basic Results

This paper adopts several empirical strategies to demonstrate that institutional change induced by European colonization, especially the institutional reversal, is key to explaining the "Reversal of Fortune." Specifically, the main empirical steps taken by the authors and their basic results are as follows:

**(1) Document the "Reversal of Fortune" using historical data**

The authors collected and organized urbanization rate and population density data for 1500, as well as modern per capita GDP data. Scatter plots visually present the negative correlation between economic performance in 1500 and today, i.e., the "Reversal of Fortune." Simple regression analysis further confirms the statistical significance and robustness of this negative correlation.

**(2) Systematically test the explanatory power of geography hypotheses**

Adding various geographic variables like latitude, temperature, humidity, land fertility into regressions, they find the "Reversal of Fortune" still holds. This suggests simple geographic determinism cannot fully explain the reversal. Testing the role of geographic variables related to industrialization (such as coal reserves, distance to navigable water) found that their explanatory power for the reversal was also limited.

**(3) Provide evidence for the institutions hypothesis**

Using multiple variables that reflect colonial strategies and early institutions (such as the proportion of European settlers, the prevalence of forced labor), they discovered these are significantly correlated with today's economic performance. This supports the importance of colonization-induced institutional change.

Using variables reflecting institutional characteristics (such as legal origin, property rights protection), they found these are excellent predictors of the "Reversal of Fortune," and this correlation through time remains robust when accounting for the addition of geographic variables. This further buttresses the central role of institutions. To address endogeneity problems, the paper selected **the mortality rates faced by European colonizers at the time of colonization** as an instrumental variable.
