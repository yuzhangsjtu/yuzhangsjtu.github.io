> This paper studies the phenomenon of GDP manipulation by Chinese local governments, using a ratio-form approach together with **bunching estimation** to quantify the extent of target-driven GDP manipulation. The analysis finds that about **9.59% of observations** among Chinese prefecture-level cities between 2000 and 2018 involved target-driven GDP manipulation, causing average annual GDP growth to be overstated by **0.24 percentage points**. After 2013, owing to the reduced weight of economic performance in evaluations and the softening of target setting, GDP manipulation declined significantly. The study recommends continuing to promote **soft target setting** and **reforming the performance appraisal system** to reduce the incentives for manipulating GDP data.

## Long Summary

This paper examines the phenomenon of Chinese local governments **manipulating** statistical data in order to hit **predetermined GDP growth targets**, which it calls "**target-driven GDP manipulation**". Building on **principal-agent theory**, the authors point out that within the governmental hierarchy, superiors (principals) find it hard to monitor subordinates (agents) promptly and accurately, so hidden actions such as data falsification arise under **information asymmetry**. GDP is one of the most important macroeconomic indicators; once manipulated, it not only misleads judgments about the current economic situation but may also affect future policy decisions, producing resource misallocation and erroneous policy recommendations. Studying the motives for and extent of GDP data manipulation is therefore of great significance.

The paper's motivation stems from multiple data falsification incidents at home and abroad. Internationally, it is not rare for governments to doctor statistics to achieve specific goals. For example, to meet EU accession or fiscal criteria, Turkey and Greece reported abnormally high GDP growth rates in the early 2000s. Such cases have raised doubts about the credibility of government data. In China, since the economy became the world's second largest in 2010, attention at home and abroad to the accuracy of China's official statistics has steadily grown. Studies have noted that competition over economic development among Chinese local governments often produces top-down "**ratcheting up**" of targets: a higher-level government sets a high growth target, and lower-level governments, unwilling to fall behind, raise their targets further. This competitive atmosphere can tempt local officials to exaggerate growth rates in order to meet targets. Although China's central government has repeatedly declared its opposition to data falsification, statistical distortions still occur from time to time. In recent years at least three provinces have admitted to inflating their GDP figures. When researchers compared regions' actual GDP growth with their targets, they found that many regions' growth landed exactly "**on the line**" of the target, which suggests the suspicion of artificial adjustment.

Against this background, the paper focuses on **"target-driven"** GDP data manipulation and attempts to **detect and quantify** the severity of this phenomenon in China. The authors adopt an innovative "ratio form" approach combined with **bunching estimation**, and use GDP statistics at China's prefecture level to identify behavior in which local governments adjust their published GDP growth rates toward predetermined target values, estimating the overall proportion and the cumulative year-by-year impact. The paper's key findings include: significant falsification of GDP growth to meet targets does exist at China's prefecture level, but the behavior has weakened in recent years. Moreover, the authors find that policies implemented by the central government around **2013** (such as lowering GDP's weight in cadre evaluation and encouraging flexible growth targets) effectively curbed such manipulation. These conclusions provide new evidence on how government performance evaluation mechanisms affect data quality and carry direct implications for designing policies against statistical falsification.

## Background

#### **Theoretical Foundations and the International Context of GDP Manipulation**

From a theoretical standpoint, the statistical reporting relationship between levels of government can be viewed as a **principal-agent problem**. Higher-level governments (principals) rely on the economic data reported by lower-level governments (agents) to evaluate performance, but because of **monitoring costs** and technological limits, superiors cannot fully verify whether the data are genuine. Under this information asymmetry, agents may take hidden actions, such as doctoring data, to project a better performance record (Shaban and Radwan, 1987). A large literature documents examples of all kinds of data manipulation, such as falsified population statistics in **Spain**, tampered air pollution data in **India**, underreported safety accidents in **China**, and inflated exam scores in **New York State**. Such falsification, usually driven by political or vested-interest motives, not only distorts the evaluation of current performance but also misleads subsequent decision-making. Among these, falsified GDP data are especially harmful because they bear on **macroeconomic decision-making**. Exaggerated GDP growth can trigger mistaken expectations for investment and consumption, accumulating year after year into an illusion of false prosperity. Once decisions are built on distorted GDP indicators, both resource allocation and policy direction can go astray, ultimately damaging the genuine, healthy development of the economy.

When a government has explicit economic growth targets, the impetus to falsify data becomes even stronger; this is called **target-driven data manipulation**. Historically, adjusting statistics to reach predetermined economic indicators has occurred in a number of countries. For instance, Turkey and Greece reported abnormally high GDP growth in the early 2000s to satisfy EU requirements. Argentina, for another, artificially suppressed its inflation statistics during 2007-2015 to meet economic targets. Such episodes have alarmed policy circles and the public. By comparison, China's GDP data have long been questioned as well. As early as the 1970s, scholars such as Rawski were skeptical of the high growth rates officially published by China, and later Holz (2004) and others continued to point out possible systematic errors in China's GDP statistics. In recent years, studies such as Koch-Weser (2013) and Nakamura et al. (2016) have further fueled the debate over the "**water content**" of China's GDP. These doubts spurred the use of alternative indicators, the most famous being the "**Keqiang Index**" proposed by the then Party Secretary of Liaoning Province: using electricity consumption, railway freight volume, and bank loans to gauge how hot or cold the economy is and thereby cross-check the credibility of official GDP. He himself admitted in 2007 that GDP figures were biased and that he trusted physical-quantity indicators more. Evidently, the problem of inflated GDP statistics has been recognized even at the highest levels in China.

Principal-agent theory also notes that data falsification can be mitigated by improving **incentives and constraints** (Laffont and Tirole, 1993). In some fields (such as environmental monitoring), technological advances have lowered the cost of supervision; for example, real-time air quality monitoring can prevent localities from tampering with pollution data. But a comprehensive economic indicator like GDP, which spans many sectors, is reported at low frequency (quarterly or annually), and is hard to observe directly, is far more difficult to supervise. Even alternative data such as satellite night-time lights used to infer economic growth (Henderson et al., 2012) cannot precisely reveal small short-run gaps between growth and targets. Therefore, when effective external oversight is absent, if a superior sets a specific GDP growth target for a subordinate and the subordinate's actual growth falls short, the local government may take the risk of "**cooking the numbers**", especially when the gap is tiny and a slight adjustment is unlikely to be detected. As Ghanem and Zhang (2014) and other studies point out, when assessment pressure is high and supervision is lax, local officials have an incentive to meet performance targets by altering reported data.

#### **The Institutional Background of China's GDP Statistics and the Manipulation Phenomenon**

China's distinctive statistical management system provides fertile ground for the distortion of GDP data:

- On the one hand, China's statistical system practices **tiered accounting**: the statistical bureau at each level of government must serve its own government while also reporting data to the statistical authority above it. Concretely, each local statistical bureau often runs "two teams": one compiles local statistics for the local government, while the other reports directly to the National Bureau of Statistics. Because statistical definitions and accounting methods differ, figures across levels are frequently inconsistent. For example, the sum of provincial GDPs should in principle equal national GDP, yet for a long time China's "**bottom-up aggregated**" GDP has exceeded the national GDP published by the center. This gap is widely regarded as evidence of local exaggeration. The data show that in 2016 the sum of GDP reported by the provinces exceeded national GDP by 3.6 trillion yuan, equivalent to 5% of national GDP. Such an enormous gap in indicators that ought to agree across levels can hardly be explained by normal error. Likewise, the sum of prefecture-level cities should equal their province, yet many prefectures report growth rates generally higher than the provincial figure, cumulatively creating the gap between provincial and national statistics. Statistics show that between 2000 and 2018, the weighted average growth rate of China's prefecture-level cities exceeded the national growth rate by 2.74 percentage points on average, a statistically significant gap. These phenomena indicate that lower-level governments systematically tend to revise figures upward when reporting.
- Local governments' enthusiasm for inflating GDP figures stems largely from **political incentives**. In China's cadre evaluation system, GDP growth was long the most important performance indicator. As Li and Zhou (2005) found, economic performance is closely tied to officials' promotion prospects. Hence, provincial and municipal leaders announce in each year's government work report the previous year's GDP growth and the coming year's growth target. To avoid falling behind in the performance rankings, lower levels usually set growth targets higher than their superiors, vividly described as layer-by-layer "**ratcheting up**". For example, if the center sets the national growth target at 6%, a province may set 7%, and a prefecture may propose 8%. Setting a high target itself raises the risk of missing it. Once actual growth falls below target, officials worry that the shortfall will hurt their careers, invite accountability from above, or even damage the local government's credibility. Especially when actual growth is just one step short of the target, localities have stronger incentives to "lift" the growth rate up to the target value, by adjusting statistical definitions or directly falsifying data, so as to publicly claim "mission accomplished". Conversely, if the gap is large (say a target of 8% with actual growth of only 6%), the falsification required is too big to be self-consistent, and the locality will usually forgo the window-dressing. Manipulation therefore mostly occurs in "**just-short-of-target**" situations.

Common techniques of local GDP falsification in China take several forms: first, **directly exaggerating the growth rate** (wholesale falsification); second, "**rounding**"-style falsification (rounding the decimal part of the growth rate to 0 or 0.5); and third, the focus of this study, **target-oriented falsification** (revising the rate up to the target when it is close to the target line). For example, if a city's actual growth is 6.7% against a target of 6.8%, officials may report 6.8% so as not to "drop the ball"; whereas if actual growth is 6.2% against a target of 6.8%, the gap is large, falsification is of little value and high risk, and no adjustment is usually made. Likewise, some regions like to round GDP growth to integers or 0.5, reporting 6.9 as 7.0% and 6.4 as 6.5%, so that the decimals of published figures are mostly 0 or 5. This "**rounding**" manipulation also produces anomalies in the statistical distribution. Absent falsification, the first decimal digit of regional growth rates (the X after the decimal point in X.X%) should be uniformly distributed over 0-9; yet studies find that China's published regional growth rates occur significantly more often at 0 and 5 and less often at other digits, revealing artificial "rounding".

It is worth noting that the Chinese government has not turned a blind eye to GDP falsification. Around 2013 in particular, the center issued important policy documents to curb the trend of data falsification:

- First, the Decision of the CPC Central Committee on Several Major Issues Concerning Comprehensively Deepening Reform (《中共中央关于全面深化改革若干重大问题的决定》), adopted by the Third Plenary Session of the 18th CPC Central Committee (2013), called for changing the evaluation system that judged heroes by GDP alone and for strengthening the weight of indicators such as **resources and the environment** and **people's livelihood**. This effectively reduced the absolute dominance of GDP growth in cadre evaluation.
- Second, at the end of 2013 the Organization Department of the CPC Central Committee issued the Notice on Improving the Performance Assessment of Local Party and Government Leading Bodies and Leading Cadres (《关于改进地方党政领导班子和领导干部政绩考核的通知》), explicitly stating that the GDP growth rate would no longer serve as the sole assessment criterion and prohibiting rankings based purely on GDP. This series of measures aimed to ease the pressure on local officials and keep them from resorting to any means for the sake of GDP numbers.

After the policies took effect, changes gradually emerged: many localities began to use **flexible wording** when setting GDP targets in their government work reports, such as "around", "up or down", or interval targets, instead of the explicit floors of the past such as "ensure growth above X%". Statistics show that the share of cities adopting **soft targets** (flexible phrasing) rose markedly after 2013; by around 2018 roughly half of prefecture-level cities used vague or interval targets. Soft targets give actual growth some room to maneuver and are believed to help reduce the incentive to falsify. By contrast, cities using **hard targets** (such as "ensure growth above X") tend to face greater target pressure and a higher likelihood of falsification. Precisely because the center attached great importance to the problem and took measures, the authors conjecture that China's GDP data falsification abated after 2013. Verifying this and quantifying the policy effect is also part of the paper's research agenda.

In sum, GDP data manipulation in China is the joint product of institutional incentives, political pressure, and statistical technology. Local officials, caught between "assessment targets from above and practical difficulties below", tend to polish their records by adjusting the numbers. This behavior inflates GDP statistics layer by layer, "ratcheting up at every level" from the nation to the province to the city, diverging from the real economy. Meanwhile, the center has at various times recognized the severity of the problem and tried to ease it by reforming the evaluation mechanism. Against this background, the paper focuses on the specific type of "**target-driven GDP manipulation**", tests its existence and trends with an innovative method, and explores the influence of policy factors.

## Methods and Data

#### **Research Methods**

To rigorously identify and quantify target-driven GDP manipulation, the authors apply econometric methods including the **McCrary test** and **bunching estimation**, and propose a key refinement: adopting a **ratio-form** indicator to avoid interference from other types of manipulation.

**(1) The McCrary test**: The McCrary (2008) method is commonly used in regression discontinuity designs (RDD) to test whether the running variable's distribution is discontinuous at the cutoff, and thus whether artificial manipulation exists. In brief, it locally smooths the data density on either side of the cutoff and checks whether the density curve jumps there. A significant jump up or down means the data frequencies near the cutoff have been artificially altered. In this study, the cutoff is the point where "**actual growth = target growth**". If localities did not falsify, the distribution of cities' actual GDP growth relative to targets should be continuous, with no unusually large number of cities exactly meeting or just missing their targets. Any excessive concentration may indicate falsification. The authors first apply the McCrary test to the growth difference (RGDP - EGDP) and find that, besides the significant discontinuity at 0, anomalies also appear at every 0.5% and 1%. These additional discontinuities correspond to **rounding growth rates to 0.5 or 1.0**. Hence, if the growth difference were used directly as the analysis variable, the effects of **target falsification** and **rounding falsification** would be mixed together.

To solve this problem, the authors propose switching to a **ratio-form** indicator, namely $\text{Ratio} = \frac{\text{RGDP}}{\text{EGDP}}$. Ratio = 1 means actual growth exactly equals the target, > 1 means overfulfillment, and < 1 means falling short. The ratio has two advantages. First, because target values differ across localities, RGDP/EGDP yields a continuous value, unlike the growth difference, which can only take discrete fixed ticks such as 0, ±0.1, ±0.5. For example, city A has a target of 10%, actual growth of 7.9%, and reports 8.0%; city B has a target of 11%, actual growth of 8.9%, and reports 9.0%. Expressed as differences, both cities sit at "-2" percentage points (8.0 - 10 and 9.0 - 11), since both rounded up by 0.1%; but expressed as ratios, A is 0.80 and B is 0.818, and the difference shows up. The difference method thus yields many repeated discrete points, while the ratio method spreads these points into a continuous distribution. Second, the ratio method eliminates the systematic bias caused by rounding. Since most target values (EGDP) are themselves integers or 0.5 (95.8% of targets, according to the data), using differences produces a series of potential falsification points at every 0.5 (such as -0.5, 0, +0.5); with ratios, different targets yield different ratio values, so the discontinuities induced by rounding manipulation are smoothed away. Comparing McCrary test results, the authors find that with the difference form there are significant jumps at 0, ±0.5, and ±1, whereas with the ratio form a significant discontinuity appears only at 1 (Ratio = 1) and nowhere else. This shows that the ratio indicator successfully filters out rounding manipulation and retains only the anomaly associated with **exactly meeting the target**. The authors therefore choose Ratio = RGDP/EGDP as the running variable for the subsequent analysis to detect **target-driven manipulation** more accurately.

**(2) Bunching estimation**: Having confirmed significant "bunching" at Ratio = 1, the next step is to **quantify** the scale of falsification that this bunching represents. Bunching estimation, developed by Kleven (2016) and others, was first used to evaluate the "crowding" of taxpayer behavior at certain critical points in tax systems (such as tax-rate kink points) and has also been used to detect abnormal clustering of data near specific thresholds. The idea is as follows: first, use the data distribution in regions away from the threshold to infer the "**counterfactual distribution**" that would prevail near the threshold absent manipulation. Then compare the actual distribution with the counterfactual one and compute the "excess part" and "missing part" near the threshold, thereby estimating how many observations were artificially moved to the other side of the threshold. Specifically, the authors bin the Ratio at a width of 0.01, count the frequencies in each interval, and obtain the empirical frequency distribution $G(x)$. After excluding data in a stretch around the cutoff affected by manipulation (called the "manipulation window"), they fit the remaining frequency distribution with a polynomial function to obtain the smooth baseline distribution $\hat{F}(x)$. This $\hat{F}(x)$ can be taken as an approximation of the frequency distribution absent falsification. Near the cutoff $x=1$, if manipulation exists, we expect to see: on the $x<1$ side (slightly below target), the actual frequency $G(x)$ falls below the fitted frequency $\hat{F}(x)$, forming a "gap"; on the $x\ge1$ side (meeting or exceeding the target), $G(x)$ exceeds $\hat{F}(x)$, forming a "pile-up". Bunching estimation requires choosing the **manipulation window** such that the "missing mass" (the part below the baseline distribution) equals the "excess mass" (the part above the baseline). In other words, the number of relocated observations is conserved. In this study's setting, falsification raises actually-low growth rates up to the target, so observations move from the left of the threshold to the right. Following the data-driven approach of Kleven and Waseem (2013), the authors **fix the upper bound of the manipulation window at the threshold 1** and keep adjusting the position of the lower bound until the total missing frequency to the left of the threshold equals the total excess frequency to the right. This window-determination process is completed by an iterative algorithm.

Once the manipulation window is determined, the **manipulation proportions** can be computed. The authors define two measures:

- Overall manipulation share (M): the proportion of all observations whose values were changed by falsification. In essence, it is the number of extra observations "piled up" at the threshold as a share of the total sample, measured by integrating, over the manipulation window, the difference between the counterfactual frequency $\hat{F}(x)$ and the actual frequency $G(x)$.

- In-window manipulation probability (Inrange-M): for observations that originally fell within the interval near the target, the probability of being revised upward. It can be roughly understood as the likelihood that a city whose actual growth is slightly below target (falling within the manipulation window) gets its data doctored. Computationally, it is generally the excess mass divided by the number of counterfactual observations within the manipulation window. To assess statistical uncertainty, the authors run a block bootstrap of these estimates 200 times and compute 95% confidence intervals.


However, bunching estimation must satisfy several **prerequisite assumptions**. The authors specifically discuss two key ones:

- Assumption (i) **Manipulation is confined to the vicinity of the threshold**: only data close to the target get adjusted, while data far from the target are not manipulated. This is quite reasonable in this study because, as noted earlier, if actual growth is far below target it is nearly impossible to reach the target with a small tweak, and if it is far above there is no need to conceal much (except perhaps to smooth future growth, which is rare). Moreover, grossly manipulated growth cannot be hidden from the local public; otherwise the statistical bulletin would diverge severely from what people experience and trigger a crisis of trust. It is therefore reasonable to hold that falsification occurs mainly near the "critical point", i.e., the region where Ratio is close to 1, which is exactly the range covered by the manipulation window.

- Assumption (ii) **Manipulation is one-directional and upward**: localities only revise below-target data upward and never revise above-target data downward. Generally, underreporting GDP growth brings officials no benefit, except for slightly suppressing growth when true growth far exceeds the target out of concern for next year's base (which is very rare). In China's environment that stresses stable growth, there is virtually no incentive to deliberately conceal genuinely high growth. It is thus reasonable to assume that falsification raises rather than lowers growth. The authors note that cases far above target are few, and since localities differ in GDP size, the same upward adjustment carries a different weight for large and small cities; taken together, this does not create systematic bias in the ratio distribution. The assumption that falsification is predominantly upward therefore holds.


To validate these assumptions, the authors also consider possible **non-falsification factors**. One concern is that local governments might achieve targets through **real actions** rather than statistical means, for example a year-end spending or promotion blitz to pump up that year's GDP to the target. This amounts to "cheating" through real operations rather than tampering with the data itself. If such behavior existed, bunching estimation might mistake it for statistical falsification. To rule this out, the authors decompose GDP into four major components (consumption, investment, government spending, and exports) and compare whether component growth differs systematically between regions that "barely met" and "barely missed" their targets. If targets were really met through last-minute spending sprees and the like, we should observe abnormal growth in certain GDP components in regions approaching the target. However, results based on regression discontinuity (RDD) analysis show no significant differences in any component at the threshold. That is, cities that just met their targets and cities that just missed them show no statistically distinguishable differences in the growth of consumption, investment, or other real economic activity. This indicates that non-falsification tactics such as "front-loading future investment" are not evident or widespread. We can therefore fairly safely interpret the anomaly at the threshold as falsification at the statistical level. In sum, the authors conclude that using bunching estimation to quantify target-driven GDP manipulation is valid and reliable.

**(3) Econometric models and tests**: After the distributional identification and quantification above, the authors further conduct a series of heterogeneity analyses and robustness checks, discussed in detail in the Results. Here we briefly mention how they handle **endogeneity**. For instance, the setting of GDP targets may itself incorporate expectations of next year's actual growth: if local leaders are confident about next year's growth, they may set a higher target, which in turn affects the probability of falsification. Would this **endogeneity of target setting** affect the estimates? The authors test it by examining data from **years in which the top party and government leaders changed**. In a turnover year, the person who set the target (the predecessor) and the person who reports the result (the successor) are not the same; the new official may be unwilling to pay for an unrealistic target set by the predecessor, so if target-setting endogeneity influenced falsification, a different pattern should show up in this subsample. The results show that the degree of falsification in the turnover subsample is not significantly different from the overall sample; it is only slightly higher and statistically insignificant. This indicates that the endogenous relationship between target values and actual growth has limited influence on falsification behavior.

#### **Data Sources and Processing**

The authors construct an original dataset covering China's prefecture-level administrative units over **2000-2018** for the paper's empirical analysis. The main data sources and processing are as follows:

**(1) GDP growth and target data**: Both **RGDP** (the reported GDP growth rate) and **EGDP** (the expected/target GDP growth rate) analyzed in the paper come from the annual **Government Work Reports** (GWR) issued by prefecture-level and provincial governments. The government work report is usually delivered by the chief executive at the People's Congress early each year, summarizing the previous year's economic and social development and proposing targets for the current year. Through web scraping and manual collection, the authors gathered the texts of government work reports for all prefectures and provinces over **2000-2019**. A report generally contains a statement of the previous year's GDP growth rate and a statement of the growth target for the coming year. The authors therefore extracted from each report the "previous year's GDP growth rate" (as the city's actual growth for that year) and the "current year's expected GDP growth target" (as the following year's target). The data cover 333 prefecture-level administrative units nationwide (including prefecture-level cities, autonomous prefectures, leagues, and so on) over 2000-2018. However, because some cities' government work reports from early years were not published online, and a few years' reports lack explicit target or outcome figures, the final dataset is an unbalanced panel with some missing values. Overall, the data volume remains large, essentially covering the main years and regions since reform and opening up.

It should be noted that the **2019** data were excluded because China conducted its fourth National Economic Census in 2019, which revised GDP data for the preceding years. The census found that GDP had been exaggerated in some regions and corrected it. To avoid the impact of these revisions, the authors drop 2019 from the sample, ensuring that the 2000-2018 data are the figures localities reported themselves under a consistent definition.

**(2) Classification of GDP target wording**: Besides extracting growth rates and target values, the authors also perform **content analysis** on the text of the government work reports to identify whether targets are expressed in **hard wording** or **flexible wording**. Specifically, they check whether words appear near the target rate such as "above", "ensure", or "strive for" (indicating hard requirements), or "around", "up or down", "approximately", or "within the range of..." (indicating flexibility). If the former appear, the city-year target is marked as a **hard restriction**; if the latter, as a **soft restriction**. The two categories are not mutually exclusive (some reports combine phrases like "strive for... around", balancing ambition and flexibility). Statistics show that about 23.9% of all city-year observations over 2000-2018 use soft target wording and 22.1% use hard wording. Note that some reports use both hard and soft words, so the two need not sum to 100%. This qualitative classification allows the authors to examine how the manner of target setting affects falsification.

**(3) Resume data on local chiefs**: Given the literature on how officials' characteristics affect their behavior (e.g., Yao and Zhang, 2015; Wang et al., 2020), the authors collected personnel information on each prefecture-level city's Party Secretary. The Party Secretary is usually the city's "number one", whose career incentives may affect the truthfulness of the data produced under their watch. Using publicly available resumes from authoritative media such as Xinhua News Agency, the authors compiled the holders and resumes of prefecture-level Party Secretary posts during 2000-2018, including age, years in office, rank (whether vice-provincial), and whether they were later promoted. Based on the method of Wang et al. (2020), the authors construct a "**Promotion Incentive**" index. Specifically, they use variables such as the secretary's age at taking office and rank in a Probit regression to estimate the probability of future promotion, and use the estimated promotion probability as a measure of incentive strength. The sample covers a wider range (333 cities and 1,902 person-tenures) than the original paper's data of only 200 cities and 974 individuals. The computed promotion incentive index lies between 0 and 1, averaging about 0.21 across secretaries, i.e., roughly a 21% chance of promotion. This index can be used to test whether officials' personal promotion motives drive them to falsify.

**(4) Descriptive statistics**: Before the formal analysis, the authors describe the assembled dataset. The results show that over the sample period, prefecture-level **actual GDP growth** averages 11.2% with a standard deviation of 4.1%, reaching a maximum of 48% (probably a high-growth year in some small western city) and a minimum of -24.4% (a few resource-based cities experienced negative growth). **Target growth** also averages about 11.2% with a standard deviation of 3.2%, roughly matching the mean of actual growth but with a narrower range (minimum 1%, maximum 50%; some regions set ultra-high targets of 50% during their economic take-off). In terms of target wording, on average nearly a quarter use soft targets and about one fifth use hard targets. As for secretary characteristics, the average tenure is 3.25 years, the average age at taking office is 51.8, and 14.3% of secretaries hold vice-provincial rank (mostly secretaries of large cities or officials parachuted in from provincial organs). These figures are broadly in line with expectations and provide control variables for the subsequent regressions.

Regarding missing values, a few cities have no data in the early years, and the authors exclude them when computing overall statistics and bunching estimates. In the time-trend analysis the authors also use split-period samples to examine different year ranges (mentioned in the Results section). In addition, some government work reports use **multi-year GDP indicators** rather than single-year growth rates; for example, Nanjing's 2007 report mentions "doubling GDP relative to 2002", giving a five-year cumulative growth rate rather than that year's rate. The authors treat such cases as missing and exclude them from the baseline analysis. As a robustness check, however, they spread these multi-year indicators back across the years as equivalent average annual growth (for example, "doubling in five years" implies roughly 14.87% average annual growth) and recompute the Ratio distribution. The results show that even with these data included, the estimates barely change: the overall manipulation share becomes 9.38% (originally 9.59%) and the in-window manipulation probability 24.77% (originally 25.01%), with insignificant differences. This further strengthens confidence in the completeness of the data.

Finally, the data are sourced from **government work reports** rather than statistical yearbooks or bulletins because report data face the public and superiors and best reflect the growth rates and targets local governments claimed at the time. Statistical yearbooks and bulletins sometimes adjust data after the fact (especially when a national economic census occurs), whereas the figures in government work reports are announced by localities on their own initiative before any correction from higher up, and thus capture falsification behavior to the greatest extent. The authors also corroborate the influence of GWRs: comparisons using search-engine indices such as Baidu's show that public attention to the government work reports released during the local Two Sessions each February-March far exceeds attention to yearbook data. This shows that the numbers in government work reports have a greater impact on public perception. Choosing GWR data for the research is therefore more meaningful in practice.

## Results

#### **Baseline Results: The Existence and Scale of GDP Manipulation**

First, through graphs and bunching estimation the authors reveal **the existence and extent of target-driven GDP manipulation in the overall sample**. As noted above, the ratio-form McCrary test already found a significant distributional discontinuity at Ratio = 1. On this basis, the authors perform bunching estimation on the full sample, **automatically identifying the manipulation window** and quantifying the excess and missing mass. According to the algorithm, the optimal manipulation window is determined to be [**0.72, 1.04**], i.e., observations whose actual growth lies between 72% and 100% of the target value are deemed most likely to have been revised up to the target or above, while the portion more than 4% above the target is largely uninvolved in falsification. Data outside this window are used to fit the counterfactual distribution. The authors use a sixth-order polynomial for the fit (high-order polynomial fits proved robust upon testing). **Figure 4** shows the curve of the full-sample bunching estimation: the vertical dashed lines mark the manipulation-window boundaries at 0.72 and 1.04; the solid line is the smooth distribution $\hat{F}(x)$ fitted from data outside the window; and the bars or scatter points represent the actual frequency distribution $G(x)$.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250307200246788.png)

As the figure shows, near Ratio approaching 1 the actual distribution clearly departs from the smooth baseline distribution: in the region where Ratio is slightly below 1, the actual frequency is below the baseline curve, forming a dip; while in the region at and slightly above Ratio = 1, the actual frequency is significantly above the baseline, forming a peak. Bunching estimation attributes this extra frequency to **target falsification**. The calculation shows that about **9.59%** of city-year observations in the full sample can be attributed to target-driven GDP data manipulation (M = 9.59%). In other words, of every 100 prefecture-year reported data points, nearly 10 involve an artificial upward adjustment to make growth meet the target. The standard error of this proportion is 2.44%, statistically highly significantly different from zero. Moreover, for cases where actual growth falls within the 72%-100% interval of the target value (i.e., situations relatively close to but short of the target), there is about a **25.01%** probability of being adjusted upward to meet the target (Inrange-M = 25.01%). This ratio indicates that when "just short of completing the task", local governments choose to falsify in order to claim completion of the target with about a one-in-four probability. This probability is likewise clearly statistically significant (standard error around 4.5%).

The above results **confirm** the widespread existence of local-level GDP data falsification in China, while also giving a quantified magnitude: about one tenth of data points are "watered down", and in situations close to the target the probability of fabrication is as high as one quarter. This scale is far from small and shows that a certain degree of artificial adjustment was mixed into China's GDP growth statistics over the past few decades. The authors stress that, because they adopt conservative assumptions (manipulation confined to the vicinity of the threshold, small upward adjustments), this estimate may still be a **lower bound**, and the true degree of falsification may be even higher. For example, if in some years localities passively corrected data in the course of the economic census but did not publish it, then our current data may still understate the earlier degree of falsification. To address this, the authors exclude and recompute for the three national economic census years (2004, 2008, 2014); the results show that after removing these years the falsification share rises slightly but not by much, still around 10%. The 9.6% proportion is therefore a fairly robust estimate.

To verify the reliability of the above findings, the authors also conduct **robustness checks** along multiple dimensions:

- **Impact of data revisions**: As noted earlier, the national economic censuses conducted in 2004, 2008, and 2014 may have adjusted data for some regions. After removing these years from the sample and re-estimating, the M obtained is about 8.94% and Inrange-M about 24%. These differ little from the full sample's 9.59% and 25%, indicating that census factors did not cause obvious bias in the estimates.

- **Impact of target endogeneity**: Examining only the subsample of years in which the main party and government leaders changed. The results show that this subsample's M rises slightly to about 11% and Inrange-M to about 30%, but neither differs significantly from the full sample. This means that even in years of handover between old and new officials, falsification behavior remains similar to usual, suggesting that the endogeneity of target setting (such as whether new officials deliberately set low targets) has little effect on the falsification rate.

- **Handling of multi-year targets**: Filling in cases where reports provide a multi-year cumulative growth rate with the equivalent average annual growth and re-estimating yields M approximately 9.38% and Inrange-M approximately 24.77%, very close to the baseline results of 9.59% and 25.01%. Evidently, omitting these data did not significantly affect the conclusions.

On the whole, the above baseline results clearly indicate that China's prefecture-level GDP data do contain artificial upward adjustments aimed at meeting predetermined targets, with roughly one such falsification occurring in every ten annual reports. This phenomenon is statistically highly significant and is not the product of random fluctuation. Moreover, the manipulation is concentrated mainly in situations not far from the target, confirming the "critical-point falsification" assumption. The study verifies the robustness of the results through a variety of tests.

#### **Heterogeneity Analysis of Influencing Factors**

Having confirmed the overall existence of falsification, the paper further examines **differences in the degree of GDP manipulation across contexts** to understand which factors intensify or mitigate this behavior. The authors focus on heterogeneity along four dimensions: **changes in the policy environment (before and after 2013)**, **the manner of target setting (hard versus soft targets)**, **the strength of the chief's promotion incentive**, and **the length of the chief's tenure**.

**(1) Policy environment (before and after 2013)**: As noted in the background in Part 2, in 2013 the center rolled out a series of measures to weaken the weight of GDP in evaluation. Using 2013 as the dividing line, the authors split the sample into the two periods **2000-2012** and **2013-2018** and estimate the bunching effect separately. The results show that falsification was more severe before 2013: the **overall manipulation share** M is estimated at 12.55%, dropping to 8.80% after 2013. The before-after difference of about 3.75 percentage points equals 30% of the earlier level, indicating that the overall falsification rate fell by about three tenths. Even more striking is the change in the **in-window manipulation probability**: before 2013 Inrange-M was as high as 42.12%, then plummeted to 12.61% afterward. The drop of nearly 30 percentage points amounts to a reduction of about 70%. This result strongly demonstrates that **the 2013 policy shift significantly curbed local GDP falsification**. Previously about one in every two "just-short-of-the-finish-line" situations involved falsification (42%), whereas after the policy the probability of falsification was only about one in eight (12%). Clearly, after the center reduced GDP's weight in performance evaluation, local officials' incentive to falsify weakened sharply. This finding also verifies the policy's effectiveness and carries important policy implications (discussed in the conclusion).

**(2) Manner of target setting (hard/soft restriction)**: The authors classify the sample by whether soft wording is used (Soft = 0 for hard targets, Soft = 1 for soft targets) and run bunching estimation separately. The results show that when targets are stated in a **hard** manner, falsification is more prevalent. The M for the hard-target sample is estimated at 10.66%, versus only 7.00% for soft targets. As for Inrange-M, it is 30.55% under hard targets and falls to 13.53% under soft targets. The two differ by more than a factor of two. This fully demonstrates that **using flexible, lenient GDP target wording can reduce falsification behavior**. The mechanism is that soft targets leave localities some leeway: "growth of around 8%", for instance, means 7.5% or even 7% can count as basically accomplished, with no need to force the figure up to 8.0%; whereas a hard target like "ensure 8%" leaves no room, so even 7.9% counts as a miss, and officials, to avoid the risk of "failing to meet performance targets", are more likely to choose to tamper with the data. This finding accords with intuition and echoes the importance of the center's encouragement of interval targets and de-emphasis of the growth indicator.

**(3) Chief's promotion incentive**: Based on each municipal Party Secretary's promotion-probability index, the authors split the sample at the median of this index into high-incentive and low-incentive groups and estimate the degree of falsification separately. Unexpectedly, the two groups show almost no difference. For the group with above-median promotion incentive, M is about 9.54%, versus 9.98% for the below-median group, and the difference is insignificant; Inrange-M is about 33% for one and about 23% for the other, and the difference is not statistically significant either. This indicates that when it comes to GDP falsification, officials' personal promotion pressure plays no significant role. A possible explanation is that GDP-target pressure, as an **institutional factor**, is universal across all officials, while differences in personal career prospects affect other behaviors more (such as investment propensity or infrastructure construction) and lack discriminatory power on the matter of falsification. In other words, whether a young, promising cadre or one nearing retirement, as long as the evaluation system emphasizes GDP, they all have an incentive to meet the targets, regardless of how high their promotion probability is. This forms an interesting contrast with the classic conclusion of Li and Zhou (2005) (that GDP is linked to promotion): GDP growth affects promotion, but in terms of falsification, a high promotion probability does not further aggravate the data's water content. From this the authors infer that **institutional factors outweigh individual factors**: GDP falsification is driven more by institutional constraints than by officials' individual characteristics.

**(4) Chief's tenure length**: Some studies (e.g., Guo, 2009) note that officials may be more proactive early in their tenure because they hope to make a mark upon taking office. The authors define secretaries with tenure ≤ 3 years as "short-term" and those with tenure ≥ 4 years as "long-term" and compare the level of falsification between the two groups. The results show that the two groups still differ insignificantly: cities with short-term incumbents have M of 9.08% and long-term ones 9.85%, a difference of less than 1 percentage point and insignificant; Inrange-M is 26.8% for short-term and 29.9% for long-term, also with no significant difference. This shows that whether the chief has newly taken office or has served for a long time, they are equally likely to falsify, with no significant regularity. This again emphasizes that **individual behavioral patterns are not the main determinant**, and that institutional and policy environments are what matter for GDP falsification across different situations.

Through the heterogeneity analysis above, the paper reaches an important conclusion: **the degree of GDP falsification depends more on institutional incentives and constraints than on officials' individual traits**. Macro-level institutional reforms (such as a change in evaluation orientation or flexibility in target setting) can significantly alter the incidence of data falsification, whereas factors such as a specific official's age or career prospects have little effect. This finding offers direct inspiration for policymaking: rather than pinning hopes on selecting "morally upright" officials, it is better to reduce the incentives to falsify at the institutional level.

#### **Estimating the Magnitude of GDP Data Exaggeration and Future Trends**

After confirming the existence and frequency of GDP falsification, the authors further explore **how large a bias this falsification introduces into the GDP growth rate itself**, as well as **its cumulative impact on the GDP level over the long run**. Put simply, the question to answer is: "By how many percentage points does this falsification make the official GDP growth rate higher than the true growth rate? And how much has GDP been cumulatively inflated over the years?"

Directly observing the true growth rate is obviously impossible, but the authors design a method of **random reassignment simulation** to estimate this magnitude. The basic steps are as follows:

1. Based on the previously estimated M = 9.59%, compute roughly how many city-year observations in total are "manipulated" (denoted A). Specifically, this is 9.59% × N (the total number of observations). These observations correspond to cases where actual growth should have been below target but was reported high.

2. **Randomly draw** A observations, all of whose Ratios lie within the manipulation window [1, 1.04) (i.e., cases actually revised up to near the target). Then "restore" these observations' growth rates to where they might have been absent falsification. Since it is impossible to know exactly what each manipulated data point originally was, the authors assume a "**random redistribution**" principle: randomly reassign these observations back into the missing region to the left of the manipulation window, [0.72, 1). This is tantamount to assuming that each upward-adjusted growth rate, had it not been adjusted, might have been some value 1%, 2%, etc. below target, randomly distributed over the [0.72, 1) range.

3. For each reassigned observation, based on its corresponding target growth EGDP, compute a "corrected actual growth rate" = EGDP × the post-reassignment Ratio. This represents what the region's actual growth should have been absent falsification.

4. Then compute **each observation's manipulation magnitude** = (the reported RGDP growth - the corrected actual growth). This difference is how many percentage points of growth the falsification artificially added.

5. To obtain the national-level impact, the authors use each city's share of national nominal GDP in that year as a weight and compute the weighted-average magnitude of growth exaggeration. Weight correction is important because a small city raising its growth rate by 1 percentage point has a negligible effect nationally, whereas an economically large city raising its growth by 1 percentage point makes a significant national contribution.

6. Repeat the above process 200 times and take the average, and compute the standard error. Such a Monte Carlo simulation can smooth out the fluctuations introduced by random reassignment.


The simulation results are summarized in the paper's Table 4. The main findings include:

- **Average annual magnitude of growth exaggeration**: Over 2000-2018, target-driven falsification made China's prefecture-level reported GDP growth rate higher by **about 0.24 percentage points** on average each year. The standard error is about 0.09, indicating that this estimate is fairly robust across different samples. 0.24 percentage points may sound small, but considering that China's average growth over this period was about 9.14%, it means about 2.6% of the official growth rate is "water content" (0.24/9.14). In other words, if true growth was 8.9%, falsification would lead officials to report 9.14%.
- **Magnitudes by period/group**: The authors also compare the falsification magnitude before and after 2013, as well as under soft and hard targets. The results show that before 2013 the average annual exaggeration was about 0.28 percentage points, dropping to 0.13 percentage points after 2013. Without soft targets (the hard-target case) the exaggeration was about 0.30 percentage points, versus only 0.07 percentage points with soft targets. These differences accord with the frequency-based results above: policy reform and soft targets did substantially reduce the degree of GDP overstatement.
- **Cumulative impact on the GDP level**: Do not underestimate 0.24 percentage points a year; accumulated year after year it produces a sizable bias in the GDP level. The authors estimate that if there were 0.24 percentage points of inflation every year over 2000-2018, then by 2018 China's officially published GDP level would be **exaggerated by about 4.1%** relative to its true size. The calculation is based on the difference between growth of 9.14% and 8.90% per year over 19 years. 4.1% means that nearly 1/25 of 2018's GDP is "watered". This is comparable in magnitude to the earlier provincial-data comparison (provinces over-reported by 5% in 2016). The authors further estimate that if similar proportions of falsification are assumed to have persisted since 1978, then over the 42 years (1978-2020) the cumulative GDP inflation could reach about 8.6%. It must be stressed, however, that this is based on a simple extrapolation of recent patterns; in reality the degree of data falsification in the early years of reform and opening up cannot be determined (perhaps it was not serious then). But at least it shows that in the era of high-speed growth, the relative proportion of falsification has a limited impact on the level, whereas after growth slows the same magnitude of falsification accounts for a larger share of the growth rate. For example, in recent years China's economic growth has fallen below 5%; if some localities still habitually over-report by 0.2-0.3 percentage points, that accounts for as much as 5-10% of the actual growth rate. Therefore, as the economy enters a phase of medium-to-low-speed growth, if statistical falsification is not further curbed, the "water content" may instead rise. This deserves vigilance. ![image-20250307200747460](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250307200747460.png)

Finally, the authors explore **scenario projections of future trends**. At the end of the paper they sketch several possible scenarios (as shown in Figure 8): first, if the falsification level after the center strengthened evaluation reform during 2014-2018 continues, the slope of future GDP exaggeration will be lower (the triangle line in the figure); second, if from 2023 onward the whole country fully adopts soft targets and no longer judges heroes by GDP, then the falsification rate may fall further and the slope of the cumulative exaggeration curve approaches almost zero (the diamond line in the figure). By contrast, if reform momentum stalls and some localities, perhaps under downward economic pressure, return to the old "growth-at-all-costs" straitjacket, the falsification rate may rebound and the cumulative degree of exaggeration may rise again in the future (not explicitly drawn in the figure, but imaginable as a curve steeper than the triangle line). All in all, the paper's simulation suggests: **the problem of water content in China's GDP statistics was curbed in the early 2010s, and if the policy is sustained it will remain at a low level; but one must still beware of the risk that the falsification share grows larger under the new normal of slowing growth**.

## Conclusions and Discussion

#### **Research Conclusions**

Through rigorous analysis, the paper reaches several important conclusions:

- First, it **confirms** the existence at China's local-government level of significant data falsification motivated by meeting GDP growth targets. This "target-driven GDP manipulation" mainly takes the form: when actual growth is slightly below the expected target, the local government revises the reported growth rate upward to claim it met the target. Through detection of distributional discontinuities and bunching estimation, the authors quantify the severity of this phenomenon: about 10% of city-year data were artificially revised upward, a relatively high share.
- Second, the paper finds that this falsification has eased over the past decade: after the center stressed in 2013 that one should "not judge heroes by GDP alone", both the incidence and magnitude of GDP growth falsification across localities fell substantially. In particular, the widespread shift of target setting to flexible intervals (soft targets) freed localities from fixating on a rigid figure, effectively reducing the incentive to massage the indicator.
- Third, the authors estimate by simulation the bias that falsification introduces into GDP statistics: about 0.24 percentage points of over-reporting per year on average, equivalent to lifting the true growth rate by 2.6%. This tiny annual difference, compounded over many years, leads the 2018 GDP level to possibly be overstated by about 4.1%. While this figure is not exaggerated, 4% of GDP is a considerable absolute amount in China (on the order of several hundred billion dollars). The authors caution that if not continually addressed, then as economic growth slows the share accounted for by similar falsification of 0.2-0.3 percentage points will rise, and the proportion of future statistical distortion may instead increase.

On the whole, the paper's research **reveals** that China's local GDP statistics were not entirely credible over a fairly long period, but were laced with a certain degree of "water content". This water content does not occur randomly but closely revolves around the growth targets set by the government. In other words, a substantial part of the GDP figures was "**squeezed out**" or "**adjusted out**" by governments at various levels, rather than spontaneously grown by the economy.

This conclusion is significant for both academia and the political world. For academia, it explains why the sum of provincial data has long exceeded the national figure, and why many localities' GDP growth so often lands exactly on the target. For the political world, it warns policymakers to treat the GDP indicator with caution, because it may contain systematic bias that needs correcting and screening. Of course, the authors also point out that the calculation of the cumulative magnitude of GDP manipulation involves some assumptions (such as assuming that the falsified sample is randomly distributed over some interval); these assumptions are mainly for quantifying the economic meaning and do not affect the paper's core contribution. The paper's main contribution still lies in **identifying and measuring the probability of target-driven falsification**, namely the two indicators M and Inrange-M. The estimate of 4.1% overall exaggeration is more about giving readers an intuitive sense of the magnitude of its economic impact.

#### **Policy Implications and the Effect on GDP Accuracy**

This research carries policy implications on multiple fronts. First, the results show that the central government's measures to strengthen the governance of macroeconomic statistics and reform the official evaluation system have been **highly effective**. After 2013 both the overall falsification share and the local falsification probability fell substantially, showing that the policy of reducing GDP's weight in performance played a role. It is therefore very important to continue along these reform directions. Specifically, the paper **recommends** two main measures:

- **Promote soft GDP targets**: When localities set GDP growth expectations, they should use interval or flexible wording, such as "growth of around X%" or giving an upper and lower bound, and avoid rigid phrasing such as "ensure no less than X%". Doing so acknowledges the volatility and uncertainty of economic operation (especially in an era of shocks such as pandemics and trade conflicts) and gives local governments room to adjust when unexpected situations arise. The paper's empirical results have already proven that soft targets can significantly lower the probability of falsification. In actual governance, therefore, the center could well explicitly require localities to adopt flexible target management. This approach does not harm the reasonable pursuit of growth, while also reducing the impulse to falsify in order to meet rigid targets, making it an effective means of optimizing governance.

- **Reform GDP-oriented evaluation**: Shift officials' performance evaluation from overemphasizing GDP growth toward more diversified and longer-term indicators of development quality. China has now entered a stage of high-quality development, and the level of GDP growth alone is no longer the sole pursuit. The paper recommends adding to the evaluation system indicators such as **improvement in total factor productivity (TFP)**, **fairness of income distribution (e.g., the Gini coefficient)**, **improvement in public services**, and **environmental protection**. These indicators can reflect the quality and sustainability of development and guide localities to turn their attention to the substance of economic development rather than mere speed. In this way, local governments will not falsify or even overdraw the future in pursuit of high growth, avoiding the wrong path of "**numbers-driven performance**". The authors note that China has already taken steps in this direction after 2013, but there is still room for improvement. Especially under the current circumstances of slowing GDP growth, continuing to hold localities to the old high-growth standards would inevitably add pressure for data falsification at the grassroots level once again. Evaluation reform therefore needs to keep pace with the times and truly embed the concept of high-quality development in the indicator system.

As for the effect on the accuracy of GDP data, the paper's findings imply that, in understanding and using China's GDP data, one needs to account for the existence of an **upward bias**. From the study's estimates, pre-reform data were inflated by about 0.3 percentage points, and post-reform there is still about 0.1 percentage points. While this has little effect on annual analysis, it may have some effect on cumulative levels and cross-regional comparisons. For example, if a province over-reported by 0.5 percentage points every year for many years, then a decade later its GDP level could be more than 5% higher than reality, misleading the center's judgment of its economic size and growth rate. In addition, this falsification may be uneven across regions: perhaps some regions with fierce competition and high targets are more severe, while some economically stable regions are milder. Thus the ranking of and gaps in GDP across regions would also be distorted. It is worth noting that the central statistical bureau has in recent years begun to directly and uniformly compute local GDP, also in order to eliminate the over-reporting caused by local exaggeration. The 2019 economic census, which revealed the water content of several provinces and adjusted historical data, is a case in point. The paper stresses that to maintain the international credibility of China's GDP statistics in the future, it is essential to continually **prevent and correct local data falsification**. Otherwise, not only might domestic macroeconomic decisions be built on sand, but the international community's trust in China's economic data would also decline. For the world's second-largest economy, this would have far-reaching consequences.
