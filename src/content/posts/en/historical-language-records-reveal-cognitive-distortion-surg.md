#### Significance

> How will the level of depression across an entire society change over time? Here, we search for historical traces of cognitive distortions and thinking patterns closely tied to internalizing disorders such as depression and anxiety, drawing on millions of English, Spanish, and German books published over the past two centuries. We find a clear "hockey-stick" (*Hockey*-*stick*) effect: throughout most of the 20th century, textual analogs of cognitive distortions rose substantially, far exceeding historical levels including those during World War I and World War II. Our findings suggest that recent socioeconomic changes, new technologies, and social media may be linked to a surge in cognitive distortions.

Depression is a leading contributor to the disability burden worldwide. Some evidence suggests that disability caused by depression has been rising over the past few decades, especially among young people. As people face stressors such as war, political turmoil, economic collapse, food insecurity, inequality, and disease, does society collectively become more or less depressed over time? This question is hard to answer over long timescales, because formal diagnostic criteria were only introduced 40 years ago, and these criteria have themselves changed over time.

Depression is associated with conspicuous, identifiable maladaptive thinking patterns called **cognitive distortions**, in which individuals think about themselves, the future, and the world in inaccurate and excessively negative ways. For example, the cognitive distortions that appear in depression occur when people label themselves in negative, absolute terms (e.g., "I am a failure"). They may talk about future events in extreme dichotomous terms (e.g., "my meeting will be a complete disaster"), or make unfounded assumptions about the mental states of others (e.g., "everyone will think I am a failure"). Typologies of cognitive distortions commonly distinguish a number of partially overlapping types, such as "catastrophizing," "dichotomous reasoning," "disqualifying the positive," "emotional reasoning," "fortune-telling," "labeling and mislabeling," "magnification and minimization," "mental filtering," "mind-reading," "overgeneralizing," "personalization," and "should statements."

The theory behind Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), the gold standard for treating depression and other internalizing disorders, holds that cognitive distortions are linked to internalizing disorders; they reflect patterns of negative affect and avoidant behavior under environmental stress. Language is closely intertwined with this dynamic. Indeed, recent research shows that people with internalizing disorders exhibit markedly higher levels of cognitive distortions in their language, to the point that their prevalence can be used as a vulnerability marker for depression.

Here, we leverage the link between depression and language to investigate whether society as a whole can, like depressed individuals, undergo changes in collective language associated with cognitive distortions. We analyze the prevalence of a large set of cognitive distortion markers across more than 14 million books (Google Books) published in English, Spanish, and German over the past 125 years.

**CDS - cognitive distortion schemata**

We examined the history of the median prevalence (z-scores) of the entire set of English cognitive distortion schemata (n = 241) in English (US) books (US only, n = 9,018,119) from 1855 to 2019 (Fig. 2A). Because these data apply only to books published in the United States, we noted notable events in US history or marked changes in the time series: the turn of the century in 1899; the outbreak of World War I; the financial crash of 1929; the outbreak of World War II; 1968 as the peak of CDS prevalence; and clear shifts in trend in 1978, 1999, and 2007.

> Fig. 2A: (A - C) Median z-scores (125 y) for the US English (A), Spanish (B), and German (C) CDS n-gram prevalence time series from 1855 to 2020, with year labels added for major historical events. All time series show that cognitive distortion levels were stable or declining throughout most of the 20th century, followed by a sharp rise in cognitive distortions over the past 30 years. From 1899 to 1978, US English levels trended downward, with small peaks around 1914 and 1940 (World War I and World War II), and especially marked in 1968. Following this decline, CDS prevalence surged starting in 1978 and continued through 2019. For Spain, we find stable levels from 1895 to the early 1980s, at which point CDS prevalence trended higher than any level previously observed. German CDS prevalence remained stable, except for strong peaks around World Wars I and II, until a sudden surge in 2007.

Throughout most of the 20th century, the overall trend in CDS prevalence clearly pointed toward its historic low in 1978, with only a few discernible peaks: one at the turn of the century in 1899 (possibly related to the Spanish-American War), a slight one between 1940 and 1945 (roughly during World War II), and a peak in 1968 (possibly related to social and political turmoil). From 1978 onward, we observe accelerating growth in CDS prevalence. This acceleration appears to fall into three periods: accelerated growth from 1978 to 1999 (when CDS prevalence first exceeded 1910s levels), faster growth from 1999 to 2007, then accelerating growth after 2007, and a possible stabilization around 2010. The so-called "bursting of the internet bubble" appears to coincide with the accelerated growth of CDS prevalence after 1999, while the accelerated growth of CDS prevalence since 2007 appears to coincide with the widespread use of social media and the onset of the Great Recession. The current level of CDS prevalence is nearly two standard deviations higher than levels observed since the 20th century (except for the 1899 peak).

While the differences among these three languages are interesting, perhaps the most important point is that over the past 30 years, the expression of cognitive distortions in all three languages has increased, producing a clear hockey-stick pattern indicating a surge in CDS prevalence, which are the lexical markers of cognitive distortions.

The timing of the CDS surge in the United States coincides with the late 1970s, when wage levels stopped tracking the steadily rising productivity of work. This trend is associated with growing income inequality, with current levels of inequality unseen since the 1930s. This phenomenon has been observed in most developed economies, including Germany, Spain, and Latin America, occurring alongside the rapid growth of automation and demand for highly skilled labor. The Great Recession of 2007 may have intensified the effects of this decades-long trend that began in the late 1970s. The widespread adoption of communication technologies such as the internet, the World Wide Web, and social media may have created greater social and political polarization at the global level. The language of this polarization may correspond to cognitive distortions, particularly "us-vs.-them thinking" (labeling and mislabeling), dichotomous reasoning, mind-reading, overgeneralizing, emotional reasoning, and catastrophizing.
