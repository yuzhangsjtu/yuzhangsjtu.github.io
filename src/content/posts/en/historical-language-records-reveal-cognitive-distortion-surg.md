#### Significance

> How will the level of depression in a society shift over time? Here, we searched for historical traces of cognitive distortions and thinking patterns closely tied to internalizing disorders such as depression and anxiety across millions of books published over the past two centuries in English, Spanish, and German. We discovered a distinct “hockey-stick” effect: textual proxies for cognitive distortions rose substantially through much of the 20th century, far surpassing levels seen during historical episodes such as the First and Second World Wars. Our findings indicate that recent socioeconomic changes, new technologies, and social media may be linked to a surge in cognitive distortions.

Depression is the leading cause of disability worldwide. Some evidence suggests that the disability burden attributable to depression has been rising over recent decades, especially among young people. Do societies collectively become more or less depressed over time as they face stressors such as war, political turmoil, economic collapse, food insecurity, inequality, and disease? This question is difficult to answer on long timescales because formal diagnostic criteria were introduced just 40 years ago and have themselves shifted over time.

Depression is associated with conspicuous, identifiable maladaptive thought patterns called **cognitive distortions**, where individuals think about themselves, the future, and the world in an inaccurate and excessively negative fashion. For example, cognitive distortions that surface in depression occur when people label themselves in negative, absolute terms (e.g., “I am a failure”). They may talk about future events in extreme dichotomous terms (e.g., “my meeting will be a total disaster”), or make unfounded assumptions about the mental states of others (e.g., “everyone will think I am a failure”). Typologies of cognitive distortions commonly distinguish several partially overlapping types such as “catastrophizing,” “dichotomous reasoning,” “disqualifying the positive,” “emotional reasoning,” “fortune-telling,” “labeling and mislabeling,” “magnification and minimization,” “mental filtering,” “mind-reading,” “overgeneralizing,” “personalization,” and “should statements.”

The theory underpinning the gold-standard treatment for depression and other internalizing disorders, Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), holds that cognitive distortions are tied to internalizing disorders; they reflect patterns of negative affect and avoidant behavior under environmental stress. Language is deeply intertwined with this dynamic. Recent work in fact shows that individuals with internalizing disorders exhibit markedly higher levels of cognitive distortions in their language, to the extent that their prevalence can be used as a marker of vulnerability to depression.

Here, we exploited this link between depression and language to investigate whether society as a collective can, like depressed individuals, undergo a shift in collective language associated with cognitive distortions. We analyzed the prevalence of a comprehensive set of cognitive distortion markers across over 14 million books (Google Books) published in English, Spanish, and German over the last 125 years.

**CDS – cognitive distortion schemata**

We examined the history of the median prevalence (z-scores) of the entire set of English cognitive distortion schemata (n = 241) across English (United States) books (United States only, n = 9,018,119) from 1855 to 2019 (Fig. 2A). Since these data are restricted to books published in the United States, we note notable events in US history or marked changes in the time series: the fin-de-siècle of 1899; the onset of World War I; the financial crash of 1929; the onset of World War II; the peak of CDS prevalence in 1968; clear indications of trend shifts in 1978, 1999, and 2007.

![img](https://www.pnas.org/content/pnas/118/30/e2102061118/F2.large.jpg?width=800&height=600&carousel=1)

> Fig. 2A: (A – C) Median z scores (125 y) for the US English (A), Spanish (B), and German (C) CDS n-gram prevalence time series from 1855 to 2020, superimposed with year labels for notable historical events. All time series show stable or declining levels of cognitive distortion throughout much of the 20th century followed by a sharp rise in cognitive distortions during recent three decades. US English shows declines from 1899 to 1978, with minor peaks around 1914 and 1940 (WWI and WWII), notably marked in 1968. This decline is followed by a surge in CDS prevalence from 1978 onward that continues to 2019. For the Spanish set we find steady levels from 1895 to the early 1980s, at which point the trend in CDS prevalence levels rises higher than from any previous observed level. The German CDS prevalence remains steady except for strong peaks around WWI and WWII until a sudden uptick in 2007.

Across much of the 20th century the general trend in CDS prevalence is clearly pointed toward the historic low of 1978, with only a few discernible peaks, one at the turn of the century in 1899 (probably correlated with the Spanish-American War), a very slight one between 1940 and 1945 (roughly during WWII), and a strong one in 1968 (possibly correlated with social and political turmoil). From 1978 onward we observe an accelerating growth in CDS prevalence. That acceleration seems describable in three periods: accelerated growth from 1978 to 1999 (once the CDS prevalence has passed levels observed in the 1910s), yet faster growth after 1999 to 2007, followed by accelerating growth after 2007, and a possible stabilization circa 2010. The so-called dot-com bust appears co-incident with the accelerated growth of CDS prevalence after 1999, while the accelerated growth of CDS prevalence since 2007 appears concomitant with broad use of social media as well as the onset of the Great Recession. The current level of CDS is nearly two standard deviations higher than what has been observed over the 20th century (excluding the 1899 peak).

While differences between the three languages are interesting, perhaps the most salient point is that for all three languages the expression of cognitive distortions has risen over the past three decades, resulting in a pronounced hockey-stick pattern signaling a surge in the prevalence levels of CDS, their lexical markers of cognitive distortions.

The timing of the US surge in CDS matches the late 1970s breaking point when wages no longer tracked increases in job productivity. This trend is tied to rising income inequality, reaching levels not seen since the 1930s. The phenomenon, observed in most developed economies including Germany and Spain as well as Latin America, was coupled with rapid increases in automation and the demand for high-skill labor. The Great Recession of 2007 might have exacerbated the effects of this already decades-long trend that started in the late 1970s. Widespread adoption of communication technologies like the internet, the World Wide Web, and social media may have contributed to greater social and political polarization at global levels. The language of this polarization may correspond to cognitive distortions, particularly “us-vs.-them thinking” (labeling and mislabeling), dichotomous reasoning, mind-reading, overgeneralizing, emotional reasoning, and catastrophizing.
