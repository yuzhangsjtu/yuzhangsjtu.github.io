> After 2010, China introduced **Home-Buying Restrictions (HBR)** in certain cities, aiming to directly intervene in real-estate transactions by limiting the number of homes a family could purchase, thereby cooling an overheated market.
>
> Families, however, strategically circumvented HBR through **fake divorces**. This study shows that in cities where HBR was enacted, both the crude divorce rate and the remarriage rate per 1  ,000 people rose. Relative to the control group, the treated group saw an increase of0.436 divorces and0.195 remarriages per thousand residents. This change accounts for 19.9 % of the overall rise in the national divorce rate, and at least 45 % of that rise can be attributed to fake divorces.
>
> In addition, the study finds that HBR reduced within-household wealth inequality but widened wealth inequality across households.

## Introduction

#### **Soaring House Prices**

- **Rapid increase of house prices in China** : Over the past two decades China’s housing prices have risen dramatically. According to the National Bureau of Statistics, the national average price of commercial housing reached 9,287 yuan per square meter in 2019, five times that of 1999. In the four first‑tier cities——Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Shenzhen——prices reached nine times their1999 levels. This real‑estate boom was a major driver of China’s overall economic growth.

- **Negative consequences of the housing boom** : Many scholars have criticized this long‑term price surge for causing real‑economy distortions, such as the misallocation of resources between real estate and manufacturing, the formation of asset bubbles, and declining enterprise productivity.

#### **Government Responses**

- **Initial economic measures** : Beginning in 2003, the Chinese government launched a series of economic instruments to cool an overheating housing market, including raising interest rates, increasing required down‑payment ratios, and imposing capital‑gains taxes and land‑transfer fees. However, most of these steps merely raised the transaction costs of housing purchases but failed to hold back the upward trend of prices.
- **The introduction of Home‑Buying Restrictions** : Because previous measures fell short of expectations, the central government decided to intervene directly in housing purchases. In early 2010 it rolled out the Home‑Buying Restriction (HBR) policy, which spelled out eligibility rules and capped the number of homes each family could own. Cities with excessively high and fast‑rising prices were ordered to implement the policy at the soonest possible time.

#### **The Effect of HBR**

- **Initial outcomes of the policy** : Between 2010 and 2014 HBR seemed to slow some of the buying frenzy, but its overall effect proved limited due to two factors: first, households’ expectation of persistent price appreciation remained strong, sustaining high housing demand; second, some families devised strategic ways of circumventing the through policy loopholes. （⬇️The chart below plots national average housing prices for 2000–2014; the vertical red line marks the enforcement of the restriction.）![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240901105149819.png)
- **Fake divorces** : A signature method for taking advantage of legal loopholes involved “fake divorce”， which allowed couples to gain extra home‑buying eligibility. Married couples would submit a nominal divorce and remarry only after purchasing a separate new home. This phenomenon made the news everywhere.; 《BBC News》, 《Global Times》, and *〈The Wall Street Journal〉* have all highlighted instances of China’s “fake divorce” (假 divorce).
