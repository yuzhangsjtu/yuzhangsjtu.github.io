> After 2010, China implemented **home-buying restriction** policies in a number of cities, aiming to intervene directly in real estate transactions by capping the number of homes a household could purchase, thereby curbing housing demand and cooling an overheated property market.
>
> However, households strategically circumvented the restriction through **fake divorce**. This study shows that in cities that implemented the restriction, both the divorce rate and the remarriage rate per thousand people rose. Relative to the control group, the treatment group recorded 0.436 more divorces and 0.195 more remarriages per thousand people. This change explains 19.9% of the overall rise in the national divorce rate, of which at least 45% can be attributed to fake divorces.
>
> In addition, the study finds that the home-buying restriction reduced within-household wealth inequality but widened wealth inequality between households.

## Introduction

#### **Soaring House Prices**

- **The rapid rise of house prices in China**: Over the past two decades, China's house prices have undergone substantial growth. According to data from the National Bureau of Statistics, in 2019 the national average price of commercial housing reached 9,287 yuan per square meter, five times that of 1999. In the four first-tier cities of Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Shenzhen, house prices reached nine times their 1999 levels. This boom in the real estate market played an important role in driving China's overall economic growth.

- **The negative effects of the real estate market**: Scholars have criticized this long-term rise in house prices for its negative effects on the real economy, including the misallocation of resources between real estate and manufacturing, the formation of asset bubbles, and the decline of enterprise productivity.

#### **The Government's Response**

- **Initial economic measures**: To restrain the overheating of the real estate market, since 2003 the Chinese government has adopted a series of economic measures, including raising interest rates, increasing down-payment ratios, and levying capital gains taxes and land transfer fees. However, most of these measures were implemented by raising the transaction costs of home purchases, yet they failed to effectively curb the upward trend of house prices.
- **The introduction of the home-buying restriction policy**: Because the earlier measures had failed to achieve the expected results, the central government decided to intervene directly in home-purchase transactions, and in early 2010 it implemented the Home-buying Restriction (HBR) policy. The policy explicitly stipulated home-purchase eligibility and the number of homes each household could buy, and it required earlier implementation especially in cities where house prices were excessively high and rising rapidly.

#### **The Impact of the Restriction Policy**

- **The policy's initial effects**: Between 2010 and 2014, the home-buying restriction appeared to curb the home-buying frenzy, but its overall effect was limited. There were two main reasons: first, people's expectations of continued house price increases remained strong, sustaining high home-buying demand; second, some households used strategic behavior to exploit policy loopholes and circumvent the restriction. (⬇️The figure below shows the trajectory of China's commercial housing prices from 2000 to 2014; the red vertical line marks the year the restriction policy began to be implemented.)![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240901105149819.png)
- **The fake divorce phenomenon**: One typical method of circumvention was to obtain additional home-purchase eligibility through "fake divorce." To qualify for a home purchase, couples would pretend to divorce and then remarry afterward. This phenomenon was widely discussed in the Chinese media; outlets such as BBC News, the Global Times, and The Wall Street Journal all reported on it, calling it China's "fake divorce."

#### **Research Questions and Purpose**

- **Core research question**: This article mainly examines the impact of the home-buying restriction policy on the fake divorce phenomenon, identifies the characteristics of the fake divorcers who responded to this policy, and further explores the effects of this fraudulent behavior on market efficiency, on wealth inequality within and between households, and on other social consequences.
- **Research methods and data**: To analyze these questions, this article uses city-level data as well as a 1% census sample, and through a quasi-experimental approach adopts the difference-in-differences (DID) method for analysis. The results show that the home-buying restriction policy significantly increased China's divorce rate, especially in the cities where the policy was implemented.

#### **Research Contribution**

- **Adds to the literature on exogenous shocks and divorce decisions**: This article complements existing research by exploring how exogenous shocks affect divorce decisions, providing evidence from the world's largest developing country. Theoretically, when the expected utility of maintaining a marriage falls below the expected utility of divorcing and possibly remarrying, couples may choose to divorce (Becker, 1974). Therefore, exogenous shocks (such as unexpected changes in income, wealth, and health) can affect the probability of divorce. Many empirical studies have explored how various exogenous shocks might affect divorce decisions, but most have focused on developed countries. Although developing countries differ significantly in institutions and culture, the literature investigating this question remains scarce. By examining the wealth shock triggered by the large-scale implementation of the home-buying restriction and its effect on marital stability, this article fills that gap. In addition, it explicitly identifies the individual characteristics that influence divorce decisions under this policy. These findings provide new micro-level evidence for the theoretical argument about how exogenous shocks affect divorce decisions, derived in a quasi-experimental setting.
- **Adds to the dialogue with the literature on the fraudulent behavior of economic agents**: Traditional research on fraudulent behavior assumes that economic agents are homogeneous and treats them as selfish egoists seeking to maximize their own expected utility. If the material and psychological benefits of cheating exceed the material and psychological costs, the agent will cheat (Gneezy, 2005). This article treats fake divorce as a kind of fraudulent behavior within the household, in which the couple makes a collective decision after weighing the costs and benefits of the fake divorce. Moreover, recent research on fraudulent behavior has relaxed the assumption of homogeneity among economic agents and explored the sources of heterogeneity in fraudulent behavior, including factors such as individual perceptions of fairness, the importance placed on social identity, and religion and culture. This article complements that literature by highlighting the role of social-identity factors (such as education).
- **Contributes to the literature studying the effect of the home-buying restriction policy on the housing market**: Most studies have examined the effect of the restriction policy on house prices, sales, housing affordability, and household savings. For example, Li et al. (2020) found that the home-buying restriction policy in megacities may produce negative spillover effects on the housing affordability of nearby unrestricted cities. Somerville et al. (2020) found that housing transaction volumes in four affected cities declined significantly, but house prices showed no obvious change. By contrast, Lu et al. (2021), by estimating a structural model of demand and supply, found that demand weakened in most affected cities and that real estate developers were willing to lower house prices to sell quickly. By identifying how the home-buying restriction policy affects marital status, this study complements that literature, which is of great significance for better understanding the effects of government intervention in the housing market.
- **Comparison with literature on the same topic**: The studies of Fan (2016) and Alm et al. (2022) are the most relevant to this study. Fan (2016), using city-level data covering the period from 2005 to 2011, studied the policy effects one year after the implementation of the home-buying restriction, finding that although the policy had no statistically significant effect on the number of divorces, it did significantly increase the number of remarriages. Alm et al. (2022) did not study actual divorce decisions but instead used internet search data from Baidu on divorce- and marriage-related keywords for 32 cities from 2009 to 2016. They found that the home-buying restriction policy significantly increased the search volume of these keywords, indicating that the policy's implementation was associated with an increased propensity for strategic divorce. This article builds on these two analyses but differs in several key respects. First, using real marriage data covering 289 prefecture-level cities and encompassing all cities that implemented the home-buying restriction, this article provides for the first time comprehensive evidence on the dynamic effects of the home-buying restriction policy on (fake) divorce decisions. This differs from Fan's (2016) analysis, which focused only on the one-year effect of the initial 17 cities' implementation of the restriction in 2010; it also differs from the analysis of Alm et al. (2022), who provided only evidence of the propensity for fake divorce. Second, by exploring restricted-access census data, this article identifies the principal responders to the home-buying restriction policy, which enriches our understanding of the policy's impact and provides empirical support for theoretical research on marriage and fraudulent behavior. Finally, this article assesses the potential welfare and policy implications associated with the fraudulent behavior, a step that goes beyond the scope of existing research.

## The Home-buying Restriction Policy

#### **The Rollout of the Home-buying Restriction Policy**

- **The central government's directive**: Because earlier market-regulation policies had failed to achieve the expected results, on 17 April 2010 the State Council issued the restriction policy, requiring cities with excessively high and rapidly rising house prices to set limits on the number of homes a household could buy, and making household registration (hukou) a condition of home-purchase eligibility.
- **Cities' response**: To implement this policy, local governments were required to act swiftly, especially in cities with rapidly rising house prices. If certain cities failed to respond in time, the Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural Development would summon the relevant city leaders for a regulatory talk. This marked the beginning of a vigorous tightening of the real estate market through administrative means.

#### **The Implementation of the Restriction Policy**

- **Beijing takes the lead**: In response to the central policy, on 30 April 2010 the Beijing municipal government issued specific implementation rules, stipulating that, regardless of the number of homes already owned, each household could purchase at most one additional home. By the end of 2010, a total of 17 cities had issued similar restriction measures.
- **Further restrictions**: In early 2011, the State Council further tightened home-purchase restrictions, requiring that local households (in which at least one member held local household registration) already owning two or more homes, or non-local households (in which no member held local household registration) already owning one or more homes, could not purchase new homes in the same city. Within less than a month, the original 17 cities and another 29 cities successively responded to this policy and implemented the same restriction measures. At that point, a total of 46 cities had implemented the restriction policy; these were mainly provincial capitals and other major cities with excessively high house prices. (⬇️The figure below shows the spatial distribution of the restriction cities.)![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240901111430508.png)

#### **The Specific Content of the Restriction Policy**

- **Limits on the number of homes per household**: The core content of the restriction policy was to treat a married couple as a single household unit and to stipulate that each household could purchase at most two homes. This provision prompted some households to obtain additional home-purchase eligibility through "fake divorce" in order to circumvent the restriction.
- **The motives and benefits of circumventing the policy**: Suppose a household already owns two or more homes; under the restriction policy, they cannot purchase new homes. However, through "fake divorce," one spouse can transfer all the homes to the other spouse through divorce and then, as a single person, purchase additional homes. The material benefit of this strategy was very substantial, because between 2011 and 2016 the average annual increase in commercial housing prices in China's 35 large and medium-sized cities was 8.2%.

#### **Adjustments to the Restriction Policy**

- **Loosening and cancellation of the policy**: This round of real estate market regulation policy continued until May 2015. As China's economy slowed, an oversupply emerged in the real estate market. To boost housing demand, China launched a "destocking" campaign, loosening the restriction measures through macroeconomic stimulus and specific policies supporting the housing sector. By mid-2014, 41 of the original 46 restriction cities had begun to cancel the restriction policy.
- **A new round of house price increases**: However, the loosening of the policy unexpectedly triggered a new round of irrational house price increases in some cities. As a result, two years after cancelling the restriction (in 2015 and 2016), some cities re-implemented the restriction policy.

## Methods and Data

#### **Baseline Regression Model**

To estimate the causal effect of the home-buying restriction (HBR) policy on marital status, the study adopts the difference-in-differences method. The basic regression equation is as follows:

$$
y_{it} = \alpha + \beta HBR_{it} + \gamma X_{it} + \mu_i + \phi_t + \omega_{jt} + S_i \times f(t) + \epsilon_{it}
$$
where:

- $y_{it}$  is the marital-status variable for city  $i$  in year  $t$  (such as the divorce rate or remarriage rate);
- $HBR_{it}$  is a binary variable indicating whether city  $i$  implemented the home-buying restriction policy in year  $t$ ;
- $X_{it}$  is a set of control variables, including the city's economic and demographic characteristics (such as GDP per capita, the share of industry, the degree of openness to the outside world, house prices, total living expenditure of urban residents, total population, and sex-ratio imbalance);
- $\mu_i$  and  $\phi_t$  denote city fixed effects and year fixed effects, respectively;
- $\omega_{jt}$  denotes province-by-year fixed effects, used to control for year-specific shocks at the provincial level;
- $S_i \times f(t)$  denotes the interaction between the selection criteria for HBR cities (such as the average house price during the pre-treatment period and its growth rate, and the 2005 sex ratio for the 15–21 age group) and a cubic polynomial function of time, used to flexibly control for trends arising from changes in these selection criteria over time;
- $\epsilon_{it}$  is the error term, and the standard errors in all regressions are clustered at the city level.

Model estimation: To address the heterogeneity problem that may arise because different cities implemented the HBR policy at different times, the study adopts the DID estimation method proposed by Sun and Abraham (2021), which is more robust to heterogeneity in treatment effects.

#### **Variables and Data Sources**

**Key Variables**

- **HBR policy variable**: The key explanatory variable is a dummy variable reflecting the implementation of the restriction policy. The authors manually collected this data by retrieving the official documents issued by each city, and created a dummy variable indicating whether HBR was implemented, as well as a variable indicating the number of homes each city's restriction permitted, in order to further test fake divorce behavior.
- **Marriage variables**: The divorce rate is defined as the number of divorces per thousand people in a city. To identify fake divorce, the study also uses the remarriage rate as a dependent variable, that is, the number of people per thousand who remarry the same person. In further analysis, the authors use census data to examine the individual attributes of those who divorced in response to the HBR policy.

**City-level Data**

- **Data sources**: The study uses city-level data covering 2004 to 2014. The marriage variables come from the National Civil Affairs Statistical Yearbook and the statistical yearbooks of individual cities; the house price and land supply data come from China Real Estate Information of the State Information Center; the other variables come mainly from the China City Statistical Yearbook and the China Regional Economic Statistical Yearbook.
- **Data screening**: To ensure the comparability of the data, the study excluded the four municipalities directly under the central government—Beijing, Tianjin, Shanghai, and Chongqing—because these cities are administered directly by the central government and are not comparable to other prefecture-level cities. At the same time, due to missing data, all cities in Tibet were also excluded. In addition, the authors winsorized the top and bottom 0.5 percentiles of the variable distributions to avoid reporting errors.

## Basic Results

#### **Divorce Rate and Remarriage Rate**

- **Divorce rate**: The restriction policy significantly increased the divorce rate in the cities that implemented it, leading to an increase of 0.436 divorces per thousand people. This result indicates that after the HBR policy was implemented, the divorce rate in treatment cities increased by 19.9% relative to control cities.
- **Remarriage rate**: After the HBR policy was implemented, the remarriage rate in treatment cities increased significantly. Specifically, relative to control cities, the HBR policy led to an increase of 0.195 remarriages per thousand people in treatment cities. This result indicates that after the policy was implemented, at least 45% (0.195/0.436) of the rise in the divorce rate can be explained as fake divorce behavior. #### **Robustness Analysis**

- The researchers conducted multiple robustness checks to verify the reliability of the results. These included using different definitions of the key variables (such as the number of divorces and remarriages per thousand adults) and addressing selection bias through Heckman two-stage estimation. The results show that the policy effects obtained by these different estimation methods are broadly consistent, further verifying the robustness of the study's conclusions.
- An event study further confirmed the reliability of the results.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240901113039396.png)

## Welfare and Further Policy Implications

#### **Efficiency Effects**

**The Loss of Market Efficiency**

- The home-buying restriction policy is a quantity-control policy that intervenes in the real estate market by restricting home-purchase eligibility, and it inevitably reduces market efficiency. To better understand this, the authors use a simplified partial-equilibrium model, assuming that housing supply is perfectly inelastic in the short run.
- In the initial situation (before the HBR policy was implemented), the market reaches equilibrium at point $E_0$, with a housing price of $p_0$ and a housing quantity of $Q^{noHBR}$. After the HBR policy is implemented, housing demand is forcibly reduced to $Q^{HBR}$ while supply remains unchanged, which causes the housing price to rise from $p_0$ to $p_1$. The total efficiency loss caused by the policy (Deadweight Loss, DWL) can be represented by the two regions A and B.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240901134436320.png)

**The Welfare Effects of Fake Divorce**

- Fake divorce behavior allows some households to circumvent the policy restriction, which is equivalent to increasing the market's housing quota  $\Delta Q$ , moving the actual equilibrium quantity from $Q^{HBR}$ to $Q^*$, with an actual equilibrium price of $p^*$. Because of the existence of fake divorce, the market efficiency loss induced by the policy is reduced to region A, while region B can be regarded as the welfare gain brought about by fake divorce behavior.
- By estimating the housing demand curve and related parameters, the authors quantified these welfare effects. The study finds that, on average, each city implementing the HBR policy incurs a total efficiency loss of about 29.81 billion yuan per year, while fake divorce behavior can reduce the loss by 552.6 million yuan per year, equivalent to 3.711% of the total efficiency loss.

#### **The Effects on Wealth Inequality**

**Within-household Inequality**

- A key step in fake divorce behavior is transferring ownership of an existing home to one spouse. This raises the issue of wealth inequality within the household (especially in terms of gender). If, during a fake divorce, the man is usually the main beneficiary of the home, then this behavior may aggravate gender inequality within the household.
- Using the records in the CFPS on whether the owner of a home's property rights is the husband or the wife, this article finds that after the restriction policy was implemented, **the probability that the woman owns the existing home increased significantly**, while the probability that the man owns the existing home decreased significantly. This indicates that women retain the existing home to compensate for the high risk involved in the fake divorce activity and for the potential reduction in within-household wealth inequality.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240901135250415.png)

**Between-household Inequality**

- Fake divorce behavior occurs mainly among wealthier households, because these households are better able to pay the extra down payment for additional homes and can further accumulate wealth by increasing the number of properties they own. Poorer households, limited by their resources, are often unable to benefit from this strategic behavior, which aggravates wealth inequality between households.
- Using data from the CHFS, the study finds that the household wealth Gini coefficient increased from 0.735 in 2011 to 0.887 in 2015, an intensification of inequality.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240901135651503.png)

#### **The Effects on Divorce Disputes and Marital Trust**

**Divorce Disputes Increase in Restriction Cities**

- **The fake becomes real**: A "trap divorce" is initially planned as fake by one spouse (the one who is fooled) but planned as real by the other. The party who genuinely wants a divorce usually first uses the pretext of buying an additional property to get the other party to agree to sign the divorce, and then, after the divorce certificate is issued, refuses to remarry.
- The analysis shows that after the restriction policy was implemented, the number of divorce disputes in the treatment cities increased significantly. **The Effects on Trust in Marriage and Family**

- The above shows that the restriction policy increased divorce disputes; following from this, a natural conjecture is that the restriction policy undermined people's belief in marital intimacy and family harmony.
- Using the relevant data from the CFPS, the results in Table D3 show that the restriction policy did indeed reduce people's belief in the importance of marital intimacy and family harmony.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240901140546680.png)

## Conclusion

- Relative to control cities, the restriction increased the divorce rate (remarriage rate) in the policy-implementing cities by an average of 0.436 (0.195) per thousand people. Based on the sample mean before the policy was implemented, the restriction policy can explain 19.9% of the overall rise in the national divorce rate, of which at least 45% can be explained as fake divorce;
- The elderly, the childless, the less educated, and the wealthier are the groups that responded most strongly to the policy's implementation;
- Fake divorce appears to have recovered a portion (about 3.711%) of the efficiency loss caused by the restriction policy;
- Fake divorce triggered significant **wealth redistribution** within and between households. Although within-household inequality decreased somewhat, because women tend to retain the property rights of the original home when completing a fake divorce, between-household inequality may increase, because the policy's main responders tend to be wealthy households that increase the value of their assets and accumulate more wealth through fraudulent behavior;
- Finally, the fake divorce behavior induced by the restriction policy may erode traditional Chinese family values and reduce people's trust in marriage.

## Extra

- I used to always feel that policies in the real estate domain, such as the **home-buying restriction** and the **90/70 policy**, were hard to use for DID or regression discontinuity, because such policies are hard to regard as exogenous, and there are always anticipatory responses and **spillover effects**. But in recent years there have been more and more related papers—in REStat, JPubE, and The Journal of World Economy. That is, "the question is primary, identification is secondary"—I suppose that's how it is.
- I think the original article's conclusion that **the restriction policy increased between-household wealth inequality** needs to be **interpreted with caution**. Figure 7A shows the increase in the between-household wealth Gini coefficient between 2011 and 2015, indicating that household wealth inequality widened over this period. But the connection of this result to the restriction policy is debatable. A more likely explanation is that the widening of wealth inequality was due to rising house prices rather than to the restriction policy. Another possibility is that, without the restriction policy, the household wealth gap brought about by rising house prices would have been even larger than what the authors measured; from this perspective, the restriction policy actually slowed the widening of the wealth gap.
- In the **efficiency analysis** part (Figure 4), because of the restriction policy, effective demand falls and supply is inelastic in the short run, in which case the price should fall. The authors also mention in the literature section that "Lu et al. (2021), by estimating a structural model of demand and supply, found that demand weakened in most affected cities and that real estate developers were willing to lower house prices to sell quickly." But in Figure 4 and the original text, the statement is that after effective demand falls, house prices rise. On this result, GPT's view is that "although total demand fell, the demand that remained is 'rigid demand,' and these buyers are more willing to accept higher prices, thereby pushing market prices up." The efficiency part of the original article is a simplified analysis, since this is, after all, not the focus of the paper.
