> Experiments conducted in rice-farming and non-rice-farming regions of China found that, among equally "fresh-faced" first-year university students, participants from rice-farming areas contributed more in cooperative games and also imposed harsher punishment on free-riders. The results show that the difference between rice and wheat cultivation is one of the factors shaping the origins of cooperative preferences. This finding is also corroborated by real-world evidence: residents of rice-farming regions provide more public goods, such as contributing more Wikipedia edits, and their streets are also cleaner.

**Title:** Rice Farming and the Origins of Cooperative Behaviour

**Journal:** The Economic Journal

**Published online:** 14 April 2023

<br/>

**Gaps:** 

- There are considerable differences in economic preferences within and across countries. Preferences, especially trust and patience, are causally related to economic development. Culture (or social norms) influences the economy (for instance, the common saying that East Asians are Heaven's chosen labourers, hh), so what influences culture in turn?
- The preference for cooperation is a key factor affecting economic development, the rule of law, and institutional quality. A growing body of evidence shows that, like trust and patience, the preference for cooperation also differs across cultures and societies. The cultural and historical origins of cooperative behaviour remain largely underexplored: did historical agricultural practices that required a high degree of cooperation and coordination shape current cultural norms of cooperation, and if so, how?

**Aims:** Recruit first-year Han university students with local household registration in rice-farming regions (Zhejiang and Hunan) and non-rice-farming regions (Hebei and Shandong) to conduct a series of behavioural experiments, investigating the relationship between crop-farming differences and cooperative behaviour. Building on the famous *Science* paper about rice in the South and wheat in the North ([Large-Scale Psychological Differences Within China Explained by Rice Versus Wheat Agriculture](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1246850)) concerning collectivism and holistic thinking, this study broadens the perspective to cooperative behaviour.

**Characteristics of rice farming:**

- Requires: labour-intensive + water-intensive
- Growing rice requires levelling the paddy field (ploughing)
- From seedling to mature rice, transplanting requires labour exchange (the window is short; a single small household cannot manage it alone and needs help from others). The labour required for rice is about twice that of wheat or maize.
- Monsoon climate, with unstable water resources, requires more human collaboration to provide a stable water supply; water flows and has the characteristic of "all thrive together, all fail together," so it requires management beyond the single household to balance risks.
- Collective action can provide the public resources needed for rice farming and lower its costs.
- Rice yields a lot per mu, so people in suitable areas are more inclined to choose to grow rice.

> ⬆️ Rice farming is mutually beneficial, and people form cultural norms from the practice, whereas wheat farming does not have the characteristics above.

**Importance of punishment:**

- Punishment internalises cooperative norms more successfully than other means; punishing free-riders is more important for the evolution of cooperative norms than the direct benefits of cooperation.
- In rice production, shirking invites punishment.
- Punishment in rice farming:
  - Punishment can serve as one of the three conditions for maintaining cooperative strategies (all of which rice farming satisfies):
    - A positive personal gain from successful cooperation
    - The observable behaviour of every member of the community to the others
    - Enforceable sanctions
- In an acquaintance society, free-riding in rice cultivation is easy to identify, and punishments include refusing to help, being excluded from the irrigation network, and facing social ostracism (there are many documented cases in East Asia).

> In summary, growing rice is highly attractive to farmers because its yields are greater than those of alternatives. At the same time, it requires small-scale and large-scale cooperation on numerous issues throughout the year. Repeated interactions across multiple domains are well suited to situations of punishing free-riders. Moreover, models of cultural evolution identify punishment as a key factor in the emergence of cooperative norms. These norms can be transmitted from generation to generation, presumably surviving to the present day, and help to sustain the cooperative equilibrium.

**Data and experiment:** 

- Experimental sites and subjects:
  - Determining the experimental sites: a region is a rice-farming area if its rice-planting area exceeds 50% of arable land area (output was not chosen, as it is hard to compare and fluctuates widely); data from 1996 (the earliest available at the county level) were used; Zhejiang and Hunan have a high proportion of paddy fields; Shandong and Hebei are non-rice-farming regions (and have long been so historically).
  - Subjects: first-year Han university students with local household registration who have resided locally for a long time (checking their fathers' household registration); recruiting freshmen reduces indoctrination effects (a blank slate is good for experiments; before university they were all numbly doing exam problems). They were randomly selected and paid for answering; they are not farmers, which excludes the influence of having personally participated in agricultural practice.
- Main experiment — Public goods games (PGG)
  - Groups of 4, an 8-period PGG, played twice; subjects knew the game would be played a second time but did not know the specifics of the second round. The meaning of the score is an individual's degree of contribution in the PGG, i.e., the individual's contribution to the collective.
  - No-punishment treatment:
    - An individual has 20 points and can donate points to the group account; the group's total points are multiplied by 1.6 and distributed among all members.
    - After all participants have made their decisions, each participant's donation amount, the income they receive from the group account, and their total income for that period are displayed on their computer screen.
    - The contribution of each group member is displayed on the computer screen in a random order in each period (anonymously).

  - Punishment treatment:
    - Each participant chooses the number of punishment tokens to assign to other group members. A maximum of 10 punishment tokens can be assigned to one group member. Each token costs the punisher one point and reduces the sanctioned participant's income by three points. Received punishment tokens cannot reduce a participant's income below zero. However, for certain combinations of tokens received and assigned, a negative payoff is possible. This information is clear to participants. (Who issued the punishment is anonymous.)

- Other experiments:
  - Triad task (pick two of three to pair): train, car, tracks; collectivism (relation-based, train + tracks) / individualism (object-based, train + car); the same as in *Science* 2014.
  - Dictator Game (DG), Ultimatum Game (UG), Stag-Hunt game (SH)
- Other data:
  - Provincial GDP per capita
  - Terrain slope, soil depth, land-cover patterns
  - Rice-farming suitability index (IV)
  - Prefecture-level patents per capita
  - 2000 Census data, used to construct a prefecture-level population-migration matrix, which was used to calculate the migration-adjusted rice-farming suitability index and net migration flow at the prefecture level.


**Results:**

- Regardless of whether there was a punishment treatment, the average contribution degree of rice-area groups was higher.
- For every 10% increase in the rice percentage, contribution increases by about 0.12 percentage points; (the coefficient is significant but small, yet the rice-share gap between the two types of regions reaches 80%, meaning the actual contribution point is about 0.8 × 0.12 = 0.96).
- Rice areas impose higher punishment on free-riders. Participants from rice areas assign on average about 0.23 (0.29 × 0.8) more punishment points than participants from non-rice areas. The percentage of paddy fields is positively correlated with both the probability and the intensity of punishing free-riders.
- Other evidence: rice areas have more Wikipedia edits and bytes contributed, and their streets are also cleaner.
