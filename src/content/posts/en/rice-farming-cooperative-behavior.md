> Experiments conducted in both rice-farming and non-rice-farming regions of China found that, among equally “fresh-faced” first-year university students, those from rice-farming areas contributed more in cooperative games and also imposed harsher punishment on free-riders. The results suggest that differences between rice and wheat cultivation are one of the factors shaping the origins of cooperative preferences. This finding is also corroborated by real-world evidence: residents of rice-farming regions provide more public goods, such as contributing more edits to Wikipedia, and their streets tend to be cleaner.

**Title:** Rice Farming and the Origins of Cooperative Behaviour

**Journal:** The Economic Journal

**Published online:** 14 April 2023

<br/>

**Gaps:** 

- Economic preferences vary considerably both across and within countries. Preferences, especially trust and patience, are causally related to economic development. Culture (or social norms) influences the economy (for instance, the common joke that East Asians are Heaven’s Chosen labourers), so what shapes culture in turn?
- The preference for cooperation is a key factor affecting economic development, the rule of law, and institutional quality. A growing body of evidence shows that, like trust and patience, the preference for cooperation also differs across cultures and societies. The cultural and historical origins of cooperative behaviour remain largely underexplored. Did historical agricultural practices requiring a high degree of cooperation and coordination shape current cultural norms of cooperation, and to what extent?

**Aims:**  Recruit first-year Han university students with local household registration from rice-farming regions (Zhejiang and Hunan) and non-rice-farming regions (Hebei and Shandong) to conduct a series of behavioural experiments, investigating the relationship between crop-farming differences and cooperative behaviour. Building on the famous *Science* paper about “rice in the South, wheat in the North” ([Large-Scale Psychological Differences Within China Explained by Rice Versus Wheat Agriculture](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1246850)), which examined collectivism and holistic thinking, this study broadens the perspective to cooperative behaviour.

**Characteristics of rice farming:**

- Requires: labour‑intensive + water‑intensive cultivation
- Growing rice demands levelling the paddy fields (ploughing)
- From seedlings to mature rice, transplanting often requires labour exchange (the window is short; a single small household cannot manage it alone and needs help from others). The labour needed for rice is about twice that required for wheat or maize.
- Monsoon climate means that water resources are unstable, requiring more human collaboration to provide a stable water supply; water flows and carries the characteristic of “all thrive together, all fail together,” so its management must extend beyond individual households to balance risks.
- Collective action can provide the public resources needed for rice farming and lower its costs.
- Rice yields are high per mu, so people in areas suitable for it will be more likely to choose to grow rice.

> ⬆️  Rice farming entails mutual benefit; people form cultural norms through this practice, whereas wheat farming lacks the traits listed above.

**Importance of punishment:**

- Punishment internalises cooperative norms more successfully than other means; punishing free-riders is more important for the evolution of cooperative norms than the direct benefits of cooperation are.
- In rice farming, shirking invites punishment.
- Punishment in rice farming:
  - Punishment can act as one of the three conditions for maintaining cooperative strategies (all of which rice farming satisfies):
      - A positive personal gain from successful cooperation
      - Observable behaviour by every member of the community to the others
      - Enforceable sanctions
- In acquaintance society, free-riding in rice cultivation is easy to identify. Punishments include refusing to help, being excluded from the irrigation network, and facing social ostracism (many case records from East Asia exist).

> In summary, growing rice is highly attractive to farmers because its yields are greater than those of alternatives. At the same time, it requires cooperation on both small and large scales across numerous issues throughout the year. Repeated interactions over multiple domains make it especially suitable for punishing free-riders. Moreover, models of cultural evolution identify punishment as a key factor in the emergence of cooperative norms. These norms can be transmitted from generation to generation, quite likely surviving to the present day, and help to sustain the cooperative equilibrium.

**Data and experiment:** 

- Sites and subjects:
  - Determining the experimental sites: a region is denoted as a rice‑farming area where the rice‑planting acreage exceeds 50% of total arable land area (output was not chosen because it is difficult to compare and fluctuates widely). Data from 1996 (earliest available at the county level) were used. Zhejiang and Hunan had a high proportion of paddy fields; Shandong and Hebei are historically non‑rice‑farming regions.
  - Subjects: first-year Han university students with local household registration who grew up long-term in the locale (their fathers’ household registration were checked). Recruiting freshmen reduces indoctrination effects (a tabula rasa of sorts for the experiments; until entering university they were largely mechanically cramming for exams). Subjects were randomly chosen and paid for their participation; they are not farmers, which excludes direct-effects of hands-on experience with agriculture.
- Main experiment — Public goods games (PGG)
  - Groups of 4, 8-period PGG, played twice; participants knew the game would be played a second time but did not know the specifics of the second round. The score here constitutes the individual’s contribution degree in the PGG, i.e., the extent of individual contribution to the collective.
  - No-punishment treatment:
    - Each individual has 20 tokens and can donate to the group account; total group tokens are multiplied by 1.6 and distributed equally among all members.
    - After all participants have decided, each participant’s donation amount, their proceeds from the group account, and their income for that round are shown on their computer screen.
    - The contributions of each group member for each period are displayed on the screen in a random order (anonymously).

  - Punishment treatment:
      - Each participant chooses a number of punishment tokens to assign to other group members. A maximum of 10 punishment tokens can be assigned to one group member. Each token costs the punisher one point and reduces the sanctioned participant’s income by three points. Received punishment tokens cannot lower a participant’s earnings below zero. However, for particular combinations of tokens received and assigned, it is possible to have negative staged payoffs. The participants fully understand these rules. (The identity of whoever-issued-the-punishment remains anonymous.)

- Other experiments:
  - Triad task (mark a pair from three alternatives): train, car, tracks; serves to distinguish collectivism (relations-based; the reported pair is “train and tracks”) / individualism (feature-based; “train and car”). This task is identical to the one used in the *Science* 2014 paper.
  - Dictator Game (DG), Ultimatum Game (UG), Stag-Hunt game (SH)
- Additional data:
  - Provincial GDP per capita
  - Terrain slope, soil depth, land-cover patterns
  - Rice-farming suitability index (IV)
  - Sub-provincial-level (prefectural-level city) data on patents per capita
  - 2000 Census data, used to construct a prefecture-level population-immigration matrix. This matrix bore into the calculation of a migration-adjusted rice-farming suitability index and net migration flow at the prefectural level.
