> Using data on U.S. male household heads born 1905–1915, observed at the 1940 census and linked to Social Security death records, this paper finds—through a **sibling fixed effects** analysis—that homeownership in young adulthood (roughly ages 25–35) yields approximately **four months** of additional lifespan. This effect is broadly similar across Black and White populations, yet because Black populations have lower overall homeownership rates, it exacerbates the racial gap in longevity. The study argues that housing affects health and lifespan not only through **wealth accumulation** but also through multiple pathways including **social capital**, **housing conditions**, and **psychological factors**, carrying important implications for advancing housing-equity policies and alleviating social inequality.

**Title:** The Longevity Benefits of Homeownership: Evidence From Early Twentieth-Century U.S. Male Birth Cohorts

**Journal:** *Demography*

**Author:**

- <u>Casey F. Breen</u>: He is a Senior Postdoctoral Fellow in Computational Social Science. Before joining Oxford, he completed his PhD in Demography and MA in Biostatistics at the University of California, Berkeley.

**Publication date:** 4 December 2024

## Introduction

#### **Housing and the American Dream**

In the American social context, homeownership (**homeownership**) has long been regarded as an important symbol of achieving the "**American Dream**" (Samuel 2012) and simultaneously serves as the primary vehicle for household wealth (Apgar and Di 2006). Existing research shows that homeownership is typically accompanied by greater wealth, better health status, and stronger social capital (Finnigan 2014; Laaksonen et al. 2009; Rolfe et al. 2020). For instance, empirical analyses based on England and other European countries indicate that homeowners tend to hold an advantage in self-rated health and mental health (Munford et al. 2020; Elsinga et al. 2007). Data from Finland even reveal that the **mortality risk** of homeowners is significantly lower than that of renters (Laaksonen et al. 2009).

However, homeowners' "inherent advantages" in wealth, education, social networks, and other respects may also give rise to a pure **selection effect**—that is, it is not homeownership itself that directly produces health or longevity benefits, but rather that those who are wealthier or of higher social status happen to be better positioned to buy homes and to enjoy superior medical and social resources (Dietz and Haurin 2003). For precisely this reason, whether this observed positive correlation can be elevated to a "**causal relationship**" has always remained contested in academic circles. The central question on which past literature has focused is: "**Does owning a home truly cause longer lifespan and better health? Or is it merely co-associated with unobserved factors such as wealth and family background?**"

Understanding the specific historical and institutional background of the United States helps in probing the essence of this question: in the early 20th century, especially between 1900 and 1940, the U.S. experienced drastic economic fluctuations (including the Great Depression) and the initial formation of its **housing credit system**. At the same time, American society has long featured marked disparities between Black and White populations in homeownership rates and housing conditions (Collins and Margo 2011; Taylor 2019). This racial inequality is reflected both in opportunities to buy homes and access to credit, and in the long-term accumulation process of property value. If homeownership truly has a causal effect on longevity, then the historical institutional exclusion of non-White groups (such as "redlining" policies) may have substantially influenced the racial health gaps of both contemporary and later generations (Rothstein 2017; Graetz and Esposito 2023).

Nevertheless, specific quantitative research on the effect of "**buying a home in early adulthood**" on "**longevity in late life**" remains relatively scarce within the U.S. domestic literature. While some European evidence (such as research on England's "**Right to Buy**" policy; Munford et al. 2020) provides inferences of a causal link between owner-occupied housing and self-rated health, its context differs markedly from the U.S. housing market and institutions, and it does not focus on the ultimate mortality outcome (**mortality outcome**). Therefore, a data study based on the U.S. context, possessing both longitudinal completeness and a sufficient sample size, is needed in order to obtain a more rigorous causal identification of the relationship between homeownership and longevity.

#### **Research Value and Core Questions**

From academic and public-policy perspectives, clarifying **whether** owning a home, and to **what extent** it extends people's lifespans, is highly significant. If homeownership is confirmed to be one of the "**causal drivers**" of longevity, then expanding access to affordable home purchases for disadvantaged groups (especially Black populations) could play a role in narrowing racial health gaps (Finnigan 2014). Conversely, if the association behind it is driven solely by shared unobserved factors such as household wealth or social status, then policies that merely encourage homeownership may struggle to produce the anticipated effects on the issues of public health and longevity inequality.

On this basis, the author poses two **core questions** in this article:

- Is there a definite **causal relationship** between owning a home and late-life longevity?
- How did this relationship manifest in early 20th-century U.S. history, and in particular, what differences exist across races (Black and White)?

#### **Research Design and Contributions**

To address the above questions, this study draws on the 1920 and 1940 U.S. census (complete-count) data, together with the 1975–2005 Social Security death records (Social Security Death Master File), to construct a longitudinal database. This enables the author to:

- Observe individuals' **homeownership status** (own or rent) in 1940;
- Obtain their complete time of death, thereby measuring lifespan (**Age at Death**);
- Trace further back to 1920, using household information to identify "**sibling pairs**" (sibling pairs), so that sibling fixed effects can be employed to remove a vast array of hard-to-observe family-background and genetic differences.

The advantage of this sibling-based study design is that parental wealth, early family environment, and even genetic factors—which cannot be observed in traditional regressions—can all be **controlled** for within siblings (Conley et al. 2007; Halpern-Manners et al. 2020). Thus, this research is not only able to quantify the independent effect of homeownership on lifespan fairly accurately, but also holds the promise of filling a gap in the existing U.S. literature on this topic, while providing a new perspective for examining the link between housing policy and public health.

## Theoretical Framework

**Homeownership** has potential pathways of influence on individual lifespan, which can be summarized into four aspects: **(1) wealth accumulation; (2) social networks and social capital; (3) housing conditions; (4) psychological benefits**. On this basis, the author emphasizes that these mechanisms have features of mutual entanglement and **dynamic accumulation** (DiPrete and Eirich 2006)—the so-called "**cumulative advantage**" process.

#### **Wealth Accumulation**

- **Core point**: A home is the largest single asset of American households (Apgar and Di 2006; Killewald and Bryan 2016). Compared with renters, homeowners can accumulate wealth through property appreciation, mortgage repayment (equivalent to passive saving), and tax benefits (such as the mortgage interest deduction).
- **Impact on health and longevity**: Holding a higher or more stable level of wealth typically means having more resources to invest in healthcare, nutrition, home environment improvements, and the like (Berkman and Syme 1979). A sense of economic security simultaneously lowers the health risks caused by poverty or financial stress, thereby raising an individual's health level and survival chances over the medium-to-long term.
- **Insights from empirical research**: Prior literature notes that an additional year, or several additional years, of homeownership can bring thousands to tens of thousands of dollars in wealth gains (Turner and Luea 2009; Killewald and Bryan 2016), and these "invisible savings" may play a key role in a person's long-term health planning.

#### **Social Networks and Social Capital**

- **A perspective at the intersection of sociology and public health**: Homeownership is generally associated with more stable community ties, a lower frequency of moving, and a stronger sense of local belonging (Rohe and Stewart 1996; Manturuk et al. 2010).

- **Specific mechanisms**:

  - Homeowners tend to be more willing and more motivated to participate in community affairs and to build long-term, stable relationship networks with their neighbors.

  - This in turn brings higher social support, social cohesion, and trust, helping individuals obtain timely assistance when facing sudden illness, managing chronic disease, and acquiring health information (Berkman et al. 1992; Smith and Christakis 2008).

  - Social networks also exert stronger oversight and influence over personal health behaviors; for example, neighbors' health initiatives and mutual supervision may all reduce the incidence of unhealthy behaviors.

- **Possible contribution to longevity**: Social capital often has a "protective" function, reducing the health risks an individual incurs from social isolation (Cobb 1976). Therefore, if homeowner status helps form more solid interpersonal circles, then over the long course of life this social-relationship resource can markedly slow aging and lower mortality risk.

#### **Housing Conditions**

- **The tight link between housing quality and health**: Existing literature notes that many rental dwellings in the first half of the 20th-century U.S., owing to overcrowding, poor sanitation, and inadequate ventilation or lighting, may have increased health risks such as infectious disease and accidental injury (Krieger and Higgins 2002). Especially at that time (the 1920s–1940s), respiratory infectious diseases such as tuberculosis, pneumonia, and influenza had a substantial impact on mortality (Feigenbaum et al. 2022).
- **The difference between homeowners and renters in improving the housing environment**: Homeowners have both the motivation and the financial means to repair or renovate on their own initiative, improving indoor ventilation, sanitary facilities, and structural safety, as well as handling potential sources of pollution in the home such as lead paint and formaldehyde (Haurin et al. 2002). Renters, by contrast, depend more heavily on landlords or property firms for housing maintenance and cannot easily undertake costly renovations or modifications.
- **Long-term cumulative effects**: Better housing conditions exert a sustained influence over the course of an individual's life across decades, especially in guarding against infectious disease, the worsening of chronic disease, and accidental injury. On this basis, the author holds that improvement in the physical quality of housing can be regarded as a "direct and substantive pathway" through which homeownership affects lifespan.

#### **Psychological Benefits**

- **Sense of control and security**: Homeowner status is typically accompanied by greater autonomy and a sense of control over life; by contrast, renter households face the risk of rent increases or being forced to move, with greater long-term residential uncertainty (Manturuk 2012). This difference may further widen the gap in mental and psychological health.
- **Social status and self-esteem**: In the American cultural context, "owning one's own home" is also a symbol of social status, able to give an individual a sense of self-esteem and accomplishment (Rakoff 1977; Tremblay and Dillman 1983). Psychological research has long noted that self-efficacy and subjective well-being are positively associated with health and longevity.
- **Coping with life stress**: Having a fixed residence allows homeowners to avoid the anxiety of repeated moves or the blow of eviction (Acharya et al. 2022). Stable housing may improve quality of life across dimensions such as marriage, child-rearing, and social participation, indirectly benefiting physical health and longevity.

## Data and Methods

#### **Data Sources**

- **1920 U.S. Census**: Provides complete individual information for the national population in 1920, including name, sex, birth year, relationship to the household head, and so on. It allows identification of brothers (siblings) still in the child/adolescent stage within the same household, laying the foundation for later constructing "sibling pairs."
- **1940 U.S. Census**: Collected information on all household heads and family members of that year, including housing ownership status (owned or rented), home value/rent, years of education, income, occupation, marital status, place of residence, and so on. It allows the homeownership status of individuals in their young-to-middle adulthood (roughly ages 24–35) to be obtained, and records their socioeconomic characteristics.
- **Social Security death records**: Social Security Death Master File, **DMF**, 1975–2005. Contains the death information of the vast majority of Social Security benefit recipients in the United States who died in the years 1975–2005, covering their dates of birth and death.

#### **Sample Linkage**

**The 1920→1940 link**

- The target population consists of **males** aged 4–15 in 1920 (corresponding to those born 1905–1915); first, in 1920 it is determined whether they lived with brothers (i.e., whether the household contained multiple individuals with the same surname or identifiable as brothers).
- Then, using standardized names, birth year, birthplace, and other information, the ABE algorithm (Abramitzky et al. 2012, 2014, 2021) is used to locate the record of the same person in the 1940 census.
- Once matched successfully, it can be observed in 1940 whether these individuals were household heads, whether they owned a home, and what their education and occupation were.

**The 1940→DMF link**

- Next, the 1940 census records are matched with the DMF in order to obtain individuals' exact time of death (if death occurred between 1975 and 2005), and ultimately to compute their age at death.
- Only individuals successfully located in the DMF with death information, and for whom birth and death dates can be confirmed to be consistent, are included in the final analysis.

**Sibling sample**

- To conduct the sibling fixed effects analysis, within the "sibling pairs" already identified in 1920, each brother must be matchable to a record in both the 1940 census and the DMF.
- The resulting "sibling sample" (roughly more than 85,000 people) possesses within-family matching information as well as 1940 homeownership status and complete death records.

#### **Methods**

The paper first targets the entire sample that was successfully matched to the 1940 census and the Social Security death records (N ≈ 1.4 million people), running **ordinary least squares (OLS)** regressions separately for Black and White populations, with the model taking the form
$$
D_i = \beta_0 + \lambda_{\text{byear}} + \delta_{\text{homeown}} + \varepsilon
$$
where:

- $D_i$: the age of death of individual $i$;
- $\lambda_{\text{byear}}$: fixed-effect controls for birth year (because each birth year can be observed over a different age range within the death-observation window of 1975–2005);
- $\delta_{\text{homeown}}$: the core independent variable, indicating whether an individual owned a home in 1940 (1 = homeowner, 0 = renter).

Under this most parsimonious model, the author can obtain a homeowner-versus-renter lifespan difference "without any adjustment for other covariates." Subsequently, in later models the author adds observed control terms such as education, income, occupation, and marital status, in order to compare the lifespan difference "before versus after controls."

To approximate the causal effect of "whether homeownership leads to longer lifespan," it is necessary to overcome the vast unobserved heterogeneity at the family level (such as parental wealth, childhood nutritional environment, genetics, etc.). The paper therefore constructs a **sibling fixed effects (Sibling FE)** analytical framework:
$$
D_i = \beta_0 + \lambda_{\text{byear}} + \delta_{\text{homeown}} + \pmb{\beta} \mathbf{X}_{\text{controls}} + \Omega_{\text{SiblingFE}} + \varepsilon
$$
where:

- $\Omega_{\text{SiblingFE}}$ denotes the sibling fixed effects, meaning that only the within-family differences between brothers in "whether they owned a home" are used to explain their differences in age at death;
- $\mathbf{X}_{\text{controls}}$ are individual-level control variables such as education, income, marital status, and birth order. The author specifically introduces a variable for "whether one is the oldest observed brother," to capture the potential influence of parents possibly investing more heavily in the eldest son.

**Principle**: By placing all brothers of the same family into a single regression and differencing within the family, the influence of shared environment and genes is removed, thereby coming closer to inferring the "**net effect**" of homeownership (Conley et al. 2007; Halpern-Manners et al. 2020). The author emphasizes that if, within the same family, one brother bought a home in 1940 and another did not, then the difference in their late-life lifespan is more likely to be caused by homeownership itself, rather than by parental wealth or childhood environment.

## Results

- **No controls → progressive controls → sibling fixed effects**: With no control variables, the difference in age at death between homeowners and renters is about 0.64 years; after adding control variables, the difference falls slightly to roughly 0.53 years, and remains significant. After adding sibling fixed effects, the difference declines further to about 0.31 years (converting to roughly 3.7–**4 months**), still statistically significant. ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250105162617511.png)

- **Subsample restrictions**: To confirm that the result is not affected by residual within-sibling differences (such as differences in geographic residence, education, etc.), the author runs sibling fixed effects regressions separately on multiple subsets, including: selecting only brothers with the same marital status; keeping only brothers whose years of education differ by no more than 2; keeping only brothers who lived in the same county in 1940; keeping only brothers whose birth years differ by no more than 5; and various combinations of these conditions. The results show that the estimates from these subsamples still largely fall around 0.25–0.35 years, fairly consistent with the 0.31 years from the full sibling sample, indicating that this conclusion is relatively robust. ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250105163125906.png)
- **Stratification by home value**: Homeowners are divided into four tiers according to their home value's percentile within their state, to observe whether "higher-value homes bring a larger lifespan advantage." Preliminary results indicate that, without sibling fixed effects, the higher the home value, the greater the "added benefit" to lifespan. But once sibling fixed effects are included, this gradient effect tends to become insignificant. The author interprets this as: **homeowner status itself** plays the main role in extending lifespan, while differences in home value do not exhibit a strong gradient effect within the same family. ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250105163241382.png)


## Conclusions and Discussion

- **Owning a home means living longer**: Buying a home in young adulthood (ages 25–35) does bring an average of about **4 months** of additional lifespan. This figure counts as a substantive effect in public-health and socioeconomic research, comparable in magnitude to the effect sizes of some educational or early-environment interventions.
- **This positive effect is similar in magnitude between Black and White populations**, indicating that homeowner status itself can produce a roughly consistent lifespan-extending effect across different racial groups. The deeper reason that Black populations actually have generally lower lifespans is the historical severe inequality in opportunities for homeownership, not that the benefits of homeownership fail to work for Black people.
- **Limitations**:
  - Sibling fixed effects can only control for factors that are the same at the "family level," but if systematic differences still exist between brothers in individual personality, personal ability, parental favoritism, and the like, and these differences are correlated with the home-purchase decision or with lifespan, then the estimates may still contain residual bias.
  - The paper's sample is limited to males who were household heads in 1940, and does not include women or non-household-heads (such as co-residing children or other relatives), which to some extent limits the external generalizability of the results.
  - The matching of historical records has a lower success rate for Black people and those of lower economic status, which to some extent weakens the representativeness for certain groups.
  - The socioeconomic and housing-finance systems underwent enormous change in the postwar and modern eras—for example, suburbanization, the expansion of federal housing assistance, the civil rights movement, and the influence of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac on the housing market—so the mechanisms by which contemporary homeownership affects health may already be quite different.
