Before you know it, the month is already drawing to a close. October passed very quickly. A great deal happened -- some anxious, some joyful, full of challenges, yet brimming with hope.

For many people, October is a season of harvest. Theses, scholarships, offers of every kind. And me? I've become a "triple-nothing" person.

This state of affairs was inevitable -- the bitter fruit I must swallow for the two or three years I let slip through my fingers in the past.

I got through a National Day holiday devoid of surprises or anticipation, an eight-day stay-in tour of Kunshan Building. I wrote no paper, nor did I revise one. I cancelled the trip back to Wuhan; everyone is busy, and each has their own path to walk. Rongrong and his girlfriend both won the National Scholarship at the same time. That is just how it is with people who are too outstanding. I admire them. I am grateful to Rongrong for helping me obtain all sorts of materials. On my senior schoolmate's advice, I finished revising the article and submitted it to the *Journal of Natural Resources*; there has been no reply so far. It seems there are too many conferences in October and the editors are busy commuting -- though, of course, the greater likelihood is that the article is too simple and lacks innovation. Little by little, anticipation turned into disappointment.

The important event in the middle of the month was going to Zhejiang University for a conference. I arranged to meet Professor Tan Rong, and out of nowhere a yearning for Zhejiang University's School of Public Affairs was kindled in me. It seems that, after all, I am bound to return to a humanities faculty, to deal with a complex society made up of "people." I have confirmed that I lack the ability to understand the world from a macro perspective; what I do have is an interest in definite, concrete events and people. The core of Professor Tan's research is "governance": starting from transaction costs, and by understanding the relationships between central and local governments, between government and market, and between market and non-governmental organizations, he seeks fairness and efficiency in governance. This, it seems, is the kind of "solving a scientific problem" I have been pursuing -- problems that have to do with people.

Zijingang's location is much better than Xianlin's, not nearly so desolate. The streets are lively, and the architecture of the Great Western Campus is very good-looking, the kind of style I like. For now, the college building still has no graduate study room, which is a bit odd. Cycling out in the rain that day, carrying my things, to go and see Professor Tan was probably something I will never forget for the rest of my life. A bit dim, a bit foolish. Looking back on my original intentions: I wanted to go to the geographic institute in order to ride on someone's coattails and churn out papers; I wanted to go to Shanghai because Shanghai has a glamorous, dazzling world. Neither of these choices was very real, nor sincere. And the geographic institute and Shanghai Jiao Tong might not accept me either. As for Hangzhou: West Lake is not particularly nice to look at, but Zijingang has an everyday, down-to-earth liveliness. Professor Tan did not say outright that he wanted me, but at least there is a certain possibility. I must seize this olive branch.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/10/29/SLMpsgOzZaivrJQ.jpg)

I had a meal with a friend at West Intime in Hangzhou. They have already stepped into society and are thinking about housing, marriage, and career, whereas I am still "not especially focused" on my studies, as if I am being quite irresponsible towards myself. When we parted, he said I really had not changed one bit, and I think so too. As everyone else, with advancing age, keeps embarking on the next stage of life's journey, I have stayed all along in the not-quite-mature student phase.

After starting a PhD, will I come to love studying and make scientific research my career?

Another important thing in October was receiving the revision notice for my FNR article. The reviewer gave over thirty comments, many of them hard to answer and striking right at the heart of the matter. The editor allowed only three weeks, and yet I still did not want to act, full of loathing. But no matter what, I have to force myself to revise it! My paper definitely can be published!



The weather is gradually turning cold, and my sleep quality has not been very good either; I want to change dormitory rooms. But after going round and round, there turned out to be no way in. Truly, I feel not the slightest sense of place attachment to Nanjing University's Xianlin. Not to the people, not to the place, not to the things. I just want to flee from here.



Registration at Zhejiang University has already begun, and I still have nothing prepared. Recommendation letters, a thesis proposal, a research plan, and so on, all need to be completed in the next twenty days. The FNR paper also needs to be finished within the next two weeks. I still have two more papers in hand that I have not revised. Oh, and there is also the fact that I have not yet got hold of the exam topics or reading list for Zhejiang University's public-affairs doctoral admission.



And so, in November, I must shake off my go-with-the-flow detachment, get myself busy, and give myself positive feedback little by little. The road ahead is thorny, but it always leaves one with something to look forward to.
