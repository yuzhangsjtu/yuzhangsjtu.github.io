Before you know it, the month is already drawing to a close. October raced by — a month packed with happenings: some anxious, some joyful; it was full of challenges, yet brimful of hope.

For many, October is a season of harvest. Theses, scholarships, offers of every kind. And me? I’ve found myself a “triple‑nothing” person.

This state of affairs was inevitable — the bitter fruit I must swallow for the two or three years I let slip through my fingers.

The National Day holiday passed without surprises or expectations — an eight‑day tour of Kunshan Tower. I wrote no paper, nor did I revise one. I cancelled the trip back to Wuhan; everyone is busy, each with their own path to walk. Rongrong and his girlfriend both won the National Scholarship at the same moment. That’s the way with people who excel: nothing else for it but admiration. I’m grateful to Rongrong for helping me gather all sorts of materials. On my senior schoolmate’s advice, I finished revising the paper and submitted it to the *Journal of Natural Resources*; not a word in reply so far. Perhaps there are simply too many conferences in October and the editors are swamped with commuting — though, of course, the likelier possibility is that the article is too slight, lacking in real novelty. Little by little, expectations crumbled into dejection.

The mid‑month landmark was going to Zhejiang University for a conference. I arranged to meet Professor Tan Rong, and out of nowhere a yearning for Zhejiang University’s School of Public Affairs was lit inside me. It seems, after all, I am bound to return to a humanities and social sciences faculty, to deal with a complex society that is constituted by human beings. What I have confirmed is that I lack the capacity to grasp the world from a macro perspective; what I do possess is an interest in definite, concrete events and people. The core of Professor Tan’s research is “governance”: he sets out from transaction costs, seeking fairness and efficiency in governance by understanding the relationships between central and local governments, between government and market, and between market and non‑governmental organisations. This, it seems, really is the sort of “scientifically solving a problem” I have been chasing — problems entangled with human beings.

Zijingang’s location is much better than Xianlin’s — nowhere near as desolate. The streets are abuzz, and the architecture of the Great Western Campus is very handsome; a style I am genuinely fond of. For the time being, however, the college building has no graduate study‑room — which I find a little peculiar. Cycling in the rain that day, laden with things, to meet Professor Tan: probably an indelible memory for a lifetime. A bit silly. A bit foolish. Reflecting on my original intentions: I wanted to go to the geographic institute in the hope of riding coattails and churning out papers; I wanted to go to Shanghai because Shanghai has a glamorous, dazzling world. Neither choice was altogether real, nor altogether sincere. And neither the geographic institute nor Shanghai Jiao Tong is certain to take me. As for Hangzhou: West Lake is hardly appealing, but Zijingang bursts with everyday bustle. Professor Tan did not say outright he would have me, but at least, there is a certain possibility. I must seize this olive branch.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/10/29/SLMpsgOzZaivrJQ.jpg)

I met a friend for a meal at West Intime in Hangzhou. Already out in society, they are preoccupied with property, marriage, career — whereas I, as ever, still treat my studies with “not‑especially‑switched‑on” detachment. To all appearances, quite irresponsible towards my own life. When we parted, he said I hadn’t changed one bit, and that is exactly how I feel too. As the rest of us, with advancing age, keep launching the next stage of life’s journey, I remain resolutely locked in the not‑quite‑mature student phase.

After starting a PhD, will I fall in love with study — turn pure research into a vocation?

Another highlight in October was receiving the revision notice for my FNR paper. The reviewer offered upwards of thirty comments, many of them difficult to address, every one striking at the heart of the argument. The editor allowed only three weeks, and even facing that, still I could not stir myself to act — recoiling with loathing. Yet however I feel, I simply must force myself into the revision! My paper absolutely can be published. It will be!



It is turning steadily colder. My sleep quality has not been doing too well either; I want to change dormitory rooms. Yet after a great deal of fuss, there is simply no route to make it happen. Truly, towards Nanjing University’s Xianlin campus I feel not the merest sliver of emotional attachment. Not to the people, not to the place, not to the things. All I feel is the urge to escape.



Registration at Zhejiang University has already opened, yet I have nothing prepared. Reference letters, thesis proposals, a research plan — all of these will have to be completed over the coming twenty days. The FNR paper will also need to be finished in the next two weeks. And I still have two more papers awaiting revision in hand. Oh — and, what’s more, I haven’t even managed to get hold of the reading lists or past exam questions for Zhejiang’s public‑affairs doctoral admission examination.



And so, in November, I must shake off my self‑dismissive laissez‑aller, get myself moving, and painstakingly accumulate a little positive feedback, bit by bit. The way ahead bristles with thorns. But that very hardship leaves me something to look forward to.
