> This paper systematically studies **China's policy experimentation**, exploring its **impact on policy learning and national policymaking**. The study finds a **positive selection bias** in the siting of policy pilots (pilots are concentrated predominantly in affluent areas), with **local governments exerting additional effort during the pilot period** (such as increasing fiscal support). However, this effort cannot be sustained after the policy is expanded nationally, leading to a **dilution of policy effectiveness**. ## Research Background

Policy experimentation refers to the process where governments **pilot** new policies in localized areas before formulating national-level policies, in order to gather information on policy effects, thereby reducing uncertainty and promoting policy learning. This approach is adopted by many countries, ranging from the informal “crossing the river by feeling the stones” to rigorous randomized controlled trials. Compared to other countries, China has conducted **large-scale and systematic policy pilots** since the 1980s. The Chinese government frequently tests new policies in different regions and across multiple batches nationally, gaining experience before scaling up. This continuous, institutionalized policy experimentation is considered one of the important mechanisms behind China's economic rise over the past few decades.

Although policy pilots are widely regarded as a key factor in the success of China’s reforms, little is known about **the characteristics of policy experiments and their impact on policy learning**. Existing research mainly discusses policy learning from a theoretical perspective, such as Hayek's discussion on the utilization of decentralized information, or Mukand and Rodrik's model of incremental trial and error. In the Chinese context, studies like Montinola, Qian, and Weingast (1995), and Heilmann (2008) describe the institutional arrangements and political logic of China's policy experiments, but they lack systematic empirical validation. This study, therefore, aims to fill this gap by utilizing data analysis to deeply understand the **operational model and political economy mechanisms** of China’s policy experiments.

The authors raise three core questions:
- **Is pilot site selection representative?** That is, can the regions chosen for policy experimentation represent the national average, thereby avoiding sample bias?
- **Does policy experimentation induce extra effort?** Do local officials, during the pilot process, invest unconventional resources and energy due to intensified incentives, and can this "extraordinary experimental scenario" be reproduced during national promotion?
- **How does the central government learn from pilots?** If pilot sites are unrepresentative and pilot processes are not implemented with standard effort, will the policy judgments and national policy designs made by the central government based on them be biased?

These questions hold significant importance: on one hand, they relate to the **effectiveness of policy learning**—if pilots fail to provide objective information, policy promotion may deviate from the optimal path; on the other hand, they involve the **incentive compatibility of central-local relations**—how local officials’ career incentives influence policy implementation and the central government’s perception. The motivation of this research is to use comprehensive data and rigorous methods to uncover the operational laws of China's policy experiments under political incentives. This is not only valuable for understanding China's past reform experiences but can also serve as a reference for policy trials in other countries or regions.

## Research Methods

#### **Data Sources and Sample Construction**

The authors constructed a database covering China's policy experiments from 1980 to 2020. The primary data came from the complete set of government documents compiled by a legal information website (PKULaw). They filtered government documents issued since 1980 **containing the terms “pilot” (试点 / 示范点) or “experimental zone”** , yielding a total of **19,812 documents**. Among these, 4,399 were central government documents and 15,413 were local government documents. Central documents are typically used to announce the launch of a policy experiment, important phases of expansion, or the decision to promote the policy nationally after successful trials. Local documents, issued by participating pilot local governments, detail the local implementation plans and administrative arrangements. By reviewing these documents, the authors identified **633 distinct policy experiments**. In the identification process, the authors adopted a **conservative consolidation standard**: consecutive pilots with **similar policy goals** (even if the specific policy content evolved and names changed) were grouped as the same policy experiment; simultaneously, if several experiments were closely related in implementation and advanced in tandem, they were merged into a single experiment, even if separate central documents triggered them. The distinct waves of an experiment were typically demarcated by central documents. After sorting this way, the final database covers **633 policy experiments launched by 98 central ministries or commissions**. Significantly, the data not only includes pilots successfully scaled up, but also trials that **likely failed**, expired programs, and explicitly repealed pilot documents. This ensures that the analysis is not biased by focusing solely on successful cases.

After constructing the pilot database, the authors linked it with **multiple-source auxiliary data** to enrich the analytical dimensions. This auxiliary data includes:

- The **policy uncertainty** within the sphere of the policy experiment (the degree of uncertainty about policy effects before the pilot launch);
- The **curricula vitae of local and central officials** (e.g., age, tenure, career promotion records);
- The **organizational characteristics of the policy-initiating ministries**;
- The **local socio-economic conditions** (e.g., GDP, per capita income, social stability status).

By linking central documents to corresponding local documents, the authors were able to track the **specific implementation locations**, **implementation timelines**, and step-by-step diffusion process of each experiment. This data forms the basis for subsequent econometric analysis.

**Pilot Site Representativeness Check**

To determine whether the selection of pilot sites is representative, the authors performed a statistical check for each policy experiment. The specific approach involved comparing the per capita GDP of **pilot areas** and **non-pilot areas** in the year before the pilot began. Per capita GDP was chosen as the benchmark indicator because it reflects the level of economic development and is a key variable measuring regional heterogeneity. The null hypothesis was: "There is no significant difference in per capita GDP between participating pilot regions and non-participating regions," meaning the selection is representative. The authors subsequently conducted 633 independent t-tests (one group per experiment). To quantify the deviation of each experiment, they used the **t-statistic** obtained from each test as a measure of that experiment's degree of representational deviation. The t-statistic formula is:
$$
t_i \;=\; \frac{\bar{Y}{i}(1) - \bar{Y}{i}(0)}{\sqrt{\frac{S^2_i(1)}{n_{i,1}} + \frac{S^2_i(0)}{n_{i,0}}}}\
$$
where $\bar{Y}_i(1)$ and $\bar{Y}_i(0)$ are the mean per capita GDP of the pilot and non-pilot areas, respectively; $S^2_i(1)$ and $S^2_i(0)$ are the corresponding variances; and $n_{i,1}$ and $n_{i,0}$ are the numbers of the two types of areas. The above formula is essentially the standardized statistic for the mean difference between two independent samples. The authors adjusted the degrees of freedom for the t-test based on the proportion of regions participating in the pilot (formula omitted). It is worth noting that Cineva’s trials can take place at different administrative levels (provincial, prefectural, or county). For province-level experiments, the non-pilot group consists of other provinces; for prefecture-level experiments, non-pilot cities from either rural province or nationally are chosen as controls; the same logic applies to the county level. The authors selected appropriate control groups based on the pilot's level and conducted robustness checks for special cases like centrally administered municipalities (results remained unchanged after excluding them). Moreover, based on the GDP-based test, the authors also used other local characteristics (e.g., population, industrial structure) and non-parametric methods to perform representativeness tests, and the results were **robust**.

## Aside

- Deep research took **9 minutes** to generate this **19,308-character** reading report. Its long-context reading ability, reasoning capacity, and long-text output capability are the best I have seen among all models so far.
- In recent days, I have used deep research to generate some industry research reports and research reviews. Its ability in this area is breathtaking; I believe this is a PhD-level model, and it’s extremely cost-effective.
- Deep research is a web-connected agent. Even when provided with local materials, it still relies heavily on internet searches. Therefore, I hope for a long-text, local agent version with better instruction following. Currently, the o1-pro model is in a league of its own in this regard, but its long-text output and generation still lag significantly behind o3.
- I haven’t thoroughly verified every detail in this reading report, so there might be some errors, and formal use requires caution.
