## Research Background

Policy experimentation refers to a government, before formulating a nationwide policy, first **piloting** the new policy in a local area to obtain information on the policy's effects, thereby reducing uncertainty and promoting policy learning. This approach is adopted by many countries, ranging from the informal "crossing the river by feeling the stones" to rigorous randomized controlled trials. Compared with other countries, China has carried out **large-scale and systematic policy pilots** since the 1980s. The Chinese government often trials new policies in different regions and across multiple batches, then rolls them out nationwide once experience has been gained. This continuous, institutionalized policy experimentation is regarded as one of the important mechanisms behind China's economic rise over the past few decades.

Although policy pilots are widely seen as a key factor in the success of China's reforms, little is known about **the characteristics of policy experimentation and its impact on policy learning**. Existing research mostly discusses policy learning theoretically, such as Hayek's discussions of the use of decentralized information, or Mukand and Rodrik's model of incremental trial and error. In the Chinese context, studies such as Montinola, Qian, and Weingast (1995) and Heilmann (2008) describe the institutional arrangements and political logic of China's policy experimentation, but lack systematic data verification. This study therefore attempts to fill this gap, using data analysis to gain an in-depth understanding of the **operational patterns and political-economy mechanisms** of China's policy experimentation.

The authors raise three core questions:

- **Is pilot siting representative?** That is, can the regions chosen for policy experimentation represent the national average, avoiding sample bias?
- **Does policy experimentation induce extra effort?** During the pilot process, do local officials invest unconventional resources and energy because incentives are dialed up, and can this "unconventional experimental setting" be reproduced when the policy is rolled out nationwide?
- **How does the central government learn from pilots?** If pilot regions are unrepresentative and the pilot process involves abnormal levels of investment, will the policy judgments and national policy designs that the central government bases on them be biased?

These questions are of major significance: on the one hand, they bear on the **effectiveness of policy learning**—if pilots cannot provide objective information, policy rollout may deviate from the optimal trajectory; on the other hand, they involve the **incentive compatibility of central-local relations**—how local officials' career incentives affect policy implementation and the central government's perception. The motivation of this research is to use comprehensive data and rigorous methods to reveal the regularities of how China's policy experimentation operates under the influence of political incentives. This is valuable not only for understanding China's past reform experience, but can also provide a reference for policy trials in other countries or regions.

## Research Methods

#### **Data Sources and Sample Construction**

The authors built a database covering China's policy experimentation from 1980 to 2020. The main data come from the complete collection of government documents compiled by the Peking University Law Information Network (**PKULaw**). They screened government documents from 1980 onward that **contained the words "pilot" (试点, demonstration site) or "experimental zone"**, obtaining a total of **19,812 documents**. Of these, 4,399 were central government documents and 15,413 were local government documents. Central documents are typically used to announce the launch of a policy experiment, mark important expansion milestones, or, after a trial succeeds, decide to roll the policy out nationwide; local documents are issued by the various local governments participating in the pilot and detail the local implementation plan and administrative arrangements. By reading these documents, the authors **identified 633 distinct policy experiments**. In the identification process, the authors adopted a **conservative consolidation standard**: any successive pilots with **similar policy goals** (even if the specific policy content evolved and the name changed) were grouped into the same policy experiment; at the same time, if several experiments were closely related in implementation and advanced simultaneously, they were merged into a single experiment even if the central government issued separate documents to launch them. The different waves of an experiment are usually marked by central documents. After this organizing, the final database covers **633 policy experiments launched by 98 central ministries or commissions**. It is worth mentioning that the data include not only pilots that were successfully rolled out, but also **trials that may have failed** as well as documents for pilots that had expired or been explicitly repealed. This ensures the analysis is not biased simply by focusing only on successful cases.

After constructing the pilot database, the authors linked it with **multi-source auxiliary data** to enrich the analytical dimensions. The auxiliary data include:

- The **policy uncertainty** in the domains involved in each policy experiment (the degree of uncertainty about policy effects before the pilot was launched);
- The **résumés of local and central officials** (such as age, tenure, career promotion records, etc.);
- The **organizational characteristics of the initiating ministry**;
- The **local socioeconomic conditions** (such as GDP, per capita income, social stability, etc.).

By linking central documents with the corresponding documents from each locality, the authors were able to track each experiment's **specific implementation locations**, **implementation timing**, and **step-by-step diffusion process**. These data provide the foundation for the subsequent econometric analysis.

#### **Econometric Methods and Models**

Targeting the research questions raised, the authors designed a series of econometric tests and causal inference tools:

**Pilot Representativeness Test**

To determine whether pilot siting is representative, the authors conducted a statistical test for each policy experiment. The specific approach was to compare the **per capita GDP** of **pilot regions** and **regions not participating in the pilot** in the year before the pilot was launched. Per capita GDP was chosen as the benchmark indicator because it reflects the level of economic development and is a key variable for measuring regional heterogeneity. The null hypothesis was: "There is no significant difference in per capita GDP between participating pilot regions and non-participating regions," i.e., siting is representative. On this basis, the authors performed 633 independent t-tests (one group per experiment). To quantify the degree of bias for each experiment, they used the **t-statistic** obtained from each test as a measure of that experiment's degree of departure from representativeness. The t-statistic is calculated by the formula:
$$
t_i \;=\; \frac{\bar{Y}{i}(1) - \bar{Y}{i}(0)}{\sqrt{\frac{S^2_i(1)}{n_{i,1}} + \frac{S^2_i(0)}{n_{i,0}}}}\
$$
where $\bar{Y}_i(1)$ and $\bar{Y}_i(0)$ are the mean per capita GDP of pilot regions and non-pilot regions respectively, $S^2_i(1)$ and $S^2_i(0)$ are the corresponding variances, and $n_{i,1}$ and $n_{i,0}$ are the sample sizes of the two types of regions. The above formula is in fact the standardized statistic for the difference in means between two independent samples (Studentized t). The authors adjusted the degrees of freedom of the t-test according to the differing share of regions participating in the pilot (formula omitted). It should be noted that China's pilots may be carried out at different administrative levels (provincial, prefecture-city, or county). For provincial-level experiments, the non-pilot group consists of other provinces; for prefecture-city-level experiments, non-pilot cities are selected within the province or nationwide as controls; the same applies at the county level. In their tests the authors chose appropriate control groups according to the pilot level, and conducted robustness checks for special cases such as centrally administered municipalities (e.g., results were unchanged after excluding them). In addition, building on the baseline test using GDP, the authors also used other local characteristics (such as population, industrial structure, etc.) as well as nonparametric test methods to test representativeness, and the results were all **robust**.

**Analysis of Siting Motives**

If bias in pilot siting was found, the authors further explored the underlying political-economy reasons. They focused on how **misaligned incentives** between higher- and lower-level governments affect siting, including factors such as local officials' promotion incentives and central officials' personnel connections. Specifically, the authors examined two aspects:

- **Local officials' promotion incentives:** They hypothesized that officials with **better career prospects** (for example, officials far from retirement with greater room for promotion) are more inclined to fight for their locality to become a pilot, in order to take the lead in performance achievements. Using individual official data, the authors constructed a quantitative "promotion incentive" indicator reflecting an official's probability of promotion. To control for endogeneity, they used the discontinuity of officials approaching retirement age together with variation in the timing of pilot launches to form a quasi-natural experiment: within the same experiment, the strength of the promotion motive driven by age differences can be regarded as exogenous variation, thereby identifying its effect on pilot participation and implementation. For example, in analyzing local willingness to participate, they compared the behavioral differences in pilots between municipal party secretaries who **still had room for promotion** and secretaries who were **about to retire**.
- **Central ministries' political connections:** They hypothesized that the **ministers of central supervising departments** are more inclined to allocate pilot slots to places where they once worked (forming political patronage). To test whether the **connection between a minister and a locality** affects siting, the authors used changes in central ministers' careers as an exogenous shock. When a new minister of a ministry had previously served in a certain province, a new political tie is formed between that province and that ministry. Since localities cannot influence the personnel appointments of senior central officials, such minister-level personnel changes can be regarded as exogenous events. The authors constructed panel data at the **ministry-province-year** level and built a regression model:

$$
y_{mpt} \;=\; \alpha \cdot \textit{Connection}_{mpt} + X{\prime}_{pt}\beta + \delta_{mp} + \theta_t + \varepsilon_{mpt}\
$$

where the dependent variable $y_{mpt}$ is the number of pilots that ministry m assigns to province p in year t, and $\textit{Connection}_{mpt}$ is a dummy variable indicating whether the minister of ministry m in year t has a history of working in province p. $X'_{pt}$ is a set of provincial-year control variables, $\delta_{mp}$ is province-ministry fixed effects (controlling for the fixed relationship between a specific province and a specific ministry, such as how much they historically cooperated), and $\theta_t$ is year fixed effects. By using fixed effects, this model locks in the baseline differences in the pilots each province received before the minister took office, and uses only the change in connection caused by the minister's transfer to estimate $\alpha$. If $\alpha$ is significantly positive, it indicates that once a province establishes an old-colleague relationship with a ministry's minister, that province immediately receives more pilot slots from that ministry, thereby supporting the hypothesis that "central personnel connections affect pilot siting."

In addition, the authors also considered the influence of **social stability factors** on siting. The central government may have other implicit objectives when choosing pilots, such as **avoiding experimentation in unstable areas to maintain political stability**. To this end, the authors collected data on **mass incidents and unrest** in various localities, and analyzed whether a locality having experienced social/political unrest in an earlier period reduces its probability of being chosen as a pilot. Using a regression with region and time fixed effects, exploiting variation in unrest across different periods within the same region, they found that **prefecture-level cities that had just experienced social instability were significantly less likely to be selected as pilots in the next round of policy experimentation**. This effect was particularly pronounced when the central government directly designated pilots, but was insignificant when localities voluntarily applied to be pilots. This shows that **out of stability-maintenance considerations**, the central government will proactively avoid unstable areas as pilot sites.

**Local Experimental Effort and Causal Identification**

To detect whether local governments invest extra effort in pilots, the authors approached the question from two angles: fiscal expenditure and differences in policy implementation:

- **Triple-differences method for fiscal expenditure:** The authors tested the impact of policy experimentation on local fiscal expenditure within a triple-differences (DDD) framework. They collected county-level government fiscal expenditure data broken down by functional category, limited the sample to each county's annual expenditure from 1980 to 2020, and divided expenditure into several functional domains (such as six major categories including infrastructure, public administration, agriculture, education and health). For each policy experiment, they determined the main expenditure functional domain it involved; for example, an agricultural reform pilot mainly involves the "agriculture" expenditure category. They then constructed the regression model:

$$
y_{ikt} = \alpha \cdot \textit{Exp}_{ikt} + \lambda_{it} + \delta_{kt} + \theta_{ik} + \varepsilon_{ikt}\
$$

where $y_{ikt}$ is the **share (or level) of category-k expenditure in county $i$'s total expenditure in year $t$**. "Category k" refers to the expenditure category associated with a given pilot; $\textit{Exp}_{ikt}$ indicates whether (or how many) category-k policy experiments county $i$ participated in during year $t$. The model includes multiple fixed effects: $\lambda_{it}$ is county-year fixed effects (controlling for the county's overall expenditure level and trend that year), $\delta_{kt}$ is expenditure-category-year fixed effects (controlling for nationwide general changes in a functional domain in that year, such as central increases in education investment), and $\theta_{ik}$ is county-expenditure-category fixed effects (controlling for the long-run average differences in a given county's investment in a given domain). Under these fixed-effects controls, $\alpha$ captures: **when a county participates in a pilot in a specific domain in a given year, does its expenditure share in that domain undergo an extra change relative to counties that did not participate in the pilot, and relative to expenditure in other domains.** Put simply, it compares the expenditure of "pilot counties in the pilot domain/period" with that of "non-pilot counties or non-pilot periods/domains."

This model amounts to a triple-differences design, with comparison dimensions including: (a) before versus after the pilot launch, (b) pilot counties versus non-pilot counties, and (c) the expenditure domain involved in the pilot versus other domains. By estimating $\alpha$, one can judge whether localities **invested abnormally** in the relevant domain during the pilot period. In addition, the authors examined in the model the **moderating role of officials' promotion incentives**, namely interacting $\textit{Exp}_{ikt}$ with the incentive strength of the locality's chief official, or splitting the sample into high/low promotion-incentive groups for estimation, to test which type of official drives a more pronounced increase in expenditure.

- **Differences in policy implementation (text-analysis method):** Beyond fiscal investment, local officials may raise the pilot's chances of success by **innovating in how the policy is implemented**. To quantify the degree of this "differentiation," the authors used **text-analysis** techniques to compare the similarity of documents issued by different localities participating in the same pilot. Specifically, for each policy experiment, they collected the implementation-rule documents issued by all the local governments participating in that experiment, and used **latent semantic analysis** (LSA) to calculate the similarity between the texts of any two localities' documents. Then, for a given locality $i$, they found the documents of other localities that participated in the same experiment in an **earlier batch**, compared their similarity pairwise with $i$'s document, and took the highest similarity score, denoted $y_{ip}$. The higher this score, the **more similar** $i$'s implementation document is to that of some earlier peer, that is, the more it **lacks differentiation**; conversely, a low score means $i$'s policy-implementation wording and content differ from its predecessors, reflecting new exploration or adjustment. The authors built a regression model to test the relationship between local officials' incentives and the degree of differentiation:

$$
y_{ip} = \alpha \cdot \textit{Incentive}{ip} + \beta X{\prime}_{ip} + \lambda_i + \delta_p + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{ip}\
$$

where $y_{ip}$ is the maximum text-similarity score of locality $i$ in experiment $p$, and $\textit{Incentive}_{ip}$ indicates the career-incentive strength of locality $i$'s main responsible official (similar to the promotion-incentive indicator above, here mainly determined by age and room for promotion). $X'_{ip}$ is a set of controls for the official's individual characteristics (such as education, experience working in the central government, etc.), $\lambda_i$ is region fixed effects, $\delta_p$ is policy-experiment fixed effects, and $\gamma_t$ is year fixed effects. Because region and experiment fixed effects are included, this model exploits the differences arising from incentive variation **within the same locality across time, or among different participants in the same experiment**. Similarly, the authors used the relative position of officials' ages to the retirement line as an exogenous factor to isolate variation in incentive strength. This model aims to test: **whether the stronger an official's incentives in a locality, the more its policy-implementation documents differ from others'**, that is, whether it pursues innovation and differentiation more in order to showcase "highlights."

**Policy Learning and Rollout Effects**

Finally, the authors assessed the impact of pilot-sample selection bias and strategic implementation on the central government's policy learning and nationwide policy effects. Several analyses were used here:

- **Analysis of the probability of a pilot being elevated to national policy:** The authors examined which pilots are more likely to be endorsed by the central government and rolled out as nationwide policy. Using logit regression or a probability model, they took **whether a pilot was ultimately rolled out nationwide** (yes/no) as the dependent variable, examining independent variables such as the economic level of the pilot site, whether an unexpected windfall occurred during the pilot (such as a surge in land-based fiscal revenue), or changes in pilot officials' incentives. To improve causal interpretation, they made use of **exogenous shocks**: for example, if during the pilot a locality unexpectedly received a huge land-transfer payment (a fiscal windfall unrelated to the policy itself), or if the locality's chief official was suddenly transferred away mid-pilot (causing the successor to potentially have insufficient incentives), these events are usually unforeseeable at the pilot's launch and affect the pilot's measured performance, but do not affect the policy's intrinsic effectiveness. The authors found that these exogenous factors did indeed affect the central government's interpretation of pilot results and its rollout decisions (see below), thereby showing that the central government **failed to filter out the noise factors in the sample** during learning.
- **Differences in effects after nationwide rollout:** The authors analyzed whether, after a pilot policy is rolled out nationwide, the benefits different regions derive from it depend on their similarity to the pilot regions. Specifically, they compared the post-rollout performance differences between regions **similar to the pilot regions in economic conditions or official incentives** and regions that are **dissimilar**. If the central government effectively learned the universal effect of the policy, then each region's post-rollout benefits should be unrelated to the region's own conditions; conversely, if the pilot was biased, then only those places that "resemble the pilot regions" can replicate the success. Using regression analysis, the authors found that indeed, **the closer a region's characteristics are to those of the pilot, the greater its benefit after the policy is rolled out**, while regions deviating from the pilot's characteristics benefited significantly less. This indicates that the information the pilot provided was skewed toward a particular type of region, and the national policy the central government formulated on that basis **failed to benefit all regions equally**, producing a skewed distributional effect.

In sum, in terms of research methods the authors combined **descriptive statistics**, **significance tests**, **panel regressions**, **natural experiments**, and **text analysis**, among other means, striving to identify causal relationships from multiple angles. They made particular use of certain **exogenous events** (such as minister turnover, unexpected revenue sources, the timing of officials' retirements, etc.) to establish causal links, making the study's conclusions more reliable. In the econometric process, multiple fixed effects and control-group comparisons were included to control for confounders, and various **robustness checks** in the appendix ensure the robustness of the results.

## Experimental Design and Analysis

This part focuses on the paper's empirical analysis process, proceeding in turn around the research questions above: first testing the **representativeness of pilot siting**, next examining local governments' **investment of extra effort** in experiments and the role of official incentives, and finally analyzing **how the central government interprets experimental results** and the possible **misjudgments** that arise.

#### **Testing Pilot Representativeness**

By comparing the characteristics of pilot regions and non-pilot regions, the authors tested whether policy-experiment siting is representative. They found that **most pilots exhibit a "positive siting bias"**, that is, pilots tend to be placed in more developed regions rather than regions at the national average level. Specifically, among the 633 policy experiments, in **over 80%** the economic development level of the pilot regions was higher than that of the non-pilot regions. This difference is statistically significant, indicating that pilot siting is neither random nor fully representative of the nation. In other words, when the central government chooses pilots, the sample is more skewed toward economically well-off places rather than typical middle-level regions. This stands in contrast to the principle of "being representative" advocated in central documents—the National Development and Reform Commission and others have emphasized that pilots should balance the eastern, central, and western regions, urban and rural, etc., to obtain representative results, but in actual practice this has not been fully achieved.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250218163917192.png)

Next, the authors explored the causes of this **lack of representativeness**. The analysis points out that a considerable portion of the bias does not stem from rational experimental-design considerations, but rather from the **distortion of political incentives**. The paper finds that **nearly half (close to 50%) of the positive siting bias can be explained by the misaligned incentives across levels of government**. This includes two forces:

- **Local officials' promotion incentives:** Officials with stronger career drive more actively seek out and participate in pilots. This causes pilot slots to fall more often to regions governed by "energetic" officials, and such officials often serve in economically better-off regions or are more willing to display their talents where resources are good. As a result, pilot siting tilts toward the more developed regions where these officials serve. For example, the paper shows empirically that cities led by prefecture-city secretaries who are far from retirement and have room for promotion are more likely to become the pilot for a policy experiment. Conversely, cities led by officials nearing retirement are relatively less active in pilots due to insufficient drive. Such differences in promotion incentives roughly explain part of the siting bias—locally "ambitious officials" (those with promotion aspirations) actively "ask for projects," pushing their own regions to be selected as pilots.
- **Central officials' personnel connections and preferences:** Central supervising departments exhibit selective favoritism when allocating pilots. Using the natural variation in ministers' work histories, the paper proves: **once a province establishes a connection with a ministry's minister through that minister's prior work experience, the province immediately receives about 28.8% more pilots**. This significant jump occurs almost entirely in cases where the central government **directly designates** pilot slots, and is not apparent in cases of voluntary local application. This reveals the **political-patronage effect of "upward career dependence"**: ministry leaders favor and promote the localities they are familiar with, placing pilots there to show care or to ensure that the locality can produce results. This is a kind of **top-down favoritism**, not site selection based on the principle of representativeness. In other words, the **"final say"** that central ministers have over pilot siting is partly influenced by personal connection networks.

**Stability-maintenance considerations:** Although not an incentive distortion, the central government has another motive in siting that also causes bias, namely proactively avoiding unstable regions. As shown in the data analysis in the previous section, the authors find that **any region that has recently experienced social or political unrest has a significantly lower probability of being chosen as a pilot afterward**. An unstable environment is treated by the central government as a "veto" factor for pilots. This strategy can be understood as risk aversion (preventing reform from triggering larger problems in sensitive regions), but a side effect is that it **excludes from the pilot list** some central-western underdeveloped but unstable regions that may have average representativeness. This further intensifies the tendency for the pilot sample to concentrate in "stable and relatively affluent" regions.

In theory, the central government may have **multiple objectives** in conducting policy experiments: in addition to obtaining universal information (representativeness), it also hopes to **ensure that reform goes smoothly without trouble**, as well as to **incentivize localities to actively explore**, and so on. The above findings show that in practice, an objective like maintaining political stability **sometimes takes precedence over** the pure principle of representativeness. In addition, the central government is also well aware of the role of using pilots to **reward advanced regions and lead by example for backward ones**, so the frequent assignment of pilots to certain developed regions may also be part of the policy consideration. These factors work together, causing pilot siting to deviate from the ideal random or fully representative distribution.

The authors also tested whether this bias exceeds what "optimal experimental design" can explain. They built a simulation model assuming the central government conducts pilots for information-acquisition purposes, and computed the optimal sampling bias under different scenarios. The results show that no matter how the design is optimized, **it is difficult to generate as strong a positive bias as is observed in reality**. For example, the simulation indicates that to improve learning efficiency, the central government might slightly favor moderately affluent regions (because the policy may have idiosyncrasies in regions that are too backward or too developed), but **it would not reach the level of 80% of pilots concentrated in affluent regions** observed in reality. Therefore, purely rational information-acquisition or policy-trial-and-error motives cannot explain such **systematic bias**. Combined with the foregoing evidence, it can be concluded that **distorted administrative incentives and a preference for stability maintenance** are the main causes of the "siting bias" in policy experiments that should have been objective.

#### **Local Governments' Extra Effort and Official Incentives**

Whether local governments pour in extra resources and energy beyond their normal state during the policy-experiment process is an important factor affecting whether pilot results can represent the normal state. Through various empirical means, the authors found that **local governments do indeed invest extraordinary effort during the pilot phase**, and this phenomenon is especially pronounced when local officials have strong promotion incentives. This causes the policy-implementation situation during the pilot phase to differ from that during ordinary policy rollout, possibly **overestimating the policy's actual effect**.

First, in terms of **fiscal investment**, the authors' triple-differences analysis reveals a striking pattern: **during the policy pilot, local governments significantly increase fiscal expenditure in the domains related to the pilot**. The specific estimates show that **for each additional pilot, the local government's expenditure share in the corresponding policy domain rises by about 2 percentage points, equivalent to an increase of more than 5% in the expenditure level**. For example, if a county participated in an agricultural reform pilot in 2010, then the county's fiscal expenditure on agriculture that year would be about 5% higher than usual (or than counties not participating in the pilot). This extra investment can be interpreted as the locality tilting resources to ensure the pilot's success. More importantly, this effect is very sensitive to official incentives: **the stronger an official's career incentives, the larger the fiscal increase in their jurisdiction's pilot domain**. The paper's split-sample regressions show that when local leaders with strong promotion motives are in charge, the surge in pilot-related expenditure is more pronounced; conversely, leaders with weak incentives do not substantially increase investment. This fits expectations: officials hope to make the pilot a success by increasing investment, in order to gain performance achievements and the recognition of superiors.

It is worth noting that this **"unconventional" increase in expenditure** is phase-specific and cannot be sustained after the policy is rolled out nationwide. The authors examined the expenditure situation when a policy went from pilot to general implementation, and found that **after the policy was rolled out nationwide, the regions that had not originally participated in the pilot did not show a similar increase in expenditure**. Even though the officials in these regions also face promotion pressure, they did not replicate the high investment of the pilot period when implementing the policy day-to-day. This means the 5% extra investment observed during the pilot period **is only a short-term behavior**, not the normal resource allocation required by the policy itself. Local governments often "go all out" during the pilot, but cannot sustain it over the long term. This result is similar to so-called **"window-period window-dressing"** behavior found in some research: officials briefly perform excellently during periods of intense attention from superiors, then return to normal afterward.

Beyond the deployment of fiscal resources, local officials also display strategic effort in **how they implement policy**. By analyzing the content of local policy documents, the paper finds that **the strength of official incentives affects whether they choose to implement policy in a differentiated way**. Specifically, officials with **strong promotion incentives** are more inclined to make their local pilot measures different from other regions, striving to create **"distinctive features."** They may adopt innovative measures or stricter/more flexible implementation, hoping to achieve eye-catching results. The authors measure this with text similarity: the results show that in localities with strong promotion incentives, the similarity between the pilot documents they issue and the earlier documents of other regions is significantly **lower**, meaning that the locality made **more localized innovative adjustments** to the policy. This differentiation is interpreted by the authors as the extra effort local officials make to compete to be a **"pilot demonstration"**—by being different, they show that their policy implementation has more strength or is more creative, thereby winning the central government's attention. Of course, the paper also cautiously notes that they cannot assert whether such differentiation is optimal: it is possible that some officials repeatedly re-explored a solution already proven effective by others (i.e., differentiation may mean not adopting best practices). In any case, the increase in differentiation reflects an increase in **officials' work intensity and investment** during the pilot phase.

These findings correspond to officials' personal **career rewards**. The supplementary analysis cited in the paper shows: if the pilot a locality participates in **is ultimately rated successful and rolled out**, the probability of the locality's main official being promoted rises significantly; conversely, participating in a failed pilot does not bring promotion, aggravating the uncertainty of their career. That is, only making a pilot succeed yields a political dividend; otherwise the effort may be wasted or even damage one's reputation. Precisely for this reason, local officials have a strong motive in pilots to **go all out to ensure success**. They spare no extra budget, adjust their policy-implementation strategies, and even complete within the pilot's time frame work that would normally take years to advance. This **"drain-the-pond-to-catch-the-fish"** style of effort drives the pilot's notable results in the short term, but also means that the effect during the pilot phase may **overestimate the policy's actual performance under normal conditions**.

In sum, the paper empirically proves that **the experimental context in policy experiments does not represent the normal state**: localities often **over-perform** during pilots. Both the increased investment shown by the fiscal data and the differentiated implementation revealed by the text analysis show that pilot results are affected by local **strategic behavior**. This poses a challenge to policy learning: if the central government is unaware of this excessive local mobilization, it may mistake the pilot effect for the effect of the policy itself. This leads to the next question: did the central government correctly interpret the pilot information, or did it produce a misjudgment?

#### **The Central Government's Interpretation of Experimental Results and Possible Misjudgments**

Finally, the authors analyzed how the central government formulates national policy based on pilot results, and how pilot bias affects the **policy-learning** process. The conclusion shows that due to the existence of **siting bias and pilot over-performance**, the central government may produce a **systematic bias** when interpreting the success or failure of pilots, thereby affecting the decisions and effects of national policy.

First, when the central government decides **whether to roll out a pilot policy nationwide**, it **tends to make judgments based on the pilot's performance**. The problem is that if the pilot's performance is affected by siting or unconventional effort, the central government may **mistake the advantage of the environment for the effectiveness of the policy**. The paper finds: **pilots conducted in more economically developed regions are more likely to be rolled out as national policy**. That is, the central government more readily believes that the success of these local pilots has universal significance. But in fact, these regions themselves possess better baseline conditions, and the success may be partly attributable to the good foundation rather than the superiority of the policy. Therefore, this tendency implies that the central government **may overestimate the policy's generalizability**. At the same time, by introducing exogenous shocks the authors prove that the central government's judgment is easily disturbed: if a pilot region happens to experience an **extra windfall** during the pilot period—for example, a sudden increase in land-based fiscal revenue, giving the locality the funds to make the pilot project look exceptionally brilliant—the central government often concludes on this basis that the pilot policy was very successful and decides to roll it out. However, these fiscal windfalls have nothing to do with the policy itself and are a **noise signal**. Similarly, if the locality's main official changes during the pilot process (causing the new official to have insufficient drive and the pilot to possibly be poorly executed), the central government may attribute the pilot's poor performance to the policy being ineffective, and thus abandon rollout, even though the policy itself may be effective. These all show that when evaluating pilots, the central government **fails to adequately correct for local environmental and behavioral factors**, possibly **mistaking incidental factors for policy factors**.

Further analysis focuses on **how policy effects materialize nationwide**. If the central government's learning is biased, then when the policy is rolled out, different regions may have different outcomes. The paper's evidence supports this concern: **after a pilot policy is implemented nationwide, places similar to the pilot regions benefit significantly more**. Conversely, places whose conditions differ greatly from the pilot regions benefit much less from rolling out the same policy, or may even have almost no net benefit. This means the actual average effect of the national policy is lower than what the pilot suggested, and the effect is **unevenly distributed** across regions, **favoring affluent regions and relatively disadvantaging underdeveloped regions**. The authors call this a **systematic bias** in policy effects and the **distributional consequence** it triggers. From the perspective of policy learning, this phenomenon reflects that the **experience the central government draws is limited**: the pilot may teach the central government "this policy is good and very successful," when in fact it is only good for regions with certain conditions and not so effective for others lacking those conditions. In other words, **the information learned is skewed**, causing the policy to fail to be as effective as expected when rolled out nationwide.

The authors liken this mechanism to a form of **regulatory capture—more precisely, "cognitive capture" or "information capture."** Classic regulatory capture refers to regulators being influenced by special interests and favoring those interest groups. Here, central decision-makers are not swayed by interest groups, but are misled by **biased information**, equivalent to having their cognition "captured" by the special political-economic environment of the pilot process. Because the pilot sample and process do not represent the general situation, but the central government believes the information conveyed by the pilot without correction, it will be **systematically biased** in decision-making toward environments favorable to those resembling the pilots. This "information bias" essentially plays a role similar to **cognitive bias in policymaking**. The paper further points out that this is similar to the concept of "cognitive capture" proposed in recent literature: decision-makers are constrained by the structure of the information they acquire and make decisions that seem reasonable but are in fact biased. In the context of China's policy experimentation, biased pilot information causes the central government to unintentionally favor affluent regions in policy design, amounting to an indirect form of **favoritism** toward strong regions; although the starting point was to pilot for the sake of national learning, it produced a kind of **unfairness in outcomes**.

As for specific examples, the paper mentions two types of exogenous events: the **unexpected surge in land-based fiscal revenue** and **official turnover**. The former, as described above, causes the central government to misjudge a policy's benefits; the latter may cause the central government to miss a good policy. In addition, another possible central misjudgment is the neglect of local **excessive effort**. For example, a policy has notable effects during the pilot phase due to extraordinary local investment, but after the central government rolls it out and resource investment returns to normal, the effect may be greatly diminished. If the central government did not anticipate this, it will be surprised by the lackluster effect after national rollout and may even mistakenly believe that localities are implementing it poorly, when in fact the effect was "overdrawn" during the pilot. This is already reflected in the paper's fiscal-analysis results (the 5% extra investment during the pilot disappears upon rollout).

In summary, if the central government **fails to fully consider** the two bias factors of an **unrepresentative pilot sample** and **abnormal pilot effort**, it will produce **bias** in policy learning. The paper's evidence shows that this is indeed the case in China's actual situation: the central government elevated quite a few pilot experiences to national policy without fully correcting these biases. The national policies formulated this way show an **expectations gap** and **structural skew** when implemented. This reminds us that although large-scale policy experimentation provides rich information, if the **quality of the information** is not guaranteed (or the central government does not recognize and adjust for bias), then **more information does not necessarily lead to better decisions**. As the authors put it, China's remarkable policy experimentation is conducted in a complex political environment, which on the one hand benefits from the system's strong mobilization capacity, allowing reform to avoid the risk of failure, but on the other hand, because of the same systemic factors, brings **limitations and distortions to the information learned**. The central government needs to find a balance between encouraging local drive and ensuring the objectivity of pilot information; otherwise it may be **"viewing flowers through a fog"** and make suboptimal national policy choices.

## Core Findings

Based on the above analysis, the paper puts forward **three core findings** that reveal the operational characteristics of China's policy experimentation within the framework of political incentives and its impact on policy learning. Below, these findings are interpreted one by one, with a discussion of their robustness and possible causes:

- **Pilot siting exhibits a systematic positive bias:** In over **80% of policy experiments, the pilots are placed in regions whose economic development level is higher than average, and the pilot sample lacks representativeness.** This bias does not stem entirely from technical considerations; nearly half of it can be attributed to **distorted political incentives**: local officials' promotion drive and central officials' personnel connections work together, making developed regions and regions with proactive officials more likely to be selected as pilots. In addition, the central government's deliberate avoidance of unstable regions (stability-maintenance considerations) further strengthens the positive bias. This finding holds under various alternative indicators; for example, using fiscal revenue, industrial output, etc., to measure development level also shows a similar siting tilt. The authors also verified through simulation that optimal experimental design cannot produce such a strong bias, highlighting the importance of political factors. This finding explains why China's pilots often cluster in eastern regions such as Shanghai and Guangdong: besides their possessing the conditions for experimentation, official incentives and central preferences played a contributing role.
- **Local governments invested unconventional extra effort during the pilot period:** Localities often **make extraordinary efforts** to ensure success during the pilot phase. Quantitatively, the fiscal expenditure of pilot regions in the relevant policy domain is about 5% higher than usual, **and especially when local chief officials face heavy career pressure, they invest more**. This extra expenditure was not sustained after the policy was rolled out, indicating that pilot achievements are partly built on unsustainable investment. At the same time, officials with promotion ambitions tend to **implement policy in a differentiated way**—the pilot implementation plans they formulate differ more from others' in order to create highlights. This differentiated implementation means more **innovative attempts** and **administrative effort**, and although not all of it is effective practice, it reflects the degree of local investment in pilots. This finding is corroborated by various methods: the results are similar under different expenditure classifications and different official-incentive indicators, and the text-similarity analysis strongly supports the link between official incentives and implementation differences. This finding explains why many policies achieve remarkable results during pilots but perform mediocrely once rolled out—**pilot success is partly the product of human striving rather than purely the policy.** Local officials treat pilots as an arena, over-performing, which both guards against the pilot failing and inflates the pilot's effect.
- **The central government did not adequately correct pilot bias, leading to biased policy learning:** When deciding national policy based on pilots, the central government did not fully recognize or eliminate the influence of the aforementioned biases. As a result, **national policies derived from pilots are skewed**: the central government is more likely to roll out pilots that succeeded in a **favorable environment**, even if the success is partly attributable to the environment rather than the policy; conversely, it may miss some policies that failed in difficult environments but are themselves valuable. Furthermore, after nationwide rollout the policy effect exhibits a **dependence on pilot conditions**: places with conditions close to the pilot regions benefit more, while other places benefit less, widening regional gaps. This indicates that policy learning is guided by "information bias," and the policies the central government formulates fit affluent regions better while relatively ignoring the reality of poor regions. This finding verifies causality by examining exogenous shocks: the success or failure of pilots is affected by random fiscal windfalls, yet the central government makes decisions on this basis, confirming the existence of the **misjudgment mechanism**. In addition, the correlation between post-rollout performance data and similarity to the pilot regions also proves the impact of learning bias on outcomes. Taken together, the central government's policy-learning process is **disturbed by pilot bias**, the information is not fully neutral, and this brings possible efficiency losses and distributional unfairness.

The above three core findings remain stable across various robustness checks. In the appendix, the authors obtain qualitatively consistent conclusions by replacing variables, using different sample screening (such as excluding centrally administered municipalities, looking only at a certain period, etc.), and controlling for more possible confounders. For example, the promotion-incentive and minister-connection factors they quantified together explain about 50% of the siting bias, and the remaining unexplained portion may be the central government's deliberate selection of superior regions (though this is hard to distinguish) or unobserved factors; in any case, the bias objectively exists and is significant. Likewise, regarding fiscal investment, they compared spending in domains involved in the pilot policy versus domains not involved, using different benchmarks, all of which support the judgment that "expenditure in specific domains increased abnormally during the pilot period." Because quasi-natural-experiment methods (minister turnover, unexpected land revenue, etc.) were used, many inferences have causal implications rather than being simple correlations. Therefore, these findings are quite **robust and credible**. The possible explanations have been discussed in the analysis: **promotion incentives** drive localities to actively seek out and work hard at pilots, **personnel connections** bring top-level preferences, and **stability considerations** lead the central government to prefer comfortable regions to be safe. These behaviors are reasonable under China's political system, but unintentionally **limit the genuine "trial-and-error and truth-seeking" function of policy experimentation**, which has partly evolved into a **political-performance arena**. This insight is an important contribution of the paper.

## Policy Implications

The findings of this study have multiple implications for China's policymaking and implementation practice. In short, although the current policy-pilot mechanism is full of drive and highly efficient, it has problems of **information bias and distorted incentives**, which need to be improved in policy design to raise the quality of policy learning and the fairness and effectiveness of national policy.

**1. Improve the pilot-siting strategy and enhance sample representativeness: When choosing pilot regions, the central government can pay more attention to representativeness and randomness.** The study shows that pilots are currently too concentrated in developed regions, which may cause the central government to overestimate policy effects and underestimate the difficulty of implementation in backward regions. To this end, the central government could consider **explicitly including regions of different development levels and different geographic types** in pilot plans, even if these regions are somewhat weaker in their capacity to advance reform, because they can provide more comprehensive feedback. For example, adopt a **stratified-sampling** approach: select a certain proportion of regions for pilots in each of the eastern, central, and western regions; include large, medium, and small cities as well as rural areas in the pilot scope. This can avoid bias in the information source. Of course, the central government could also **introduce an element of random assignment**, randomly selecting some regions to be pilots on a voluntary-application basis, rather than determining the list entirely by subjective judgment. This helps prevent the skew caused by administrative interference and personal connections. At the same time, for regions that **have experienced instability** but are representative, the central government might give small-scale pilot opportunities when the situation is under control, to verify the policy's effect in difficult environments and thereby gain more comprehensive experience. In short, a more scientific and transparent site-selection mechanism will enhance the applicability of pilot results to the whole country and **reduce the interference of sample-selection bias** with policy learning.

**2. Adjust the official-incentive mechanism to guard against excessive "vanity projects": The study reveals that local officials tilt large amounts of resources into pilots and pursue short-term effects to achieve performance. Although this guarantees the pilot's success rate, it also brings unrealistic factor inputs. To guide officials to treat pilots rationally, the central government can improve the cadre-evaluation system.** Specifically, it should **avoid excessively tying the success or failure of a single pilot to one's career**. It could consider:

- Making the evaluation of pilots emphasize **genuine effectiveness** rather than mere indicator improvement, and encouraging officials to truthfully feed back problems and shortcomings. For officials whose pilots failed but who were objectively diligent, their ability should not be simply negated; their contribution to exposing problems and helping the whole country avoid detours could even be recognized in the evaluation.
- **Extending the observation period:** When evaluating a pilot, look not only at performance during the pilot period but also track the sustained effects after policy rollout. If an official's jurisdiction had eye-catching data during the pilot but it fell sharply after rollout, one should question whether they over-invested during the pilot phase, and deduct points accordingly in the evaluation. This would make officials realize that "putting on a show" by unconventional means cannot yield long-term benefits.
- **Uniform resource guarantees:** When arranging pilots, the central government could provide **relatively equal support** to each pilot region, or at least clearly limit how much localities can increase their own investment, to prevent regions with the means from investing without limit and creating an "arms race." For example, set a budget range for a pilot or provide matching central special funds, so that different regions compete under equal resources, avoiding affluent regions winning by throwing money at the problem.
- **Diversifying career incentives:** Expand cadre promotion evaluation from pure economic development and project success/failure to include dimensions such as **the soundness of reform pilots** and **contributions to summarizing experience**. Reward officials who can report valuable experience and problems, rather than only rewarding those who make the numbers look the prettiest.

Through the above adjustments, the **utilitarian pressure on officials in pilots can be reduced**, prompting them to approach pilot tasks with a more ordinary mindset. This will reduce short-term "piloting for the sake of piloting" behavior, making pilot results closer to the true level of ordinary policy rollout.

**3. Improve the pilot-evaluation and rollout-decision process: At the pilot-evaluation stage, the central government should fully correct for the influence of pilot conditions** to raise the quality of policy learning. Based on this study:

- The central government should establish a **more scientific evaluation indicator system** that distinguishes which results come from the policy itself and which may come from external luck or extra investment. For example, it could require pilot regions to report the **cost of policy implementation** as well as the **supporting conditions**, and mark policies that require high investment to work as "low cost-effectiveness," rolling them out cautiously. For certain pilot "miracles," it should check whether there were special economic-environment circumstances at the time (such as unexpected fiscal revenue or temporary campaign-style governance), to avoid being misled by surface results.
- Introduce **independent third-party evaluation.** It could consider commissioning research institutions and think tanks to conduct independent studies of key pilots, in order to analyze policy effects more objectively. Third parties could use methods such as randomized control groups, selecting non-pilot cities and counties within the pilot province as controls, to technically assess the net effect of the policy. These independent reports can be referenced by the central government in decision-making, balancing the reporting bias from local governments.
- **Transition trials from pilot to rollout:** Before full-scale rollout, for regions with widely differing conditions, the central government could arrange a **second pilot** or **simulated rollout**. For example, if a policy initially succeeded only in eastern trials, before rollout it could draw some regions in the central and western areas for "small-scale replication trials" to verify the effect under different environments. If the effect is found to be unsatisfactory, the policy design can be adjusted in time (for example, adding supporting measures) before rollout. Such an intermediate step amounts to **remedial work**, making up for the shortfall in the original pilot's representativeness.
- **Policy flexibility:** When formulating national policy, the central government should consider **regional differences** and grant a certain degree of flexible latitude. The study shows that regions differ in their foundations and that a uniform policy has different effects, so policy documents could allow localities to adjust implementation intensity or supporting measures according to actual conditions. This authorization for differentiated implementation can avoid the efficiency loss of a one-size-fits-all approach, and also serves as a remedy for pilot bias—since pilots mainly represented the situation of affluent regions, central policy should have special provisions for underdeveloped regions.

**4. Strengthen feedback and learning mechanisms: Policy experimentation is essentially a learning process.** To improve learning effectiveness, both central and local governments should establish better information-feedback loops. The central government should encourage localities to **report the problems they encounter in pilots and the reasons for failure**, fostering a "fault-tolerant" atmosphere so that negative information can also reach the top. This can prevent the partial, biased phenomenon of listening only to success stories while ignoring lessons of failure. Localities, for their part, should strengthen **pilot record-keeping**, including implementation details, inputs and outputs, public reactions, etc., providing detailed data—not just a few indicators—to the central government and other regions. These improvements all help achieve **collective learning**, allowing policy experimentation to truly fulfill its "trial-and-error" function rather than become a mere formality.

In sum, one important reminder of this study for real-world policy is: **the pilot is not the goal; learning is the goal.** Therefore, how to design pilots so that their information is more reliable, how to design incentives so that officials implement policy without distortion, and how to scientifically evaluate and distill genuinely useful experience are all questions to which policymakers need to pay attention. By optimizing pilot siting and scale and adjusting the incentive and evaluation mechanisms, the tool of policy experimentation will be able to better serve reform decisions, reduce misjudgments caused by information bias, and improve the effectiveness and fairness of national policy.

## Academic Contributions and Limitations

#### **Academic Contributions**

This study makes multiple contributions in the fields of policy experimentation and political economy:

- **Data contribution:** The authors built the **most comprehensive database of China's policy experimentation** to date. Past research on China's local pilots was mostly case-based or qualitative, whereas this paper comprehensively quantifies all pilots since 1980, filling the gap in empirical data. This provides a valuable data foundation for subsequent scholars studying the pilot mechanism in China's reform process, and also sets a benchmark for the empirical analysis of policy experimentation internationally.
- **Empirical findings:** Through rigorous data analysis, the paper **systematically confirms for the first time** that China's policy experimentation exhibits **positive sample-selection bias** and **strategic experimental effort**, as well as the resulting **policy-learning bias**. These findings enrich our understanding of China's governance model. For example, while earlier works such as Heilmann (2008) proposed that China's **"experimentalist federalism"** has the advantages of flexibility and incremental reform, this study further points out the hidden drawback within it, namely that information may be distorted. Such empirical evidence corrects part of the theoretical view and provides a new perspective for explaining why some Chinese policies perform worse than expected upon rollout.
- **Theoretical significance:** This paper elevates China's experience to a general political-economy proposition, proposing the mechanism of **"political distortion of information"**, which can be seen as an extension of **policy-learning theory** and **regulatory/cognitive-capture theory**. Under past theoretical assumptions, the information that local experiments provide was often regarded as objective. This study shows that under authoritarian systems and bureaucratic-incentive environments, the information itself is shaped, and decision-makers must guard against this "cognitive bias." This insight is also instructive for other forms of policy learning; for example, in democratic countries, pilots may be influenced by electoral politics and likewise need to watch out for information distortion.
- **Methodological innovation:** Methodologically, the paper combines **quasi-experimental strategies** (such as using exogenous events like minister turnover and land windfalls) with **novel text analysis**, broadening the toolbox of political-economy research. In particular, using **text similarity** to measure differences in policy implementation, and the carefully designed triple-differences framework to measure fiscal effort, provide new approaches for quantitatively studying official behavior. These methods can be extended and applied to analyzing the relationship between the language of government documents and policy-implementation effects, the policy impact of cadre turnover, and other areas.
- **Deepening understanding of China's reform model:** Through quantitative analysis, this study breaks down the grand question of "why China can reform successfully" into specific mechanism examinations. It confirms that **decentralized experimentation** does indeed bring flexibility and local participation (the drive for reform), but also emphasizes the role and limits of **central concentration of power** in coordination. For instance, the article points out that under China's centralized environment, localities competing to reform may produce a tendency toward **"over-experimentation"**—which contrasts with the classic theory in which decentralization may lead to **under-experimentation** (free riding, where localities are unwilling to take risks). This difference deepens our understanding of policy experimentation under different governance structures, and also explains why China can conduct pilots so intensively yet must still beware of learning bias.

In sum, the paper organically combines **empirical fact** with **theoretical analysis**, providing both detailed new evidence and a conceptual framework of general significance, enriching the academic discussion of the interaction between policy experimentation and political incentives.

#### **Research Limitations**

Although this study is innovative and thought-provoking, it also has some limitations that point the way for future research:

- **Causal identification and mechanism details:** Although the authors did their best to use natural-experiment conditions to identify causality, a number of inferences are still mainly based on correlational reasoning. For example, although the difference in post-rollout benefits across pilot regions is correlated with the similarity of conditions, this does not necessarily fully rule out the influence of other factors (for instance, affluent regions may have stronger implementation capacity, which itself yields higher benefits). The authors used reasonable assumptions and controls, but in the absence of a **randomized experiment**, some mechanisms still cannot be confirmed with 100% certainty. In addition, the micro-level details of some key mechanisms could not be directly observed, such as the weight the central government assigns to information in internal discussions when evaluating pilots, which can only be inferred from outcomes.
- **Limitations in measuring policy effects:** This paper focuses on the **policy-learning process** itself, and devotes little attention to the **ultimate socioeconomic impact** of policy. In other words, it reveals the bias in the process, but does not assess how much efficiency loss this bias actually causes, nor does it compare with "rolling out directly without piloting." The authors also acknowledge that they do not answer the question of "whether, overall, piloting is better than not piloting." Future research could, from the perspective of social welfare, assess the net benefit of pilots and how improvements can raise the net benefit.
- **Limitations in data scope:** Although the data are extensive, there may still be **measurement error** and **coverage blind spots**. For example, they used document keyword searches to delimit policy experiments, which may miss reform projects that did not use the wording "pilot" but were essentially pilots. In addition, what is recorded in documents may diverge from actual implementation (some localities may pay lip service and not really run the pilot, which cannot be seen in the documents). Moreover, indicators such as official incentives and social stability are all approximate proxies, and do not necessarily precisely reflect officials' mindsets or social realities. These data limitations may affect the precision of the conclusions, but are unlikely to change the direction of the main findings.
- **External validity:** The study focuses on China's specific institutional background, so the extent to which the conclusions apply to other countries requires caution. The promotion tournament of Chinese officials and central-local relations have their particularities; in federal or democratic countries, under different political logics, policy experimentation may exhibit different problems. For example, U.S. state policy trials may be influenced by electoral cycles rather than bureaucratic promotion. Although this study proposes the general concept of "politically distorted information," its specific form is unique to China, and future research needs to verify and compare it in other countries.
- **Dynamic long-term impact:** The paper concentrates on the impact during the pilot period and immediately after rollout, and lacks examination of **longer-term dynamics**. For example, a policy may have over-investment during the pilot and be inefficient after rollout, but perhaps after a few years of adaptation the rollout regions gradually raise their capacity and the policy still succeeds in the long run. Such long-term tracking is beyond the scope of this paper. Future work could study the performance of some major policy pilots several years after rollout, to see whether the central government adjusts and corrects the policy, thereby evaluating the policy-experimentation mechanism more comprehensively.

#### **Future Research Directions**

Based on the above limitations, follow-up research could pursue the following work:

- **Research on optimizing the design of the policy-experimentation mechanism:** Regarding how the central government trades off between incentivizing officials and ensuring information quality, explore mechanism-design schemes in theory. For example, can an incentive contract be designed so that localities have drive yet do not exaggerate their achievements? This is a complex incentive-compatibility problem, awaiting in-depth study by scholars from a game-theoretic perspective.
- **Comparative effects of piloting versus not piloting:** As the authors mention in a footnote, one could compare the pros and cons of the two paths of "rolling out a policy directly without piloting" and "piloting first and then rolling out." This requires finding cases where similar policies adopted different strategies in different provinces, for a comparative analysis. Such research could quantify the total value of the policy-experimentation mechanism and test whether the bias the authors identify weakens the benefits of piloting.
- **Cross-national comparison:** Compare China's policy-experimentation experience with that of other countries/regions (such as the post-Soviet Eastern European transition, China's Hong Kong and Macau special zones, or small-scale policy trials in Western countries) to discern the functions and problems of experimentation under different political systems. For example, one could study the diffusion of innovative policies across U.S. states to see whether phenomena such as "successful projects coming from affluent states" also exist, or whether there is a different pattern. Such comparisons can test the generalizability of this study's conclusions and enrich the general understanding of policy learning.
- **Micro-level mechanisms:** Further mine micro data, such as interviewing decision-makers and studying internal central evaluation reports, to understand the actual process by which the central government **evaluates pilots** and the behavioral details by which localities **seek out pilots**. Such qualitative research can corroborate the quantitative findings and provide targeted, detailed recommendations.
- **Use of new tools:** This paper used text analysis; in the future more **big-data techniques** could be introduced, such as using news reports and social-media data to assess public-opinion reactions to policy pilots, or using machine learning to predict which pilots are more likely to succeed, thereby helping improve siting and evaluation methods.

All in all, the paper "Policy Experimentation in China: The Political Economy of Policy Learning," with its detailed data and innovative methods, deeply dissects the internal mechanisms of China's policy pilots—this unique governance tool—affirming its role while also revealing hidden concerns. It reminds policymakers: **experimentation is good, but must be used with caution.** Academically, it opens a new path for understanding **how political incentives affect policy experimentation and learning**. Although there is still room for deepening, this study is undoubtedly an important milestone in the field, laying a solid foundation for subsequent research.
