> It is well known that, on the whole, economic growth or innovation is beneficial. Yet why, in many cases, are governments unwilling to adopt policies that promote economic growth, and why do they even obstruct technological and institutional change?
>
> The paper proposes the concept of a "**Political Replacement Effect**," pointing out that political elites may block technological and institutional changes that favor economic development, because such changes could weaken their political power and increase the likelihood that they will be replaced. Hence, even though economic development could bring greater wealth, political elites may choose to obstruct these changes in order to maintain power.

## Why Political Elites May Oppose Economic Growth

Government policies and institutions shape economic incentives, and through this channel they exert a direct and significant impact on economic development. Why, then, do many societies adopt policies that **impede investment** and maintain institutions that lead to economic backwardness? Perhaps the politically powerful groups (the elites) do not favor economic growth. But why? Economic growth would seem to provide these groups with more resources to expropriate or tax, thereby increasing their economic gains. Why are these powerful groups not always supportive of economic development? Let us review history below and look at some concrete cases.

#### **The Uneven Pace of Industrialization in the 19th Century**

Bairoch (1982) estimated that, between 1830 and 1913, world manufacturing output grew fivefold, but very unevenly across regions and countries ⬇️. Over the same period, manufacturing output in **the developed countries** (Europe and North America) grew more than tenfold, while in **the Third World** it declined. Among the developed countries there were also marked differences: Britain and the United States adopted new technologies and rapidly industrialized, whereas Russia, Austria-Hungary, and Spain lagged behind. Why did these countries fail to adopt new technologies that could have increased their incomes?

The differences in performance across regions during the period of industrialization inspired Gerschenkron's celebrated essay *Economic Backwardness in Historical Perspective* (1962). In this essay, Gerschenkron recognized that there were great differences across countries in the effort devoted to the **institution-building** required to promote industrialization; in fact, in the most severely backward countries, political elites, far from actively promoting industrialization, were actually impeding it. Gerschenkron writes:

> … economic progress began to be viewed with great suspicion and the railroads came to be regarded, not as welcome carriers of goods and persons, but as carriers of the dreaded revolution. Then the State clearly became an obstacle to the economic development of the country. 
>
> …… Economic progress began to be regarded with extreme suspicion, and the railroads were no longer seen as welcome carriers of goods and persons, but as carriers of the dreaded revolution. Thus, **the State clearly became an obstacle to the country's economic development**.

Therefore, understanding why industrialization proceeded rapidly in some countries while never getting off the ground in others is closely related to understanding why, in some countries, the state encouraged industrialization, while in others it did not. More precisely: why, in some societies, did the state and political elites not only fail to encourage industrialization, but even obstruct the introduction of the new technologies and economic institutions that are vital to industrialization, such as the establishment of efficient factor markets, property rights, and legal systems?

#### **The Explanation of the Political Replacement Effect**

In this paper, the authors propose the **political replacement effect**, namely: when political elites fear that reforms favorable to economic development and institutional progress will undermine the existing system and increase the likelihood that they will lose political power and future rents, they will obstruct these changes.

This explanation is based on the following two basic analyses:

- **Considering only economic gains**: In theory, if political elites adopt technological and institutional changes that increase output, they themselves will obtain greater economic gains and will become more popular by benefiting the public. In this case, political elites will promote technology adoption and institutional change.
- **Considering political gains**: The economy and politics are closely related. Other groups that benefit economically may weaken the elites' relative political advantage and increase the likelihood that the elites will lose power, producing a political replacement effect. In this case, political elites will weigh the gains from adopting technological and institutional change against maintaining their existing power, and this may lead them to obstruct change.

The authors argue that the required technological and institutional changes will affect the political gains of different groups.

The existence of the political replacement effect means that logic similar to the **Coase Theorem** (which holds that all investments capable of increasing the social pie will be carried out) does not apply here. This is because there is no (credible) way to **compensate ex post** those political elites who lose power.

> On why there is no political Coase theorem, see Acemoglu's 2003 paper *Why Not a Political Coase Theorem?*, published in the Journal of Comparative Economics.

#### **The Theoretical Contribution of This Paper**

- What political elites fear is not losing **economic gains** in the course of technological change, but losing **political power**. This paper is most closely related to the literature on interest group politics. That literature emphasizes that existing powerful interest groups may block the introduction of new technologies in order to protect their established economic rents. This paper refutes that view: after all, if political elites could retain their political power after introducing technological change, why could they not use that power to redistribute the gains back to themselves? What matters, therefore, is that technological change leads to a redistribution of power.
- Acemoglu and Robinson (2000) advanced the view that the greatest obstacle to economic development is not those groups whose economic interests are affected by economic change, but those groups whose political power is threatened. This paper formalizes that view by introducing the political replacement effect into a forward-looking dynamic political economy model, and derives a new result: namely, that the relationship between political competition and political elites' desire to block innovation may be **non-monotonic**.

## Propositions (GPT-4o's Interpretation)

#### **Proposition 1: The Social Planner's Solution**

Proposition 1 explains how, in the absence of political considerations, a **social planner** chooses the optimal technological and institutional change.

**Core content**:

- The social planner's objective is to maximize total social output and welfare. Therefore, they will choose to introduce new technologies and institutions that raise total social output.
- Since the social planner has no political-interest considerations, their decisions are based entirely on maximizing economic gains. At each point in time, the social planner compares the output under the current technology level with the output after introducing new technology, and chooses the option that maximizes future welfare.
- The social planner's decision function is usually expressed as:

$$
S(A) = \max \left\{ A + \beta S(\alpha A), A + \beta S(A) \right\}
$$

where $S(A)$ denotes the social planner's value function, $A$ is the current technology level, $\alpha$ > 1 denotes the multiple of technological progress, and $\beta$ is the discount factor.

**Implications**:

- In theory, if there are no political obstacles, society will always choose the optimal technological and institutional change in order to achieve maximum economic development.
- In practice, the interests of political elites may deviate from the social planner's objective, leading them to be unwilling to support such change.

#### **Proposition 2: Political Elites and the Tradeoff Between Power and Rent**

Proposition 2 describes how, when facing technological and institutional change, political elites weigh maintaining political power against increasing economic gains.

**Core content**:

- In making decisions, political elites must weigh two key factors: first, increasing **economic gains** through technological and institutional change, and second, the **political instability** that change may bring, which in turn carries the risk of losing power.
- This tradeoff gives rise to the so-called "Political Replacement Effect": even if change can increase economic output, political elites may still choose to obstruct change in order to avoid losing political power.
- The specific equilibrium condition is:

$$
\alpha \cdot P\left[\frac{1}{2} + \mu\right] \cdot P\left[\frac{\alpha}{\phi \cdot \delta}\right] \geq P\left[\frac{1}{2} + \gamma \cdot \mu - (\alpha - 1)\right] \cdot P\left[\frac{1}{\phi \cdot \delta}\right]
$$

where $\alpha$ is the coefficient of technological improvement, $\mu$ denotes the intensity of political competition, $\gamma$ denotes the coefficient of the effect that introducing new technology has on the ruler's position, $\phi$ denotes the intensity of external threats, and $\delta$ is the ratio of the external country's technology level to the home country's technology level.

**Implications**:

- Political elites often face complex choices between short-term economic gains and long-term political stability.
- In some cases, out of fear of losing power, political elites may prefer to sacrifice potential economic growth opportunities rather than risk supporting institutional and technological change.

#### **Proposition 3: The Impact of Political Rents and Human Capital**

Proposition 3 examines the impact of political rents and the level of human capital on whether political elites choose to obstruct change.

Core content:

- Political rents $R$ are the additional economic gains that political elites can obtain under the existing system. These gains often come from traditional economic structures (such as land rents) rather than from the gains brought by new technologies or institutions.
- Human capital $h$ denotes the level of skills and knowledge of workers and entrepreneurs in society. The higher the human capital, the greater the economic gains from technological progress, and the stronger the elites' incentive to support change.
- The model indicates that when political rents are high and the level of human capital is low, political elites are more likely to choose to obstruct change, because the gains from change are relatively small and may threaten their political position.
- The key decision condition is:

$$
\text{If } \frac{R}{h} > \text{Threshold}, \text{ then no innovation.}
$$

That is, when the ratio of political rents to human capital exceeds a certain threshold, political elites will choose not to support innovation.

**Implications**:

- High political rents usually make political elites more inclined to maintain the status quo rather than support change.
- Raising the level of human capital can reduce the likelihood of obstructing change, because the higher the human capital, the greater the gains from technological progress, and the more attractive change becomes to the elites.

#### **Proposition 4: The Impact of External Threats**

Proposition 4 explains how external threats affect political elites' attitudes toward change.

**Core content**:

- When a country faces an external threat (such as military aggression or economic sanctions), political elites may realize that if they do not carry out reforms, they will be unable to retain power or national sovereignty.
- In this case, an external threat may prompt the elites to support technological and institutional change in order to strengthen the country's defensive capabilities or international competitiveness.
- The key equilibrium condition is:

$$
\text{If } \phi \text{ (external threat) is high, then more likely to support innovation.}
$$

This means that when the external threat $\phi$ is high, political elites are more likely to support innovation and change.

**Implications**:

- **External threats** can become an important driving force for technological and institutional change, especially when a country faces an existential crisis.
- In this case, political elites may choose to temporarily give up some internal interests in order to meet a greater external challenge.

## Historical Evidence and Explanation

#### **Britain: Gradual Reform and the Success of Industrialization**

- **Strategy**: Britain's industrialization was accompanied by gradual political reform. Although the early aristocracy was skeptical of industrialization, as the power of Parliament grew and the aristocracy compromised with the emerging industrial classes, Britain successfully achieved industrialization while maintaining political stability.
- **Outcome**: Britain became the world's first industrialized country and occupied a dominant position in the global economy. Through compromise and gradual reform, the political elites successfully balanced economic development with the maintenance of power.

#### **Germany: The Combination of Authoritarianism and Industrialization**

- **Strategy**: Germany's industrialization was carried out under the leadership of the Prussian state. Through an alliance with the industrial classes (the "alliance of iron and rye"), the Prussian elites (especially the Junker aristocracy) ensured that their own political position would not be weakened while promoting industrialization.
- **Outcome**: Germany rose rapidly to become an industrial power in the second half of the 19th century. Although industrialization brought economic growth, political authoritarianism was nevertheless preserved, and the elite class was able to continue to control the state.

#### **Japan: Defensive Modernization and the Consolidation of Centralized Power**

- **Strategy**: Japan's industrialization and modernization were a response to external threats (such as pressure from the Western powers). Through the Meiji Restoration, Japan not only promoted economic modernization but also successfully carried out defensive modernization by strengthening centralized power and consolidating the power of the bureaucratic elite.
- **Outcome**: Japan rapidly transformed into a modern industrial nation, and the central government and bureaucratic elite maintained strong control over the country throughout the process of industrialization.

#### **Russia: Industrialization Delayed Under High Political Rents**

- **Strategy**: Before the Crimean War, Russia's tsarist regime did its utmost to obstruct industrialization, fearing that industrialization would lead to social unrest and threaten the existing political order. Only after the defeat in the war was Russia forced to begin modernizing reforms.
- **Outcome**: Although reform brought progress in industrialization, Russia's economic development lagged for most of the 19th century, and political tensions intensified accordingly, ultimately leading to the later revolution.

Finally, let us take a closer look at the Habsburg dynasty ⬇️.

#### **The Habsburg Empire: Conservatism and Economic Backwardness**

**Background: The Decline of the Habsburg Empire**

- **"The China of Europe" or "the Sick Man on the Danube"**: In the 19th century, the Habsburg Empire (Austria-Hungary) was called "the China of Europe" or "the Sick Man on the Danube," and was widely regarded as lagging far behind the other countries of Europe in the process of industrialization. Historians generally believe that this slowness of economic development was one of the reasons for the empire's disintegration during and after the First World War.
- **The State's Opposition**: The consensus among historians is that the main reason for the slow economic growth of the Austro-Hungarian Empire in the 19th century was the state's opposition to industrialization. For example, Gross (1973) noted: "In domestic and foreign policy, from 1815 to 1848, the Vormärz regime was determined to prevent another French Revolution from occurring anywhere in Europe. On this principle, Francis I opposed not only the growth of industry (and the attendant emergence of a proletariat), but was generally unwilling to allow any change at all."

**Political Elites' Resistance to Change**

- **Fear of the French Revolution**: As in the case of Russia, the Austro-Hungarian elites' fear of the French Revolution was an important factor in their opposition to industrialization. Freudenberger (1967) noted: "In the 1790s, fear of the French Revolution added a new dimension to government policy. The senior officials in the government, particularly the young Emperor Francis I, were deeply worried that industrialization would create an industrial proletariat, regarding these people as carriers of revolutionary ideas, and therefore not only opposed large-scale enterprise but also supported policies to keep the population predominantly agricultural."
- **Obstruction of Railroad and Infrastructure Construction**: Like the tsar, the Habsburg emperor opposed the construction of railroads and infrastructure, and made no attempt to establish an effective education system. Blum (1943) noted that the legacy of traditional economic institutions was the main obstacle to industrialization, arguing that "these vital elements of the traditional economic institutions were the greatest obstacle to development. Their chief supporter was … Emperor Francis. He knew that advances in production technology threatened the survival of the old order that he so firmly defended. Because of his unique position as the final arbiter of all proposals for change, he was able to hold back this tide for a time. Thus, when a plan for steam-railroad construction was put before him, he refused to consent to its execution, 'lest revolution might come into the country.'"

**The Delay of Reform**

- **The Postponement of Railroad Construction**: Construction of the first railroad was not begun until Francis I died in 1835. Yet even after that, the government under Metternich continued to adhere to the same conservative policies. This state of stagnation persisted until the revolution of 1848. Eduard März noted that "the beginning of the industrial age came in the 1850s … when the old regime was no more." As in Russia after the Crimean War, the response of the domestic elites was the emancipation of the peasants and agricultural reform, after which economic policy turned to relatively active promotion of industrial development.

**Obstacles in the Economic and Social Structure**

- **High Rents from Feudal Land/Labor Relations**: As in Russia, the Austro-Hungarian elites derived substantial rents from the feudal land/labor relations they never reformed. Moreover, as landowners, they were vulnerable to expropriation in the course of political change, which further reinforced their resistance to industrialization.
- **The Heterogeneity of the Empire**: The multiethnic character of the Austro-Hungarian Empire was also an important factor. The Austrian aristocracy believed that modernization would destroy the social bonds that held the empire together.
- **Low Human Capital**: The low level of human capital once again reduced the elites' incentive to promote industrialization, because it increased the importance of land rents relative to income from industrial activity.

**Summary**

- **The Conservatism of the Habsburg Empire**: The history of the Habsburg Empire illustrates how political elites maintained their power by obstructing technological and institutional change, ultimately leading to the country's economic backwardness. Fear of the French Revolution and worry about the social unrest that industrialization might bring led the political elites of the Austro-Hungarian Empire to oppose economic modernization for most of the 19th century.
- **Application of the Model**: The situation of the Austro-Hungarian Empire was similar to that of Russia: high political rents and the threat to landownership made the elites more inclined to maintain the status quo rather than promote change. In addition, the heterogeneous social structure and the low level of human capital further intensified their resistance to modernization.

We believe that the main contribution of this section is that there is direct historical evidence showing that the mechanism that is the central focus of this paper was important for 19th-century industrialization.

## Conclusion

- **The Political Replacement Effect**: This paper proposes an explanation for why, in certain cases, political elites obstruct the technological and institutional changes that could promote economic development, namely that a political replacement effect exists. Even though change would increase total output and raise the elites' income, it would also shake their political position and make them more easily replaced. Hence, out of a motive of self-protection, the elites may choose to obstruct change.
- **The Effect of Political Competition on the Obstruction of Change Is Nonlinear**: When political competition is intense or almost nonexistent, the elites tend to accept change, whereas under a moderate degree of political competition, they may choose to obstruct it.
- **External Threats**: When external threats are strong, the elites are more likely to promote technological and institutional change in order to meet those threats.

## Extra

- This paper appears to answer a question everyone already knows the answer to, given that history has seen episodes of closing the country off from the outside world. But the paper's modeling and case studies are still very good! The two authors' writing is excellent, and they tell the story well. Readers will gain something from skimming it.
- This paper also answers why, in some countries, effective **factor markets**, **property-rights protection institutions**, and **legal systems** can never be established; why "996" is a blessing, and why the eight-hour workday cannot be guaranteed.
- This paper also supplements modernization theory. Modernization theory tends to view the relationship between economic development and political change as a linear and progressive process, holding that economic growth will naturally bring about democratization and political modernization. However, the "political replacement effect" proposed in the paper shows that political elites' response may be nonlinear, especially when political competition and the configuration of interests are complex.
