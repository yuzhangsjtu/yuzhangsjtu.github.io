As is well known, economic growth or innovation is, on the whole, beneficial. Yet why, in many instances, are governments unwilling to adopt policies that promote economic growth – and may even obstruct technological and institutional change?

The paper introduces the concept of the **"Political Replacement Effect**," arguing that political elites may block technological and institutional changes beneficial to economic development because such changes could weaken their political power, raising the likelihood of their replacement. Hence, even though economic development might create greater wealth, political elites may opt to obstruct such change in order to retain power.

## Why Political Elites May Oppose Economic Growth

Government policies and institutions shape economic incentives and, through this very channel, exert a direct and significant impact on economic development. Why, then, do so many societies adopt policies that **impede investment** and maintain institutions that lead to economic backwardness? Perhaps the politically powerful (the elites) are not in favor of economic growth. But why? Intuitively, economic growth should afford these groups more resources to expropriate or tax, thereby increasing their economic gains. Why are these powerful groups not uniformly supportive of economic development? Look at history below, for some concrete examples.

#### **The Uneven Pace of Industrialization in the 19th Century**

Bairoch (1982) estimated that, between 1830 and 1913, world manufacturing output increased fivefold, but that growth was extremely uneven across regions and countries ⬇️. During the same period, manufacturing output in **the developed countries** (Europe and North America) increased more than tenfold, while in **the Third World** it *fell*. Among developed countries, marked divergence also existed: Britain and the United States adopted new technologies and rapidly industrialized, whereas Russia, Austria-Hungary and Spain fell behind。why did these countries fail to adopt new technologies capable of raising their incomes？

We see sizeable growth in **Britain, Germany, the US, and France**, while **China** and some other countries experienced almost complete stagnation, or even decline.  

The differing performance across regions during the era of industrialization inspired Gerschenkron's celebrated essay *Economic Backwardness in Historical Perspective* (1962). In this essay, Gerschenkron perceived large cross-country disparities in the effort directed towards building the **institutions** necessary for promoting industrialization; in fact, in the most severely backward countries, political elites, far from actively encouraging industrialization, were actually impeding it. Gerschenkron writes：

> … economic progress began to be viewed with great suspicion and the railroads came to be regarded, not as welcome carriers of goods and persons, but as carriers of the dreaded revolution. Then the State clearly became an obstacle to the economic development of the country. 

Thus, the problem of understanding why industrialization proceeded fast in some countries and never got off the ground in others is closely related to the problem of understanding why, in some countries, the state promoted industrialization, while in others, it did not. Put more concretely: why, in some societies, did the state and political elites not only fail to encourage industrialization, but even hinder the introduction of the very new technologies and economic institutions — the establishment of efficient factor markets, property rights, and legal systems, for example — that are vital for industrialization??
