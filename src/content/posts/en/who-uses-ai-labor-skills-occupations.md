> Drawing on millions of **Claude.ai** conversations, this paper uses O*NET to match economic tasks and occupations, examining AI use in real work settings. It finds that AI usage is highly concentrated in **software development** and **writing** tasks (nearly half), with limited adoption in **physical** or high-barrier fields. Application is broad (36% of occupations use AI in at least a quarter of their tasks) but integration remains shallow (only 4% of occupations use it in more than three-quarters of their tasks). In interaction patterns, augmenting human capabilities (57%) slightly outweighs automation (43%), and peak usage is found in **upper-middle-wage** occupations with moderate requirements. The study's key contribution lies in providing a novel empirical framework to measure and dynamically track AI's economic role, offering an important data foundation for understanding technology's impact.

## Research background and significance

The rapid advance of artificial intelligence (AI) in recent years has had a profound impact on the labor market. Scholars broadly agree that AI technology will significantly change **the nature and distribution of future work**. However, most existing research on AI's impact on employment is based on **forecasting models**, **controlled experiments**, or **user surveys** — methods that struggle to dynamically track the relationship between advances in AI capability and actual application. For example, some studies build models to predict the likelihood of each occupation being replaced by AI; others measure, through experiments, AI's productivity gains for specific tasks; still others use questionnaires to understand AI adoption. Although these methods offer certain insights, they lack systematic empirical evidence on how AI is actually used in **real economic tasks**. Given the possible gap between AI technology and its practical application, relying on theoretical forecasts alone cannot adequately capture AI's real-time impact on the labor market.

To address this gap, Handa et al. propose a new empirical framework that measures patterns of AI use across various economic tasks by analyzing millions of **AI conversation records**. They draw on real user conversation data from *Anthropic*'s *Claude.ai* platform, mapping it to the task and occupation categories in the U.S. Department of Labor's O*NET occupational database in order to discover how AI is applied across different task domains. This study is the **first** large-scale empirical measurement of AI use in economic tasks, providing valuable first-hand evidence for understanding how AI is integrating into existing workflows. The framework enables **automated**, **fine-grained** tracking of AI's role in the economy, and can provide timely clues to policy-makers and academia about which domains may be the first to feel AI's effects. In sum, this study fills the chasm between theory and reality; its contribution lies in revealing, in a data-driven way, the actual extent to which AI is penetrating the labor market, laying a foundation for future research and policy responses.

## Data and methodology

#### **Data sources**

This study uses a large volume of conversation data from Anthropic's Claude.ai platform as the analytical basis. Specifically, the authors collected over four million **Claude.ai conversations** (a snapshot of conversations from Claude.ai's free and Pro users between December 2024 and January 2025). These conversations were anonymized, with sensitive information and enterprise/API-user data removed, to protect user privacy and to focus on individual users' usage patterns. The study considered only conversations with genuine task meaning (filtering out irrelevant chit-chat), and required that a given task be mentioned by at least five or more distinct users and across at least fifteen or more conversations before being counted in the analysis, ensuring representative and stable results. Through these strict data filters and privacy-protection measures, the authors ensured that the analysis rests on reliable and compliant real-world data.

#### **Analytical framework** 

The authors **map** the content of Claude.ai conversations onto the occupational tasks in the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics' O\*NET database. The **O\*NET database** contains roughly 20,000 fine-grained task descriptions, spanning various occupations and their duties. Because the variety of tasks is so vast, the study uses a hierarchical classification approach: first, clustering and a large language model are used to create a task hierarchy; then each conversation is classified, layer by layer, into the most relevant concrete task. This hierarchical classification alleviates the difficulty of classifying directly across 20,000 categories. Once a conversation is assigned to a bottom-level task, O\*NET is then used to link that task to the corresponding occupation and higher-level occupational category, so that statistics on AI usage across occupations and industry categories can be compiled.

#### **Classification tool** 

The core methodological instrument is the **Clio system** developed by Anthropic. Clio is a privacy-preserving conversation-analysis framework that uses a large language model (Claude) to perform aggregate analysis of large volumes of human–machine conversations. In brief, Clio uses pre-designed questions (facets) to have the AI assistant extract the required information from conversations. For example, the facets set in this study include: "Which O*NET task does this conversation correspond to?" and "What mode of human–machine collaboration does the conversation reflect?", among others. Without exposing the specific content of any individual user, Clio generates a summary and classification labels for each conversation, thereby achieving **automated, large-scale annotation of conversation content**. The authors used Clio to annotate the conversation content along three dimensions: 

- **Task classification:** Each conversation was mapped to the most relevant O*NET task (via the hierarchical task structure described above). This yields the frequency of AI usage across concrete tasks and occupations.
- **Skill classification:** The occupational skills that AI exhibited in conversations were retrieved (based on O*NET's 35 generic occupational skills, such as critical thinking, programming, communication, etc.). Clio judged which skills Claude deployed in its responses, thereby compiling the frequency with which different skills appear in AI conversations.
- **Interaction-mode classification:** The mode of human–machine collaboration was analyzed by assigning each conversation to one of five pre-defined **collaboration modes**. According to the role AI plays in the task, these modes are grouped into two broad categories — **automation** (AI does the work) or **augmentation** (AI assists) — including **Directive** (letting AI complete the task outright), **Feedback loop** (iteratively improving based on environmental feedback), **Task iteration** (human and machine refining the task back and forth), **Learning** (acquiring knowledge or understanding), and **Validation** (letting AI check or improve human work). The authors also performed a small amount of manual verification of Clio's classification results to ensure the reliability of the collaboration-mode judgments.

Through the above data and methods, the study characterizes the distribution of AI's uses in economic activity at the **task level**, and from that infers the degree to which different occupations and skill domains are affected by AI. Combined with the collaboration-mode analysis, it explores whether users mainly use AI as an automation tool or as an assistive partner. This methodological framework offers a new approach to dynamically monitoring AI's penetration of the labor market: compared with traditional forecasts, it is based directly on **real-world data on AI usage behavior**, and can therefore more accurately reflect the current interaction between AI and various industries.

## Key findings

#### **AI usage is unevenly distributed across tasks and occupations**

Concentrated in **software** and **text**-type tasks, with the least involvement in physical-operation tasks.

The study finds that current AI usage is mainly concentrated in tasks related to **software development** and **writing**; together, these two task categories account for nearly half of all AI usage. Concretely, occupations related to **computer and mathematics** (such as software engineers and data scientists) are associated with the most queries in Claude.ai conversations, accounting for **37.2%** of all queries. They are followed by **content-creation** occupations such as arts, design, entertainment, and media (about 10.3%), and by education and library occupations. Tasks in many business, finance, administrative-support, and management occupations also see a sizable share of AI use. Conversely, occupational tasks requiring **physical operation** or **highly specialized training** rarely appear in Claude.ai — for example, tasks associated with transportation and warehousing, healthcare support, and agriculture and forestry have the lowest usage rates. Even in high-paying professions such as anesthesiologist that require hands-on operation, AI participation is minimal. This distribution indicates that AI is currently used mainly for "**cognitively intensive**" tasks such as coding, copywriting, and data analysis, while its penetration of tasks requiring **on-site operation** or **manual skills** is limited. Even so, AI usage has already spread broadly across all economic domains: the study counts that roughly 36% of occupations use AI in at least one-quarter of their tasks. In other words, more than one-third of occupations have already incorporated a substantial proportion of AI support into their task mix. This usage, however, is often selective: in most occupations AI is used only for some tasks rather than all.

#### **The depth of AI penetration within an individual occupation is limited**

Only a tiny fraction of occupations have the vast majority of their tasks involving AI; for most occupations, only some tasks use AI.

The authors further examined what proportion of each occupation's tasks exhibit AI usage. The results show that only about 4% of occupations use AI on at least 75% of their tasks. In other words, almost no occupation can have the vast majority of its tasks assisted by AI. For example, **foreign-language teachers** are one of the few examples: about 75% of their tasks (such as instructional collaboration and curriculum planning) showed AI usage in the data, but some tasks (such as writing grant applications and managing student records) showed no AI involvement. Only about 11% of occupations use AI on at least half of their tasks — for example, marketing managers had AI support for 50% of their typical tasks (such as market-research analysis and strategy formulation), but many other duties (such as communicating product specifications and organizing trade shows) did not yet use AI. **About 36% of occupations used AI on more than a quarter of their tasks** — for example, physical therapists used AI for 25% of their tasks (mainly related to research and patient education), but the large remainder of their tasks (such as actual physical-therapy operations) had no AI involvement. Overall, most occupations currently introduce AI only on some tasks and have not yet achieved comprehensive coverage of the entire occupation's tasks. This means that, at this stage, AI is more of a **complement** to existing positions than a complete substitute; the core work content of most occupations has not yet been penetrated by AI on a large scale.

#### **Distribution of occupational skills exhibited in conversations**

AI excels at cognitive skills and is less involved in physical and interpersonal-management skills. By analyzing the O\*NET occupational skills that AI displays in Claude.ai conversations, the study finds that **cognitive-type skills** appear most frequently in AI outputs, while **physical-manipulation skills** appear the least. Concretely, among the 35 generic occupational skills, the ones Claude most often displays are those requiring mental thought and knowledge processing, such as **reading comprehension, writing, critical thinking, and programming**. The tasks corresponding to these skills (such as writing documents, debugging code, and logical reasoning) appear abundantly in the conversations. By contrast, **skills requiring hands-on operation** such as **installation, equipment maintenance, and repair** very rarely appear in AI conversations. In addition, some **management and social skills** are also uncommon — for example, leadership and management abilities such as **negotiation** register a very low frequency in Claude's conversations.

This result clearly reflects the **bias** of AI capabilities at the present stage: AI is better able to **complement human cognitive skills** (such as quickly writing text and code, and providing information and analysis), but **cannot function** in skills involving actual hands-on work or subtle social coordination. It should be noted that the study counts only the skills Claude exhibits in its responses, and does not mean users necessarily came to practice or test those skills. For example, "Active Listening" is the second most common skill in Claude's responses, but this is most likely because Claude often restates user input, asks clarifying questions, and engages in other default conversational behaviors, rather than because users specifically requested AI to listen. Overall, AI currently mainly displays an extension of human **cognitive ability**, and is hardly involved in abilities requiring **physical fitness or social interaction**.

#### **The relationship between AI usage rate and occupational wages and barriers to entry**

Upper-middle-income occupations use AI more, while the lowest-wage and highest-wage occupations have lower usage rates; occupations with moderate barriers see the highest AI penetration.

The authors analyzed occupational median wages in relation to AI usage frequency, and found that **AI usage is lower at both ends of the wage distribution and peaks in the upper-middle range**. That is, both **very low-income** jobs and the **highest-income** professional occupations use AI relatively little, while occupations with annual wages in the upper quartile but not at the very top have the highest AI usage. For example, low-wage service-industry jobs (such as restaurant servers) and top high-wage jobs (such as anesthesiologists) both rarely appear in the Claude.ai data, consistent with the earlier finding: jobs requiring physical labor or highly specialized skills see less AI involvement. By contrast, **upper-middle-wage technical occupations** such as computer programmers and web developers are a standout group for AI usage. This shows that, beyond technical feasibility, **the nature of the occupation itself (such as whether it is primarily cognitive labor) determines the AI adoption rate**, and that in the highest-income industries (such as doctors and lawyers), factors such as regulation, risk, and implementation cost may limit the widespread application of AI.

Beyond wages, the authors also examined the relationship between an occupation's barrier to entry (Job Zone) and AI usage. The Job Zone is O\*NET's grading of the level of preparation an occupation requires, ranging from 1 (no special training required) to 5 (requiring long-term specialized training/an advanced degree). The results found that as occupational requirements rise, AI usage first rises and then falls: from Zone 1 to Zone 4, AI usage gradually increases, peaking in Zone 4, which requires "considerable preparation"; but in the highest, Zone 5, it falls back. That is, occupations requiring upper-middle-level preparation such as a **bachelor's** degree use AI assistance most often, while occupations requiring the **lowest skills** and those requiring the highest skills such as a **doctorate** both have lower usage rates. This matches the wage trend: many Zone 4 occupations belong to fields such as software and engineering (above-average wages but not at the very top), while Zone 5 is mostly top occupations such as doctors and professors (occupations that, even where AI is capable, often involve high risk or professional ethics and are not easily replaced by AI). The above pattern shows that the barrier of human capital and AI usage are **not in a linear relationship**: AI currently penetrates more easily into occupational fields that require a certain amount of professional knowledge but not the most advanced specialized skills.

#### **In tasks, AI is used both to augment humans and to automate**

Conversational collaboration modes are mainly augmentation, but the proportions are close, and most occupations have both uses at once.

Through analysis of the interaction modes of Claude.ai conversations, the authors found that 57% of conversations present an "**augmentation**" mode, in which human and AI interact repeatedly during the process and iterate together to complete the task; 43% of conversations belong to the "**automation**" mode, in which the user hands a task directly to the AI to complete. This overall proportion indicates that users currently lean slightly more toward using AI as a collaborative partner to improve efficiency, rather than letting AI execute entirely on its own. However, the **difference** between the two modes across different tasks and occupations **is not absolute**: in most occupations, AI usage includes both automation and augmentation cases, presenting a mixed-use characteristic. This means that AI plays the roles of both an **"efficiency tool"** and a **"collaborative assistant"** at the same time, with no single use dominating exclusively.

In augmentation conversations, common cases are users having the AI participate in **task iteration** (such as polishing copy together, or progressively refining code) or **learning and explanation** (such as asking the AI for knowledge or to explain concepts), as well as having the AI **validate** human output (such as checking whether a SQL query is correct). In automation conversations, the main modes include **directive** (commanding the AI directly to complete a task, such as formatting a document) and **feedback loop** (having the AI continuously correct based on feedback, such as debugging code errors). The existence of both kinds of use shows that, on the one hand, AI can completely take over some tasks, while on the other hand, in more cases people treat AI as an assistive means to extend their own abilities. Notably, the authors point out that because users may adjust and edit AI output outside the conversation window, the actual augmentation use may be higher than the data suggest. In sum, AI's role in the workplace is currently neither a complete tool nor a complete agent, but rather the two roles coexisting, which provides important clues for understanding how AI affects the nature of work.

#### **Usage preferences differ across models**

Newer models lean toward technical tasks; older models are used more for creative and educational content.

The study also compared differences in task usage across different versions of the Claude model. During the study period, Anthropic released the newer model Claude 3.5 (codename "**Sonnet**") and the earlier model Claude 3 (codename "**Opus**"). The authors extracted the conversations of these two models separately and found that the newer Claude 3.5 (Sonnet) and the older Claude 3 (Opus) had distinct emphases in task usage. Specifically, Claude 3.5 was more often used for **programming** and technical tasks, such as developing and maintaining software, and writing and debugging code. This is consistent with external assessments noting the Sonnet model's stronger coding ability. By contrast, Claude 3 was more frequently used for **creative writing** and education-related content generation, such as writing literary and film/TV works, managing publishing workflows, designing course syllabi, and conducting academic research. This difference also echoes the common view in the user community that the Opus model is more distinctive in linguistic style and good at long-form text creation, while Sonnet performs better in technical accuracy and code generation. This **division of usage patterns** between models shows that **improvements in AI models' capabilities will guide them toward specialization in practical application**: when a new model is stronger in a certain area, users naturally use it more to perform tasks in that domain. This finding suggests that, in looking to the future, we must consider that different AI models may each have their own emphasis in the economy, and that as the direction of capability optimization differs, AI's impact on various industries will evolve accordingly.

In sum, the study sketches a **picture of AI's current application** in the labor market: concentrated on cognitively intensive tasks, broadly but shallowly diffused across many occupations, showing a complementarity to human intelligence rather than substitution for physical labor, with upper-middle-skill positions benefiting more clearly, and AI serving both as an assistant and as an executor of independent tasks. These insights provide empirical evidence for understanding how AI is changing various industries. Below, the key results are further interpreted in conjunction with the figures from the paper.

## Limitations analysis

Although this study is groundbreaking, the authors prudently point out several limitations, which bear on the explanatory power and external generalizability of the findings:

- **Representativeness of the data source (External Validity):** The study's data come entirely from Anthropic's Claude.ai platform. Although Claude is one of the mainstream LLMs, its user profile, model-capability preferences, product interaction design, and so on may differ from other platforms (such as ChatGPT, Gemini), so the findings may not fully generalize to the entire AI ecosystem. The constraint of the time window (late 2024–early 2025) and the restriction to the text modality alone also limit the timeliness and coverage of the conclusions. In addition, mapping global usage data onto the U.S. O*NET framework may overlook differences in economic structure and occupational tasks across countries and regions;
- **Validity and reliability of the classification method (Construct Validity & Reliability):** Relying on an LLM for conversation classification and task mapping itself introduces the possibility of measurement error. Although manual verification provides supporting evidence, the model's understanding of task semantics, the ambiguity and overlap of O*NET task descriptions, and the complexity of user query intent could all introduce some degree of noise into classification accuracy and consistency;
- **Limitations of the O\*NET framework:** As a classification system designed for the traditional economy, O*NET has an inherent lag in capturing the **emerging tasks and occupations** brought about by AI, a rapidly evolving technology. The granularity and standardization of its task descriptions may also be insufficient to match every nuance of all AI interactions with full precision;
- **Lack of downstream behavior data:** This study analyzed only the human–machine conversation process itself and cannot observe **how users actually apply this information** after obtaining AI output. Users may modify, integrate, validate, or ignore it, or use it only as a preliminary draft. This unknown about the "last mile" of application limits our ability to directly assess AI's actual value creation and ultimate degree of impact.

## Implications and outlook

Despite these limitations, the study's findings carry important implications for understanding AI's economic impact and for formulating corresponding strategies.

**First**, the empirical results both agree with and diverge from some earlier forecasting studies, showing that **technical feasibility** is not the only factor determining AI's actual adoption. For example, Webb (2019) once forecast that the highest-paid occupations (around the 90th income percentile) would be most affected by AI, whereas this study finds that the peak of AI usage occurs in the upper-middle wage band and is lower in the highest-income occupations. This suggests that factors such as **implementation cost**, **regulatory barriers**, and **organizational readiness** may be slowing AI's landing in top-tier industries. As another example, Eloundou et al. (2023) forecast that 80% of the U.S. workforce would have at least 10% of their work content affected by language models, but this study measures that about 57% of occupations currently reach this level. Although lower than the forecast, this proportion may trend toward the forecast value as AI capability improves and diffuses. Notably, the forecasts held that AI's impact would be large in industries such as healthcare, whereas this study's data do not yet show widespread adoption in healthcare; instead, usage in fields such as scientific research is higher than expected. These differences underscore the importance of relying on real-time data to adjust our understanding of AI's impact — what is technically possible does not equal what will be applied immediately, and policy and industry should consider real-world frictions and enabling factors.

**Second**, the method provided by this study demonstrates the feasibility and value of continuously monitoring AI's application in work settings. Compared with intermittent surveys or static forecasts, directly analyzing AI usage logs can **promptly capture newly emerging usage patterns**, helping us identify which industries may be approaching a technological inflection point and which fields lag in adoption owing to barriers. This **dynamic tracking** is especially beneficial for policy-making: regulators and decision-makers can use it to spot trends early, supporting promotion in time when AI substantially boosts efficiency in some fields, and, when automation accelerates in others, anticipating potential unemployment risks and preparing in advance. For example, if monitoring detects that an occupation's AI task coverage is rising markedly year over year and approaching full automation, government and enterprises can formulate training and transition plans ahead of time; conversely, if certain industries are seen lagging behind the wave of AI application over the long term, the barriers within (such as data privacy, cost investment) can be studied and corresponding support provided. In sum, observing AI usage continuously as an economic indicator can win all parts of society valuable reaction time, shifting from passive adaptation to proactive guidance.

**Third**, the study emphasizes the importance of analyzing AI's impact at the **task level**. The current evidence shows that AI often only enters some tasks of an occupation, rather than replacing the entire occupation. This means that, in the foreseeable future, many occupations may **evolve the composition of their duties** rather than be eliminated entirely — that is, adapting to AI assistance through **restructuring of work content**: humans take on more of the parts AI is not good at, AI takes on the parts that can be automated, and together they complete what was originally done by a person alone. However, if AI's task coverage across occupations continues to expand with no sign of saturation, that may portend a more **comprehensive workplace transformation**, in which some positions could be redefined or even replaced. Therefore, tracking the breadth and depth of AI usage at the task level can serve as a weathervane for predicting where occupations are headed. If AI remains confined to a limited set of tasks over the long term, one can well expect occupations to co-evolve; but once AI begins to master most of an occupation's tasks, one must be vigilant about more drastic changes to the employment structure.

**Fourth**, the impacts of using AI for augmentation versus for automation differ, and should be treated differently. When AI serves as an **augmentation tool**, humans remain deeply involved in the task and merely use AI to improve efficiency or capability; in this case it can often raise productivity while preserving the human's value and role. Multiple studies show that the human–machine collaboration mode aided by AI can let individuals complete work more efficiently without being marginalized, thereby achieving a "1+1>2" effect. Conversely, if AI completely **automates and replaces** a task, human labor may be excluded from that step; although short-term efficiency rises, in the long run this involves labor substitution and job loss, which society needs to address. Based on this study's finding that the two modes currently coexist with augmentation slightly ahead, policy-makers should **encourage and support augmentation-style applications of AI** — for example, developing friendlier human–machine collaboration interfaces and training employees in the skills to use AI tools, so that AI becomes employees' "assistant" rather than their "competitor." At the same time, for those fields where a clear automation trend does emerge, transition plans should be formulated in advance, including **re-employment training and social security**, to reduce the impact of full automation.

**Finally**, we must recognize that the current pattern of AI usage is only a beginning; in the future, as AI capabilities expand, occupational skill demands and the structure of positions may undergo more profound change. At present Claude.ai can only handle text, but the next generation of AI will master images, speech, and even **the execution of physical actions through robots**. This means that many tasks AI could not previously touch will gradually become automatable or assistable, potentially giving rise to entirely new task types and occupational forms. For example, AI robot assistants may take over part of caregiving and logistics work, and new occupations such as "AI operations monitor" may emerge. To this end, the **education** and training system needs to adjust proactively, emphasizing the cultivation of human abilities that are indispensable in collaborating with AI, such as creativity, critical thinking, cross-domain communication, and the supervision and management of AI decisions. This will help workers stay competitive amid the AI-driven evolution of positions. At the same time, the analytical framework demonstrated in this study also needs to iterate continuously: as the fields of AI application expand, we must update the definitions of tasks and skills, bringing newly emerging work into the scope of monitoring. Only in this way can we promptly capture the changes to the employment landscape triggered by AI and provide a basis for policy and social adjustment.

In sum, AI is gradually integrating into economic activity in the dual identity of **collaborative partner** and **automation tool**. Through large-scale data, this study demonstrates that this process has already begun, but its impact is still confined to specific fields and tasks. The key in the future lies in whether we can use similar empirical monitoring methods to continuously track AI's dynamic role across industries, and accordingly formulate wise strategies to shape a labor market in which humans and AI **coexist and advance together**. As the authors put it, the challenge is not only to measure these changes, but also to use our understanding of them to help shape a better future. We should plan ahead, letting policy and training keep pace with technological evolution, maximizing the productivity dividend AI brings while minimizing its impact on employment. Only in this way can occupational change in the AI era develop in a direction beneficial to the broad mass of workers and to society.
