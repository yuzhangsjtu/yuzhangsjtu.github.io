I sent out quite a few emails this month. Some replied; some ignored me.  

Among those who replied, there were big names. Among those who ignored me, there were big names too.  

So I suppose it’s a strangely unpredictable thing — which emails get a reply and which don’t.  

Some wrote at length; others offered only a line or two.  

But waiting for a reply, the whole time, has always been a fluttering mix of anxiety and joy.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/09/30/bC5QKNXralg3eOu.png)

I wrote an email in English to a professor at our own university who publishes in top-tier sub-journals, and the professor replied promptly — in Chinese. That was truly delightful.  

When I reached out to a heavyweight who has published in *ES&T*, I ended up receiving replies from both the professor and the first author, very promptly.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/09/30/BswxuGfS3IZWQM5.png)

The first author did not actually answer my question, so I followed up with a new one. And then something miraculously odd happened:  

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/09/30/kvtaloA694IXjHM.png)

The professor still offered no direct response.  

Then there was a journal editor-in-chief from a Nordic country — exceedingly courteous, patiently answering my trivial little questions and telling me not to worry.

![](https://i.loli.net/2020/09/30/JMIyhNX4WgbSkaw.jpg)

When I send an email, I look forward to receiving a reply. The moment that reply arrives, I feel a small happiness — like a tiny speck of starlight that has managed to connect with a great mind, and been allowed the happiness of simply looking up.

To all the professors who gave me patient, timely and sincere replies: may you publish in *Nature*. Or *Science* — that works too.  

They are like angels.  

Tomorrow is the Mid-Autumn Festival, and also National Day.  

October is surely a season full of harvest.
