> Did socialism truly promote gender equality? Using a sample of 88 countries across Asia and Europe from the 1910s to the 1980s, this study innovatively proposes the "adjusted **gender height gap**" as an indicator to measure the gendered allocation of health and nutrition resources during childhood, and on that basis evaluates the impact of socialist regimes on gender equality. The results show that socialist countries generally achieved **higher levels of gender equality** in both health and education, manifested in particular as increases in women's height and years of schooling. Through fixed-effects models, the instrumental variable method, and staggered DID, the authors provide robust causal identification evidence, demonstrating that institutional change can effectively weaken the family preference for "valuing sons over daughters" (son preference) and drive gender convergence in structural welfare.

**Title:** Gender equality in Asia and Europe during the 20th century: The role of socialism 

**Journal:** *Journal of Comparative Economics*

**Authors:**

- <u>Sophia Jung</u>: Ph.D. Student, University of Tübingen.

## Introduction

#### **Did socialism promote gender equality**

"Gender Equality is clearly crucial for at least half of humanity," and whether or not it is achieved also has profound implications for the overall well-being of all humankind. In the early 21st century, however, the state of gender equality in some regions has shown signs of **stagnation** or even **regression**—for example, the deteriorating situation in Afghanistan, and the backsliding in some developed countries (such as Italy, where the gender wage gap actually widened between 2000 and 2015). It is widely held in academia that improving gender equality can let men and women **benefit together** and promote an overall rise in welfare. Therefore, understanding the factors and mechanisms that affect gender equality is of great significance for achieving the fifth of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (promoting gender equality).

Among the many influencing factors, the status of women under different **political and economic systems** has long been a focus of debate. One unresolved question is: Does the **socialist** system actually raise gender equality, or does it lower gender equality because of the "**double burden**" effect on women?

- On the one hand, socialist ideology proclaims equality between men and women, carries out women's liberation movements and equal employment and education policies, and may help improve women's status. For instance, countries such as the Soviet Union and **China** promulgated a series of laws guaranteeing women's education and labor participation after the founding of their states. In theory, socialist countries liberate women by regarding them as "members of society," encourage women to leave the household to take part in production, and provide socialized services such as nurseries, in the hope of simultaneously removing domestic constraints;
- On the other hand, many researchers point out that in these countries women often **both** have to take on full-time work **and have not** been freed from household and domestic responsibilities—that is, a "**double labor**" burden emerges—which may offset the rights and interests they have gained in the public sphere, and even reduce their life satisfaction.

In academia, there has long been disagreement around the question of "whether socialism helps to raise gender equality":

- Some studies emphasize the improvement in gender equality under the socialist system. Alesina and Fuchs-Schündeln (2007) find that the socialist system of **East Germany** shaped a more egalitarian outlook among the people, an attitude that remained influential long after reunification. Campa and Serafinelli (2019) point out more specifically that socialism had a positive impact on women's notions of equality and on their workplace participation;
- However, some studies have also raised doubts about this. Scholars such as Becker et al. (2020, 2021) remind us to note the structural differences that existed between East and West Germany even before World War II—for example, the eastern region industrialized earlier and the communist movement was more active there—which may **weaken** the inferential validity of the "natural experiment."

Therefore, there is still a lack of systematic empirical research that is **multi-country**, long-term, and capable of **causal identification** regarding whether socialism truly improved women's status and gender equality.

#### **The height gap as a novel indicator**

An important theoretical and methodological innovation of this paper is its proposal to use the "**adjusted male–female height gap**" as a new structural indicator for measuring gender equality (especially at the health and nutrition level). Traditional gender-equality indicators such as years of schooling, income gaps, or life expectancy, though important, were scarce in the early 20th century and are easily subject to **government manipulation** (such as the "political whitewashing" of life-expectancy data).

**Height**, by contrast, is different:

- It is a comprehensive reflection of childhood nutrition and health, especially during the growth spurts at ages 2–5 and during adolescence;
- In a culture of "valuing sons over daughters," male children may obtain more resources, leading to greater height in adulthood;
- If, in socialist countries, women's height gradually approaches that of men, this may reflect an improvement in family and societal investment in women.

Of course, the height gap is affected by physiology and cannot simply be used directly. The authors adopt an existing research method and, by comparing against countries at the intermediate level of the "**Gender Development Index**" (GDI), extract a mechanism for distinguishing **biological differences** from **social differences**, thereby establishing an adjusted gender height-equality index. This index becomes the core measurement tool of the entire paper.

Around the above problem awareness and measurement innovation, the article puts forward three core hypotheses:

- H1: After controlling for physiological differences, the gender height gap can reflect gender inequality in childhood health and nutrition;
- H2: The socialist system can narrow the gender gap at the health and nutrition level, manifested as an improvement in the height difference;
- H3: Similar trends of improving gender equality should also be observed in other domains such as education and life expectancy.

To test these hypotheses, the authors construct a cross-sectional time-series dataset covering the 1910s to the 1980s and comprising 88 countries, with a particular focus on **Asian** and **European** countries. Asia and Europe underwent large-scale institutional transformations during the 20th century, with many countries becoming socialist states in different periods, constituting an ideal scenario of institutional variation.

The empirical results show that, after controlling for each country's inherent characteristics and common time trends, the implementation of the **socialist** system has a robust **positive correlation** with significant improvements in gender equality. This improvement is reflected not only in the biological welfare that captures childhood nutrition and health, but is also consistently corroborated in the field of education. Tested through multiple causal-inference methods such as the instrumental variable method and the staggered difference-in-differences model, the study's conclusions further indicate that the socialist system's promotion of gender equality has a **causal effect**, rather than being merely a correlation.

#### **Contributions of the study**

- **Tests the gap between official declarations and actual equality, and concretizes the "double burden" debate**. This study assesses whether the legal and political declarations of socialist countries translated into real gender equality. By measuring the reduction in the "son preference" bias, it provides a quantifiable, biological-welfare-based empirical test for the otherwise abstract debate over the "double burden" effect (Schwekendiek and Baten, 2019), thereby deepening our understanding of women's actual situation under the socialist system;
- **Goes beyond a single "natural experiment" case and provides more generalizable cross-national evidence**. Existing research relies heavily on case analyses of North Korea/South Korea (Goodkind, 1999; Pak et al., 2011) or East Germany/West Germany (e.g., Alesina and Fuchs-Schündeln, 2007; Becker et al., 2020). By means of a large sample covering numerous Eurasian countries, this study systematically tests the relationship between socialism and gender equality, and its conclusions have greater external validity than studies based on specific cases;
- **Provides an innovative, systematic measurement tool for the study of gender inequality over the long run**. Facing the limitations that traditional gender-equality indices (such as GDI, GII) can only be traced back to the end of the 20th century, and that historical indices (such as HGEI by Dilli et al., 2019) begin in the 1950s, this study systematically develops and applies the "adjusted gender height gap" as a measurement indicator. This inherits and greatly extends earlier scattered attempts in specific countries (such as Costa-Font and Gil, 2008; Schwekendiek and Baten, 2019), and is the **first** to use this indicator for large-scale cross-national analysis, thereby filling a key methodological gap in the quantitative study of gender inequality in the early 20th century.

## Data and Methods

#### **The construction logic of the gender height gap**

The authors adopt a data-driven approach to construct this indicator:

- **Basic idea**: The study's starting point is that changes in the male–female height difference can reflect changes in gender equality. The underlying mechanism is that differences in family investment in boys and girls in terms of nutrition, medical care, and so on affect their growth, ultimately reflected in adult height. This idea draws on the research of Gray and Wolfe (1980), Guntupalli and Baten (2009), and others.
- **Core challenge: stripping out the biological effect**. This paper clearly points out that measuring gender height inequality **cannot simply** use male height minus female height. The biggest challenge is that the height gap is strongly affected by biological laws. When a group's overall nutrition improves and average height increases, the increase in male height usually exceeds that in female height, causing the height gap to widen naturally. Ignoring this might lead to the absurd conclusion that "an improvement in living standards actually aggravates gender inequality."

In order to precisely strip out, from the observed total height gap, the portion caused by **social inequality**, the authors design a sophisticated, three-step calibration method. This method was first systematically proposed by Schwekendiek and Baten (2019), and this paper applies it on that basis:

- **Step one: establish a "biological benchmark" sample**: The authors adopt a data-driven, pragmatic strategy. They first use a recognized, non-height gender-equality indicator—the **Gender Development Index (GDI)**—to select from the world's countries a group of countries with "medium" or "typical" performance in gender equality. This sample includes Canada, China, France, Poland, Russia, Spain, Thailand, the United States, and the United Kingdom. The **core assumption** of this approach is that within this group of countries, although social gender discrimination still exists, its degree is relatively moderate and internally fairly homogeneous. Therefore, the relationship between the height gap and average height in these countries can be approximately regarded as a "normal" relationship dominated mainly by **biological laws**. The function of this benchmark sample is to provide a "frame of reference" for the world;

- **Step two: estimate the "biological baseline"**: Using the data points of these 9 benchmark countries across different historical periods (the 1910s–1980s), the authors conduct a simple linear regression analysis. They take the "gender height gap" as the dependent variable and the "male–female average height" as the independent variable. The regression analysis yields a very stable linear relationship, as shown in formula (1) and Figure 1 of the paper. This regression line (expected height gap = constant term + coefficient * average height) represents how the height gap should change with the overall nutrition level in the absence of extreme social gender bias. This line is the "**biological baseline**" constructed by the authors. ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250630104227857.png)

- **Step three: calculate the gender inequality indicator**: With this baseline, it becomes possible to measure the gender-equality situation of any country (whether or not it is in the benchmark sample). For any country-period observation point, its **gender inequality indicator** is defined as the **difference** between its actual height gap and the "biological expectation" height gap predicted on this baseline according to its average height—that is, the **residual**.

- **Intuitive understanding**:
  - If a country's observation point (such as Bangladesh in Figure 2 of the paper) lies far **above** this baseline, this means its actual height gap is much larger than biological laws can explain. This "excess" gap is caused by the social "son preference." In this case, the residual is a large positive value, representing severe gender inequality. ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250630104309534.png)
  - If a country's observation point falls exactly on or near the baseline, it means its height gap is consistent with biological expectations, and gender relations are relatively equal.
  - If a country's observation point lies **below** the baseline (for example, Russia after implementing socialism), it means its height gap is even smaller than biologically expected, which represents a very high level of gender equality.

For ease of interpretation, in subsequent analyses the authors usually take the negative of this residual indicator, thereby obtaining a "**gender equality index**" (Gender Equality of Height, GIh), where **a higher value represents greater gender equality**.

The core height data come from the **NCD Risk Factor Collaboration (NCD-RisC, 2016)**, a global academic collaboration project that pools a vast amount of height measurement data.

#### **Other variables**

- **Socialism**: the core independent variable of this paper. For a country to be defined as socialist, it must meet the following conditions: it is ruled by a **single party** that **self-identifies as socialist** and follows **Marxist theory**. This definition excludes democratic countries governed by socialist parties under a multi-party system, ensuring the strictness of the definition. The authors also set a threshold of at least five years in power, in order to exclude short-lived regimes.

- **Other dimensions of gender equality**:
  - **Education**: Using data from Barro and Lee (2013), the gap in overall male and female enrollment rates is calculated as an indicator for measuring gender equality in the field of education.
  - **Life expectancy**: Using data from multiple databases (such as the Human Mortality Database), the gap in male and female life expectancy is calculated.
- **Control variables**: In order to control for other factors in the regression analysis that may affect gender equality, the authors introduce a series of control variables:
  - **Legal origins**: Following the classification of La Porta et al. (2008), distinguished into English, French, German, Scandinavian, and **socialist** legal systems. This aims to control for the long-term impact of different legal traditions on institutional and economic outcomes.
  - **Religion**: Using the World Religion Database, the population shares of Catholics, Protestants, and Muslims are calculated, in order to control for the influence of different religious cultures on gender views.
  - **Family systems**: Following the classification of Todd (1985) and Rijpma and Carmichael (2016), countries are divided into seven different family systems (such as the absolute nuclear family, the authoritarian family, etc.), in order to control for the influence of deep-rooted family structures and inheritance systems.
  - **Level of economic development**: Using the **per capita GDP** data of the Maddison Project as a variable to control for the level of economic development.

## Results

#### **Descriptive results**

The figure below (Figure 5) presents the descriptive results. The sample countries are divided into two groups: "**early socialist countries**" (which became socialist before 1950) and "**countries that were never socialist**." The results show that in the early 20th century, the two groups of countries had roughly the same level of gender equality (as measured by height). However, starting in the 1920s, the level of gender equality in socialist countries began to climb rapidly, opening up a significant gap with non-socialist countries in the 1940s and 1950s. Although the gap narrowed somewhat in later periods, socialist countries maintained a higher level of gender equality during most of the period.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250630105135338.png)

To avoid the heterogeneity problem that an overall average might mask, the authors conduct a finer "paired" comparison (Figure 6). They select **Azerbaijan** and **Iran**, two neighboring countries with deep geographic, cultural, and historical ties. Before Azerbaijan was incorporated into the Soviet Union in the 1920s, the two countries had similar gender-equality situations, with Iran even slightly higher. However, after the institutional divergence, the two countries' paths diverged dramatically: Azerbaijan's gender equality index rose steadily, while Iran lingered at a low level for a long time. This case vividly demonstrates how, against similar cultural backgrounds, a change in political institutions can bring about entirely different social outcomes.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250630105320646.png)

#### **Results of the regression analysis**

**Baseline regression**

In the baseline regression part, the authors use a two-way fixed-effects model, and the results show that there is a **significantly positive** relationship between the socialist system and the gender equality index (after taking logarithms). Specifically, implementing socialism raises the gender equality index by about **0.17** points. Considering that the sample standard deviation of this index is about 0.31, the size of this effect is close to **half a standard deviation**, which in economics is considered to have considerable **economic significance**, rather than being merely statistically significant.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250630105632146.png)

⬇️ After dividing the sample into Asia and Europe, it is found that this positive effect exists in both continents, indicating that this conclusion has a certain generalizability.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250630105854354.png)

The authors conduct a series of robustness checks, the results of which are, of course, consistent with the baseline regression.

#### **Instrumental variable estimation**

To further mitigate the endogeneity problem (i.e., whether "more equal countries are more likely to choose socialism"), the authors use the **geographic distance from Moscow** as an instrumental variable.

- The first-stage regression shows that the farther the distance from Moscow, the lower the probability that a country turns to socialism, and the F-statistic far exceeds 10, indicating that it is a strong IV;
- In the second-stage regression, the coefficient of the socialism variable rises to **0.29–0.36 (Eurasia)** and **0.48–0.79 (Asia)**, slightly higher than in the OLS model.

The strengthening of the instrumental variable results indicates that OLS may have measurement error and thus underestimate the true effect. The authors also test the exogeneity of the instrument, controlling for the distance "from London" and "from the United States," and the results remain robust.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250630110057754.png)

**Staggered DID estimation**

Since the socialist system was introduced in different countries at different times, the authors adopt the **staggered DID method** proposed by Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) to estimate **the dynamic path of the impact of introducing the system**.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250630110326901.png)

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250630110620422.png)

The dynamic effects revealed by the event study show that in the multiple periods **before** the "treatment" occurs (i.e., before a country becomes socialist), the coefficients of the treatment and control groups both fluctuate around the zero line, with no significant difference. **After** the "treatment" occurs, the coefficient representing the treatment effect begins to be significantly positive and remains so persistently. This clearly demonstrates the causal effect brought about by institutional change. (I always feel this figure looks a bit odd.)


## Conclusions and Discussion

- **Core finding**: Socialist countries did **raise** gender equality in the 20th century, especially reflected in the improvement of women's health and nutrition conditions; using the "adjusted gender height gap" as an indicator, a significant positive correlation is found between the socialist system and the improvement of girls' growth conditions;

- **Mechanism discussion**: Because the study uses macro-level cross-national data, this paper **cannot directly test** the specific micro-level mechanisms. However, based on rich country studies and existing literature, the authors propose a most likely explanatory framework that integrates two levels—culture and incentives:

  - **Cultural-level change**: Socialist regimes vigorously promoted the ideology of gender equality through powerful propaganda machinery, cultural products (such as films and novels), and school curricula. This continuous "indoctrination" aimed to change deep-rooted traditional cultural notions, a relatively slow, subtle process;
  - **Change in the incentive structure**: Compared with cultural change, institutional changes in incentives may take effect more quickly. Through a series of concrete policies, socialist governments directly changed the decision-making environment faced by families and individuals. These policies included:
    - **Expanding public services**: making education and medical care universal, and establishing large numbers of nurseries.
    - **Guaranteeing economic rights**: legislating equal pay for equal work, and encouraging and mobilizing women to enter the labor market.


  These measures directly lowered the **opportunity cost** of families raising and educating girls, and raised the returns to women's participation in socioeconomic activity. The family, as a rational decision-making unit, responds to these changes in incentives.
