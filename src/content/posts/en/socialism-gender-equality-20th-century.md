> Has socialism genuinely promoted gender equality? Using a sample of 88 countries across Asia and Europe from the 1910s to the 1980s, this study innovatively proposes the “adjusted **gender height gap**” indicator to measure the gendered allocation of health and nutrition resources during childhood, and on that basis evaluates the impact of socialist regimes on gender equality. The results show that socialist countries broadly achieved **higher levels of gender equality** in both health and education, manifested notably through increases in women’s height and years of schooling. Employing fixed-effects models, instrumental variable approaches, and staggered difference-in-differences methods, the authors provide robust causal identification evidence demonstrating that institutional change can effectively weaken “son preference” within the family and drive gender convergence in structural welfare.

**Title:** Gender equality in Asia and Europe during the 20th century: The role of socialism 

**Journal:** *Journal of Comparative Economics*

**Authors:**

- <u>Sophia Jung</u>: Ph.D. Student, University of Tübingen.

## Introduction

#### **Has socialism promoted gender equality**

“Gender Equality is clearly crucial for at least half of humanity,” and whether or not it is realized also has far-reaching consequences for the overall well‑being of all humankind. In the early 21st century, however, progress on gender equality has shown signs of **stagnation** or even **backsliding** in some regions—for instance, the worsening situation in Afghanistan and the rollback in certain developed countries (e.g., the gender wage gap in Italy actually widened between 2000 and 2015). Scholars widely agree that advancing gender equality **benefits both men and women** and enhances overall welfare. Understanding the determinants and mechanisms that shape gender equality is therefore crucial for achieving Sustainable Development Goal No. 5 (promoting gender equality).

Among the many influencing factors, the position of women under different **political and economic systems** has long been the subject of heated debate. One unsettled question is: Does **socialism** raise gender equality, or does it instead diminish it through the “**double burden**” effect on women?

- On one hand, socialist ideology explicitly proclaims gender equality and carries out women’s liberation movements alongside equal opportunity employment and education policies, which may help improve women’s status. The Soviet Union and **China**, for example, enacted a raft of laws guaranteeing women’s rights to education and workplace participation after the founding of their new regimes. In theory, socialist states set out to liberate women “as members of society,” encouraging them to leave the household to take part in production and providing socialized services such as childcare, with the aim of simultaneously freeing them from domestic entanglements;
- On the other hand, many researchers point out that, in these very countries, women often acquired **full‑time jobs** **while still not** being relieved of domestic responsibilities—resulting in the so‑called “**double burden**” of labor—which may offset any gains they won in the public sphere and even reduce life satisfaction.

The question “does socialism help advance gender equality?” has long divided academic opinion:

- Some studies emphasize gender‑equality gains under socialism. Alesina and Fuchs-Schündeln (2007) find that the socialist system in **East Germany** forged attitudes of greater egalitarianism that continued to exert wide‑ranging influence even after reunification. Campa and Serafinelli (2019) more specifically show that socialism positively shaped ideas about female equality as well as female labor-force participation;
- Nevertheless, other research has challenged such findings. Scholars such as Becker et al. (2020, 2021) warn about pre‑WWII structural differences between East and West Germany—for instance, earlier industrialization in the East and the more active communist movement there—differences that potentially **weaken** the inferential force of the “natural experiment.”

Thus, systematic, empirical studies with **multi‑country**, long‑run scope and solid **causal identification** capacities are still lacking with regard to whether socialism genuinely improved female status and gender equality.

#### **Height gap as a novel indicator**

A major theoretical and methodological contribution of the paper is its proposal of the “**adjusted female–male height gap**” as a new structural measure of gender equality, particularly in terms of health and nutrition. Conventional gender‑equality indicators such as years of schooling, income gaps, or life expectancy are important, but scarce early in the 20th century and susceptible to **government manipulation** (the “political prettification” of life‑expectancy statistics, for instance).

**Height**, by contrast, is different:

- It integrally reflects childhood nutrition and health, especially during the rapid‑growth windows from ages 2 to 5 and during the adolescent growth spurt;
- In cultures of “son preference,” young boys may capture more resources, resulting in taller adult stature;
- If women substantially close the height gap with men under socialist auspices, this may reflect improved household‑ and society‑level investments in girls.

Certainly the height gap is affected by biology and cannot be used simply in its raw form. The authors draw on established research methods and compare countries at intermediate positions of the **Gender Development Index** (GDI) to extract a mechanism that isolates the **biological difference** from the **socially produced** difference, thereby producing an adjusted gender equality‑of‑height index. This index serves as the paper’s core measurement instrument.

Around this problematic and methodological innovation, the paper proposes three central hypotheses:

- H1: After controlling for biological differences, the gender height gap can capture gender inequality in childhood health and nutrition;
- H2: Socialist systems narrow the gender gap in health and nutrition, indicating improvements that show up as a shrinking of the height difference;
- H3: Parallel improvements in other spheres, such as education and life expectancy, should also be observable.

To test these hypotheses, the authors assemble a panel dataset covering from the 1910s to the 1980s and comprising 88 countries around the world, with a special geographic focus on **Asia** and **Europe**. During the 20{{< sup 20th >}}} century both regions experienced large‑scale institutional transitions, and many countries within them at one point or another became socialist states, creating ideal institutional‑variation settings for research.

The empirical results demonstrate that, with inherent country characteristics and common time trends controlled for, the introduction of **socialism** is associated with a robust, statistically meaningful **positive relationship** with meaningful improvements in gender equality. Such improvement is saliently present not only in biological welfare captured by early‑life nutrition and health (height) but is also corroborated in the sphere of education. Through the application of multiple causal‑inference approaches—including instrumental variable estimation and staggered difference‑in‑differences designs—the study findings further indicate that socialism’s contribution to strengthening gender equality possesses a **causal effect**, rather than merely a correlational association.

#### **Contributions of the study**

- **It verifies the gap between official pronouncements and actual equality, and concretizes the “double burden” debate**. This study assesses whether socialist law and political rhetoric truly translated into actual gender parity. By directly measuring the decline in the “son preference” cultural bias driving childhood surplus height gaps, it offers a quantified, biological‑welfare‑based empirical test amidst long‑standing abstract discourse about the “double burden” effect (Schwekendiek and Baten, 2019), thereby deepening our knowledge of women’s real situation under socialism;
- **It moves beyond single “natural experiment” cases to supply large‑N cross‑national evidence with broader universality.** Current works draw heavily on comparisons between cases or duos (e.g., North‑ and South Korea [Goodkind, 1999; Pak et al., 2011] or East‑ and West Germany [Alesina and Fuchs‑Schündeln, 2007; Becker et al., 2020]). The present work underpins its examinations upon wide‑scoped panel samples covering numerous European and Asian nations that tested systematically the relation between systems and gender equality.
