Waiting is the knot of growing old.

Yesterday a close friend and I argued about what maturity means. I thought maturity was telling right from wrong, having things you like and things you don’t, being upright and brave. My friend, however, said: “Maturity is being able to handle things in an orderly way, plan clearly, know what you want, and not swing between emotional highs and lows—stuff like that.”

My friend’s definition of maturity only threw my own immaturity into sharper relief.

That’s a sad thing to realise.

<br>

This whole week, I’ve been in a storm of extreme highs and lows.

Sunday midday, I’d already left Kunshan Building when I got an email from a professor asking me to write back. After I replied, more anxious waiting. I woke from a nap and found that an email had arrived right after I dozed off: book a Tencent Meeting for an interview that afternoon.

I went to the faculty building and picked up the key to the meeting room. The security uncle said, *xiān xià shǒu wéi qiáng*—strike first to gain the upper hand. I settled in quietly. Roped in Dada for backup; checked the connection. Self-introduction: stumbling and full of pauses. Once the meeting began, a relentless stress test followed—it felt exactly like getting hammered by reviewers. After nearly fifty minutes, the interview ended. I asked and learned the professor had other candidates, which instantly felt like being kept on the hook. Toward dusk I chatted with senior WQ and Dada. It didn’t look promising. Right in the middle of that, another message from the professor: if I were admitted, would I definitely go?

> Yes!
>
> Of course! Definitely!
>
> Four reasons, topic sentences all the same length, one solid argument. I’ll go!

A glimmer of hope crept back in.

The next day my mood had already steadied somewhat, but at noon I got a WeChat message from the professor saying she’d sent two emails and I’d replied to neither. Panic. I scrambled to open a mail app that never bothered to notify me. Flat out, prepping materials due before 4 PM that same day.

Forget about a nap—rush to the lab. Grateful for the guardian-angel help from friends. I submitted the materials at 15:59. In the hurry, I didn’t even get the file naming right; the professor gave me a talking-to about that. Much obliged for the guidance. After that she told me to sign up in the system, get everything ready. But… I couldn’t get the registration through. In limbo once more: the kind of waiting that comes with deep uncertainty.

“The result will be out quickly—it’ll be out before the 7th.”

Tuesday, no result. Wednesday, still nothing. Thursday, the same.

Friday was the end of the working week. Would Friday bring news? Friday: morning, nothing. Afternoon, nothing. All the way to quitting time—still nothing.

The following Monday was the 7th. Could news come that day?

With senior WQ’s help, I dug up more info about this joint programme and realized that the chance of getting in was very, very slim.

That Monday evening, the professor told me the partner university was UNSW — a sudden wave of longing welled up. I dreamed that night that I’d gone to Sydney.

I kept hoping for good news, and then share it with Mum and Dad.

Even a close friend said, “It feels like overnight you suddenly have a place to land.”

For the first two days, I was genuinely upbeat, convinced I’d found my harbour.

But this brief, brief spell of waiting, like a journey that stretches on and on: all the joys and sorrows that had lain buried during the last two years staged themselves at high intensity across those few days.

Like a crush suddenly leaving messages unanswered.

Like her giving me the cold shoulder forever, then suddenly reaching out again — wanting to ignore her, but finding myself ecstatic all the same.

Maybe, I ought to accept that version of maturity too: being able to handle my own emotions.

Updated 10 December

Monday morning brought no news. At 4 PM I asked the professor, who said, “Keep waiting.”

Tuesday, no news either.

Wednesday morning something began to take shape; that evening I got the official word, and after I had a long WeChat call with the professor.

As senior WQ put it, it felt like being someone who had miraculously survived a disaster.

Just that morning, I had been ready to surrender to Professor TR.

By evening, I had found a place to land.

Thank you, Dada, senior WQ, Kui-ye, fellow-student Deng Xiaomin, Dr Wang, Monkey-ge, Rongrong — you poured in help like angels!

The light and warmth you gave me flow now like blood through my veins.
