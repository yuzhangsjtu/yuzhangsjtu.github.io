> This paper examines how the promotion incentives of Chinese mayors affect the manipulation of local GDP data. Employing a regression discontinuity design (RDD), the study takes advantage of the sharp decline in promotion probability at age 57 and compares official GDP data with economic indicators that are difficult to manipulate (such as nighttime lights).
>
> The findings indicate that prior to 2013, the promotion incentives of mayors resulted in a 3.4 percentage point increase in the statistical GDP growth rate, yet had no significant effect on indicators resistant to manipulation. This discrepancy (approximately 3 percentage points) is attributed to GDP data manipulation. After 2013, this pattern disappeared as GDP’s prominence in promotion evaluations diminished.

**Title:** Mayors’ promotion incentives and subnational-level GDP manipulation

## Introduction

- In comprehending the driving factors behind the spatial distribution of regional economic growth, the role of local officials is especially important. Across distinct political systems, the incentive mechanisms for local officials differ. In democracies, officials are held responsible through elections, whereas, in authoritarian regimes, they are incentivized through performance evaluations by superiors (**elections vs. promotions**). This paper probes the unintended effects that the promotion incentives of Chinese local officials have on regional development.
- The economic reforms initiated in China in 1978 markedly raised the nation's economic growth rate from roughly 4% to over 9% average annual growth. Many studies attribute this growth to the performance-based cadre evaluation system, which established regional **GDP growth** as the central metric in promotion decisions for local officials. Local officials’ promotion incentives directly led to more effective management of local economies. Existing empirical analyses largely rely on the officially reported regional GDP figures.
- **Research Question**: Did officials cheat in the ultimate promotion examination designated by GDP growth-driven promotion competitions? If so, what was the degree of GDP overstatement?

## Institutional Background of Chinese Official Promotions

#### **Performance-Based Cadre Evaluation System**

In China, the political reassignments of local officials are typically decided by upper-level governments. A case in point is that city-level officials, like mayors, are overseen by provincial organizational departments. Following the reform and opening up, provinces and cities have played a significant role in economic management, holding more power over resource allocation than in earlier periods. Provincial and prefectural leaders, to some degree, mirror mid-level managers in a large, diversified corporation, each bearing responsibility for the performance of their specific division.

Through decentralization, which aimed to allot more authority to local leaders, central and provincial authorities intended to induce efforts by linking local economic performance to promotions. Government reports and provincial almanacs detail the comparative rankings of provincial performance, enumerating accomplishments spanning GDP growth, steel output, and kilometers of roads built.

- In 1993, the Central Organization Department of the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) created a merit review policy across central ministries, putting in perspective how government employees are evaluated for different levels of service performance.
