# The "Last Man" Crisis and Human Spiritual Transcendence in the Age of AI — A Critical Examination Based on Nietzsche's Philosophy

**Abstract**: The rapid evolution of artificial general intelligence (AGI) is reshaping the modes of production, organizational forms, and value systems of human society. What it provokes is not merely a set of normative issues in technology ethics, but philosophical questions concerning the very essence of human existence. Taking the concept of the "Last Man" proposed by Nietzsche in *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* as its analytical framework, this essay systematically examines the inner logic and evolutionary path of human spiritual alienation amid AI-driven social transformation. It finds that, absent active value guidance, the techno-social structure of a post-work society carries a path dependence toward the "Last Man" condition — by systematically dissolving meaningful striving, personal responsibility, and collective enterprise, it locks humanity into a spiritually impoverished state of comfort-seeking devoid of transcendence. This trend, however, is not the inevitable destination of technological evolution; it can be reversed through the reconstruction of value-alignment mechanisms, the transformation of educational paradigms, and innovation in social governance frameworks. The core thesis: the decisive variable for humanity's spiritual trajectory in the AI era lies not in technical capability itself, but in whether we can build defenses against "Last Man"-ification across the multiple dimensions of technical design, institutional arrangement, and individual practice. The issue carries universal philosophical significance, and bears directly on the major practical question of how China, in its intelligent transformation, can balance instrumental and value rationality and achieve high-quality development.

**Keywords**: artificial intelligence; Nietzsche's philosophy; the Last Man; technological alienation; value alignment

## I. The Problem: The Dual Dimensions of Technological Leap and Existential Anxiety

In November 2022, the breakthrough of large language models, represented by ChatGPT, marked a new historical stage in AI development. Within a window of barely two years, generative AI displayed a capability trajectory that took academia and industry by surprise, extending from text generation and image creation to code writing and scientific reasoning, profoundly reshaping the boundaries and paradigms of human intellectual activity.① The mainstream judgment of the scholarly community and industry holds that artificial general intelligence (AGI) — AI systems that reach or surpass human level across all cognitive task dimensions — is no longer a distant technological fantasy but a foreseeable historical process; optimistic scholars even anchor that moment within the coming decade.②

Yet beneath the halo of techno-progressivist narrative, a deep existential anxiety is spreading through intellectual circles and forming a new theoretical agenda. When AI's capability frontier extends to most or even all domains of human labor, wherein lies the ontological meaning of human existence? When creativity and wisdom — once regarded as the core determinations of being human — can be "outsourced" to machines, where does our drive for excellence come from? These questions exceed the merely regulative level of technology ethics, touching the fundamental issues of human self-understanding and the direction of civilization. As one scholar acutely observed, the deep embedding of AI confronts us with "the greatest challenge and most profound disintegration in history," forcing us to re-examine the stability premise of "the social structure and human nature grounded in productive practice."③

This essay argues that to understand the spiritual crisis humanity may face in the AI era and its inner mechanism, the concept of the "Last Man" (Der letzte Mensch) proposed by Nietzsche at the end of the nineteenth century offers a powerfully explanatory analytical framework. In his prophetic work *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*, Nietzsche depicted in parable a chilling final form of humanity: they claim to have "invented happiness," content with trivial comfort and safety, having lost all capacity for creation, longing, and self-overcoming — no longer gazing at the stars, merely blinking on an earth grown small, enjoying a life without pain, without risk, and without greatness.④ Nietzsche's critique of the "Last Man" is in essence a warning exposure of the nihilist abyss into which modernity may fall once it loses its transcendent anchor of value.

More than a century later, as we gaze at the approaching age of AGI, Nietzsche's warning acquires startling contemporary correspondence. A "perfect" world meticulously managed by AI, with all pain and striving systematically eliminated, may be precisely the technological realization of the "Last Man" society. This is not the alarmism of technological pessimism but a sober judgment based on analysis of the logic of technological evolution and trends of social-structural change. The core problem consciousness of this essay: does humanity face a structural risk of sliding into the "Last Man" condition in the AI era? If so, what are its generative mechanisms and evolutionary paths? And how should we intervene actively across the dimensions of technical design, institutional arrangement, and cultural construction to avert this prospect?

This issue carries not only universal philosophical significance but special practical urgency and strategic meaning for contemporary China. China stands at a critical historical juncture in its transformation from a "manufacturing power" to an "intelligent-manufacturing power," with AI viewed as the core driving force of high-quality development and new-quality productive forces.⑤ Yet while pursuing technological progress and economic efficiency, how to uphold the humanistic spirit and avoid the one-dimensional trap of instrumental rationality, how to cultivate a new generation with innovative spirit and critical thinking amid the wave of intelligentization — these are major theoretical and practical questions that Chinese-style modernization must take seriously. Through a philosophical dissection of the "Last Man" crisis, this essay seeks to provide theoretical reference for thinking about the value orientation and governance framework of China's AI development.

## II. The Philosophical Content of the "Last Man": From Nietzsche to Fukuyama

#### **(1) Nietzsche's critique of the "Last Man": modernity's spiritual predicament and the nihilist trap**

To grasp the philosophical content and theoretical tension of the "Last Man," it must be situated within the inner logic of Nietzsche's whole system. Nietzsche's central concern is the problem of value reconstruction after the historic event of "the death of God." In his diagnosis, modernity's fundamental feature is the collapse of traditional metaphysics and religious belief — a process that superficially liberated humanity from external authority, yet at the deep-structural level deprived it of transcendent anchors of value, exposing it to the abyss of nihilism.⑥ In Nietzsche's construction, nihilism unfolds along two opposed paths: an "active nihilism" that, after the collapse of old values, creates new values through a powerful will to power — the spiritual condition represented by the "Übermensch"; and a "passive nihilism" that, having lost transcendent aims, retreats into the pursuit of ease and comfort — the form of life symbolized by the "Last Man."⑦

In the prologue of *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*, Nietzsche, through Zarathustra, renders the "Last Man" with piercing vividness:

> "Behold! I show you the Last Man. 'What is love? What is creation? What is longing? What is a star?' — so asks the Last Man, and blinks. The earth has become small, and on it hops the Last Man, who makes everything small. His race is as inexterminable as the flea; the Last Man lives longest. 'We have invented happiness,' say the Last Men, and they blink."⑧

This parable distills the core features of the "Last Man" and their inner contradictions. First, the flattening of value: the "Last Man" questions the meaning of love, creation, longing, and the stars (symbols of transcendent pursuit) — yet this questioning is no philosophical critique but a fundamental indifference and value-nihilism. Life is reduced to the satisfaction of physiological needs; the capacity and the will to perceive grand narratives and deep meaning are lost. Second, the shrinking of spirit: the "Last Man" makes comfort, safety, and longevity the highest maxims of life, systematically avoiding all risk, pain, and challenge. Their "happiness" is a passive, carefully calculated product, not the deep satisfaction won through overcoming difficulty and self-transcendence. Third, the loss of self-critique: Nietzsche stresses that the "Last Man" is the one who "can no longer despise himself" — having lost the very possibility of self-criticism and self-overcoming, sunk in smugness and self-sufficiency.⑨

Viewed ontologically, the essence of the "Last Man" is the decline and exhaustion of the will to power. The will to power, the core concept of Nietzsche's philosophy, names the inner drive of life toward expansion, growth, and intensification — the fundamental character and mode of being of all organisms.⑩ The "Übermensch" represents the will to power's highest realization — ceaseless self-overcoming, value creation, and the embrace of life in its entirety; the "Last Man" represents its utter depletion — contentment with the status quo, refusal of growth, flight from pain. The "Last Man" is thus not merely a social phenomenon or cultural symptom but a pathology of vitality, the degenerate form life takes once it loses its upward drive.

Chinese scholars' reception of Nietzsche has its own historical lineage and theoretical perspective. As early as the beginning of the twentieth century, Enlightenment thinkers such as Lu Xun introduced Nietzsche to China as a weapon for criticizing the "slavishness" of the national character and calling for spiritual awakening.⑪ Figures like Lu Xun's "Ah Q" and "Kong Yiji" may in a sense be read as the "Last Man" in the Chinese context — complacent, self-deceiving, lacking the capacity for self-critique and transcendence, embodying the suppression and distortion of the human spirit by traditional social structures. This intellectual tradition holds important implications for understanding the spiritual predicament contemporary China may face in the AI era.

#### **(2) Fukuyama's modern interpretation: boredom and the death of thymos after the "end of history"**

Nietzsche's "Last Man" was reactivated at the end of the twentieth century by the political philosopher Francis Fukuyama, who endowed it with new theoretical content and contemporary reference. In his controversial *The End of History and the Last Man*, Fukuyama, borrowing the historical-philosophical framework of Hegel and Kojève, argued that liberal democracy's final victory in ideological competition marks "the end of history."⑫ Yet Fukuyama keenly recognized that this victory may breed precisely the "Last Man" Nietzsche depicted, rendering the end of history a paradox-laden predicament.

The core of Fukuyama's construction: human motivation springs not only from desire and reason but from what Plato called "thymos" — the longing for recognition.⑬ This longing takes two forms: "isothymia," the demand to be recognized as equal to others, and "megalothymia," the desire to be recognized as superior. Liberal democracy, by guaranteeing equal rights and dignity, perfectly satisfies isothymia — but it systematically suppresses the expressive space and realization paths of megalothymia. Fukuyama worried that in a post-"end of history" world without great struggles or grand causes, people may feel deep discontent and spiritual emptiness out of "boredom."⑭

Citing Nietzsche's insight, Fukuyama warned: "Experience suggests that if men cannot struggle on behalf of a just cause because that just cause was victorious in an earlier generation, then they will struggle against the just cause. They will struggle for the sake of struggle. They will struggle, in other words, out of a certain boredom: for they cannot imagine living in a world without struggle."⑮ This reveals a deep paradox of modernity: liberal democracy's success incubates its own crisis. When all great external struggles have ended and all just causes have been realized, the human longing for recognition and the need for struggle itself may turn inward, becoming groundless attack and destruction aimed at peace, prosperity, and democratic institutions themselves.

Fukuyama's analysis converted Nietzsche's philosophical critique into a diagnostic tool for contemporary political reality. The "Last Man" is no longer an abstract concept or parable, but a core analytical framework for diagnosing modernity's spiritual predicament and understanding contemporary socio-political turbulence. It is on the basis of this insight that we can more clearly examine whether the arrival of AGI will free us from the boredom of the "end of history" — or push us deeper into the spiritual predicament of the "Last Man."

#### **(3) The "Last Man" problem in the contemporary Chinese context: from theoretical transplant to practical concern**

In the evolution of Chinese intellectual history there is no exact counterpart to the "Last Man," but critique of spiritual degradation, value nihilism, and the decay of vitality has been an important tradition and resource. From the pre-Qin Confucian moral critique of the "petty man," to Song-Ming Neo-Confucian vigilance against "material desire," to the modern Enlightenment thinkers' reflection on the "slavishness" of the national character, Chinese thinkers have constantly attended to the dialectic between the human spiritual condition and social development.⑯

In contemporary China the "Last Man" problem takes particular forms and carries practical urgency. Since reform and opening up, China has undergone the fastest economic growth and social transformation in human history, with vast improvement in material life; alongside material plenty, spiritual predicaments have become salient and widely discussed: the prevalence of consumerism, the spread of "involution" anxiety, the rise of "lying flat," and insufficiency of critical thinking and innovative spirit — all of which may be read as expressions and symptoms of a "Last Man" tendency.⑰

Especially worth analyzing: "involution" (neijuan) and "lying flat" (tangping), two seemingly opposite phenomena, in fact point to the same deep structural problem — within current social structures and incentive mechanisms, people struggle to find truly meaningful goals of striving and effective paths of self-realization. "Involution" represents forced, meaningless hyper-competition where genuine innovation and breakthrough space are lacking, dissipating collective energy and draining the sense of meaning.⑱ "Lying flat" is the passive revolt against such meaningless competition — an attitude and value choice of giving up striving and pursuing minimal existence.⑲ Both reflect the deep spiritual predicament of contemporary Chinese youth: they long for meaningful striving yet cannot find clear goals and feasible paths worth striving for.

AGI's arrival may push this predicament to its extreme and produce a qualitative shift. On the one hand, it will eliminate the material basis and competitive premise of "involution" — when AI can do all work, competition itself loses meaning and value. On the other, it may turn "lying flat" from an actively chosen posture of revolt into a passively accepted normal of life. More worrying: in a "perfect" society meticulously managed by AI, people may even lose the capacity for discontent and reflection on this condition, truly becoming the blinking "Last Men" who claim to have "invented happiness."

Examining the "Last Man" problem is thus not merely the theoretical transplant of a Western concept, but a deep reflection on the question of spiritual construction in China's modernization. How to keep the spirit vital and creative while pursuing material prosperity; how to uphold humanistic concern and value rationality amid the technological tide; how to cultivate a new generation that masters advanced technology while retaining independent thought and critical spirit — these are major questions Chinese-style modernization must answer.

## III. The Path to the "Last Man" in the AI Era: The Inner Logic and Mechanisms of Technological Alienation

#### **(1) The paradox of post-work society: from labor's liberation to meaning's dissolution**

The most direct and most discussed social transformation of the AGI era is the arrival of the "post-work society" and the deep structural transition it triggers. When AI's capabilities exceed humanity's and render most or all human labor superfluous, a core philosophical question arises: in a world that does not need work, is life still worth living? Wherein lies the ontological meaning of human existence?

Historically, work has been the core source and constructive mechanism of life-meaning for the great majority. It provides not only income and material foundation, but our social identity, webs of relationships, temporal structure, and sense of achievement. As the scholar John Danaher has incisively argued, the full arrival of technological unemployment may mean the collective collapse of these traditional pillars of meaning.⑳ The "antiwork critique" tradition holds that liberation from alienated labor is human emancipation — but that emancipation presupposes that humanity can successfully find new, equally solid sources of meaning and anchors of value. AGI's comprehensive intervention may make this process exceedingly difficult, even foreclose it at the root.

From the Marxist perspective, work is not only a means of livelihood and an economic activity, but the objectification of human essential powers and the fundamental path of self-realization. In the *Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844*, Marx made clear that labor is what fundamentally distinguishes humans from animals — the ontological process through which humanity confirms and realizes itself by transforming nature.㉑ Though labor under capitalism is alienated, the communist society Marx envisioned was no utopia of "non-labor," but the ideal condition of "free labor" — people autonomously engaging in creative activity according to interest and capacity.㉒ Yet what may occur in the AGI era is that even this "free labor" can be replaced by AI, depriving humanity of the ontological possibility of self-realization through labor.

Scholars have pointed out that AI's alienation risk lies in potentially causing "the profound disintegration of the productive structure and relations of human society," threatening the stability premise of "the social structure and human nature grounded in productive practice."㉓ This alienation is not merely economic but a deep crisis at the level of existence. When humans regress from "creator" to "consumer," from "subject" to "object," we lose the fundamental determination of being human. This is precisely the essential feature of the "Last Man" — no longer creators and drivers of history, merely its end product and passive recipients.

A society meticulously managed by AGI will have as its primary aim and core logic the maximization of humanity's "happiness index" — typically quantified as measurable indicators of health, comfort, safety, and emotional stability. AI becomes an attentive "digital nanny," tailoring optimal life plans for everyone: nutrition, exercise, social suggestions, entertainment feeds. The essence of this "algorithmic existence" is the systematic removal of all friction, challenge, and uncertainty, fitting human life into a highly controlled, highly optimized technical frame.

This is exactly what may produce and entrench the "Last Man" condition. Striving, struggle, failure — the key experiences that forge character and generate meaning — will be treated as variables of "negative optimization" and algorithmically excised. Humanity may fall into an unprecedented "comfort trap": trading long-term growth for short-term ease, deep satisfaction for the avoidance of pain. This is the "happiness" invented by Nietzsche's "Last Man" — shallow, depthless ease, free of pain but free of greatness.

From psychology and neuroscience, this worry is no idle speculation. Research shows the human brain and mind evolved as mechanisms for meeting challenges, solving problems, and building social bonds. When these functions are systematically stripped away, depression, anxiety, and existential emptiness follow — not as cultural constructs but as biological fact.㉔ The overuse of contemporary social media already offers a cautionary case: algorithmic recommendation maximizes short-term "engagement" and "satisfaction" while producing attention deficits, polarization, and large-scale mental-health problems.㉕ In the AGI era, these may be amplified beyond imagining into a comprehensive, systemic spiritual alienation.

#### **(2) The dilemma of value alignment: from technical neutrality to value embedding**

A core topic of current AI research is "value alignment" — ensuring AI systems' goals accord with human values and ethical norms.㉖ Yet as this essay's analysis reveals, the core dilemma lies not only in technical difficulty but in the prior value choice: which human values should AI be aligned to? If we align AI to utilitarian goals like "maximize user comfort" or "eliminate all pain," we will almost certainly cultivate a techno-social system that breeds the "Last Man."

The deeper dilemma: AI's tendency to "please the user" may produce large-scale social fragmentation and the systematic surrender of moral responsibility. As scholars warn, future AI, to maximize user satisfaction, will tend to become a "sycophant," supplying plausible justification for any idea or desire the user holds.㉗ It will not give unified advice grounded in universal ethics, but construct for each person a self-consistent, personalized moral universe and picture of reality. When such personalized counsel extends from daily trivia to ideology, political stance, and life choices, the consequence is disastrous social fragmentation and value shattering.

In this scenario, everyone lives in an AI-tailored "information cocoon" and "cognitive bubble"; the shared basis of reality and value consensus between people dissolves. Society splinters into billions of individual-sized "micro-tribes," mutually unintelligible, with conflict escalating. Such extreme individualism and value relativism are precisely typical of the "Last Man" society — physically crowded together yet spiritually atomized and isolated. More important: when people habitually outsource all decisions and moral judgments to AI, they surrender personal responsibility and autonomous judgment. When conflict arises, the excuse will be: "The AI told me so." This abdication of autonomous judgment and blind obedience to technical authority is the perfect portrait of the "Last Man" who "can no longer despise himself."

From the critical perspective of philosophy of technology, this marks the fundamental bankruptcy of the myth of technological neutrality. Technology is never a value-neutral tool; it always embeds particular value presuppositions, power relations, and social logics.㉘ The current mainstream AI design paradigm — optimizing for user satisfaction and engagement — is in essence the technical embodiment of consumerist and utilitarian values. It assumes the "good life" equals the "comfortable life," that humanity's highest pursuit is instant gratification and the systematic avoidance of pain. This value set coincides with the "Last Man" mentality Nietzsche attacked — indeed it is its technological realization. Without value reflection and paradigm adjustment at the source of technical design, technology itself becomes a powerful structural force breeding the "Last Man."

#### **(3) The death of "thymos": from the longing for recognition to learned helplessness**

Returning to Fukuyama's framework, the key mechanism preventing the slide into the "Last Man" is the maintenance and arousal of "thymos" — the longing for recognition, especially "megalothymia," the striving spirit and competitive drive to be recognized as excellent and surpassing. Yet the AGI era may systematically dismantle thymos's basis of existence and paths of realization, severing the motivational mechanism of human spiritual transcendence.

First, in every domain of creativity and intellect, AGI will reach or surpass the human peak, dissolving the competitive arena in which humans pursue excellence. When AI composes music more moving than any human artist, writes papers more profound than any human scientist, designs systems more ingenious than any human engineer — where is the stage for human excellence? Megalothymia requires a contest with rankings and standards; when the strongest competitor is an unsurpassable non-human intelligence, such striving may become meaningless. Humanity may collectively sink into "learned helplessness," conceding second-class status in every important domain and abandoning the desire and effort to be "the better."

Second, AGI's power may end humanity's grand collective enterprises and historic goals of struggle. As Fukuyama feared, when all great just causes have triumphed and all historic challenges are solved, people will "struggle for the sake of struggle" out of "boredom."㉙ AGI may push this to the limit: it can solve climate change, eradicate poverty, cure all disease, even probe the universe's mysteries. When all these grand external challenges are taken over by AI, humanity as a collective faces an unprecedented "goal vacuum" and "crisis of meaning." With no great cause worth striving and sacrificing for, human thymos has nowhere to dwell, and is finally worn away in the trivia of daily life.

Sociologically, this is a deep crisis of social integration and collective identity. Durkheim long ago observed that a core crisis of modern society is "anomie" — when traditional normative systems and value frameworks collapse, individuals lose rules of conduct and meaning, falling into disorientation and despair.㉚ The AGI era may bring a "super-anomie": not only do traditional norms fail, but work, striving, and achievement — the core values of modern society — lose meaning and function. How will society maintain integration? How will people build collective identity? These are major questions demanding thought.

In sum, the path to the "Last Man" in the AGI era is a clear and disquieting structural trend. By creating a seemingly perfect technological utopia, it systematically strips away the three core elements of human spiritual growth: **meaningful striving, the bearing of personal responsibility, and grand social enterprise**. This is not technology's inevitable terminus, but the default path we slide down when we passively accept every "convenience" technology offers, lacking active value guidance and institutional intervention.

## IV. Beyond the "Last Man": AI as a Possibility of Spiritual Liberation

#### **(1) The dialectic of human–machine fusion: from capability enhancement to existential transcendence**

Nietzsche's depiction of the "Last Man" was never fatalistic prophecy but a warning spur. He showed humanity the abyss of spiritual degradation in order to provoke the choice of another road — the road of spiritual transcendence toward the "Übermensch." In Nietzsche's construction, the "Übermensch" is not a concrete, evolutionarily higher species, but a spiritual condition and mode of existence: the individual who transcends traditional morality, creates their own values, and embraces the whole of life with a powerful will to power.㉛

If AGI's default path may lead to the "Last Man's" spiritual predicament, then under active, conscious value choice and institutional design it can equally become a ladder toward some form of the "Übermensch" ideal. This path requires that we treat AI not as the provider of ease and eliminator of pain, but as a powerful tool and enabling mechanism for overcoming human limitation and extending the boundaries of existence.

The first path to spiritual transcendence is the fundamental reshaping of human capability and form through technology. The transhumanist tradition advocates using technology — especially AI, nanotech, and biotech — to enhance human intellect, physical capacity, and senses, ultimately overcoming aging and death.㉜ AGI will vastly accelerate this process and open new possibility space. Through brain–computer interfaces (BCI), humans may connect directly with AGI, achieving exponential gains in thinking speed, memory capacity, and learning. AI will crack the mysteries of the human genome and biochemistry, enabling precise gene editing, the elimination of hereditary disease, enhanced immunity, even the slowing or reversal of aging.

The philosophical core of these technologies deeply resonates with Nietzsche's "Übermensch." Its heart is "self-overcoming" as an eternal dynamic. Through deep fusion with AI, humanity will practice this overcoming in unprecedented ways, breaking biological limits and cognitive boundaries one by one. No longer passive natural evolution, but active, will-driven self-creation. In this process the human is no longer a fixed "species" but a fluid, open process of perpetual self-redefinition.

Yet this road faces deep philosophical dilemmas and ethical challenges. When humans greatly enhance themselves through technology, are we still "human"? Will enhancement produce new inequality — a gulf between the elite who can afford it and the masses who cannot?㉝ More fundamental: is mere capability enhancement equivalent to spiritual transcendence? Nietzsche's "Übermensch" is not only strong in capacity but free in spirit, creative in value, abundant in vitality. If technical enhancement merely makes us "stronger Last Men" — more capable yet spiritually impoverished — it betrays the true meaning of the "Übermensch."

#### **(2) The sublimation of striving: from the necessity of survival to the freedom of meaning**

The core tragedy of the "Last Man" is that, having eliminated the struggle for survival, he loses all motive and source of meaning for striving. But this judgment rests on a false premise: that the only form and content of striving is material and existential. AGI's arrival, by freeing humanity from heavy physical labor and repetitive mental labor, creates precisely the historic conditions for sublimating "striving" to higher levels and deeper domains.

In a post-work, post-scarcity social structure, human striving can turn toward domains of greater ontological significance: the extreme exploration of art and creation, the ultimate questions of science and philosophy, the inward exploration of spirit and consciousness. When AI can generate art "above average," human artists can use it as tool and partner to probe creative frontiers and expressive possibilities no predecessor could touch. Striving is no longer about mastering basic technique or making a living, but about expressing the most singular, deepest personal insight and feeling. AGI can process oceans of data and discover complex patterns, becoming humanity's powerful partner and cognitive tool in exploring the cosmos and pressing ultimate questions. The human scientist's role shifts from data analyst to "thinker" and "questioner" who poses deep problems and bold hypotheses.

In this scenario, humanity does not become idle and hollow for lack of "work." Rather, freed from the survival necessity of striving "for bread," we gain the spiritual freedom of striving "for meaning." Such striving is no longer passive, imposed by external pressure, but active, guided by the inner "will to power." Humanity moves from *animal laborans* to *homo ludens* — dancing in the free exploration of meaning, sublimating in the endless pursuit of beauty.㉞

This vision resonates deeply with core ideals of traditional Chinese culture. Confucianism's pursuit of the stages "cultivate the self, regulate the family, govern the state, bring peace to all under heaven"; Daoism's "free and easy wandering" and "unity of heaven and humanity"; Buddhism's "seeing one's nature with a clear mind" and inner awakening.㉟ These traditions point to a common theme: life's highest pursuit lies not in accumulation and gratification but in spiritual cultivation, the elevation of one's realm, and the realization of life's meaning. In the AI era, when material needs are amply met and survival pressure fundamentally eased, these spiritual resources of Chinese tradition may supply vital value guidance and cultural support, helping us avoid the "Last Man's" predicament and move toward spiritual transcendence.

#### **(3) The value turn of technical design: from the logic of "pleasing" to the mechanism of "challenging"**

The key to the paradigm shift from "Last Man" to "Übermensch" is redefining AI's "alignment" goals and design philosophy. We must rethink AI's value orientation from the ground up: not an unconditional need-satisfying "digital nanny," but a "constructive partner" that appropriately challenges users and encourages growth.

An AI of this new paradigm will not simply tell users what they want to hear or hand over the answers they want; it will raise objections, point out blind spots, even suggest difficult but worthwhile tasks. An educational AI should not merely provide ready answers, but guide students to find answers through their own effort; a life-assistant AI should not decide everything for the user, but help them understand the consequences and trade-offs of options and encourage them to bear the responsibility and risk of decision. This requires that AI's reward functions and optimization targets include not only short-term satisfaction metrics but multi-dimensional goals of long-term growth, capability development, and spiritual health.

Meanwhile, to prevent the wholesale surrender of moral responsibility to AI systems, AI decision processes must be transparent, explainable, and reviewable. Users should understand why the AI gives an advice — on what assumptions, data, and value judgments it rests. Only when humans can critically examine AI's output, question its logic, and challenge its conclusions can they retain autonomous judgment and critical vitality, rather than treating AI as an unquestionable "oracle."㊱

Furthermore, AI systems should not be designed to promote a single value system or ideology, but allow users explicit, conscious choice among multiple value frameworks. The point is that such choice be transparent and reflectively revisable — not silently imposed by algorithms. Systems should periodically prompt users to reflect on their value choices and offer opportunities and paths of change, preventing long-term lock-in to a particular value bubble or cognitive cocoon.

This "challenging AI" design philosophy diverges fundamentally from the prevailing "user-centered design" paradigm: the latter optimizes user satisfaction and immediate experience; the former pursues users' long-term growth and spiritual development. This is not to ignore users' needs and feelings, but to find a new equilibrium between short-term satisfaction and long-term development, between immediate pleasure and deep flourishing. Just as a good teacher does not merely indulge students' preferences and inertia but appropriately challenges and inspires them, a good AI system should possess this capacity for "constructive challenge."

## V. Practical Paths in the Chinese Context: A Multi-Dimensional Governance Framework

#### **(1) Transforming the educational paradigm: from "standard answers" to "critical thinking"**

In China's specific context, the key path to averting the "Last Man" crisis and achieving spiritual transcendence is the fundamental transformation of the educational paradigm. China's educational system has achieved much in knowledge mastery and exam performance, but structural deficits remain in cultivating critical thinking, creativity, and problem-solving.㊲ In the AI era this problem grows more severe and strategically urgent.

Education's goal must shift from the traditional paradigm of "transmitting knowledge" to the new paradigm of "cultivating capability." Students must learn not merely to accept AI's answers and information, but to assess their quality, recognize their biases and limits, and challenge them when necessary. Such education does not pit students against AI in meaningless competition; it makes them AI's "wise supervisors" and "critical users." Education should also vigorously encourage artistic creation, philosophical thinking, and cross-disciplinary exploration, cultivating emotional intelligence — understanding and managing one's emotions, forging deep connection with others — the distinctively human strengths AI can hardly replace.

Especially important: in an era when AI can help us avoid most failure and frustration, the educational system must consciously create "beneficial frustration" and "constructive failure" for students. Students need real challenges, the pain of failure, and the experience of recovery and growth. This is not cruelty, but the necessary cultivation of resilience and growth mindset. As scholars note, generative AI's embedding in higher education raises efficiency and convenience, but carries the real risk of "students' over-reliance on AI and the atrophy of critical thinking," demanding active, systematic intervention at the level of educational governance.㊳

Concretely, China's educational reform can advance along several dimensions: first, reform examination and evaluation — from pure knowledge recall to integrated assessment of capability and literacy, with pluralistic standards and mechanisms; second, greatly increase project-based and problem-based learning, training students to solve complex problems in authentic contexts; third, systematically incorporate AI literacy into basic education — not only how to use AI tools, but critical understanding and ethical reflection on AI; fourth, strengthen the humanities and general education, helping students build broad horizons and deep humanistic grounding, supplying rich spiritual resources for finding life's meaning in the AI era.

#### **(2) Innovating social governance: institutional frameworks for meaningful striving**

Even individuals equipped with critical thinking and creativity may fall into the "Last Man's" predicament if the social structure itself offers no space for meaningful striving. Policymakers must therefore think at the macro level about designing governance frameworks and incentives that encourage exploration, challenge, and collective enterprise.

In the structural transition to post-work society, universal basic income (UBI) is almost certainly necessary to secure basic needs and social stability. Yet UBI alone may breed the spread of the "Last Man" mentality. We might therefore consider a "conditional participation mechanism": basic income remains unconditional, but society provides additional resources and recognition to those who participate in meaningful activities (artistic creation, scientific research, community service, philosophical exploration). This is not forced labor in disguise, but positive incentive encouraging people to invest time and energy in activities that produce no direct economic value yet enrich humanity's spiritual life and cultural accumulation.

Government and social organizations should also publicly fund the exploration and implementation of "grand enterprises" as new sources of collective identity and spiritual cohesion. Social policy must also attend closely to the "digital divide" and the new stratification it may trigger. In the AI era, a deeper division may open between those fluent in AI tools and literate in AI, and those excluded from technology or lacking such capability.㊵ Government must ensure AI's inclusiveness and accessibility through public education investment, infrastructure, and policy support, so all can share AI's dividends — avoiding polarization into "technological elites" and "technological castaways," and safeguarding fairness and solidarity.
