### Preface

- **Political polarization** has already become a major distinguishing feature of contemporary American society. Democrats and Republicans are not only at odds over political stances but also increasingly display significant differences in every aspect of life, from consumption preferences to value systems. As partisan identity becomes an ever more important dimension of social identity, people's negative sentiments toward the political "other side" are also continuously heating up. Against this backdrop, the concept of "**affective polarization**" has emerged, referring to people's strong dislike and hostility toward political out-groups.
- Is the influence of affective polarization confined to the level of attitudes, or does it also affect people's actual behaviors and decisions? This is an important question urgently in need of empirical research. Although existing literature has found that affective polarization affects people's choices in areas such as marriage and employment, systematic examination of its role in other socioeconomic domains is still lacking.
- The **housing market** offers an ideal research setting in this regard. First, housing is the most important **asset** for most households and is closely linked to their well-being. Second, housing choices reflect not only people's economic considerations but also their preferences for factors such as the **community environment** and **neighborly interaction**. Furthermore, **moving** is a high-cost decision, and when making it, people inevitably have to weigh various influencing factors. Therefore, the housing market provides an excellent litmus test for examining the effect of affective polarization on real-world decisions.
- More importantly, studying the role of affective polarization in the housing market helps us understand the current state and consequences of political division in American society. If, due to partisan opposition, people tend to retreat into an **echo chamber** when choosing where to live, then the degree of homogenization within communities will inevitably rise, while the divide between communities will deepen. In the long run, this could weaken cross-community cohesion, hinder social mobility, and thereby sow hidden risks for social division. Therefore, clarifying the effect of affective polarization in the key domain of the housing market has important guiding significance for both understanding the **social cost** of political polarization and for the public policies that address it.
- This paper, based on real estate transaction and voter registration data from the U.S. state of North Carolina, uses empirical analysis to explore the mechanism and consequences of affective polarization in the housing market. This paper examines: when a new neighbor who supports a different party moves in next door, are existing residents more inclined to move away? To what extent does such moving behavior stem from pure **partisan preference**, rather than from reasonable considerations of factors such as local public goods provision and community governance? Are there significant differences in how different social groups react to partisan heterogeneity? Answering these questions not only helps reveal the role of affective polarization in important economic decisions but also helps us understand, from a micro-foundational perspective, the increasingly severe political division in American society and its deep-seated impacts.
- **Empirical challenges**:
  - The political segregation observed in cross-sectional data does not necessarily mean that households prefer to have co-partisans as neighbors. Households holding similar political stances may choose to live together because they prefer similar **neighborhood amenities**, rather than out of a preference for one another. Therefore, if residents are observed moving away from neighbors of a different party, it may not be because they dislike those neighbors themselves, but because neighbors of different political stances differ in their preferences for neighborhood amenities and public goods (i.e., the **Tiebout mechanism**).
  - **Immediate neighbors** may not be randomly distributed: if households have already moved away from neighbors they dislike, then the current distribution of neighbors is endogenous and cannot be used to identify the causal effect of political polarization.

### Research Design and Identification Strategy

#### **Strategy One: Constrain the Research Sample and Include Multidimensional Fixed Effects (DID)**

**Background**: Ideally, we would want to randomly assign some residents a new neighbor of a different party and then compare their moving behavior. In reality, however, a new neighbor's party affiliation may be correlated with other characteristics of the community, and these characteristics may themselves influence residents' moving decisions. Therefore, simply comparing residents who do versus do not get a new neighbor of a different party may confound the influence of other factors.

**Approach**: To approximate a randomized experiment as closely as possible, the authors take two key steps:

- Constrain the research sample to existing residents who have just had a new neighbor move in (regardless of party). This ensures that both the treatment group (new neighbor of the opposite party) and the control group (new neighbor of the same party or no party affiliation) face the shock of a new neighbor, thereby controlling for sample comparability to a large extent.

- Include multidimensional fixed effects in the regression model, including community, time, the existing resident's party, and demographic characteristics. This amounts to comparing only residents who are identical along these dimensions, further ensuring the comparability of the treatment and control groups.

**Model Specification**:

- Dependent variable: whether the existing resident moves after the new neighbor moves in
- Core explanatory variable: whether the new neighbor is of the opposite party from the existing resident
- Fixed effects: interaction terms of community, time (e.g., year), the existing resident's party, and demographic characteristics (such as race, age, income, etc.)
- Test: whether the coefficient on the core explanatory variable is significantly positive. If it is positive, it indicates that the arrival of an opposite-party new neighbor does increase the existing resident's probability of moving, supporting the effect of political polarization.

**Interpretation**: By constraining the sample and incorporating multidimensional fixed effects, this strategy largely simulates a randomized experiment:

- Constraining the sample ensures that both the treatment and control groups have experienced the shock of a new neighbor, thereby ruling out the influence of the new neighbor itself (apart from political stance)
- The multidimensional fixed effects control for other factors that may be correlated with the new neighbor's party and that affect moving behavior, such as community characteristics, time trends, and resident backgrounds
- On this basis, the coefficient on the core explanatory variable reflects the additional effect of partisan difference itself on moving behavior

#### **Strategy Two: Within-Street Analysis**

Building on Strategy One, this strategy further constrains the scope of the treatment and control groups in order to better control for the influence of the **micro-community environment**.

**Background**: Although Strategy One, by constraining the sample and incorporating fixed effects, controls for the comparability of the treatment and control groups to a large extent, it still has a potential limitation: the micro-community environment in which the treatment group (opposite-party new neighbor) and the control group (same-party or no-party new neighbor) reside may differ, and this difference may happen to be correlated with the new neighbor's party, thereby affecting residents' moving behavior.

**Approach**: To further mitigate this problem, Strategy Two constrains the scope of the treatment and control groups to within the **same street**:

- Treatment group: existing residents living relatively close to the new neighbor (the two houses in front and behind, and the four houses across the street)
- Control group: existing residents living relatively far from the new neighbor, but still within the same street
  By comparing the moving behavior of residents with neighbors of different parties within the same street, this strategy controls to a large extent for other influencing factors at the street level, such as new amenities and changes in public goods, thereby more precisely identifying the influence of partisan difference itself.

**Model Specification**:

- Dependent variable: whether the existing resident moves after the new neighbor moves in
- Core explanatory variable: whether the new neighbor is of the opposite party from the existing resident, and the existing resident is an immediate neighbor of the new neighbor (the two houses in front and behind, and the four houses across the street)
- Fixed effects: interaction terms of street, time (e.g., year), the existing resident's party, and demographic characteristics (such as race, age, income, etc.)
- Test: restricting the sample to cases where the new neighbor is of the opposite party from the existing resident, test whether the coefficient on the core explanatory variable is significantly positive. If it is positive, it indicates that when facing an opposite-party new neighbor, immediate-neighbor residents are more inclined to move than far-neighbor residents, further supporting the effect of political polarization.

**Interpretation**: By comparing immediate-neighbor and far-neighbor residents within the same street, this strategy better controls for the influence of the micro-community environment:

- Residents on the same street face similar community characteristics and trends of change, so the street fixed effect can control these factors well
- On this basis, the main difference between immediate-neighbor and far-neighbor residents is whether they directly come into contact with the new neighbor. Therefore, the difference in their moving behavior more directly reflects the influence of partisan difference itself
- Furthermore, by restricting the sample to cases where the new neighbor is of the opposite party, this strategy focuses on the core scenario of political polarization, providing more targeted evidence

#### **Strategy Three: Event Study Method**

**Background**: The first two strategies focus on individual residents, comparing differences in existing residents' reactions when neighbors of different parties move in. But these strategies still face a potential problem: although the new neighbor's party may be random, the party distribution of existing residents may be correlated with other characteristics of the community, and these characteristics may themselves influence moving behavior.

**Approach**: To address this problem, Strategy Three starts directly from the event of a new neighbor moving in. Specifically, the authors treat each instance of a new neighbor moving in as a quasi-experiment, with its eight nearest neighboring households (two in front and behind each, and four across the street) constituting a small sample. If political polarization causes people to be unwilling to have neighbors of a different party, then among these eight neighboring households, the proportion of residents whose party differs from the new neighbor's should be systematically higher than 50% (i.e., the level of random allocation).

**Model Specification**:

- Include fixed effects for the new-neighbor move-in event. This amounts to comparing differences in the reactions of neighbors of different parties within the same event, rather than differences across different events, thereby controlling for other factors at the event level that may influence moving behavior.
- Define the dependent variable as the **proportion of the eight neighboring households whose party differs from the new neighbor's**, rather than whether a specific neighboring household moves. This specification avoids the individual heterogeneity of particular neighbors and instead focuses on the overall distribution.
- Test whether this proportion is significantly higher than 50%. If it is significantly positive, it indicates that neighbors of a different party are indeed more inclined to move away, supporting the effect of political polarization.

**Advantages**: This strategy focuses identification on the micro-community near the new neighbor and incorporates event fixed effects. Compared with the first two strategies, it better controls for other influencing factors at the community level, thereby more precisely identifying the influence of partisan difference itself. In addition, it examines the overall heterogeneity of neighbors rather than individual behavior, which further mitigates the endogeneity problem of individual choice.

### Data

- CoreLogic Solutions real estate data. This data contains information on real estate transactions and property characteristics. The researchers designed an algorithm that uses full addresses and precise geographic locations to identify adjacent properties.

- North Carolina voter registration data. Using homeowners' names and addresses, the researchers merged in individual-level party affiliation, voting history, and demographic characteristics from the voter registration data.

- The final dataset is a quarterly panel dataset for 2005–2019, containing information on single-family homes, with detailed records of the home-sale decisions and party affiliations of existing residents and their immediate neighbors.

### Results

- Strategy One: After controlling for a series of homeowner characteristics (party, race, age, state of birth, and length of residence), property characteristics (size and year built), and street-quarter-party-race fixed effects, if a new neighbor of a different party moves in, the probability that the existing homeowner sells their home within two years is 0.31 percentage points higher than when a new neighbor of the same party or no party moves in, equivalent to a 4% increase. This indicates that homeowners are more inclined to move away from neighbors of a different party.
- Strategy Two: By comparing homeowners within the same street who do versus do not have a new neighbor move in, it is found that homeowners with a new neighbor adjacent or next door are more likely to move than homeowners slightly farther away on the same street. This further confirms the previous result.
- Strategy Three: Treating the arrival of a new neighbor as an event, the study examines the 8 existing residents nearest the new neighbor (two on each side, four across the street). The results show that among these 8 nearest neighbors, the moving rate of existing residents belonging to the opposing party of the new neighbor is significantly higher than that of existing residents with no party affiliation or of the same party as the new neighbor.
- Analysis of how the effect changes over time shows that the move-out rates of the treatment and control groups do not begin to diverge significantly until one year after the new neighbor moves in. This lagged effect indicates that the move-out behavior is indeed caused by the new neighbor.
- During periods and in streets where the housing market is active, the estimated effect is much larger. This indicates that although homeowners are unwilling to have a neighbor of a different party, the cost they are willing to bear to realize this preference is limited.
- Further research finds that when the new neighbor of a different party who moves in has very high political participation (measured by electoral participation rate), the homeowner is more likely to move. This is consistent with the affective polarization mechanism, in which the homeowner dislikes people of the opposing party and is unwilling to have them as neighbors. This finding cannot be fully explained by other factors such as differences in preferences for community public goods; after controlling for the relevant variables, the effect remains significant.
