### Preface

- **Political polarization** has become a defining feature of contemporary American society. Democrats and Republicans are not only at odds over political stances but also display increasingly significant differences in every aspect of life, from consumption preferences to value systems. As partisan identity becomes an ever more important dimension of social identity, people's negative sentiments toward political "others" are steadily heating up. Against this backdrop, the concept of "**affective polarization**"—meaning people's strong dislike and hostility toward political out-groups—has emerged as an urgent topic for empirical research.
- Does the influence of affective polarization remain at the level of attitudes, or does it also affect people's actual behaviors and decisions? This question urgently needs empirical investigation. Although existing literature has found that affective polarization affects choices regarding marriage and employment, systematic examination of its role in other socioeconomic domains remains scarce.
- The **housing market** offers an ideal research setting in this regard. First, housing is the most important **asset** for most households and is closely linked to their well-being. Second, residential choices reflect not only households’ economic considerations but also their preferences for **community environment**, **neighborhood interactions**, and similar factors. Third, **moving** is a high-cost decision—when making it, people inevitably must weigh various influencing factors. Thus, the housing market offers an excellent litmus test for examining the effect of affective polarization on real-world decisions.
- More importantly, studying the role of affective polarization in the housing market helps us understand the current state and consequences of political sorting in American society. If partisan opposition causes people to gravitate toward an **echo chamber** in choosing where to live, the degree of within-community homogeneity will inevitably rise, while the gulf between communities deepens. Over the long run, this could weaken cross-community cohesion, hinder social mobility, and further sow the seeds of social fragmentation. Therefore, clarifying the effect of affective polarization on the housing market—a key domain of daily life—has profound implications for understanding the **social cost** of political polarization and for designing public policies to address it.
- This paper draws on home transaction data and voter registration records from North Carolina and uses empirical analysis to explore the mechanism and consequences of affective polarization in the housing market. It examines: When a new neighbor supporting a different party moves in, do existing residents become more inclined to move away? To what extent does such moving behavior stem from pure **partisan preference**, as opposed to reasoned considerations of local public goods provision and community governance? Are there significant differences in how different social groups react to partisan heterogeneity? Answers to these questions will not only help reveal the role of affective polarization in important economic decisions, but also contribute to understanding—from a micro-foundational perspective—the deepening political division in American society and its far-reaching impact.
- **Empirical Challenges**:
  - The political segregation observed in cross-sectional data does not necessarily mean that households prefer to live with co-partisans. Households holding similar political stances may end up living together because they prefer similar **neighborhood amenities** rather than out of explicit preference for each other. Therefore, if residents are observed moving away from opposite-party neighbors, it may not be because they dislike those neighbors per se but because neighbors of different political views differ in their preferences for neighborhood amenities and public goods (the **Tiebout mechanism**).
  - One's **immediate** neighbors may not be randomly allocated: If households have already moved away from neighbors they dislike, the current distribution of neighbors becomes endogenous and cannot be used to identify the causal effect of political polarization.

### Research Design and Identification Strategy

#### **Strategy One: Constrain the Sample and Include Multidimensional Fixed Effects (DID)**

**Background**: Ideally, we would randomly assign some residents a new neighbor of a different party and then compare their moving behavior. In reality, however, a new neighbor’s party affiliation is very likely correlated with other community characteristics that may independently influence residents’ moving decisions. Consequently, simply comparing residents who did versus did not get an opposite-party new neighbor risks confounding the influence of those other factors.

**Approach**: To approximate a randomized experiment as closely as possible, the authors take two critical steps:

- Constrain the sample to existing residents who just had *any* new neighbor (irrespective of the neighbor’s party). This ensures that both the treatment group (opposite-party new neighbor) and the control group (same-party or unaffiliated new neighbor) have faced the shock of a new neighbor, thus substantially controlling for baseline comparability.

- Include multidimensional fixed effects—such as census tract, time, the existing resident’s party registration, and demographics—directly in the regression. This effectively restricts comparisons to residents identical along those dimensions, further ensuring comparability between the treatment and control groups.

**Model Specification**:

- Dependent variable: Whether the existing resident moves out after the new neighbor moved in
- Key independent variable: Whether the new neighbor belongs to the opposite party
- Fixed effects: Census tract, time (e.g., year), the existing resident’s party registration, interactions of demographic characteristics (race, age, income, etc.)
- Hypothesis test: Whether the coefficient on the key independent variable is significantly positive. A positive value indicates that the arrival of an opposite-party neighbor indeed increases the exiting resident’s probability of moving, supporting the effect of political polarization.

**Interpretation**: By constraining the sample and incorporating multidimensional fixed effects, this strategy simulates a randomized experiment to a considerable extent:

- Restricting the sample to residents who have all experienced the new-neighbor shock eliminates the influence of a new neighbor *per se* (non-political aspects of the new arrival).
- Multidimensional fixed effects control for other factors—such as community characteristics, time trends, and resident demographics—that could be correlated with the neighbor’s party and simultaneously affect mobility behavior.
- Under these design elements, the coefficient on the key variable captures the additional effect of ideological difference on moving behavior.

#### **Strategy Two: Within-Street Analysis**

Building upon Strategy One, this strategy further narrows down the scope of the treatment and control groups to better control for influences from the **micro-neighborhood environment**.

**Background**: Though Strategy One significantly improves comparability between treatment and control groups via sample restrictions and fixed effects, it still carries a potential limitation: the micro-neighborhood environment around residents with an opposite-party new neighbor may differ from the environment around residents with a co-partisan or unaffiliated new neighbor, and that difference could be correlated with the new neighbor’s partisanship, affecting native residents’ mobility.

**Approach**: To further mitigate this problem, Strategy Two constrains both the treatment and control groups to observing people living within the *same* street:

- Treatment group: Existing residents living physically very close to the newly arrived neighbor (the two houses in front, the two behind, and four across the street).
- Control group: Existing residents living somewhat farther from the new neighnor but still within the same street.
  By comparing the moving behavior of residents closer versus farther from neighbors of opposite-party people on the same street, this strategy hence controls for other street-level influential factors—like changes in amenities or public goods—in this setup, thereby producing a purer identification tied more directly to political differences themselves.
