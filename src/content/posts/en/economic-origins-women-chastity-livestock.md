This paper explores the persistent influence of **pastoralism** in pre-industrial societies on modern gender norms. The anthropological literature notes that in pastoral societies, men’s **prolonged absence for herding** makes it difficult to monitor women’s behaviour, leading to the formation of strict constraints on women’s chastity and sexual behaviour to ensure the purity of patrilineal bloodlines. By analysing extensive cross-cultural data, the study shows that historical dependence on pastoralism is significantly correlated with modern societal restrictions on female sexual freedom, supporting the theory of **cultural persistence** and revealing how subsistence patterns shape and perpetuate gender conceptions.

## Introduction

#### **Information Asymmetry in Reproduction**

Information asymmetry in the human reproductive process leads to “**maternal certainty**”, i.e., a woman knows with certainty after giving birth that the child is hers, whereas a man cannot be completely certain of the biological relationship (paternal uncertainty). Specific reasons include:

- The concealed nature of female ovulation;
- Fertilisation occurring inside the body;
- And the potential for female infidelity.

This uncertainty affects men’s success in transmitting their genes. Evolutionary psychology theories suggest that such **paternity uncertainty** caused men to gradually evolve **jealousy** and a strong **desire for control**, particularly the wish to prevent women’s “**promiscuous**” behaviour, thereby ensuring their own biological relationship with the child (see Trivers, 1972; Symons, 1979; Buss et al., 1992; Buss, 2000; Platek and Shackelford, 2006). Consequently, in environments where women’s behaviour is difficult to **monitor**, men tend to worry more about women’s chastity and fidelity, and are thus more inclined to adopt measures to suppress female sexual freedom.

#### **The Formation of Chastity Norms and Customs**

A large body of anthropological literature points out that human societies possess a range of so-called **chastity norms** and **customs** that control women’s behaviour, aimed at suppressing women’s extramarital affairs. Restricting women’s behaviour is not only common in certain specific societies but manifests in diverse forms. These customs and norms specifically include:

- **Restricting women’s freedom of movement**: For example, many societies impose limits on when and where women may go out, particularly restrictions on going out alone;
- **Social expectations regarding female chastity**: Many societies hold strong expectations of female purity, believing that women should remain chaste before marriage and maintain chastity and restraint afterwards;
- **Extreme forms of customs, such as female circumcision**: Female circumcision practices existing in some societies (especially **infibulation**) are an extreme method of behavioural control, making intercourse extremely painful and difficult, thereby restricting female sexual activity;
- **Restrictions on abortion**: Prohibitions or opposition to abortion are seen in many societies as a way to reduce women’s extramarital affairs, as this practice raises the risk of pregnancy from non-marital sex for women, reducing their motivation for sexual activity.

> **Infibulation**: An extreme form of Female Genital Mutilation (FGM), characterised by the excision of part or all of the external genitalia and the stitching or sealing of the vaginal opening, leaving only a small hole for urination and menstruation. This practice causes pain during intercourse, intending to restrict women’s sexual behaviour.
>
> Infibulation often causes severe effects on women's physical and mental health, including infection, difficulty urinating, menstrual problems, childbirth complications, and psychological trauma. Such practices are carried out in some regions due to cultural or religious factors to “ensure” women’s chastity and "purity," but are widely condemned and illegal in many countries.

⬆️The core aim of these measures is to reduce women’s ability or motivation to seek extramarital affairs. They have numerous direct impacts on women, including on their health, freedom regarding abortion, or ability to participate freely in social activities. This makes understanding the **origins** of these restrictive practices very necessary!

#### **Core Hypothesis: The Link between Pastoralism and Women’s Chastity**

The **core hypothesis** of this paper is: In the pre-industrial era, societies relying primarily on **pastoralism** for subsistence were more inclined than agricultural societies to develop these customs restricting women’s sexual behaviour. The reason is that the pastoral mode of production requires men to frequently **leave home** to herd, remaining unable to **monitor** women’s behaviour for long periods, which increases the need for behavioural control over women during men’s absence.

- **Male absence and the problem of behaviour monitoring**: Men in pastoral societies typically need to leave home for days, weeks, or even months; this prolonged absence makes it impossible to directly monitor women’s behaviour, thus requiring cultural and customary measures to reduce the possibilities of women’s affairs during that time;
- **Need for customs restricting women’s chastity**: Numerous records from pastoral societies indicate a high concern for women’s chastity. For example, herding communities in the Mediterranean place great importance on women’s chastity and virgin identity, believing that women should maintain purity and chastity after marriage. Among pastoral groups such as the Bedouin, Kazakhs, Mongolians, and Armenians, strict norms regarding female fidelity and sexual self-restraint have also been documented. These norms often develop into institutionalised forms, such as infibulation and restrictions on leaving the house, to ensure women remain chaste during men’s absences.

To test this hypothesis, the author employs multilevel data sources, combining historical and modern ethnographic data for empirical analysis. The study finds that individuals from historically **pastoralism-dependent** ethnic groups are more inclined to **support anti-abortion measures**, **accept genital restriction practices**, and **approve restrictions on women's free movement**, among other behaviours. These are closely related to historical backgrounds demanding men’s prolonged absence from home (the need to leave), rather than stemming simply from patriarchal structures or economic development differences. The subsequent use of an **instrumental variables** strategy confirms the **causal nature** of this finding.

#### **Contributions to the Literature**

**Connections to the existing literature**:

This research relates to literature exploring the historical origins of gender inequality. Existing studies encompass:

- The influence of historical divisions of labour on contemporary norms for women’s labour markets (Alesina et al., 2013; Baiardi, 2016);
- The role of legal institutions in explaining female HIV infection rates (Anderson, 2018);
- The origins of the Chinese custom of foot-binding (Fan and Wu, 2024);
- The historical role of women in production and their status in society (Qian, 2008; Carranza, 2014; Xue, 2016);
- The impact of traditional customs on women's educational attainment, intimate partner violence, spousal cooperation, and female economic outcomes (e.g., Ashraf et al., 2020; Tur-Prats, 2017; Lowes, 2018; Bursztyn et al., 2017, 2020);
- Related research on female circumcision, such as the effect of regime stability on its prevalence, the impact of related interventions, and geographic diffusion (Poyker, 2023; Bellemare et al., 2015; Vogt et al., 2016; Corno et al., 2020);
- Cross-cultural variation in jealousy responses and paternal investment (Scelza et al., 2020), and the role of “culture” in economics, its persistence, and determinants (e.g., Guiso et al., 2006; Giuliano, 2007; Galor and Özak, 2016, etc.).

**The unique contribution of this paper**

- This paper is the first to examine the **functional** origin of customs restricting women’s interaction with males outside their household;
- It introduces “**pastoralism**” as an explanatory variable, providing systematic evidence to support the functionalist theory of “reducing paternity uncertainty”;
- It demonstrates that economic phenomena like limiting women’s access to certain medical services, restricting women's agency, and implementing circumcision primarily function to **mitigate men’s uncertainty regarding fatherhood.**

## Pastoralism and Paternity Uncertainty

#### **Pastoralism in Pre-industrial Societies**

Pastoralism refers to the husbandry, care, and use of livestock, typically including the **herding** of animals such as sheep, goats, camels, cattle, horses, reindeer, and yaks. Unlike agriculture, pastoralists need to take their livestock to **natural pastures** for grazing.

- **Geographic distribution of pastoralism**: Pastoralism isn't confined to a single region; it exists ubiquitously across global ecological settings. For example — from the tundra in Scandinavia and Northern Russia, the Eurasian steppe, the deserts of the Arabian Peninsula and North Africa, to the Andes mountains in South America — various ecologies have witnessed primarily pastoral groups. The wide array of milieus enables pastoralism to endure persistently in various regions;
- **Diversity of pastoralist subsistence modes**: Pastoral societies feature diverse lifestyles — patterns span from **fully sedentary** to **semi-nomadic**, **seasonally mobile**, and even **fully nomadic** modes exist. Even predominantly pastoral societies rarely ever depend entirely on livestock; and partly constrain on agriculture as means of subsistence like horticulture alongside livestock in complementary ways.
