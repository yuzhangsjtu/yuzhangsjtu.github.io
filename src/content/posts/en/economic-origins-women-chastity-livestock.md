> This paper explores the persistent influence of **pastoralism** in pre-industrial societies on modern gender norms. The anthropological literature points out that, because men in pastoral societies need to **go out herding for long periods** and find it difficult to monitor women's behaviour, strict restrictions on women's chastity and sexual behaviour took shape in order to ensure the purity of patrilineal bloodlines. By analysing extensive cross-cultural data, the study shows that historical pastoral dependence is significantly associated with restrictions on women's sexual freedom in modern societies, supporting the theory of **cultural persistence** and revealing how subsistence patterns shape and perpetuate gender conceptions.

**Title:** On the Economic Origins of Concerns Over Women's Chastity

**Journal:** *The Review of Economic Studies*

**Author:**

## Introduction

#### **Information Asymmetry in Reproduction**

Information asymmetry in the human reproductive process gives rise to "**maternal certainty**", i.e., a woman knows with certainty after giving birth that the child is hers, whereas a man cannot be completely certain of the biological relationship (paternal uncertainty). Specific reasons include:

- The concealed nature of female ovulation;
- Fertilisation occurring inside the body;
- And the potential for female infidelity.

This uncertainty affects men's success in transmitting their genes. Evolutionary psychology theories hold that such **paternity uncertainty** caused men to gradually evolve **jealousy** and a strong **desire for control**, particularly the wish to prevent women's "**promiscuous**" behaviour, thereby ensuring their own biological relationship with the child (see Trivers, 1972; Symons, 1979; Buss et al., 1992; Buss, 2000; Platek and Shackelford, 2006). Consequently, in environments where women's behaviour is difficult to **monitor**, men tend to worry more about women's chastity and fidelity, and are thus more inclined to adopt measures to suppress female sexual freedom.

#### **The Formation of Chastity Norms and Customs**

A large body of anthropological literature points out that human societies possess a range of so-called **chastity norms** and **customs** that control women's behaviour, aimed at suppressing women's extramarital affairs. Restricting women's behaviour is not only common in certain specific societies but also manifests in diverse forms. These customs and norms specifically include:

- **Restricting women's freedom of movement**: For example, many societies impose limits on when and where women may go out, particularly restrictions on going out alone;
- **Social expectations regarding female chastity**: Many societies hold strong social expectations of female purity, believing that women should remain chaste before marriage and continue to maintain chastity and restraint after marriage;
- **Extreme forms of customs, such as female circumcision**: The female circumcision practised in some societies (especially infibulation) is a relatively extreme means of behavioural control, making intercourse extremely painful and difficult and thereby restricting female sexual activity;
- **Restrictions on abortion**: Prohibiting or opposing abortion is regarded in many societies as a way to reduce women's extramarital affairs, because this practice exposes women to the risk of pregnancy from non-marital sex, reducing their motivation for sexual activity.

> **Infibulation**: A type of female circumcision referring to an extreme form of Female Genital Mutilation (FGM), whose main feature is that, after part or all of the external genital tissue is excised, the vaginal opening is stitched or sealed, leaving only a small hole for urination and menstruation. This practice causes pain during intercourse, with the aim of restricting women's sexual behaviour.
>
> Infibulation usually causes severe effects on women's physical and mental health, including infection, difficulty urinating, menstrual problems, childbirth complications, and psychological trauma. Such practices are carried out in some regions for cultural or religious reasons to "ensure" women's chastity and "purity", but they have been widely condemned and are regarded as illegal in many countries.

⬆️The core aim of all these measures is to reduce women's ability or motivation to seek extramarital affairs. They have numerous direct impacts on women, including on their health, their freedom to have an abortion, or their ability to participate freely in social activities. This makes understanding the **origins** of these restrictive practices very necessary!

#### **Core Hypothesis: The Link Between Pastoralism and Women's Chastity**

The **core hypothesis** of this paper is that, in the pre-industrial era, societies relying primarily on **pastoralism** for subsistence were more inclined than agricultural societies to develop these customs restricting women's sexual behaviour. The reason is that the pastoral mode of production requires men to frequently **leave home** to herd, leaving them unable to **monitor** women's behaviour for long periods, which increases the need to control women's behaviour during men's absence.

- **Male absence and the problem of behaviour monitoring**: Men in pastoral societies typically have to leave home for days, weeks, or even months; this prolonged absence makes it impossible for men to directly monitor women's behaviour, and thus they need to use cultural and customary measures to reduce the possibility of women's affairs during that time;
- **The need for customs restricting women's chastity**: Many records from pastoral societies indicate a high degree of concern for women's chastity. For example, herding communities in the Mediterranean place great importance on women's chastity and virginity, holding that women should continue to maintain purity and chastity after marriage; and among pastoral groups such as the Bedouin, Kazakhs, Mongols, and Armenians, there are also strict norms regarding female fidelity and sexual self-restraint. These norms often develop into institutionalised forms, such as infibulation and restrictions on going out, to ensure that women remain chaste while men are away.

To test this hypothesis, the author employs multilevel data sources, combining historical and modern ethnographic data for empirical analysis. The study finds that individuals from historically **pastoralism-dependent** ethnic groups are more inclined to **support anti-abortion positions**, **accept circumcision-related restrictions**, and **impose limits on women's free movement**, among other behaviours. These are closely related to the historical background in which men were absent from home for long periods (the need to go out), rather than stemming simply from structures of gender dominance or from differences in economic development. The subsequent use of an **instrumental variable** strategy confirms that this finding is **causal**.

**Connections to the existing literature**:

This research relates to the literature exploring the **historical origins of gender inequality**. Existing studies cover:

- The role of legal institutions in explaining female HIV infection rates (Anderson, 2018);

**The unique contribution of this paper**

- This paper is the first to study the **functional** origins of customs that restrict women's interaction with men outside the household;
- It introduces "**pastoralism**" as an explanatory variable and provides systematic evidence supporting the functionalist theory of "reducing paternity uncertainty";
- It demonstrates that the main function of economic phenomena such as limiting women's access to certain medical services, restricting women's freedom of movement, and performing female circumcision is to **reduce men's uncertainty about the father-child relationship**.

## Pastoralism and Paternity Uncertainty

#### **Pastoralism in Pre-industrial Societies**

Pastoralism refers to the husbandry, care, and use of livestock, typically including the **herding** of animals such as sheep, goats, camels, cattle, horses, reindeer, and yaks. Unlike agriculture, pastoralists need to take their livestock to **natural pastures** to graze.

- **Geographic distribution of pastoralism**: Pastoralism is not confined to any single region but exists widely across many ecological environments around the world. For example, the tundra of Scandinavia and northern Russia, the Eurasian steppe, the deserts of the Arabian Peninsula and North Africa, and the Andes of South America all have groups whose subsistence is based primarily on pastoralism. The diversity of these ecological environments allows pastoralism to persist across different regions;
- **Diversity of pastoralist subsistence modes**: Pastoral societies have a wide variety of lifestyles, ranging from **fully sedentary** to **semi-nomadic**, **seasonally migratory**, and even **fully nomadic** modes. Even societies that are primarily pastoral usually do not rely entirely on livestock; they also depend in part on subsistence modes such as agriculture or horticulture. This diversity provides pastoral societies with relatively flexible means of survival, while also producing differences in the degree of pastoral dependence between different societies.

#### **The Problem of Male Absence in Pastoralism**

Unlike agriculture, one important feature of pastoralism is that, during herding, men need to leave the **family settlement** frequently and for long periods to go out and tend the livestock. This feature gives rise to the so-called problem of "**male absence**". The author analyses in detail the specific causes of male absence and its effects on the control of women's sexuality from the following aspects:

- **Frequency and duration of going out to herd**: The frequency and duration of men's absence depend on many factors, including the length of the dry and wet seasons, the size of the herd, whether there are other herds nearby, population density, soil conditions, and distance from markets for agricultural products. In the cold or wet season, herds may need to be taken out frequently for short periods, whereas in the warm or dry season herders may be away for weeks or even months. Moreover, even when the grazing grounds are nearby, men may need to spend the night in the open to protect the livestock from predators or thieves. This pattern of absence further aggravates the difficulty men face in monitoring women's behaviour;
- **Male absence driven by trade needs**: In societies heavily dependent on pastoralism, because they are not fully self-sufficient, they often also need to obtain agricultural products through **trade**, which is another reason men leave home. This pattern further increases men's time of absence from the household;
- **The gendered division of labour in pastoralism**: In most pre-industrial societies, pastoralism is almost entirely **male-dominated**. The author conjectures that this gendered division of labour may have formed because women's needs related to childbearing, child-rearing, and breastfeeding make them physically less suited than men to prolonged herding away from home. In addition, men have a physical advantage in protecting livestock from predators or thieves, which also makes them more suited to taking part in herding activities;
- **The "male absence" feature of pastoralism versus agriculture**: In agriculture, even where it is patriarchal, men usually do not need to be away for long periods but mainly engage in local cultivation, which makes **monitoring women in agricultural societies** easier. Therefore, even in patriarchal agricultural societies, the need to restrict women's sexual freedom is not as strong as in pastoral societies.

#### **Pastoral Data in Pre-industrial Societies**

To build a model of the influence of pastoralism on gender norms, the author uses data from the *Ethnographic Atlas* (an old friend). This atlas is an anthropological database that has collected ethnographic information on more than 1,200 ethnic groups worldwide, recording each group's way of life before colonisation and industrialisation, covering aspects such as kinship organisation, religious beliefs, settlement patterns, political organisation, and institutional complexity.

The author takes the "**historical pastoral dependence of an ethnic group**" as the main explanatory variable and constructs this variable from two pieces of data in the *Ethnographic Atlas*:

- **Degree of dependence on animal husbandry**: indicates the proportion (0-100%) to which an ethnic group depends on pastoralism for its subsistence;
- **Main type of animal raised**: identifies the main type of animal raised by the group.

The author creates an indicator: when an ethnic group's main raised animal is a "**herd animal**" (for example, sheep, cattle, horses, reindeer, alpacas, or camels), the indicator takes the value 1; if the main animal is a "non-herd animal" (for example, pigs, dogs, or poultry) or there is no animal, it takes the value 0. By multiplying this indicator by the group's pastoral dependence, the main explanatory variable, the "**historical pastoral dependence of an ethnic group**", is generated. The mathematical expression of this variable is:
$$
\text{pastoralism}_j = \text{animalhusbandry}_j \times \mathbb{1}_j^{\text{herd_animal}}
$$
where:

- $\text{animalhusbandry}_j$ denotes the degree to which a society depends on animal husbandry;
- $\mathbb{1}_j^{\text{herd_animal}}$ is an indicator function used to determine whether the society's main type of animal is a **herd animal**. If it is a herd animal (such as sheep, cattle, horses, reindeer, alpacas, or camels), the value is 1; otherwise (such as pigs, dogs, poultry, or no animal) the value is 0.

#### **Historical Validation of the Hypothesis**

Based on other cultural and customary data provided by the *Ethnographic Atlas*, the relationship between historical pastoral dependence and the control of women's sexual behaviour is validated, namely "**whether a pastoral orientation promoted customs and norms restricting women's sexual behaviour**".

The *Ethnographic Atlas* contains not only information about subsistence modes but also records of many other aspects of life in pre-industrial societies, which allows the author to validate the hypothesis from multiple angles. The specific validation indicators include:

- **Patrilocal Residence**: records whether, after marriage, a couple customarily lives with the husband's family or close to it. This residence pattern makes it convenient for the husband and his family to monitor and restrict the bride's behaviour. The relevant economic theory (Guha, 2016) suggests that this pattern may be a mechanism for preventing women's promiscuity. The results in Table 1 show that the higher a society's pastoral dependence, the more likely it is to adopt a patrilocal residence pattern.
- **Insistence on Virginity**: the database also contains societies' attitudes toward premarital sex. Columns 3 and 4 of Table 1 show that societies more dependent on pastoralism are more inclined to require women to remain chaste before marriage.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241113155507048.png)

- **Gender conceptions in folklore and oral traditions**: The author further analyses Yuri Berezkin's compilation and classification of the folktales of nearly a thousand ethnic groups worldwide, in order to assess whether descriptions of women's sexual attractiveness in folk culture are related to pastoral dependence. The study finds that **among groups with high pastoral dependence, the proportion of folktales describing women's sexual attractiveness is lower**. This suggests that the cultural traditions of pastoral societies may be more inclined to emphasise women's chastity, reflecting stronger social constraints on women's sexual behaviour. This finding further validates the association between pastoral dependence and the restriction of women's sexual freedom.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241113155738922.png)

## Data and Methods

#### **Data**

**Data Sources**

The author mainly uses the following two modern datasets to obtain detailed individual-level information on gender conceptions and behaviour:

- **Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS)**: The DHS is a nationally representative household survey covering more than 90 countries, focusing on women's health, empowerment, and living conditions. This dataset is especially suitable for studying gender norms and health issues, because its sample is composed mainly of women and surveys in detail variables such as their reproductive health, attitudes toward sexual behaviour, and freedom of movement. The DHS has a large sample size in each country, typically containing 5,000 to 30,000 households, and can therefore provide strong statistical power;
- **World Values Survey (WVS)**: The WVS is a nationally representative survey covering nearly 100 countries, aimed at understanding the values, beliefs, and motivations of people in different countries. The WVS contains specific questions about respondents' attitudes toward abortion, an indicator the author uses to measure attitudes related to gender norms. The WVS sample size in each country is usually around 1,000 people; although its scale is smaller than that of the DHS, it can provide data on additional dimensions for the study by supplementing the abortion-attitude variable.

**Data Matching**

To examine the relationship between modern individuals' gender conceptions and their ancestors' pastoral dependence, it is necessary to link individuals in the modern data with their historical ethnic groups. The specific matching methods are as follows:

- **Ethnic-group and language matching method**: Using the individual ethnic-group information recorded in the DHS and WVS, or the language used, individuals are matched with the historical ethnic groups in the *Ethnographic Atlas*. In many cases, the DHS directly provides the respondent's ethnic-group information, which can then be linked directly to the corresponding group in the atlas;
- **Language-based matching**: When the DHS and WVS do not record specific ethnic-group information, the respondent's mother tongue is used for matching. Specifically, modern ethnic groups are first linked to the languages they use through a linguistics database (such as Glottolog), and this language is then matched with the groups in the atlas. The rationale for this language-matching method is that two ethnic groups speaking the same language are likely to have similar cultures and customs, so it can serve as an alternative matching method;
- **Handling the multi-group matching problem**: In a few cases, multiple historical ethnic groups use the same language. In this situation, the pastoral dependence of the several groups is averaged for matching. Although this method reduces the precision of the matching, it can still preserve the validity of the data reasonably well in the absence of precise information.

#### **Baseline Regression Strategy**

The baseline regression model takes the following form:


$$
y_{i,j} = \alpha + \beta \times \text{pastoralism}_j + \sum_c \delta_c \times \text{Country}^c_i + \epsilon_{i,j}
$$


where:

- $y_{i,j}$ denotes the gender-conception indicator in the modern data for the $i$-th individual of the $j$-th ethnic group (for example, anti-abortion attitudes, restricted freedom of movement, etc.);
- $\text{pastoralism}_j$ is the historical pastoral dependence of the $j$-th ethnic group, measuring the degree to which that group depended on pastoralism in the pre-industrial period;
- $\text{Country}^c_i$ is a dummy variable for the country $c$ in which individual $i$ resides;
- $\epsilon_{i,j}$ is the error term, with standard errors clustered at the ethnic-group level of the Ethnographic Atlas to address the potential correlation in conceptions among individuals within the same group.

**Control Variables**

To strengthen the robustness of the analysis, the author gradually introduces several control variables into the baseline model. The choice of control variables aims to rule out other factors that are unrelated to pastoral dependence but may affect gender conceptions.

- **Exogenous control variables**: First, a set of exogenous control variables that may be unrelated to pastoral dependence is introduced, including the respondent's age, year-of-survey fixed effects, and the year in which the ethnographic data were collected. These variables are considered not to directly affect gender norms in modern society, and can therefore serve as part of the robustness tests;
- **Endogenous control variables**: Second, a set of endogenous control variables is introduced in some models to test the robustness of the model. These control variables may be related to historical pastoral dependence and are therefore called "endogenous control variables" (*bad controls*), including individual-level variables such as religion, urban or rural residence, marital status, and educational attainment, as well as group-level variables such as polygyny, power structure, and kinship system. These variables are used for sensitivity tests to ensure the robustness of the results. These control variables are not suitable for use in all regressions, because they may distort the estimation of the main explanatory variable, that is, the bad-controls problem exists.

#### **Instrumental Variable Method**

The baseline regression may suffer from endogeneity problems. In particular, if some unobserved factors simultaneously affect pastoral dependence and modern gender norms, the estimates in the baseline model may deviate from the true values because of confounding factors.

To address this problem, the author introduces an instrumental variable method, using "**relative suitability for pastoralism**" as the instrumental variable, in order to establish the causal effect of pastoral dependence on gender norms. Relative suitability for pastoralism is an ecological factor that affects how much a society depends on pastoralism but does not directly affect modern gender conceptions, and can therefore serve as an ideal instrumental variable.

## Results

#### **Baseline Regression Results**

The author analyses the influence of pastoral dependence on modern gender norms from four angles (five variables) (⬇️see Figure 3 below):

- **Anti-abortion attitudes**: The study uses the WVS abortion-attitude data to measure the degree of opposition to abortion. The regression results show that modern individuals from ethnic groups whose ancestors depended on pastoralism are more inclined to oppose abortion. This result is consistent with the author's hypothesis, namely that opposition to abortion may serve to increase the risk that women become pregnant through extramarital behaviour, thereby reducing women's sexual freedom;
- **Female circumcision**: In 13 African countries, the DHS provides data on whether women have undergone circumcision (especially infibulation). Infibulation is an extreme form of female circumcision that controls women's sexual behaviour by reducing sexual pleasure and increasing the inconvenience of sexual activity. The regression results show that in modern society, women from ethnic groups with high ancestral pastoral dependence are more likely to have undergone circumcision;
- **Restrictions on freedom of movement**: Restrictions on freedom of movement reflect a society's limits on women's mobility and activities. The data in the DHS include restrictions on women's activities such as going out and visiting relatives. The study finds that ethnic groups with higher ancestral pastoral dependence are more inclined to restrict women's freedom of movement;
- **Sexual behaviour norms**: Sexual behaviour norms include variables such as women's **number of sexual partners** and whether they **remain faithful within marriage**. In the DHS sample, the author analyses women's number of sexual partners and within-marriage infidelity to measure cultural norms regarding women's sexual behaviour. In a sample of 447,834 respondents from 37 countries, the results show that the higher an ethnic group's ancestral pastoral dependence, the fewer sexual partners its descendants have and the more inclined they are to remain faithful within marriage. This result supports the hypothesis that pastorally dependent societies suppress women's extramarital sexual behaviour through cultural norms.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20241113161922270.png)

## Mechanism Analysis

In the preceding analysis, the author proposes the "**monitoring problem**" as the main driver of the restriction of women's sexual freedom in pastoral societies. Specifically, in societies based primarily on pastoralism, men are frequently away for long periods because of herding and the like, making it difficult to effectively monitor women's sexual behaviour; these societies are therefore more likely to develop strict gender norms to control women's behaviour and thereby reduce "**paternity uncertainty**". In this section, the author provides further evidence to verify whether the "monitoring problem" is indeed the core mechanism driving the development of gender norms.

To verify whether the "monitoring problem" is the key driving factor in the relationship between pastoral dependence and gender norms, the author rules out other possible explanations from the following angles:

- **Distinction from patriarchal societies**: The mere fact that men hold a dominant position in male-dominated societies does not necessarily lead to strict restrictions on women's sexual freedom. The author argues that the **root cause** that truly produces differences in gender norms is whether men are able to **continuously** monitor women's behaviour, rather than the patriarchal structure alone. The author therefore needs to provide evidence that the monitoring problem (rather than patriarchal culture itself) is the driving mechanism;
- **Contrast with agricultural societies**: Although agricultural societies also have patriarchal structures, the nature of agricultural production usually **does not require** men to leave their place of residence for long periods, so the need to monitor women's behaviour is relatively small in agricultural societies. This distinction provides logical support for the monitoring problem as the key mechanism.

The specific approach is to use **plough agriculture as a control group**: comparing pastoral societies with plough-agriculture societies. The regression results show that there is no significant association between plough agriculture and gender norms, which indicates that the patriarchal structure itself is not the cause of the differences in gender norms, further supporting the plausibility of the "monitoring problem" as the mechanism;

In this section, the author also finds that economic factors are not the main cause influencing gender conceptions.

## Conclusion and Discussion

- This paper provides evidence supporting a theory: **the norms and customs that suppress women's sexual behaviour originate from the difficulty men have in monitoring women's behaviour**. The study shows that customs such as anti-abortion attitudes, female circumcision, and restrictions on women's freedom of movement are associated with historical dependence on pastoralism. This indicates that seemingly unrelated customs are in fact functionally closely related, all serving to restrict women's sexual behaviour.

- **Pastoralism as a representative of the difficulty of monitoring women's behaviour**: Although pastoralism is not the only mode of production that causes men to be frequently away and women's behaviour to be difficult to monitor — for example, deep-sea fishing, and in the modern economy oil extraction, long-haul trucking, and the like, present similar situations — using pre-industrial pastoralism as a variable to measure the difficulty of monitoring women's behaviour has unique advantages, because the distribution of pastoralism is mainly determined by **ecological conditions**, which helps in deriving **causal relationships**. In addition, pastoralism has a **long history**, providing a time span for studying the evolution of customs, and it is found all over the world.
- **The diversity of pastoral societies**: Although the article finds an association between pastoral dependence and customs that suppress women's sexual behaviour, this does not mean that all pastoral societies have this pattern. For example, the expansion of pastoralism in England after the Black Death did not give rise to the problem of monitoring women's sexual behaviour, but instead created a demand for women to take on service roles in pastoral work. Therefore, the control of women's behaviour in pastoral societies varies by time and place.
- **The relationship between culture and environment**: This paper supports the anthropological theory of the "functional relationship between economic modes of production and social phenomena", namely that the environmental conditions in which humans find themselves not only affect their subsistence modes but also ultimately shape their culture. This is consistent with the view put forward by early anthropologists that the environment, while determining the mode of survival, also shapes society and culture.
