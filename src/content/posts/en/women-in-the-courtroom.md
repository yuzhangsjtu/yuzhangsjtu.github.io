> This study systematically examines the impact of information-technology-driven **judicial transparency** reform on **gender inequality** in China's civil litigation. By analyzing approximately 6 million judicial judgments, the paper shows the following: First, **before the reform, female plaintiffs had a significantly lower probability of winning than male plaintiffs**. Second, exploiting a nationwide judicial reform that mandated **online live broadcasting of courtroom trials** as a natural experiment, and applying difference-in-differences and instrumental variable methods for causal inference, the study finds that **an increase in trial broadcasting intensity effectively narrowed the gender win-rate gap**. Finally, mechanism analysis indicates that the reform works primarily by **changing judge behavior**: under public scrutiny, judges paid greater attention to the claims of female plaintiffs and invested more effort in drafting judgments. This study demonstrates the critical role of technological oversight in enhancing public-sector accountability and promoting judicial fairness, offering new micro-level evidence for understanding how transparency improves governance.

## Introduction

#### **Research Background**

As a pervasive socio-economic phenomenon, **gender disparity** and its effects have been widely studied across many fields, including the **labor market** and **political participation**. However, in **civil litigation**—a key arena for safeguarding social fairness, and especially in the context of developing countries—a significant knowledge gap remains in the systematic understanding of gender disparity. This paper argues that **gender bias** in the civil justice system may carry substantial economic consequences. For example, by affecting expectations about contract enforcement, it may deter women from participating in entrepreneurial activity, thereby leading to a misallocation of talent and resources at the societal level. Existing literature (e.g., Duflo, 2012; Hsieh et al., 2019) has shown that removing gender barriers can generate considerable economic gains, which further underscores the real-world importance of this study.

Despite the significance of the issue, rigorous empirical research faces two major challenges:

- First, systematic **micro-data** on civil litigation, especially in developing economies, are extremely scarce.
- Second, because of uncertainties in institutional implementation (such as the principle of random case assignment), establishing a credible **causal identification** strategy is particularly difficult.

To address these challenges, this study uses China as its analytical setting, drawing on the vast judicial judgment data made available since 2013 by "**China Judgments Online**" (CJO) to construct a comprehensive dataset comprising approximately 6 million civil judgments. This unprecedented data resource not only allows the paper to quantify gender bias on a large scale, but also enables the study to probe deeply, through text analysis, into the inner mechanisms of the judicial decision-making process.

More critically, the paper exploits a nationwide "**judicial openness reform**" launched in China in **2016** as a quasi-experiment. The reform mandated that courts conduct **online live broadcasting** of trial proceedings through a unified platform, and its staggered implementation across different courts provides exogenous variation for the paper's causal inference. Because the main goal of this reform was to enhance **judicial transparency** rather than to target gender issues directly, this largely mitigates concerns about policy **endogeneity**.

#### **Basic Findings**

The paper's analysis begins with an examination of the pre-reform period (2014Q1–2016Q2). The study finds that, after controlling for a wide range of variables at the case, region, and judge levels, **female plaintiffs' probability of winning was significantly lower than that of male plaintiffs, by about 5 percentage points**.

Building on this, the paper uses a generalized difference-in-differences (DID) model, taking the proportion of broadcast cases at the "court–business-area–quarter" level as a continuous treatment variable, to assess the causal effect of the reform. The study finds that an increase in trial broadcasting intensity **significantly narrowed the gap** in win rates between male and female plaintiffs. To further ensure the robustness of the conclusions, the paper adopts a Bartik-like IV approach to handle potential endogeneity problems, and its results are highly consistent with the DID analysis.

#### **Contribution to the Literature**

This study aims to contribute to three strands of literature:

- First, this paper's work provides new findings on gender disparities in civil litigation. Prior research has focused mainly on criminal cases and documented a gender gap that **favors** female defendants. For example, Bindler and Hjalmarsson (2020) find that female defendants in the UK tend to receive more lenient treatment. The same pattern has also been documented in the US (Sorensen, Sarnikar, and Oaxaca 2012; Starr 2015; and Butcher, Park, and Piehl 2017). However, it remains unclear whether these findings can be generalized to civil cases or to developing-country contexts. This paper studies the gender effect of litigants in civil cases between private individuals in China, and reveals a distinct pattern: **female plaintiffs in civil courtrooms face a disadvantage**.
- Second, this study advances the literature on the **role of information technology in public affairs** (Pierce, Snow, and McAfee 2015; Mastrobuoni 2020). Introducing live-broadcast cameras into the courtroom is analogous to equipping police officers with body-worn cameras (BWCs); both serve as monitoring devices to enhance transparency and accountability. Research on body-worn cameras (BWCs) has shown that their presence can induce behavioral changes in both officers and civilians, but distinguishing their effect on officers from their effect on civilians is challenging (Cubukcu et al. 2021; Zamoff, Greenwood, and Burtch 2021; Ferrazares 2023). This paper's study evaluates the impact of surveillance cameras in the courtroom. Drawing on a large corpus of judicial decision texts, the paper shows that judges changed their behavior in response to this technology, thereby influencing judicial outcomes. This finding provides further support for the **disciplinary** effect of recording cameras in public institutions.
- Finally, this paper provides new evidence on the **impact of transparency on public-sector decision-making and governance** (Kosack and Fung 2014). Hansen, McMahon, and Prat (2018) show how the public's right to be informed about monetary policy deliberations affects decision-makers, whereas this paper demonstrates that real-time monitoring induces judges to behave more professionally and to deliberate more thoroughly. Whereas the existing literature focuses on the effects of transparency improvements brought about by institutional change, this paper examines the effects of greater transparency made possible by the adoption of new technology, which is a novel contribution to this field of study.

## Institutional Context

#### **China's Courts and Judges**

China's court system is composed of **four levels**: the Primary People's Courts, the Intermediate People's Courts, the Higher People's Courts, and the Supreme People's Court. Primary courts handle ordinary cases at the county level, whereas intermediate courts have jurisdiction over major, complex cases and appeals from primary courts. Higher courts handle appeals from intermediate courts and cases with significant influence within their respective provinces. As of January 2021, the Chinese court system comprised the Supreme People's Court, 33 higher courts (typically one per province), 416 intermediate courts (typically one per prefecture-level city), and 3,087 primary courts (typically one per county). Within this hierarchical structure, higher courts exercise supervision and guidance over the lower courts within their jurisdiction. As the nation's highest judicial organ, the **Supreme People's Court** was the primary driving force behind the judicial reform discussed in this paper.

Courts are typically organized into several business divisions (sub-courts), each staffed with judges who specialize in a particular area. This study focuses on **civil cases**, which are divided into nine major areas: (1) Personality Rights, (2) Marriage, Family, and Inheritance, (3) Property Rights, (4) Contracts, (5) Intellectual Property, (6) Labor Disputes, (7) Financial, Securities, and Insurance, (8) Tort Liability, and (9) Special Procedures. Cases that do not fall into these categories are classified as "other." Specific business divisions are responsible for subsets of these areas, and their scope of responsibility varies depending on the region's population and level of economic development.

Within this judicial system, judges enjoy **considerable power** during trial proceedings while facing relatively limited **scrutiny** (Peerenboom, 2002). Judges' attitudes and preferences exert a strong influence on court decisions and litigation outcomes. For cases with **"clear facts and minor disputes,"** a single judge typically adjudicates the case under simplified procedure, and their opinion dominates the final outcome. In **"complex cases"** under ordinary procedure (such as those involving more litigants), although an adjudication panel of 3, 5, or 7 judges is convened, the influence of the **presiding judge** remains dominant in determining the litigation outcome.

#### **Judicial Reform**

The vast discretionary power granted to judges, combined with limited external oversight, effectively turned the judicial decision-making process into a **"black box."** To address these problems, China's Supreme People's Court has, since 2014, advanced a series of initiatives aimed at enhancing the transparency of the judicial system. As part of the reform, four major information disclosure platforms were established to enhance the transparency of judicial procedures. These platforms focus, respectively, on publishing **legal instruments**, conducting **live trial broadcasts**, providing **information on litigation procedures**, and disclosing the **list of dishonest persons subject to enforcement**.

The focus of this paper is the impact of the judicial openness reform, particularly **live trial broadcasting**. The Supreme People's Court mandated that courts at all levels broadcast trials live on "China Open Court Trials Online," with the long-term goal of broadcasting all trials (except in special circumstances). The website officially launched in September 2016, allowing the public to watch trials in real time or to view recordings afterward. By the end of 2021, more than 16 million cases had been broadcast live. The platform has attracted widespread attention from the general public, including citizens, journalists, and legal practitioners.

Although this reform swept across the entire judicial system in a relatively short period of time (about a year and a half), there was considerable variation in the point at which individual courts connected to the website. As of September 2016, 383 courts (10.89% of all courts) had completed connection; by January 2017, another 762 courts had connected; and by December 2017, all 3,517 courts had connected. Beyond the variation in connection timing, there was also enormous variation in the proportion of cases broadcast by each court.

The Supreme People's Court set ambitious goals for this reform, demonstrating the authorities' determination to modernize the judicial system. Lower courts, however, did **not necessarily** share the same enthusiasm or possess the technical and financial capacity needed to implement the reform, even though their cooperation was crucial to its success. The solution adopted in response had Chinese characteristics: higher courts quantified the expected progress and incorporated it into the **performance evaluation** system for lower courts. Although practices varied across localities, intermediate courts would typically set a trial-broadcasting quota for primary courts and incorporate it into their evaluation system.

Local court presidents determine broadcasting quotas based on the requirements of higher courts and allocate the tasks across the business divisions. The heads of the business divisions then select specific cases for broadcasting and submit the plans to the president. In principle, the president reviews the plans and makes the final decision; in practice, approval is typically automatic.

## Data Construction

#### **Sample Construction**

- The paper's data foundation comes from "China Judgments Online" (CJO), China's official platform for publicly disclosing judicial instruments. Through a commercial data company, the authors obtained all civil judgment documents from **January 2014 to December 2018**. The choice of this time span is crucial, because it precisely **straddles the 2016 trial broadcasting reform**, providing the basis for a before-and-after comparison.

- To focus on the core variable of "**gender**," the authors restricted the sample to cases in which both parties were **individual litigants**, excluding cases with institutions or corporations as litigating parties. This makes gender identification more direct and accurate.

- **Subsample construction:** In addition to the "main sample" containing all individual litigants, the authors also constructed a **"two-litigant subsample"** (two-litigant subsample), i.e., cases in which there was only one plaintiff and one defendant. The advantage of this subsample is that the litigants' gender is very clearly defined, with no confounding factors, so the results are more precise and more interpretable when estimating the magnitude of the effect.

- **Matching broadcast data:** The authors obtained all **11,016,416** trial broadcasting records from "China Open Court Trials Online" up to April 2021. They then matched these broadcast records with the judgment-document sample, thereby identifying which cases had been broadcast.

#### **Structure and Content of Judicial Judgments**

A standard Chinese judgment typically contains five parts:

- **Basic information:** case number, adjudicating court, dates of trial and publication, litigant information (name, gender, date of birth, etc.), and lawyer information.
- **Litigation claims:** the plaintiff's claims and reasons, and the defendant's response and defense.
- **Facts found by the court:** the evidence accepted by the court and the case facts it found.
- **Application of law and reasoning:** the judge's legal analysis and the logic of the judgment.
- **Judgment outcome:** the degree to which the claims are supported, the apportionment of litigation costs, etc.

#### **Variable Construction**

**Basic case variables:** From the "basic information" section, the authors extract the case number, adjudicating court, level of adjudication (first instance or second instance), the number of plaintiffs and defendants, the number of lawyers, and whether the parties appeared in court.

**Gender variables:**

- **Litigant gender:** extracted directly from the judgment. In cases with multiple litigants, the gender of the **first-ranked litigant** is used.
- **Judge gender:** The judgment does not directly provide the judge's gender. The authors used the **Ngender algorithm**, an algorithm widely used in Chinese industry to predict gender from names. The authors also validated the algorithm against a list of nearly 2,000 "National Model Judges," with an **accuracy as high as 92%**, demonstrating its reliability.

**Case type:** This is a highly innovative variable. The authors recognized that even cases within the same "business area" (such as contract disputes) exhibit enormous internal heterogeneity. To control for case characteristics more finely, they constructed the "case type" variable.

- **Method:** It is defined based on "the combination of laws applied in the judgment." If two cases apply similar primary laws, they are grouped into the same category.
- **Technique:** Using an **unsupervised machine-learning topic model**, all 6 million cases are automatically clustered into **50** distinct "case types." This avoids the subjectivity of manual classification and achieves objective, fine-grained control.

**Core outcome variable: plaintiff win rate**

- **Definition:** This cleverly exploits the principle that "litigation costs are borne by the losing party." **The win rate is defined as the proportion of litigation costs borne by the defendant.** For example, if the defendant bears 100% of the litigation costs, the plaintiff's win rate is 100%; if the defendant bears 80%, the plaintiff's win rate is 80%. This is a **continuous variable**, which captures more detail about the judgment outcome than a simple "win/lose" binary variable.

**Core treatment variable: broadcast intensity**

- **Definition:** At a given **"court–business-area–year–quarter"** level, the number of broadcast cases as a proportion of the total number of cases within that cell.
- **Advantage:** This is an **aggregate-level intensity measure**, rather than a case-level binary indicator. Its great advantage is that it **avoids selection bias**. Because judges may selectively broadcast "simple" or "less error-prone" cases, directly comparing broadcast and non-broadcast cases would lead to biased conclusions. Analyzing the impact of "broadcast intensity," by contrast, examines how the intensity of policy implementation changed the overall judicial environment, rather than its impact on individual cases, which makes the causal identification more credible.

**Other control variables:** From the *China City Statistical Yearbook*, the authors add prefecture-level-city variables such as GDP per capita, population, and internet penetration rate, to control for regional economic development and the level of informatization.

## Results

#### **Pre-Reform Gender Disparity**

Before the judicial openness reform was fully rolled out, was there a systematic disparity disadvantaging female plaintiffs in China's civil courts?

The paper first focuses on the period **before the reform was fully implemented** (2014Q1–2016Q2) to establish an undisturbed reference baseline. By constructing a regression model that controls for multidimensional characteristics at the case, region, judge, and court business-division levels, the paper precisely evaluates the relationship between gender and litigation win rates.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250804144019086.png)

**Table 1** (Table 1) presents the regression results. In the main sample containing all individual litigants (column 1), the results show that female plaintiffs' average win rate was **3.56 percentage points** lower than that of male plaintiffs, a difference that is statistically significant at the 1% level. In the "two-litigant subsample" containing only one plaintiff and one defendant (column 3), this win-rate disadvantage widens to **5.3 percentage points**. The paper argues that the more pronounced effect in the subsample is due to its clearer gender definition, which avoids the estimation dilution that mixing the genders of multiple litigants may cause in the main sample.

To test the generality of this disparity, the paper excludes "marriage, family, and inheritance" cases from the sample. After exclusion, in both the main sample (column 2) and the subsample (column 4), female plaintiffs' win-rate disadvantage, although somewhat smaller, **remains negative and highly statistically significant**. This indicates that the disadvantage facing female plaintiffs is not confined to a specific case area, but is a more broadly existing phenomenon.

#### **Gender Disparity and the Implementation of the Reform**

Before introducing the analysis of the reform's effect, the paper must first address the non-randomness of case broadcasting selection. The paper infers that courts may tend to selectively broadcast certain particular types of cases, which would introduce bias into the causal identification.

To verify this point, the paper conducts a balance test, comparing the differences in observable characteristics between broadcast and non-broadcast cases. The results confirm that the two differ significantly across multiple dimensions, including plaintiff gender, indicating that **the selection process for case broadcasting is indeed non-random**.

This finding led the paper to abandon the strategy of comparing broadcast versus non-broadcast at the case level. Instead, it adopts a more robust approach: analyzing the impact of **aggregate-level "broadcast intensity"** (broadcast intensity)—i.e., the proportion of broadcast cases at the "court–business-area–quarter" level—on litigation outcomes. This strategy aims to examine the overall impact of the intensity of policy implementation on the judicial environment, thereby avoiding micro-level selection bias.

The paper uses a generalized difference-in-differences (DID) model to test the causal effect of the trial broadcasting reform on the gender win-rate gap:

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250804144707699.png)

**Table 2** (Table 2) reports the estimation results of the DID model. Across all model specifications, the coefficient on this interaction term is **significantly positive**. Taking the two-litigant subsample (column 3) as an example, the results show that when broadcast intensity increases from 0 to 10%, the win-rate gap between female and male plaintiffs narrows by about 0.54 percentage points. This demonstrates that an increase in trial broadcasting intensity does indeed effectively alleviate the disadvantaged position of female plaintiffs in litigation.

To further validate the model, the paper adopts an event-study approach (see Figure 2). The results show that, in the several periods before the reform, the trends in the gender disparity for the treatment and control groups did not differ significantly, **satisfying the parallel-trends assumption**. After the reform, the interaction-term coefficient exhibits a trend that is consistently positive and increasing over time, which is consistent with the reality of the reform's gradual rollout and strengthens our confidence in the causal interpretation.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250804144813684.png)

To address the possible endogeneity of "broadcast intensity" itself, the paper adopts a Bartik-like IV strategy. This instrumental variable is constructed from two parts: each court's **historical case-type shares before the reform (2014)**, and **the average change in broadcast intensity across case types of other courts in the region where that court is located**.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250804144927178.png)

**Table 3** (Table 3) reports the results of the IV regression. The first-stage regression shows that the instrumental variable we construct has strong predictive power for actual broadcast intensity, with an F-statistic far exceeding the critical value. The second-stage regression results show that, after addressing the endogeneity problem, **the coefficient on the core interaction term remains significantly positive and is numerically slightly larger than the estimate from the DID model**. The high consistency between the IV estimates and the DID estimates indicates that potential endogeneity is not the main driver of our findings, thereby providing the strongest empirical support for our causal conclusions.

## Mechanisms and Exploration

#### **Judicial Attention**

The pre-reform disadvantage facing women may stem from judges paying less **"judicial attention"** (Judicial Attention) to the claims of female litigants. This may be an unconscious bias or stereotype—that women's statements are "**emotional**" or "**irrational**"—leading judges to take them less seriously during adjudication or to be more inclined to accept the claims of the male opposing party. The external monitoring pressure brought by the reform may compel judges to behave more professionally and impartially, giving equal attention to the claims of all litigants.

This is a variable that is difficult to observe directly. The authors use a similarity measure to construct this variable. Specifically, they compute the textual similarity among three key parts of the judgment: 1) the plaintiff's litigation claims; 2) the defendant's defense; and 3) the facts ultimately found by the court.

**∆Similarity = (similarity between plaintiff's claims and found facts) − (similarity between defendant's claims and found facts)**

It measures **the extent to which a judge's judgment leans toward adopting the plaintiff's, rather than the defendant's, version of the narrative**. A higher "∆similarity" means the judge gave more attention to, and adopted more of, the plaintiff's claims.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250804145436706.png)

**Table 4** presents a new finding: before the reform, the "∆similarity" of female-plaintiff cases was significantly lower than that of male-plaintiff cases. This provides direct textual evidence for the theoretical conjecture that "judges paid less attention to women." Using the same DID model as before, the analysis finds that **the coefficient on the "female plaintiff × broadcast intensity" interaction term is significantly positive**. This means that, as broadcast intensity increases, the relative degree to which judges adopt the claims of female plaintiffs rises significantly, and the gender "attention **deficit**" is effectively closed.

#### **Judicial Effort**

Beyond being more "impartial," judges under scrutiny may also become more "diligent"—that is, they invest more **"judicial effort"** (Judicial Effort) in adjudicating cases and drafting judgment documents in order to avoid criticism for being careless or imprecise. Higher-quality judicial input naturally reduces arbitrary, bias-based rulings, thereby benefiting litigants in a disadvantaged position.

The authors use two measures to gauge "judicial effort":

- **Number of legal citations**: A more rigorous, more fully argued judgment typically cites more, and more precise, legal provisions to support its arguments. This is widely regarded as an effective measure of the quality of judicial documents and of the judge's level of effort.
- **Textual entropy of writing style**: If a judge invests little effort, he or she may be more inclined to use formulaic, "template" language to draft the judgment. Such text is highly repetitive and low in information content, and is therefore **highly compressible, with low entropy**. Conversely, a carefully considered judgment that elaborates in detail on the case's facts and the focal points of dispute will use richer, more varied language, and is therefore **poorly compressible, with high entropy**.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250804145929305.png)

**Table 5** finds that, as broadcast intensity increases, the number of legal provisions cited in judgments increases significantly. More importantly, this increase is mainly driven by **cases with female plaintiffs**.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250804150025694.png)

**Table 6** finds that, as broadcast intensity increases, the overall entropy of judgment documents rises significantly, indicating that judgments become **less "template-like" and more substantive in content**.

#### **How Judges of Different Genders Responded to the Reform**

This section explores whether the reform's effect exhibits gender heterogeneity—that is, did male and female judges respond to the reform in the same way? The authors infer the gender of the presiding judge from names, then split the sample into "female judge presiding" and "male judge presiding" subsamples, and separately repeat the core DID analysis for each.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250804150229586.png)

The core finding is in **Table 7**: before the reform, in cases presided over by male judges, the disadvantage facing female plaintiffs (the absolute value of the Female coefficient) was larger than in cases presided over by female judges. Both male and female judges responded to the reform (the interaction-term coefficients are both significantly positive), but **the interaction-term coefficient is larger in the male-judge sample**. This indicates that the "disciplinary effect" of trial broadcasting works for both male and female judges, but its impact is **especially significant for male judges**. This may be because their original level of bias was higher, so they had more "**room for improvement**" under external scrutiny.

#### **Ruling Out Alternative Explanations**

Is the macro phenomenon observed earlier—"trial broadcasting reform narrowed the gender gap"—necessarily driven by the micro mechanism of changes in judge behavior? Are there other possible pathways?

The paper identifies two main categories of alternative channels:

- **Behavioral change during the trial:** Could the reform's impact operate by changing the behavior of **litigants** or **lawyers** at the trial itself?
- **Behavioral change before the trial:** Could the reform's impact stem from changing **litigants' decisions to file cases**?

If these alternative channels were the main cause, then although the earlier mechanism analyses of "judicial attention" and "judicial effort" are interesting, their importance in the overall causal chain would be diminished.

**Testing the "behavioral change during the trial" channel**

When litigants or lawyers realize that the trial is being broadcast nationwide, they may:

- prepare more thoroughly and argue more forcefully;
- behave more rationally and professionally, to make a good impression on the "audience";
- if female litigants or lawyers improve their courtroom performance in this way, then judges might be making more favorable decisions for them based on this **new, higher-quality trial information**, rather than because the judges' own subjective behavior (such as bias) was suppressed.

The paper uses a special subsample for counterfactual reasoning—**cases in which neither litigant appeared in court**.

In Chinese civil litigation, many cases (especially under simplified procedure) allow parties to retain a lawyer and not appear in court themselves. In these cases, there is simply no plaintiff or defendant at the trial, so their "trial behavior" cannot change. If the reform's effect persists in these cases, then it cannot be attributed to changes in litigants' on-site behavior. In addition, in about 69% of Chinese civil cases, neither party retains a lawyer. In these "no lawyer" cases, changes in lawyer behavior are naturally not an explanatory factor either.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250804151315223.png)

In **Table 8**, columns 1 and 3, the results for "no lawyer" cases indicate that changes in lawyer behavior are not the main driver of the reform's effect. In columns 2 and 4, although the results for "no litigant appearance" cases show the effect size halved, this in fact illustrates the complexity and authenticity of the story: the reform's effect is very likely jointly driven by "changes in judge behavior" and "changes in litigant behavior," but even in the extreme case that completely rules out the latter's influence, the effect driven by the former remains robust and constitutes "**half**" of the effect. Therefore, one can say with great confidence that changes in judge behavior are one of the **main, indispensable** contributors.

**Testing the "behavioral change before the trial" (case screening) channel**

The deterrent power of trial broadcasting may cause those **potential plaintiffs with weaker cases and insufficient evidence** (especially women) to give up suing, because they worry about their "weak case" "embarrassing" them in front of the public. This is a more subtle "sample selection bias" (Sample Selection Bias) or "screening effect" (Screening Effect).

If this effect exists, then the average quality of the cases of female plaintiffs who enter the litigation process after the reform would **endogenously improve**. Consequently, the rise in their win rate might simply be because their cases were stronger to begin with, rather than because judges became more impartial toward cases of equivalent quality.

The paper answers this question by testing whether case composition and quality changed:

- **Testing for changes in gender composition**: Aggregating the data to the "court–business-area–quarter" level, and using **the proportion of female plaintiffs** as the dependent variable, the authors regress on "broadcast intensity." If the reform deterred more women than men, then the proportion of female plaintiffs should decline.
- **Testing for changes in average case quality**: First, using all pre-reform case data, the authors train a model based on a series of observable case characteristics (such as case type, scale, etc.) to **predict each case's win rate**. This "predicted win rate" can serve as a proxy variable for the **intrinsic quality** of a case. Then, using this "predicted win rate" as the dependent variable, they repeat the core DID analysis. If the quality of female plaintiffs' cases truly improved after the reform, then the coefficient on the "female plaintiff × broadcast intensity" interaction term should be significantly positive.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250804152017747.png)

In **Table A9**, columns 1 and 2 find that the coefficient on "broadcast intensity" is not significant, indicating that the reform did not significantly change the gender composition of plaintiffs. Columns 3 and 4 show that the coefficient on the interaction term is very close to zero and entirely insignificant—that is, the reform did not significantly raise the average intrinsic quality of the cases of female plaintiffs entering the litigation process.

#### **The Effectiveness and Limitations of the Judicial Openness Reform**

The evidence presented above indicates that, when constrained by an additional monitoring mechanism, judges adjust their **judicial behavior**—that is, they exhibit greater impartiality and invest more effort during deliberation. These findings provide empirical support for the mechanism behind the reform's impact, confirming the link between enhanced public scrutiny and changes in judicial behavior.

To further interpret and contextualize these results, we can draw on a range of **behavioral theories** in the existing literature:

- First, enhancing judicial visibility can heighten **"image concerns"** (image concerns, Ariely, Bracha, and Meier 2009) and motivate judges to behave **more professionally**. When judges know they are being observed by stakeholders, legal professionals, and the public, they may adhere more strictly to courtroom rules, scrutinize claims more carefully, and draft legal documents with greater prudence. This enhanced professionalism may in turn lead to more impartial judgments.
- Second, **when a judge acts on biased preferences, higher judicial visibility is very likely to increase the cost of discrimination**. Parsons et al. (2011) documented this mechanism in baseball games: under camera surveillance, umpires' racial bias was reduced. Likewise, Ferrazares (2023) finds that body-worn cameras, by disciplining officer behavior, can reduce complaints by Black civilians against white officers. These findings suggest that, under camera surveillance, decision-makers—whether judges, umpires, or police officers—are very likely to be more vigilant about their own behavior and to consciously correct their bias toward minority groups.
- In addition, **specific features of China's judicial system** may amplify these effects. The hierarchical structure of China's judiciary includes 12 levels, requiring judges to undergo continuous performance evaluation for career promotion and higher income. The average age of Chinese judges is 39 (compared with 51 for US district judges), which makes frequent performance evaluation an effective incentive tool. As such, the existence of monitoring records that can be used to hold judges accountable provides a powerful incentive for professional and diligent conduct.

Although this paper's results demonstrate the reform's positive effects, courtroom monitoring is no panacea. This paper's findings and prior research suggest that its effectiveness depends on two key conditions: (1) the identity of the discriminated-against group must be **readily observable**; and (2) the discrimination must be **preference-based**. When litigants have identical identities, but one party has an undisclosed connection to the judge that may lead to intentional favoritism, this approach may be ineffective. Therefore, although enhanced transparency can mitigate certain forms of discrimination, it cannot resolve all judicial bias.

## Conclusion

This paper's results provide evidence on how information technology can monitor courtroom proceedings in general, and how enhanced judicial visibility can specifically reduce the gender gap in civil litigation. These insights contribute both to the literature on judicial bias and to the broader research on the relationship between technology and public affairs.

There are two promising directions for further extending this paper's discussion. Crucially, it is important to recognize that this reform may generate hidden social costs that extend beyond the courtroom. For example, Adams, Adams-Prassl, and Adams-Prassl (2022) raise concerns about privacy abuse arising from publishing judicial judgments online in the UK. When litigants' images and videos can be freely and publicly accessed, this privacy concern may be further exacerbated. To address this issue, future reforms should consider **striking a balance between transparency and privacy protection**.

Finally, this paper focuses on the impact of trial broadcasting on judicial outcomes before and after the reform's introduction. However, its **long-term impact** on judicial behavior remains a fascinating area of research. Prolonged exposure to enhanced scrutiny may permanently reshape judicial behavior, fostering lasting professionalism even in the absence of continuous monitoring. These long-term effects warrant further investigation, in order to understand the enduring impact of enhanced judicial transparency.
