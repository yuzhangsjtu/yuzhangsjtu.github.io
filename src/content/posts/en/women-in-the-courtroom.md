> This study systematically investigates the impact of an information-technology-driven **judicial transparency** reform on **gender inequality** in China's civil litigation. Analyzing approximately 6 million judicial judgments, the paper demonstrates the following: First, **before the reform, female plaintiffs had a significantly lower win rate than their male counterparts.** Second, treating a nationwide judicial reform that mandates **the live online streaming of courtroom trials** as a natural experiment and employing difference-in-differences and instrumental variable methods for causal inference, the study finds that **increased trial broadcast intensity effectively narrowed the gender win-rate gap.** Finally, mechanism analysis suggests that the reform works primarily by **altering judge behavior**: under public scrutiny, judges paid greater attention to the claims of female plaintiffs and invested more effort in drafting judgments. This study demonstrates the critical role of technological oversight in enhancing public-sector accountability and promoting judicial equity, offering new micro-level evidence for understanding how transparency improves governance.

## Introduction

#### **Research Background**

**Gender disparity**, a pervasive socio-economic phenomenon, has been extensively studied in fields such as the **labor market** and **political participation**. However, in the domain of **civil litigation**—a crucial arena for upholding social justice—a systematic understanding of gender disparity remains a significant knowledge gap, particularly in developing-country contexts. This paper argues that **gender bias** within the civil justice system can carry substantial economic consequences. For example, it may deter women's entrepreneurial participation by influencing expectations of contract enforcement, thereby leading to the misallocation of talent and resources at the societal level. The existing literature (e.g., Duflo, 2012; Hsieh et al., 2019) has already demonstrated that dismantling gender barriers can generate considerable economic gains, further underscoring the real-world importance of this study.

Despite the significance of the issue, rigorous empirical research faces two major challenges:

- First, systematic **micro-data** from civil litigation, especially in developing economies, are extremely scarce.
- Second, establishing credible **causal identification** strategies is particularly difficult due to uncertainties in institutional implementation, such as the principle of random case assignment.

To address these challenges, this study takes the Chinese context as its analytical background. We leverage the massive repository of judicial judgment data provided by "**China Judgments Online**" (CJO), dating back to 2013, to construct a comprehensive dataset comprising approximately 6 million civil judgments. This unprecedented data resource allows us not only to quantify gender bias on a large scale but also to delve into the internal mechanisms of the judicial decision-making process through textual analysis.

More critically, we exploit a nationwide **judicial openness reform** initiated in China in **2016** as a quasi-experiment. This reform mandated courts to **live-stream courtroom trials online** through a unified platform. Its staggered implementation across different courts provides the exogenous variation needed for our causal inference. Since the primary objective of this reform was to enhance **judicial transparency** rather than directly target gender issues, it significantly mitigates endogeneity concerns about the policy.

#### **Basic Findings**

Our analysis begins with an examination of the pre-reform period (2014Q1–2016Q2). The study reveals that, after controlling for a wide range of variables at the case, region, and judge levels, **female plaintiffs' win rates were approximately 5 percentage points lower than those of male plaintiffs, a statistically significant difference.**

Building on this, we employ a generalized difference-in-differences (DID) model. Using the proportion of cases that were live-streamed at the "court–sub-court–quarter" level as a continuous treatment variable, we evaluate the causal effect of the reform. We find that the increase in trial broadcasting intensity **significantly narrowed the gap** in win rates between male and female plaintiffs. To further ensure the robustness of our conclusions, we adopt a Bartik-like IV strategy to address potential endogeneity issues. Its results are highly consistent with those from the DID analysis.

#### **Literature Contribution**

This study aims to contribute to three strands of literature:

- First, our work provides new insights into gender disparities in civil litigation. Previous research has concentrated primarily on criminal cases and documented a gender gap that **favors** female defendants. For instance, Bindler and Hjalmarsson (2020) found that female defendants in the UK tend to receive more lenient treatment. Similar patterns have been recorded in the US (Sorensen, Sarnikar, and Oaxaca 2012; Starr 2015; and Butcher, Park, and Piehl 2017). However, whether these findings can be generalized to civil cases or developing-country contexts remains unclear. This paper investigates the gender effect of litigants in civil cases between private individuals in China and uncovers a distinct pattern: **female plaintiffs in civil courtrooms face a disadvantage.**
- Second, this study advances the literature on the **role of information technology in public affairs** (Pierce, Snow, and McAfee 2015; Mastrobuoni 2020). Introducing live-feed cameras into courtrooms is analogous to equipping police officers with body-worn cameras (BWCs); both serve as monitoring devices to enhance transparency and accountability. Studies on BWCs have shown their presence can induce behavioral changes in both officers and civilians, but it is challenging to distinguish the effect on the former from that on the latter (Cubukcu et al. 2021; Zamoff, Greenwood, and Burtch 2021; Ferrazares 2023). Our study evaluates the impact of surveillance cameras in courtrooms. Drawing on a large corpus of judicial decision texts, we demonstrate that judges altered their behavior in response to the technology, which in turn influenced judicial outcomes. This finding provides further support for the **disciplinary effect** of recording cameras in public institutions.
- Finally, this paper contributes new evidence to research on the **impact of transparency on public-sector decision-making and governance** (Kosack and Fung 2014). While Hansen, McMahon, and Prat (2018) show how informing the public about monetary policy deliberations affects policymakers, our study demonstrates that real-time monitoring spurs more professional conduct and more thorough deliberations from judges. Whereas the established literature concentrates on transparency enhancements arising from institutional changes, this paper investigates the impact of greater transparency made possible by the adoption of new technology. This constitutes a novel contribution to this field of study.

## Institutional Context

#### **China's Courts and Judges**

China's court system is organized at **four levels**: the Supreme People's Court (SPC), the Higher People's Courts, the Intermediate People's Courts, and the Primary People's Courts. Primary courts handle ordinary cases at the county level, whereas intermediate courts deal with major, complex cases and appeals from primary courts. Higher courts handle appeals from intermediate courts and cases with significant influence within their respective provinces. As of January 2021, the Chinese court system comprised the Supreme People's Court, 33 higher courts (typically one per province), 416 intermediate courts (typically one per prefecture-level city), and 3,087 primary courts (typically one per county). Within this hierarchy, higher-level courts exercise supervision and guidance over the lower courts within their jurisdiction. As the nation's highest judicial organ, the **Supreme People's Court** was the primary driving force behind the judicial reforms discussed in this paper.

Courts are typically organized into specialized divisions known as "courtrooms" (*yewuting*) or sub-courts, each consisting of judges specializing in particular areas. This study focuses on **civil cases**, which are categorized into nine major areas: (1) Personality Rights, (2) Marriage, Family, and Inheritance, (3) Property Rights, (4) Contracts, (5) Intellectual Property, (6) Labor Disputes, (7) Financial, Securities, and Insurance, (8) Tort Liability, and (9) Special Procedures. Cases that do not fit these categories are classified as "other." Specific sub-courts are responsible for subsets of these areas, and their scope of responsibility varies depending on the local population and level of economic development.

Within this judicial system, judges enjoy **considerable authority** during trial proceedings while facing relatively limited **scrutiny** (Peerenboom, 2002). Judges' attitudes and preferences exert a strong influence on court decisions and litigation outcomes. For cases involving **"clear facts and minor disputes,"** a single judge typically adjudicates the case under simplified procedural rules, and their opinion dominates the final result. In **"complex cases"** that involve standard procedural rules and more litigants, an adjudication panel composed of 3, 5, or 7 judges, known as a *heyiting*, is convened. Even there, however, the influence of the **Presiding Judge** remains dominant in shaping the litigation outcome.

#### **Judicial Reforms**

The substantial discretionary power granted to judges, coupled with limited external oversight, essentially turned the judicial decision-making process into a **"black box."** Addressing these problems, the Supreme People's Court in China launched a series of initiatives aimed at enhancing judicial system transparency beginning around 2014. As part of the reform, four major information disclosure platforms were established to enhance the transparency of judicial procedures. These platforms are specifically designed for publishing **legal instruments**, conducting **public trial broadcasts**, providing **information on litigation procedures**, and disclosing the identities of **untrustworthy executees.** 

The focus of this paper is the impact of the judicial openness reform, especially **live online trial streaming.** The Supreme People's Court mandated that courts at all levels broadcast their trials live on the "China Open Court Trials Online" platform, with the long-term goal set on the live broadcast of all trials (with exceptions for specific situations). Officially launched in September 2016, the platform permits the public to watch trials in real time or access video recordings later. By 2021, more than 16 million cases had been broadcast live. The platform has attracted widespread attention from the general public, including citizens, journalists, and legal practitioners.

Although the reform swept across the system in a relatively short time period of about eighteen months, there was considerable variation in exactly *when* individual courts connected to the platform. As of September 2016, 383 courts had been set up, accounting for 10.89 percent of all courts; by January 2017, another 762 courts had joined; and finally by December 2017, all 3,517 courts had become connected. Beyond the difference in access timing, substantial variation in the *proportion* of cases broadcast live also exists across courts.

The Supreme People’s Court established ambitious targets for this reform, demonstrating a strong determination to modernize the judicial system. However, these ambitions were **not necessarily** met with equal enthusiasm or accompanied by the technical capacity and financial means necessary for implementation among lower-tier courts, though their cooperation is critical to ground-level implementation. The response to this challenge was characteristically Chinese: higher-level courts quantified the expected level of progress and incorporated it into the **performance evaluation** system on lower-level courts. Although practices varied locally, it is common that intermediate courts would set a quota on its subordinate basic courts’ trial broadcasting performance, incorporating it into their evaluation scheme.

Local court presidents establish live-streaming quotas based on the requirements set by higher courts, and they then allocate assignments across the six *fating*[courtrooms]. Following this, the *tingzhang* of the sub-courts select specific cases for broadcasting and present the plans to the presidents for approval. In principle, presidents review plans and make the final decision; in practice, authorization is typically automatic.
