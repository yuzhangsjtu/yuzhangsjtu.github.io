> It is generally held that the fertility transition and modern economic development are two sides of the same coin, because industrialization lowers fertility by raising the opportunity cost of raising children. Yet in the 19th and 20th centuries, France's level of economic development lagged behind Britain's, but France began its fertility decline ahead of Britain! Scholars of the Princeton European Fertility Project discovered in the last century that changes in European fertility behavior diffused outward from **France**, spreading mainly along cultural and linguistic lines.
>
> In this study, the authors construct a **linguistic distance index** among European regions and confirm that the behavior of fertility decline diffused from Francophone areas to the rest of Europe. Societies culturally close to France faced lower barriers in acquiring new information and in adopting new attitudes toward fertility control.

## Preface

### **The European Fertility Transition**

The figure below shows fertility changes in France, Britain (England and Wales), and Germany in the 19th and 20th centuries (the image is taken from the appendix of the earlier RES paper). It can be seen that after 1877 all three countries experienced a clear decline in fertility, but at the same fertility level (for example, 25 live births per 1,000 population), France preceded Britain by half a century, while Germany lagged behind Britain by about 30 years.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240510105654433.png)

### **The Princeton European Fertility Project**

The Princeton European Fertility Project—**PEFP**—was a large-scale, interdisciplinary research effort carried out by the Office of Population Research at Princeton University during the 1960s and 1970s; it charted in detail the dynamics of fertility across 19th- and 20th-century Europe. PEFP scholars proposed two modes of fertility control:

- In pre-modern times, fertility control was achieved mainly through forms such as delaying marriage and remaining single, affecting the possibility of having children but not how many children an individual bore;
- Modern fertility control centers on parity-specific limitation, defined as the use of restraint to avoid additional births after the desired number of children has been born. That is, couples should "marry, and stop bearing children once they reach the ideal number." It is regarded as a **modern innovation** that became widespread among European populations only in the last two centuries.

PEFP scholars specifically argued: **Changes in fertility behavior diffused outward from France, spreading mainly along cultural and linguistic lines.**

### **Two Perspectives on Fertility Transition Research**

- Economics: changes in fertility incentives arising from urbanization or from improvements in health and human capital; fertility decline and modern economic development are two sides of the same coin, the mechanism being that industrialization lowers fertility by raising the opportunity cost of raising children;
- Demography, sociology, anthropology, and so on: interpret fertility change in terms of new attitudes toward fertility and the diffusion of cultural norms.

PEFP scholars noted that the European fertility decline cannot be explained as the direct result of rising per-capita income and industrialization. In 19th-century Europe, regions with similar levels of human capital made the transition to lower fertility at different times, and the earlier transitions were linked to linguistic proximity to France. One **fact** is that French-speaking families in Belgium lowered fertility to modern levels decades earlier than did Dutch-speaking families with comparable income and education.

This study reconciles the economic forces of the fertility transition with cultural channels, arguing that the transition from high to low fertility is the result of innovation and social influence. Higher costs or lower benefits of childbearing are necessary but insufficient to reduce actual fertility; a change in the traditional beliefs and norms surrounding marriage and childbearing is also required. Only when traditional attitudes are abandoned will people change their behavior. At first, only societies close to the **cultural innovator** experience fertility decline. Over time, the innovation spreads to more distant societies.

## Why Did France Lead the Fertility Transition?

Why did the romantic French start having fewer children so early? This is a question, and it is discussed in the original paper, but there is no definitive answer. The main views are as follows:

- Secularization and the development of modern norms: During the Enlightenment (or even earlier), secular modern norms and values spread among French elites and other groups, and the French Revolution accelerated this process. This may have weakened traditional fertility ideas;
- Political and institutional change: The French Revolution affected the traditional power structure (especially the Church), creating conditions for changes in norms and behavior;
- Shifts in moral standards: Some contemporary observers attributed the fertility decline to a change in moral standards. That is, people became more concerned with their own interests, unwilling to bear the high cost of raising children, and no longer felt that childbearing was a religious or civic duty;
  - Blanc (2019) found that the traditional religious piety of French départements in 1791 (measured by the proportion of clergy refusing the revolutionary government's religious authority) had a major effect on fertility a generation later (1831);
  - The number of subscriptions to Diderot's *Encyclopédie* (an important source of secular philosophy and scientific knowledge) is negatively correlated with département-level fertility in 1831;

- The spread of contraceptive knowledge: As traditional social norms weakened, knowledge about fertility control and contraceptive methods may have circulated more widely in France. It should be noted, however, that the 19th-century fertility decline took place before the spread of modern contraceptive methods and relied mainly on primitive natural methods long known to people (abstinence, withdrawal—**none of those worldly desires** 🙏).
- A dreamlike crossover: Besant and Bradlaugh (the BB of the BB trial) defended their pamphlet on contraception and family planning by invoking the doctrines and practices that arose in France from the weakening of traditional religious faith and the emergence of secular attitudes. See: [RES—A Court Case That Triggered a Fertility Decline](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/yGkpuCaWb10LeoTvzBkMPg).

## Linguistic Distance, Fertility Transition, and Modernity

- Why linguistic distance matters:
  - In societies linguistically closer to the innovator, individuals face lower barriers in learning new information, behaviors, and attitudes;
  - The effect of linguistic distance on the spread of the demographic transition is an important example of how cultural relatedness influences the cross-societal diffusion of innovation. On average, individuals who are linguistically close to one another are also more closely related, and therefore tend to share intergenerationally transmitted traits, which makes them more likely to interact and learn from one another.

- The fertility transition is also an aspect of modernity:
  - Britain's Industrial Revolution fostered modernity, but modernity has more than one facet;
  - Behind the fertility transition lie new cultural norms and a shift in secular beliefs—this too is a key feature of modernization!
  - Modernity at the industrial level diffused according to cultural distance from Britain, while modernity at the fertility level diffused according to cultural distance from France.

The figure below, drawn by PEFP scholars, shows the time (year) at which a region's fertility level had fallen 10% relative to "an unrestricted, natural fertility level"; it can be seen that the Francophone areas are the most advanced regions.

## Predictions to Be Tested

In the paper the authors build a model of fertility choice to depict the relationship between fertility choice and socio-cultural norms, and put forward the following three predictions:

- **Prediction 1**: Societies that are closer to the innovator in culture, language, and other respects are more easily influenced by the innovator and adopt the new fertility behavior earlier. Here the innovator refers to France (the Francophone area of France);
- **Prediction 2**: The negative correlation between a society's fertility transition status and its distance from the innovator is U-shaped over time. In the early stage when the new fertility behavior begins to spread, this negative correlation is weak; as time passes, the negative correlation strengthens; but in the final stage of the transition, the negative correlation declines again;
- **Prediction 3**: In the early stage of the diffusion of fertility decline, there is a strong positive correlation between the fertility level and distance from the innovator, but as more and more societies adopt the modern social norm over time, this relationship weakens.

## Identification Strategy

### Measurement of Key Variables

**Index of marital fertility $I_g$** :
$$
I_{gi} = \frac {B^m_i}{\sum_{j=1}^NM_{ij}G_j}
$$
It means the total number of children born to married women divided by the maximum imaginable number of children (data on the Hutterites, a denomination that practices no form of fertility limitation).

**Fertility transition date (FTD) and transition status**:

FTD—fertility transition date, defined as the year in which $I_g$ falls by 10%. In the figure above, the marital fertility index of many French regions had already fallen by 10% in 1830 or even earlier;

Tt—transition status, which equals 0 before a region's fertility transition (FTD) occurs and 1 thereafter.

**Measuring social distance**:

- Measured primarily through **linguistic distance**;
- Linguistic classification: the researchers obtained classification information for each language from the Ethnologue website. Languages are grouped into families, branches, sub-groups, and so on, forming a hierarchical classification system;
- Node-based computation: find the most recent common ancestor of two languages in the language classification tree, then count how many nodes one must pass through to get from one language to the other. The larger the number of nodes, the greater the difference between the two languages, and the larger the linguistic distance.

### Regression Equations

Testing **Prediction 1**: fertility transition date and socio-cultural distance,

$$
FTD_{jc} = \delta_1LD^f_{jc} + X'_{jc}\delta_2 + \alpha_c + \epsilon_{jc}
$$
where $FTD_{jc}$ is the fertility transition date of region J in country C, and $LD^f_{jc}$ is the social distance from region J to France.

Testing **Prediction 2**: the U-shaped effect of social distance from France on the fertility transition,
$$
T_{jct} = \gamma_0 + \gamma_1LD^f_{jc} + X'_{jc}\gamma_2 + \epsilon_{ijt}
$$
where $T_{jct}$ is the binary indicator of a region's fertility transition status.

Testing **Prediction 3**: the influence of France weakens over time,
$$
Ig_{jc\tau} = \eta_1LD^f_{jc} + X'_{jc}\eta_2 + \alpha_c + \epsilon_{jc\tau}
$$
The variable definitions are the same as described above.

### Testing the Predictions

- Linguistic distance relative to France is positively correlated with the date the fertility transition began: that is, the larger the linguistic distance, the later a region's fertility transition began; ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240510112137847.png)

- The article also compares the effect of social distance from Britain (English distance) for other regions, and finds that the coefficient on English distance is small and often insignificant;
- It also compares distances between various other languages, and most results are insignificant. For all other languages, their cultural distance from French is significant for FTD, with a large coefficient; ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240510112507087.png)

- As for transition status, the diffusion of fertility decline resembles a logistic distribution, inconsistent with the linear pattern of inertial diffusion, the accelerate-then-decelerate pattern of contagion, and the linear pattern of social influence; rather, it resembles a process of **social learning**. Prediction 2 holds that the effect of linguistic distance on fertility transition status (that is, whether a region has already begun its fertility transition) is small at the start of the fertility transition, increases over time, but begins to decrease again in the late stage of the fertility transition. The results in the figure below are consistent with the description in Prediction 2; ![image-20240510112938172](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240510112938172.png)

- Prediction 3 concerns how the relationship between fertility and social distance from the cultural innovator (France in this paper) changes over time. Specifically, this prediction holds that in the early stage of the fertility transition there is a strong positive correlation between fertility and social distance from the cultural innovator (measured by linguistic distance), but that over time this relationship gradually weakens and eventually disappears. The figure below shows the time path of the effect of linguistic distance from French on fertility. It can be seen that this effect peaks between 1871 and 1900, then gradually declines, falling to a very low level by the middle of the 20th century. ![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240510113438183.png)

## Conclusion

- This paper bridges the gap between population-transition analyses that emphasize cultural mechanisms and those that focus on economic incentives;
- The transition from higher traditional fertility to lower modern fertility is the result of a process of social innovation and social influence, in which the adoption of new behaviors and norms depends on the social distance between early adopters and later adopters;
- Social distance from the innovator (France) is positively correlated with the timing of the fertility transition across different populations and regions, and positively correlated with the level of marital fertility across different periods. Moreover, the diffusion process of the fertility transition matches the model's predictions: the effect of linguistic distance from French on fertility is high in the early and peak stages of the transition, but weakens as more and more regions adopt modern behaviors;
- The diffusion of fertility decline and the spread of industrialization follow different patterns, because societies at different distances from their respective innovators (France and Britain) face different barriers to imitation and adoption, while societies culturally and linguistically closer to the innovator face lower barriers.
