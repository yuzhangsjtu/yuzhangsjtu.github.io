It is generally held that the fertility transition and modern economic development are two sides of the same coin, since industrialization lowers fertility by raising the opportunity cost of raising children. Yet during the 19th and 20th centuries, France’s level of economic development lagged behind Britain’s, but France began its fertility decline *ahead* of Britain! Scholars of the Princeton European Fertility Project discovered in the last century that changes in European fertility behavior **diffused outwards from France**, spreading mainly along cultural and linguistic lines.

In this study, the authors construct a **linguistic distance index** among European regions and confirm that the behavior of fertility decline diffused from Francophone areas to the rest of Europe. Societies culturally close to France faced lower barriers in acquiring new information and in adopting new attitudes toward fertility control.

## Preface

### **The European Fertility Transition**

The figure below shows fertility changes in France, Britain (England and Wales), and Germany during the 19th and 20th centuries (taken from the appendix of the earlier RES paper). It can be seen that after 1877 all three countries experienced a clear decline in fertility, but at the same fertility level (e.g., 25 live births per 1,000 population), France preceded Britain by half a century, while Germany lagged behind Britain by about 30 years.

### **The Princeton European Fertility Project**

The Princeton European Fertility Project–**PEFP** was a large‑scale, interdisciplinary research effort carried out by the Office of Population Research at Princeton University during the 1960s and 1970s; it charted in detail the dynamics of fertility across 19th‑ and 20th‑century Europe. PEFP scholars identified two modes of fertility control:

- In pre‑modern times, fertility control operated mainly through delayed marriage and celibacy, influencing the *possibility* of having children but not the number of children an individual bore;
- Modern fertility control centers on parity‑specific limitation, defined as ceasing childbearing after the desired number of children is reached so that additional births are avoided. That is, couples should “marry, then stop bearing children once the ideal family size is achieved.” This was regarded as a **modern innovation** that spread through European populations only in the last two centuries.

A particular claim by the PEFP scholars was this: **Changes in fertility behavior diffused outwards from France, spreading principally along cultural and linguistic lines.**

### **Two Perspectives on Fertility Transition Research**

- Economics: changes in fertility incentives that arise from urbanization or improvements in health and human capital; fertility decline and modern economic development are two sides of the same coin, with the mechanism being that industrialization reduces fertility by raising the opportunity cost of child‑rearing;
- Demography, sociology, anthropology, etc.: interpret fertility change in terms of new attitudes toward fertility and the diffusion of cultural norms.

PEFP scholars noted that the European fertility decline cannot be explained simply as the direct result of rising per‑capita income and industrialization. In 19th‑century Europe, regions with similar levels of human capital made the transition to lower fertility at different times, and the earliest transitions were linked to linguistic proximity to France. One **stylized fact** is this: French‑speaking families in Belgium lowered fertility to modern levels decades earlier than did Dutch‑speaking families with comparable income and education.

This study reconciles the economic forces of the fertility transition with cultural channels, arguing that the transition from high to low fertility results from innovation and social influence. Higher costs or lower benefits of childbearing are necessary but insufficient to reduce actual fertility; a change in the beliefs and norms that uphold traditional marital fertility is also required. Only when traditional attitudes are abandoned will people alter their behavior. In the beginning, only societies that are close to the **cultural innovator** experience fertility decline. Over time, the innovation spreads to more distant societies.

## Why Did France Lead the Fertility Transition?

Why did the romantic French start having fewer children so early? This is a puzzle. The paper discusses it but offers no definitive answer. The main views are as follows:

- Secularization and the development of modern norms: During the Enlightenment (or even earlier), secular modern norms and values spread among French elites and other groups, and the French Revolution accelerated the process. This likely weakened traditional pro‑natalist ideas;
- Political and institutional change: The French Revolution affected the traditional power structure (especially the Church), creating conditions for changes in norms and behavior;
- Shifts in moral standards: Some contemporary observers attributed the fertility decline to a change in moral standards. People became more concerned with their own interests, unwilling to bear the high cost of raising children, and no longer felt that fertility was a religious or civic duty;
  - Blanc (2019) found that traditional religious piety at the *département* level in France in 1791 (measured by the proportion of clergy refusing the revolutionary government’s religious authority) strongly influenced fertility a generation later (1831);
  - The number of subscriptions to Diderot’s *Encyclopédie* (an important vehicle of secular philosophy and scientific knowledge) is negatively correlated with *département*‑level fertility in 1831;

- The spread of knowledge about contraception: As traditional social norms weakened, knowledge about fertility control and contraceptive methods may have circulated more widely in France. Note, however, that the 19th‑century fertility decline took place *before* the spread of modern contraceptive technology and relied mainly on primitive, natural methods long known (abstinence, withdrawal, **freedom from those worldly desires** 🙏).
- A fascinating connection: Besant and Bradlaugh (the “BB” of the Bradlaugh‑Besant trial) defended their pamphlet on contraception and family planning by invoking doctrines and practices that had emerged in France from the weakening of traditional religious faith and the rise of secular attitudes. See: [RES‑A Court Case That Triggered the Fertility Decline](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/yGkpuCaWb10LeoTvzBkMPg).

## Linguistic Distance, Fertility Transition, and Modernity

- Why linguistic distance matters:
  - In societies linguistically closer to the innovator, individuals face lower barriers in learning new information, behaviors, and attitudes;
  - The effect of linguistic distance on the spread of the demographic transition is a key example of how cultural proximity influences the cross‑societal diffusion of innovations. On average, individuals who are linguistically close to one another are also more closely related in other dimensions and therefore tend to share intergenerationally transmitted traits that make interaction and mutual learning more likely.

- The fertility transition as an aspect of modernity:
  - Britain’s Industrial Revolution fostered modernity, but modernity has more than one facet;
  - Underlying the fertility transition is a shift toward new cultural norms and secular beliefs — a defining feature of modernization as well!
  - Modernity at the industrial level diffused along distances from Britain; modernity at the fertility level diffused along distances from France.

The figure below, drawn by PEFP scholars, shows the year in which a region’s fertility level had declined 10% relative to an “unrestricted, natural fertility” level; Francophone territories are evidently the earliest movers.

## Testable Predictions

The authors build a model of fertility choice that links fertility decisions to socio‑cultural norms and generate the following three predictions:

- **Prediction 1**: Societies that are culturally and linguistically closer to the innovator are more strongly influenced by the innovator and adopt the new fertility behavior earlier. In this context the innovator is France (specifically, French‑speaking French areas);
- **Prediction 2**: The negative association between a society’s fertility transition status and its distance from the innovator is U‑shaped over time. In the early stage of diffusion, when the new fertility behavior begins to spread, this negative association is weak; it strengthens as time passes; at the final stage of the transition, the negative association declines once more;
- **Prediction 3**: In the early stages of diffusion, there is a strong positive correlation between the fertility *level* and distance from the innovator, but this relationship weakens as, over time, more and more societies adopt the modern social norm.

## Identification Strategy

### Measurement of Key Variables

**Index of marital fertility $I_g$** :
$$
I_{gi} = \frac {B^m_i}{\sum_{j=1}^NM_{ij}G_j}
$$
Intuitively, it represents the number of children actually born to married women divided by the maximum imaginable number of children (using data on Hutterites, a denomination that is assumed to practice no form of deliberate parity‑specific control).

**Fertility transition date and transition status**:

FTD–fertility transition date, is defined as the year by which $I_g$ had fallen by 10%. In the figure above, marital‑fertility indices in many French regions had already declined 10% by 1830 or even earlier.

Tt–transition status, a binary indicator that equals 0 before a region’s fertility transition date and 1 thereafter.

**Measuring social distance**:

- Measured primarily through **linguistic distance**;
- Linguistic classification: the researchers obtained information on language family classification from the Ethnologue website. Languages are grouped into families, branches, sub‑groups, etc., forming a hierarchical tree;
- Node‑based computation: find the most recent common ancestor of two languages in the language tree and then count the number of nodes one must traverse from one language to the other. The larger the number of nodes, the greater the difference between the two languages, hence the larger the linguistic distance targeted by the paper.

### Estimating Equations

Testing **Prediction 1**: Fertility transition date and socio‑cultural distance,

$$
FTD_{jc} = \delta_1LD^f_{jc} + X'_{jc}\delta_2 + \alpha_c + \epsilon_{jc}
$$
where $FTD_{jc}$ is the fertility transition date for region $j$ in country $C$, and $LD^f_{jc}$ is the social (linguistic) distance from region $j$ to France.

Testing **Prediction 2**: The non‑monotonic (U‑shaped) temporal pattern of the effect of distance from France on the fertility transition,
$$
T_{jct} = \gamma_0 + \gamma_1LD^f_{jc} + X'_{jc}\gamma_2 + \epsilon_{ijt}
$$
where $T_{jct}$ is the binary indicator of a region’s fertility transition status.

Testing **Prediction 3**: The effect of distance from France weakens over time,
$$
Ig_{jc\tau} = \eta_1LD^f_{jc} + X'_{jc}\eta_2 + \alpha_c + \epsilon_{jc\tau}
$$
Variable definitions follow the earlier description.
