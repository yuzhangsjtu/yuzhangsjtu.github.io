> In the 1980s, China's sex ratio at birth became imbalanced. Yet at the time the cost of **sex selection** was very high and the total fertility rate fluctuated little. What caused this phenomenon?
>
> This study, published in JPE, argues that China's rural **Household Responsibility System** reform of 1978-1984 triggered the sex-ratio-at-birth imbalance of that period, with effects concentrated in multi-child families whose firstborn was an older sister. Overall, the Household Responsibility System reform can account for the "disappearance" of about one million girls. In their empirical analysis the authors rule out the influence of the One-Child Policy, which was implemented over the same period.

**Title:** Land Reform and Sex Selection in China

**Journal:** [Journal of Political Economy](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/toc/jpe/current)

### Preface

- **Missing Women**: The biological standard for the human sex ratio at birth is roughly 1.05 (male births to female births), but the sex ratio at birth in many Asian and Muslim countries is significantly higher than this value, and at the level of total national population, males also outnumber females. This indicates that many women have "disappeared" because of factors of **human selection**. This phenomenon was raised by Nobel laureate in economics Amartya Sen and is called "missing women"; some research shows that "missing women" worldwide number more than 100 million.
- China's sex ratio at birth rose from 1.06 in 1978 to 1.20 in 2000. Data from the 2020 "Seventh National Census" show that nationwide the sex ratio at birth was 1.13 for the first child, 1.07 for the second child, and 1.33 for the third child.
- Existing research shows that imbalance in the sex ratio at birth is related to a reduction in the cost of sex selection (such as using ultrasound technology to determine fetal sex). China's sex ratio at birth began to rise around 1980, when the cost of sex selection was very high. What factors caused the imbalance in China's sex ratio at birth at that time?

![img](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/fg1_online.jpeg)

(Some research also holds that the decline in fertility caused by economic development is related to imbalance in the sex ratio at birth, yet the data show that China's total fertility rate was relatively stable around 1980.)

### Core Hypothesis

- In this study, the authors propose that China's rural **Household Responsibility System** reform of 1978-1984 (referred to as "land reform") triggered the imbalance in the sex ratio at birth that began at that time; the rural population then accounted for 86% of the total.
- Confirming this hypothesis requires above all ruling out the influence of the **family planning** policy that began over the same period (the family planning of that time can be regarded as a "one-child policy," the One Child Policy, **OCP**).
- **Mechanism**:
  - The Household Responsibility System affects family sex selection mainly through an **income mechanism**;
  - How was sex selection at birth accomplished? — **By taking the train to the provincial capital for a B-ultrasound scan.** Ultrasound technology was introduced into provincial capital cities in the 1970s. Combining data on the diffusion of ultrasound machines to provincial capitals with the 1980 railway network data, the authors find that when a county was connected to a provincial capital by railway (and the provincial capital had an ultrasound machine), families were more likely to carry out sex selection at birth.

### Policy Background

#### **Household Responsibility System**

- After 1978, the Chinese government launched a number of top-down reforms to raise productivity, including raising the procurement prices of major crops, reducing grain procurement quotas, and opening up inter-regional trade; these policies were considered too radical by the leaders of the time.
- At the end of 1978, 18 farmers in Xiaogang Village, Fengyang County, Anhui Province launched a grassroots action of dividing the land among households and achieved a substantial increase in grain output. Subsequently the state's stance gradually softened; in January 1982, the No. 1 Central Document confirmed that both contracting output to households (包产到户) and contracting work to households (包干到户) were production responsibility systems of the socialist collective economy.
- The Household Responsibility System broke with the path of agricultural collectivization; it granted land-use rights to individual households on a long-term basis, significantly promoting agricultural growth.

#### **The One-Child Policy in Rural Areas**

- The One-Child Policy began to be implemented in 1979, and violating it incurred economic penalties; urban areas always strictly enforced the One-Child Policy, but in rural areas, having two children was permitted **before 1984**. From 1979 to 1983, rural families were penalized only if they had three or more children. Later, the One-Child Policy in rural areas was gradually tightened.
- From 1979 to 1987, China's total fertility rate was relatively stable.

### Data

#### **The Rollout of the Household Responsibility System Reform and the One-Child Policy**

- Information on the county-level rollout of the two policies comes from local **county annals** (县志). The authors compared the county-annal data with yearbook data and tested them using **Benford's Law**, concluding that the county-annal records are fairly reliable.
- Data were collected for 914 counties in total, accounting for half of the agricultural counties at the time.
- The year a county began to implement the Household Responsibility System is determined by the year an individual village in that county began to contract output to households.
- The timing of the One-Child Policy's implementation at the county level is determined by the year the county government issued a policy document imposing penalties on families that exceeded the stipulated limit by having a third child.

> **Benford's Law**: Also known as the first-digit law, it is an observation about the distribution of the leading nonzero digit across a variety of natural, social, and scientific datasets. It states that in many naturally occurring datasets, the probability of a smaller digit appearing in the leading position is far greater than that of a larger digit. Specifically, Benford's Law indicates that the probability of the digit 1 as the leading digit is about 30.1%, that of the digit 2 about 17.6%, and so on, while that of the digit 9 as the leading digit is about 4.6%. This law was first discovered and analytically explained by the physicist Frank Benford in 1938, after which it has been widely applied in fields such as detecting whether accounting data have been tampered with and distinguishing natural data from artificial data. The mathematical principle behind it is the law of **logarithmic distribution**. Although simple, Benford's Law is considered one of the most magical applications in mathematics.

#### **The Diffusion of Ultrasound Technology in Provincial Capital Cities**

- Obtained from the study of Chen et al. (2013), also drawn from county annals (this paper is also research by Prof. Hongbin Li hh).
- By 1982, only 4% of agricultural counties had an ultrasound machine, but by that time the rural Household Responsibility System reform was nearly complete.
- In 1965, Xi'an, Shaanxi Province had the first ultrasound machine; other provincial capital cities began to have ultrasound machines in the latter half of the 1970s.

#### **Sex-Ratio-at-Birth Data**

- Derived from the micro-data of the 1990 census, covering the 914 agricultural counties mentioned above; one advantage of using the 1990 census data is that it mitigates the influence of population mobility (at the time population mobility was strictly restricted, so cases in which the place of residence at the census differed from the place of birth were rare; the authors also excluded the samples of families that had migrated).
- The focus is on individuals born from 1974 to 1986, who were between 4 and 16 years old in 1990.
- The sample includes the second child of all families with at least two children. In this study, 92% of women aged 35 and above had at least two children.
- The relationship between children and the household head is used to determine the children's **sex**; the year and month of birth are used to determine **birth-order** ranking.
- Families with multiple births were not examined.

- The proportion of firstborn boys stayed stable at around 0.51 before and after land reform, with no change; if the firstborn was a boy, the proportion of second-born boys also did not change, at about 0.5; if the firstborn was a **girl**, then the proportion of second-born boys rose from 0.53 before land reform to 0.56 afterward.

- **Sex ratio at birth before and after land reform**: likewise, the horizontal axis in the figure below is the relative year. Panel A below is the sex ratio of the firstborn; one can see that before and after land reform this value stayed stable near the biological standard of 1.05, indicating no sex selection among firstborns; Panel B below shows the change in the sex ratio of second children before and after land reform: if the firstborn was a boy, the sex ratio of the second child shows no obvious trend before and after land reform; but if the firstborn was a girl, the sex ratio of the second child increased significantly and continuously after land reform, rising from 1.1 to 1.3.

![img](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/fg4_online.jpeg)

#### **Econometric Model**

To assess how the slope of the sex ratio of the second child changes after land reform when the firstborn is a girl, the following **trend break model** is first estimated:
$$
y_{ijt} = \alpha + \beta_1E_{jt}\times \text{GirlFirst}_{ijt} + \beta_2\text{Reform}_{jt}\times E_{jt}\times \text{GirlFirst}_{ijt} + \beta_{3}\text{GirlFirst}_{ijt} + \gamma_{jt} + \epsilon_{ijt} \quad(1)
$$
where:

- $y_{ijt}$ is a dummy variable, with $i$ for family, $j$ for county of birth, and $t$ for year of birth; if the second child of family $i$ is male, then $y_{ijt} = 1$;
- $E_{jt}$ is event time, defined as the year of birth minus the year of reform;
- $\text{GirlFirst}_{ijt}$ is a dummy variable that equals 1 when the firstborn is a girl;
- $\text{Reform}_{jt}$ is a dummy variable that equals 1 if the individual was born after land reform;
- $\beta_2$ measures the average change in the slope of the sex ratio of the second child (when the firstborn is a girl) after land reform; the increase in the proportion of boys $k$ years after the reform is $\beta_2 \times k$.

Equation (2) estimates the average treatment effect:
$$
y_{ijt} = \alpha + \beta_4\text{Reform}_{jt}\times \text{GirlFirst}_{ijt} + \beta_5\text{GirlFirst}_{ijt} + \gamma_{jt} + \epsilon_{ijt} \quad(2)
$$
where $\beta_4$ measures the average increase, after land reform, in the sex ratio of the second child of families whose firstborn was a girl, compared with families whose firstborn was a boy.

### Results

#### **Land Reform and the Sex Ratio**

- The first column of Table 1 shows that the effect of land reform on the sex ratio of the firstborn is statistically insignificant;
- After the implementation of the Household Responsibility System, the sex ratio of the second child in families whose firstborn was a girl increased by 0.7 percentage points each year, and the average treatment effect of land reform over the study period was 3 percentage points; this effect was larger in families where the mother had a higher level of education (had completed primary school).

![image-20240311095831224](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240311095831224.png)

- The paper also estimates the effect of land reform on the overall sex ratio, which was 3.8%. From 1978 to 1986, the sex ratio rose from 1.06 to 1.13, an increase of 6.6%, so land reform contributed about 58% of the rise in the rural sex ratio during this period.

#### **Comparison with the Effect of the One-Child Policy**

- The result in the first column shows that if a person was born after the One-Child Policy, the effect of the One-Child Policy on the sex ratio is insignificant (no trend break);
- Whether or not the One-Child Policy was implemented, the average treatment effect of land reform on the sex ratio of the second child is consistently and significantly positive;
- During the study period, whether or not land reform was carried out, the effect of the One-Child Policy on the sex ratio of the second child is insignificant.

![image-20240311101705361](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240311101705361.png)

#### **Land Reform and Fertility**

- The Household Responsibility System reform had insignificant effects on both the total birth rate and the second-child birth rate;
- The One-Child Policy lowered the total birth rate by 3% during the study period;
- The result in the third column shows that if a family's first child was a girl, then under the One-Child Policy its odds of having a second child increased by 17.3%.

![image-20240311102751658](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240311102751658.png)

### How Land Reform Increased Sex Selection

- The coefficients on the interaction terms in the table below indicate that if the provincial capital had an ultrasound machine, then the effect of land reform on the sex ratio of the second child (when the firstborn was a girl) was positive, and in this case if the county was connected to the provincial capital by railway, the effect was larger;

![image-20240311111101225](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240311111101225.png)

- The authors also conducted further analysis using data from a 1992 UNICEF survey on postnatal child mortality in China; the results show that if the firstborn was a daughter, then the mortality of second-born boys declined while the mortality of second-born girls rose. This indicates that **parents seem to have given more of the dividends of land reform to boys**, thereby increasing the sex ratio of surviving second children.

### Discussion

- In the paper, the authors test the possible mechanisms through which land reform affected sex selection, and the results show that the **income mechanism** is the most important. Land reform affected the sex selection of the second child in families whose firstborn was a girl, and sex selection was more likely in highly educated families and in counties where grain output grew faster after land reform. The authors also conducted a **rough** test of possible mechanisms such as: (a) boys having higher productivity, (b) sons being likely to be allotted more land, (c) raising sons for old-age support, (d) land reform reducing villages' capacity to provide health services and thereby affecting girls, (e) land reform weakening management authority at the village level so that the supervision and enforcement of the ban on sex selection was impaired, and (f) land reform loosening restrictions on population mobility so that farmers could more easily travel to provincial capitals for ultrasound examinations; they found no evidence supporting these mechanisms. (Strictly ruling out these mechanisms would require more data and more rigorous analysis.)
- From the late 1970s to the mid-1980s, the Household Responsibility System reform caused about one million girls to "disappear."

### Extra
