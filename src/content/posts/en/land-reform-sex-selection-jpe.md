>  In the 1980s, China experienced an imbalance in the sex ratio at birth. However, at that time, the cost of **sex selection** was high, and the total fertility rate fluctuated little. What caused this phenomenon?
>
> This JPE study finds that China’s rural **Household Responsibility System (HRS)** reform from 1978–1984 triggered the sex ratio imbalance at birth during that period, an effect concentrated in multi‑child families where the first‑born was a daughter. Overall, the HRS reform explains the “disappearance” of roughly one million girls. In their empirical work, the authors rule out the impact of the concurrent One‑Child Policy.

**Title:** Land Reform and Sex Selection in China  

**Journal:** [Journal of Political Economy](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/toc/jpe/current)

### Preface  

- **Missing Women**: The biologically standard sex ratio at birth for humans is roughly 1.05 (male to female births), yet many Asian and Muslim countries exhibit significantly higher sex ratios at birth, and overall national populations also show more males than females. This suggests that numerous women have “disappeared” due to **deliberate human selection**. Nobel laureate Amartya Sen proposed this concept and labeled it “missing women”； some research estimates there are over 100 million “missing women” worldwide.  
- Between 1978 and 2000, China’s sex ratio at birth rose from 1.06 to 1.20. Data from the 2020 “Seventh National Census” show the sex ratio at birth was 1.13 for first births, 1.07 for second births, and 1.33 for third births.  
- Previous research has linked imbalances in the sex ratio at birth to declines in the cost of sex selection (e.g., using ultrasound technology to determine fetal sex). China’s sex ratio at birth began to rise around 1980, when the cost of sex selection remained very high. What triggered sex‑ratio imbalances at birth in China at that time?

### Core Hypothesis  

- In this paper, the authors hypothesize that the rural **Household Responsibility System (HRS)** reform in China from 1978–1984 (referred to as “land reform”) triggered the emerging sex‑ratio imbalance at birth. At that time, the rural population share was about 86 %.  
- Validating this hypothesis requires carefully ruling out the influence of the simultaneous **Family Planning policy** (the “One‑Child Policy,” or **OCP**).  
- **Mechanisms**：  
  - The HRS chiefly affects family sex selection through an **income mechanism**；  
  - How was prenatal sex selection carried out? — **By taking a train to a provincial capital for a B‑ultrasound scan**. Ultrasound technology was introduced to provincial capitals in the 1970s. Combining data on the spread of ultrasound machines to provincial capitals with the 1980 railway network data, the authors show that when a county is connected to a provincial capital by rail (and that capital had an ultrasound machine), families were more likely to engage in sex selection at birth.

### Policy Background  

#### **Household Responsibility System**  

- After 1978, the Chinese government launched several top‑down reforms to boost productivity, such as raising procurement prices of staple crops, reducing grain procurement quotas, and introducing inter‑regional trade. These policies were considered too radical by some leaders at the time.  
- In late 1978, 18 farmers in Xiaogang Village, Fengyang County, Anhui Province carried out a grassroots initiative of dividing collectively owned land among households and achieved substantial grain output increases. Subsequently, the central government’s stance gradually softened. In January 1982, No. 1 Central Document confirmed that contracting output to individual households and the “all‑round contract” were both forms of the Socialist Collective Economy’s production responsibility system.  
- The HRS transformed the path of agricultural collectivization； it granted long‑term land‑use rights to individual households, markedly promoting agricultural growth.

#### **The One-Child Policy in Rural Areas**  

- The One‑Child Policy began in 1979, with economic penalties for non‑compliance. In urban areas, it was strictly enforced, but in rural regions, **prior to 1984**, having two children was still permitted. Severed penalties for rural families only applied when they had three or more children between 1979–1983. Later, the One‑Child Policy in rural areas was progressively tightened.  
- China’s total fertility rate from 1979–1987 remained comparatively stable.

### Data  

#### **HRS Reform and Diffusion of the One-Child Policy**  

- Data on the county‑level rollout of both policies are derived from local **county annals** (县志). The authors compare annal‑derived data with yearbook‑derived data and employ tests using **Benford’s Law** to argue that the county‑annal records are fairly reliable.  
- They collect data covering 914 counties, roughly half of China’s agricultural counties at the time.  
- The year HRS implementation began in a county is defined as the year an individual village in that county first adopted contracting output to the household.  
- The timing of the One‑Child Policy at the county level is determined by the year the county government promulgated a policy document stipulating penalties for exceeding the stipulated three births.  

> **Benford’s Law**: Also known as the first‑digit law, it describes an observed distribution of the first nonzero digits across many natural, social, and scientific datasets. It states that in many naturally occurring datasets, smaller digits appear as the first digit far more frequently than larger ones. Specifically, Benford’s Law suggests that the probability of digit 1 is about 30.1 %, digit 2 about 17.6 %, with probabilities declining, so that around 4.6 % of leading digits are 9. The law was first discovered and formalized by physicist Frank Benford in 1938. Since then, it has often been used to check for manipulation of accounting data and distinguish natural records from artificial ones. Its mathematical flipside relates to a **logarithmic distribution**. Although simple, Benford’s Law is considered one of the “magic” cases of real‑world mathematics.  

#### **Diffusion of Ultrasound Technology in Provincial Capitals**  

- Obtained from the work of Chen et al. (2013) and also drawn from county annals (this is also a study authored in part by Prof. Hongbin Li).  
- By 1982, only 4 % of agricultural counties owned an ultrasound machine, by which time the rural HRS reform was almost complete.  
- The provincial capital of Xi’an, Shaanxi Province, received its first ultrasound machine in 1965； other provincial capitals began getting them during the latter half of the 1970s.

#### **Sex Ratio at Birth Data**  

- Derived from micro‑data from the 1990 Census, which overlap with the previously mentioned 914 agricultural counties. A key advantage of using the 1990 Census data is its ability to mitigate the impact of migration (migration was still strictly limited at the time； hence census enumeration largely involved people still living in their birthplace—the authors also drop households that experienced migration).  
- The analysis focuses on individuals born between 1974–1986, who were 4 ‑ 16 years old in 1990.  
- The sample comprises the second child of every family with at least two children. Among women aged 35 or above at the time, 92 % had earlier given birth to at least two children.  
- The child’s **sex** is derived from the household‑head‑relation variable； parity **ordering** is constructed using month and year of birth.  
- Households with multiple births are excluded.  
- The fraction of first births that are male remains stably at roughly 0.51 both before and after land reform； if the first‑born is a boy, the fraction of second‑born‑males also remains unchanged around 0.5. If the first‑born is a **girl**, then the fraction of second‑born‑males rises from 0.53 before land reform to 0.56 afterward.

### Identification Strategy  

#### **Event Study**  

- **Per capita grain output around land reform**: the X‑axis is in **relative year** measured against a locality’s adoption of the rural HRS reform (rather than calendar year). Before land reform, per‑capita grain output shows no strong trend， whereas afterwards the trend smoothly increases each year.  

![img](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/fg3.jpeg)  

- **Sex ratio at birth around land reform**: again, X‑axes show time relative to land reform year. In Panel A, for first‑births, the sex ratio pre‑ and post‑land reform is stably centered on the biological norm of 1.05, indicating full absence of selection at the 1st parity. Panel B displays the sex ratio for second births. When the first‑born is male, the second-birth ratio shows little evident trend around land reform； when the first‑born is female, the second‑birth ratio rises systematically from 1.1 to 1.3 after land reform.
