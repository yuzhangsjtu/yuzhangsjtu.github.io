## Globalization

> The various processes that bring geographically dispersed populations closer together, increase their interactions, and turn the entire planet into a community of fate or a global society.

### 1 Origins and History

The idea of building a global human community can be traced back to discussions during the eighteenth-century Enlightenment period about whether a unified "humanity as a whole" was possible. By the nineteenth century such discussions had gradually multiplied, as in Marx's insight into the global expansion of capitalism, Durkheim's judgment that the division of labor would transcend geographic limits, and so on. Yet it was not until 1961 that "globalization" in its modern sense first appeared in a dictionary-type publication, and economists did not begin using the concept frequently until the late 1980s.

A famous landmark in sociology's early research on globalization is Wallerstein's world-systems theory. Wallerstein argued that the capitalist economic system transcends national borders and constitutes a world-system, with relatively wealthy countries at its core, poor countries on its periphery, and semi-peripheral countries in between. The deepening of the globalization debate stemmed from the accelerating development of the globalization process in the 1970s: at that time, multinational corporations were developing rapidly and growing ever more powerful, many people began to worry that the nation-state was heading into decline, transnational trade blocs and regional political-economic entities (such as the EU) were on the rise, the development and convenience of transportation made cross-border tourism and migration increasingly common, and the advent of the internet made instant international communication possible. By the 1990s, the concept of globalization had entered the mainstream discourse of sociology, exerting a profound influence on almost every subfield of the discipline.

> Thirty years later, under the COVID-19 epidemic, the broad trend of globalization has run into setbacks and has even begun to reverse.

### 2 Meaning and Interpretation

Where does globalization come from, and how does it affect us? On these questions opinions are many and no consensus exists. Globalization reminds us that this is a process of change, or a social current, in which all corners of the world become interdependent and ever more closely connected. But this does not mean that a unified global society is bound to arrive. Globalization has multiple dimensions, including the economic, the political, and the cultural. For some, globalization is mainly about the economic dimension, including the globalization of financial transactions, trade, production, and consumption, a global division of labor, and a global financial system. Economic globalization accelerates migration, transportation and habitation change accordingly, and human ways of living become ever more mobile. Others hold that cultural globalization matters more. Robertson (1995) coined a concept, **glocalization**, the fusion of global characteristics with local elements, to capture and grasp how local communities actively modify the globalization process so as to adapt it to their distinctive local cultures. That is to say, the globalization process is a multidirectional flow of cultural products on a world scale. Finally, still others focus on political globalization, emphasizing the establishment and development of regional and international mechanisms of intergovernmental cooperation, such as the United Nations and the European Union. These institutions bring nation-states and international non-governmental organizations together onto a common decision-making platform to coordinate this rapidly developing globalization process.

The acceleration of globalization is generally dated to the 1970s. This process of rapid globalization has had a profound impact on our lives. A decision made in one corner of the world can have an enormous impact on another, far-distant corner, while the nation-state, hitherto the most important protagonist on the world stage, seems to have lost its former glory, its old power and capacity for control gradually fading away.

### 3 Criticism and Discussion

Globalization theorists hold that the globalization process has dramatically changed the way people live; some scholars, however, have reservations, regarding this view as somewhat overstated. Scholars critical of globalization (also called skeptics) argue that although exchanges and connections between countries have indeed increased considerably compared with the past, a unified world market system has not yet emerged. On the contrary, most trade remains confined within certain specific regions, such as the EU, the Asia-Pacific, and North America. These three regional economies operate relatively independently of one another, which is why the skeptics point out that the idea that a global world economy has already emerged is clearly somewhat ahead of its time.

Likewise, the view that globalization has weakened the role of the nation-state has also been questioned and challenged to varying degrees. Reality tells us that national governments still play an indispensable role in managing and coordinating economic activity, signing trade agreements, and formulating policies of economic liberalization. Alliances and cooperation between sovereign states do not necessarily lead to the weakening of the nation-state. Even as global ties between countries grow ever closer, national governments retain a great deal of power and adopt a more proactive and open attitude toward the external environment that rapid globalization has brought about. Globalization, therefore, is not a one-way process moving toward tight integration, but a two-way, interactive current that may lead to a variety of outcomes.

> Seen from the present moment of 2021, the criticisms above may be growing more and more important. For countries with the same cultural background and institutions, globalization may indeed have dissolved borders. But for countries with a different ideology, different institutions, and a different cultural background, once they have benefited from the globalization process, nationalism in these countries rises, and what emerges is a kind of resistance to globalization, or a rewriting of the existing global discourse system.

### 4 Significance and Value

For sociology, globalization is a basic conceptual backdrop, and a great deal of research therefore touches on this theme, for example transnational terrorism, social movements, conflict and war, migration studies, environmental sociology, cultural pluralism, and other related research topics. As research has deepened and developed, the unintended consequences of large-scale globalization have gradually come to light. One example is Renard's (1999) work on the budding and growth of the "fair trade" market. The aim of this mode of trade is to give small-scale producers in developing countries a larger and fairer share of the profits by marketing goods to responsible, ethically minded consumers in developed countries. The globalization process is dominated mainly by large multinational corporations, and for small and micro enterprises, squeezing into this market is extraordinarily difficult. Yet Renard found that economic globalization has actually narrowed this gap: small and micro enterprises are able to find means and channels to participate in global trade and to grow step by step. This is a very interesting study, showing us how globalization (in this case, the fair trade in coffee beans) has opened its doors to small and micro producers and helped them succeed. This is possible on one premise: that some people in developed and developing countries alike hold the basic values of fairness and solidarity and take part together in fair trade activities.

If globalization also has a political dimension, then we should be able to see social movements that transcend local and national boundaries; research shows, however, that such movements seem to be uncommon. The demands of the vast majority of movements are directed at the locality or the country in which they occur, and few are aimed at global goals.

Indicators for assessing globalization vary widely, but a study by Martell (2016) returns to a theme familiar to us: inequality. In this study, he argues that although many sociologists view globalization partly or mainly as a cultural phenomenon, we should pay more attention to the key role played by the capitalist economic system and material interests. Martell directs his criticism at cosmopolitan theories (in political theory, the belief that all people are entitled to equal respect and consideration, no matter what their [citizenship](https://www.britannica.com/topic/citizenship) status or other affiliations happen to be), arguing that these theories are overly optimistic in predicting the emergence and development of a transnational political sphere. Even granting that such a political space does exist, globalization is an uneven process, and the imbalances and the divergences in power and opportunity that already exist in reality will be continually replicated and reproduced in the course of globalization. For example, a global movement of free migration would in the end mean that "the rich elites who least need opportunities for free movement gain the most freedom, while the poor, who crave opportunities for free movement the most, face the most physical restrictions." For Martell, cultural change does matter, but the capitalist economic system remains the most critical driving force.

> This theory is rather like shaking and jolting the loose sand in a bag and pressing it down: the distance between the grains shrinks, and their degrees of freedom decline? The rich are on top, the poor underneath. And globalization is the one applying the force.

> I notice that after all this constant encapsulation, the discourse system grows more and more complex. Will there come a day when it is so complex that it descends into chaos, to the point where dialogue becomes impossible?
