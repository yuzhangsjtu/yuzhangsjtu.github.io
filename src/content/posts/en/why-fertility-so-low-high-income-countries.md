> This NBER working paper systematically examines the **low fertility** that high-income countries broadly face. The study argues that the root of this phenomenon lies not in traditional economic factors (such as income or the cost of raising children) or in any single policy, but in a deeper sociocultural transformation, namely a "**shift in priorities**." In modern society, the priority placed on individual career development, leisure, and self-fulfillment has systematically come to exceed that placed on "**being a parent**."

**Title:** Why Is Fertility So Low in High Income Countries?

**Published by:** NBER Working Papers

**Authors:**

- <u>Melissa Schettini Kearney</u>: Neil Moskowitz Professor of Economics at the University of Maryland. Scholar affiliate and member of the board of the Notre Dame Wilson-Sheehan Lab for Economic Opportunities (LEO).
- <u>Phillip B. Levine</u>: Katharine Coman and A. Barton Hepburn Professor of Economics, Wellesley College.

**Publication Date:** July 2025

## Introduction

In high-income countries, and in many countries more broadly, birth rates are falling to historic lows. In nearly every member country of the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), the total fertility rate (TFR) has fallen below the **replacement level**, and many of these countries have experienced sustained fertility below 1.5, including Japan, Italy, Canada, Greece, and Italy. In some countries in **East Asia**, including South Korea, Singapore, and **China**, the TFR has now dropped to 1.0 or below.

Such low fertility has raised concerns about its potential economic and social consequences, which include a shrinking labor force, declining economic vitality, and the fiscal unsustainability of social insurance systems. At the same time, they also raise grander, more existential questions: what population decline means for the climate, for living standards, and more broadly for human welfare (Spears and Geruso 2025). In response, society at large has taken a broad interest in the question of "**why has fertility declined so sharply and so universally**." This Working Paper sets out to explore this question.

Our reading of the evidence leads us to the conclusion that the decline in fertility across the industrialized world—including the rise in childlessness and the fall in completed fertility—reflects less a response to specific economic costs or policies than a broad re-prioritization of the role that "**being a parent**" plays in people's adult lives. This may reflect the combined effect of a complex set of factors that have produced what we call "**shifting priorities**," that is, changes in how people choose to spend their time, money, and energy. These factors potentially include evolving opportunities and constraints, changing social norms and expectations regarding work, child-rearing, and gender roles, and the influence of social and cultural factors that are difficult to quantify.

This paper begins its analysis by assessing trends in fertility outcomes across countries over time, and it stresses the need to shift the analytical focus from **period measures** toward measures that capture the life-cycle patterns of women's **cohorts**. Demographers have long emphasized the importance of tracking the life-cycle fertility of cohorts in order to understand fertility change, and economists, too, began decades ago to consider life-cycle models of fertility decisions. This approach reveals the important and distinct roles played by birth timing, completed fertility, and childlessness. This paper incorporates this perspective into both its theoretical and its empirical examination.

This paper acknowledges that the neoclassical economic framework that has long dominated the analysis of fertility—most prominently represented by the seminal work of Gary Becker—provides a clear and compelling explanation for the key fertility transitions of the past. Many of its insights remain relevant today. However, the limitations of the traditional model have become increasingly apparent, especially in light of new empirical findings. As Doepke et al. (2023) summarize, the model can no longer easily explain several core features of contemporary fertility dynamics. Consistent with the view of Doepke et al. (2023), we recognize that the standard neoclassical approach that economists tend to favor, however analytically elegant and powerful, can no longer fully capture the complexity of the modern fertility landscape. Fertility is not merely a matter of fixed individual preferences or a direct response to economic incentives; it is shaped jointly by many dynamically evolving factors, many of which are not well captured in parsimonious economic models.

This leads us to emphasize the need for deeper reflection and additional research on factors that typically fall outside the usual focus of economists' study of fertility decisions (namely opportunity costs, prices, and income). What role do peer influences and social norms play in shaping fertility patterns? How does the nature of bargaining within the household matter? What is the influence of the media and social media and their portrayals of family life? Has the rise of the "**intensive parenting**" norm—whereby parents invest more time and resources in each child—deterred potential parents?

For economists, it is also important to take the question of preference formation more seriously. As we discuss below, the evidence inclines us toward the view that the recent decline in fertility may have less to do with changes in current constraints and more to do with the **cumulative cultural** and economic forces that have long shaped fertility decisions. Ordinarily, economists are reluctant to rely on changes in **preferences** to explain behavior, because doing so can explain almost anything. But there is reason to believe that the kind of lifestyle (broadly defined) that is compatible with having one or more children is becoming less appealing to many adults. This is **difficult** to capture with standard economic models or to identify with standard econometric techniques, but it is a shift that an economist concerned with fertility needs to grapple with.

In what follows, we will refer to the theoretical contributions that best reflect the empirical reality of fertility outcomes today. We will also examine the most relevant evidence on the potential explanations for declining fertility in high-income countries. A final section offers concluding observations for future research and policy.

## Patterns in Period and Cohort Measures of Fertility

To understand fertility patterns deeply, we must first distinguish between two basic approaches to measuring fertility behavior: period measures and cohort measures. Each reveals a different facet of fertility behavior and offers a distinct perspective—and a necessary caveat—for interpreting trends across different population groups, time periods, and countries.

#### **Period Fertility**

Period measures provide an immediate snapshot of fertility in a particular year. Of these, the **total fertility rate** (TFR) is the tool most commonly used in international comparisons; it estimates the total number of children a woman would bear over her lifetime if she experienced the current age-specific fertility rates. A TFR of 2.1 is generally regarded as the "replacement level" needed to maintain long-run population stability.

Historical data for six high-income countries (the United States, Canada, Japan, the Netherlands, Norway, and Portugal) show (figure below ⬇️) that the TFR has experienced a marked decline from its mid-twentieth-century "baby boom" peak in all of them. By 1980, the TFRs of all but Portugal had already fallen below the replacement level. This general downward trend constitutes the broad backdrop for our discussion.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250708154957898.png)

There is, however, a key limitation of period measures: they are extremely sensitive to changes in the tempo of childbearing. When society broadly begins to postpone childbearing, the TFR can fall in the short run even if the total number of children women bear over their lifetimes is unchanged, thereby creating a misleading impression of the long-run trend. Demography defines this kind of change in timing as the "**tempo effect**," and defines the true change in lifetime fertility quantity as the "**quantum effect**." To distinguish the two accurately and to probe the true change in the level of fertility, cohort analysis is an indispensable tool.

#### **Cohort Fertility**

Cohort analysis offers more stable and more profound insight by tracking the fertility behavior of a single birth-year group (a cohort) over its entire life cycle. The core measure is "**children ever born**" (CEB), which gauges the average number of children that women of a particular birth cohort have already borne by a given age.

A deeper analysis of the data reveals several key patterns:

- **The overlap of postponed childbearing and a decline in quantum**: The data show that younger birth cohorts (for example, those born after 1987) have systematically lower cumulative numbers of children at any observable age than do older cohorts. For example, at age 30, women in younger cohorts have on average about 0.25 fewer children than earlier cohorts. More importantly, their fertility curves at later ages do not exhibit a sufficiently strong "catch-up," which strongly suggests that the current decline in fertility is not merely postponement but includes genuine reductions in the number of children born.
- **A marked rise in childlessness**: A core component of the decline in quantum is an increase in the share of women who remain childless over their lifetimes. The data show that women in younger cohorts have a significantly higher share of childlessness at any age than women in older cohorts, indicating that "not having children" is becoming an increasingly common choice.
- **The relative stability of subsequent fertility behavior among those who already have children**: In contrast to the sharp rise in childlessness, once a woman has had her first child, the probability that she has a second or third child shows relative stability across cohorts.

Taken together, these findings point to a core conclusion: the main driver of the current decline in fertility lies more in **a rising threshold for entering the stage of "being a parent" (that is, rising childlessness)** than in an increase in single-child families among those who already have children.

#### **The Link Between Marriage and Fertility**

Finally, marriage—as the institution that has traditionally provided the context for childbearing—has also undergone changes in its patterns that are closely tied to fertility trends. The data show that, in countries for which relevant data are available, the share of women in a cohort who are unmarried has **risen systematically** over time, which coincides closely in time with the decline in fertility. Although this is only a descriptive association, it underscores the importance of considering changes in marriage patterns when analyzing fertility behavior.

## Explaining Low Fertility Among Recent Female Cohorts—Conceptualization

Before probing the causes of low fertility, we need to establish a theoretical framework that reflects contemporary realities. This section proposes three core conceptual considerations aimed at updating and extending the traditional analytical paradigm of the economics of fertility.

#### **Market Work and Fertility**

Our understanding of the trade-off between "work and fertility" needs to keep up with the times. Early economic models focused mainly on a **binary choice** for women between "participating in market work" and "staying home to raise children," treating women's wages as the "opportunity cost" of childbearing. In contemporary high-income countries, however, the vast majority of women (including mothers) participate in the labor market.

The central tension, therefore, is no longer "whether to work" but "**how to raise children while pursuing a career**." This means that the focus of analysis shifts from simple opportunity cost to the question of the **"compatibility" between family and career**. Given this new locus of tension, a set of new factors becomes crucial:

- **Institutions and policy**: Institutions that can ease this tension—such as high-quality childcare and paid maternity leave—should, in theory, raise fertility;
- **Labor-market structure**: "Greedy jobs" that demand long, inflexible hours intensify the conflict between work and child-rearing and thereby suppress fertility;
- **Within-household dynamics**: The division of child-rearing responsibilities between spouses directly affects the magnitude of the tension that women face;
- **Social norms**: Norms about "intensive parenting" raise the time and resources that child-rearing requires, thereby intensifying its conflict with career pursuits. At the same time, society's expectations regarding gender roles also profoundly shape this tension.

#### **The Relevant Decision-Making Time Horizon**

A fertility decision is not a short-term, immediate decision but rather a long-term planning process that spans the entire life cycle. **Life-cycle models** stress that, in making fertility decisions, individuals prospectively take into account their lifetime income streams, costs, career trajectories, and other relevant opportunities and constraints.

This perspective yields several important corollaries:

- **The long-run nature of the decision**: A young woman's fertility decision is based on her overall expectations about her life trajectory over the coming decades, rather than merely a response to her current circumstances.
- **Path dependence**: Early life choices (such as education and career) constrain the space of subsequent fertility decisions. As age increases, the difficulty and cost of altering one's life trajectory both increase.
- **The limitations of short-term policy**: Policies designed to provide short-term, temporary incentives (such as a one-time baby bonus), therefore, are unlikely to fundamentally change long-term fertility planning based on the entire life cycle. They may be able to affect the "tempo" of childbearing, but they have difficulty changing its "quantum."

#### **The Role of Preferences**

Traditional economic models usually treat individual preferences as exogenous and stable. In explaining the contemporary decline in fertility, however, it is necessary to examine the process of **preference formation** more seriously.

The evidence suggests that the recent decline in fertility may be due not only to changes in external constraints but also to the **cumulative cultural** and economic forces that have long shaped fertility decisions. There is reason to believe that the kind of lifestyle that is compatible with having one or more children is becoming less appealing to many adults.

While relying on "**changing preferences**" to explain behavior requires great caution in economics, because it can lead to circular reasoning, ignoring the possibility of a systematic shift in values and priorities within the domain of fertility would leave us unable to fully understand the nature of the problem. This shift, namely the "**shift in priorities**," though difficult to capture with standard economic models and econometric techniques, is nevertheless an indispensable part of understanding modern fertility behavior.

## The Role of Income, Prices, and Housing in Fertility Choices

#### **The Link Between Income and Fertility**

- **Micro-level evidence**: A large body of research based on exogenous income shocks (such as a husband's job loss, energy booms, lottery winnings, and the like) shows that, at the micro level, children are a "normal good." That is, when household income increases unexpectedly, fertility tends to rise. These studies provide strong evidence of a positive causal effect of income on fertility decisions.
- **The macro-level paradox**: This micro finding, however, stands in sharp contrast to the macro trend. Over the past several decades, per capita income in high-income countries has risen continuously, yet fertility has declined systematically. This indicates that short-term, exogenous income shocks and long-term economic development accompanied by changes in social structure operate on fertility through very different mechanisms.
- **Conclusion**: Thus, while income effects are real in particular settings, they cannot explain the macro-level trend of persistently low fertility in high-income countries over recent decades.

#### **The Effects of Child Benefits and Tax Credits**

Governments directly reduce the "price" of having children by providing cash subsidies and tax preferences, a pronatalist policy that is common across countries.

- **The generality and modesty of policy effects**: Evaluations of the relevant policies across countries (such as Israel, Spain, and Poland) generally find that these pronatalist financial incentives do indeed bring about an increase in fertility, but the effect is usually **"modest"**.
- **The limits of the elasticity**: Specifically, even sizable cash subsidies have a relatively limited effect in boosting fertility. Studies show that a benefit increase equivalent to 10% of household income yields only a 0.5% to 4.1% increase in fertility. This implies that trying to raise fertility to the replacement level purely through cash incentives would entail enormous fiscal cost.
- **The effect by birth order**: Some studies also find that these policies may have a larger effect on having a second or higher-order child than on having a first child.

#### **The Evidence on Housing and Fertility**

Housing is a key component of the cost of family life, and its price and accessibility have an important influence on fertility decisions.

- **The dual effect of house prices**: Studies show that rising house prices have heterogeneous effects on different groups. For households that already own a home, rising house prices generate a "wealth effect" that may promote fertility; for young people who do not own a home, rising house prices constitute a "price effect" that may suppress fertility.
- **The key role of credit access**: The ability to obtain housing credit is a key variable. Both historical evidence (such as government-guaranteed mortgages in the United States in the 1930s and 1940s) and modern research (such as a housing-credit lottery in Brazil) show that improving young people's access to housing credit can significantly raise fertility, and this effect may show up as an increase in completed fertility.
- **The "relative status" perspective**: The housing issue can also be understood through the framework of "relative income" or "relative status." When owning an independent home is regarded by society as a precondition for forming a family, young people's ability to achieve this goal directly affects their fertility decisions. Stagnant or declining homeownership may lead them to postpone or forgo childbearing.

## The Tension Between Market Work and Raising Children

#### **The Evolution of the Tension and the "Child Penalty"**

In modern economies, the broad participation of women in the labor market has become the norm. The "**work-fertility**" trade-off has therefore shifted from a past binary choice over "whether to participate in work" to the complex problem of balancing **"how to raise children while pursuing a career."** One might say that market work has become the default option, while childbearing has become a major decision that must be coordinated with career planning.

A striking manifestation of this tension in the empirical data is the **"child penalty"**—that is, after a woman has her first child, her earnings undergo a large and persistent decline relative to those of men or other women who have not had children. (There is also the concept of the "**motherhood penalty**.")

- **The prevalence of the "child penalty"**: This phenomenon is broadly present across high-income countries and reflects the structural impact of motherhood on women's careers.
- **The complexity of its causes**: The reasons behind it are not singular and may include: **external constraints**: a lack of flexible work arrangements or affordable childcare. **Individual preferences**: women actively choosing to reduce work in order to devote more time to family. **Social norms**: deeply rooted notions of a gendered division of labor that cause the career costs of child-rearing to be borne disproportionately by women. Relevant research shows that the gender attitudes embedded in regional culture and family background are significantly correlated with the magnitude of the "child penalty."
- **The link with fertility**: Cross-country data, however, show that the severity of a country's "child penalty" and its total fertility rate (TFR) do **not exhibit a strong, systematic negative correlation**. This "counterintuitive" finding suggests that the "child penalty" measured purely in terms of lost income may not fully capture the full meaning of the work-child-rearing tension, such as the mental stress and logistical complexity it brings. Alternatively, even if the income-based "child penalty" were completely eliminated, this would not necessarily resolve the problem of low fertility at its root.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250708160303143.png)

#### **An Assessment of the Effectiveness of Policy Interventions**

The two main types of public policy aimed at easing the work-child-rearing tension are paid maternity leave and childcare. A rigorous assessment of the effects of these policies reveals the limits of their influence.

- **Paid Leave**: A large body of evidence shows that extending paid maternity leave has **little effect on completed fertility**. It may affect the tempo of childbearing (for example, prompting families that already have children to have the next one sooner), but it generally does not lead people to have more children.
- **Childcare**: Universal, affordable childcare has been found to have a positive effect on fertility, but the effect is likewise **modest**. Studies estimate that an enormous fiscal investment (such as 1% of GDP) would be needed to produce a small increase in the TFR.

## Why Have Priorities Shifted

Having shown in the preceding analysis that traditional economic factors and standard policies have difficulty fully explaining low fertility, this section explores a deeper driver: a broad sociocultural transformation whose core is a systematic shift in individual values and life priorities. This shift, namely the "**shift in priorities**," may be affecting fertility decisions through a variety of social mechanisms.

#### **Theoretical Background: The Second Demographic Transition**

This analytical framework echoes the theory of the "Second Demographic Transition" (SDT) in demography. SDT theory holds that, in developed societies, the main driver of persistently low fertility, late marriage, rising cohabitation, and the like is not economic factors but a change in cultural values, particularly the rise of notions such as **individualism, self-fulfillment, and gender equality**. The concept of a "shift in priorities" inherits and develops this line of thinking, seeking to investigate how these values are concretely formed and how they affect behavior.

**Direct Evidence of Changing Values**

Various social surveys provide direct empirical evidence of a "shift in priorities" and paint a picture of changing social attitudes.

- **The remaking of life goals**: Survey data show that, in the ranking of contemporary young people's life goals, **the importance of career satisfaction, personal leisure time, and having close friendships has systematically come to exceed that of marriage and childbearing**. For example, Pew Research Center data show that only a minority (22%) of Americans aged 18 to 34 consider having children "extremely or very important" to living a fulfilling life.
- **The evolution of the ideal family size**: Cross-country research finds that, although "being a parent" itself is still broadly valued in the abstract, people's endorsement of the idea that an ideal family should have "multiple children" is weakening. This suggests that society's preference for the traditional "two-child" family model may be loosening.

**The Transmission Mechanisms of Social Influence**

Individual values and preferences do not form in a vacuum but are profoundly shaped by a set of social factors. This section identifies several key transmission mechanisms.

- **Peer Effects**: An individual's fertility decision is influenced by the behavior of other members of his or her social network. When not having children, or having them late, becomes a common way of life among a person's friends and family, the social cost of choosing not to have children falls, while the opportunity cost of having children (such as missing out on adult-only social activities) rises correspondingly.
- **The influence of media and social media**: Media content—including traditional television programming and emerging social media platforms (such as Instagram and TikTok)—powerfully shapes social norms and individuals' views of family, marriage, and child-rearing through its portrayals of different ways of life. The content on these platforms may be subtly and imperceptibly influencing the younger generation's views on fertility.
- **The "involution" of parenting norms (Intensive Parenting)**: In recent decades, the social norm of "intensive parenting" has become increasingly prevalent. This norm requires parents to invest unprecedented amounts of time, energy, and money in each child, turning child-rearing into a high-intensity "project." This pressure comes into sharp conflict with modern people's pursuit of career development and personal leisure, and it may directly lead people to choose to have fewer children or none.
- **Conflicts in attitudes toward gender roles**: Against the backdrop of a sharp rise in female labor-force participation, the question of the gendered division of labor within the household has become increasingly prominent. Research shows that women's expectations for their partners' equal participation in child-rearing and housework are rising, while men's attitudes and behavior have changed comparatively slowly. The gap between these expectations and reality may lead to relationship strain, thereby dampening the desire to marry and have children.

**The Decline of Marriage and Its Link to Fertility**

In high-income countries, the decline in the marriage rate is highly synchronized with the decline in fertility.

- **A strong correlation**: Because the vast majority of childbearing still occurs within marriage or a stable partnership, a decline in the marriage rate directly constrains fertility.
- **The possibility of two-way causation**: The causal relationship behind this association is complex. On the one hand, an inability to find a suitable partner may lead people not to marry and therefore not to have children. On the other hand, when the desire to have children itself declines, the necessity of marriage as a traditional life path may decline along with it.
- **The moderating role of social norms**: In societies where the rate of nonmarital childbearing is extremely low (such as Japan), a decline in the marriage rate translates almost one-for-one into a decline in fertility.

## Uncertainty Surrounding Fertility Outcomes

Having explored the many factors that influence the "desire" to have children, this section turns to an analysis of the various uncertainties and frictions that exist between "fertility plans" and "fertility reality." Together, these factors produce a "gap" between the number of children planned and the number ultimately realized, and they are an indispensable part of understanding the overall level of fertility.

#### **The Gap Between Planned and Realized Fertility**

Empirical data show that, in high-income countries, the number of children people report as "ideal" or "planned" is generally higher than the number they ultimately realize. This "fertility gap" phenomenon reveals two coexisting trends:

1. **A downward revision of fertility plans**: The number of children that younger cohorts plan to have is itself declining, which is consistent with the "shift in priorities" view discussed above.
2. **Plans still exceed reality**: Even as the number of planned children declines, it still exceeds the actual level of fertility. This indicates that, beyond the active choice to have fewer children or none, there is also a problem of "wanting children but being unable to realize them."

Interpreting this "gap" requires caution, because it may reflect both people's inability to realize their ideals owing to real constraints (such as economic pressure or being unable to find a partner) and the unexpected frictions encountered in carrying out fertility plans (such as infertility).

#### **The Accessibility of Effective Contraception**

Modern contraceptive technology, especially long-acting reversible contraception (LARC), gives individuals the ability to control the timing and number of births more precisely.

- **Historical role and recent impact**: Although the spread of the oral contraceptive pill had a profound historical effect on the decline in fertility, the role of contraceptive technology in explaining the persistent decline in fertility over **recent decades** may be limited.
- **Evidence suggests it is not the main driver**: Many effective contraceptive technologies have long been widespread, and their timelines do not fully match the recent acceleration of the fertility decline. In the United States, the spread of LARC has mainly affected particular groups (such as low-income individuals and adolescents) and has insufficient explanatory power for the decline in overall population fertility. Countries with relatively low rates of contraceptive use, such as Japan, have some of the lowest fertility rates in the world, which provides counterevidence to the view that "contraceptive technology is the main driver of low fertility."
- **Conclusion**: More effective contraceptive technology is a tool that helps people **realize their fertility intentions**, rather than a motive that determines those intentions themselves. It may amplify the effect of other factors that lower fertility intentions, but it is not itself a root cause.

#### **Abortion Policy**

Abortion is another important factor that influences fertility outcomes.

- **The period-specific nature of its impact**: Historical evidence shows that major changes in abortion policy (such as the legalization of abortion in the United States in the 1970s) can indeed have a significant and lasting effect on fertility.
- **Limited recent explanatory power**: In most high-income countries, however, abortion policy has remained relatively stable over the past several decades, and it is therefore unlikely to be a main variable explaining the recent persistent decline in fertility.

#### **Infertility and Fertility Treatment**

As society broadly postpones the age of childbearing, the problem of infertility caused by aging has become increasingly prominent. At the same time, assisted reproductive technology (ART, such as in vitro fertilization, IVF) is also becoming increasingly widespread.

- **The dual role of assisted reproductive technology**: **As a solution**: ART offers many infertile couples the opportunity to have children, and its use is on the rise in high-income countries. **As a source of "moral hazard"**: The existence of ART may lead young people to **form overly optimistic expectations** about overcoming the age-related decline in fertility. Such optimistic expectations may encourage them to postpone childbearing more boldly, thereby increasing the probability that they will face the risk of infertility in the future.
- **Evidence and reality**: Research finds that expanding insurance coverage for ART, while it does postpone people's age at first birth, **does not significantly change their completed fertility**. This supports the "moral hazard" hypothesis. At the same time, one must recognize that the success rate of ART is far from 100%, especially for older women. Infertility caused by postponing childbearing, therefore, cannot be fully resolved by technology.

## Conclusion and Discussion

#### **Prospects for Research Directions**

Future research needs to expand both methodologically and thematically in order to understand the phenomenon of low fertility more comprehensively.

- **Integrating the insights of demography and economics**: Future economic research should more fully integrate the analytical methods of demography. Demography is good at finely decomposing the components of fertility trends (such as distinguishing tempo from quantum and attending to childlessness), while economics is good at identifying the causal mechanisms behind behavior. Combining the two perspectives is the most effective path toward advancing our understanding of fertility.
- **Going beyond the limitations of traditional empirical methods**: The quasi-experimental methods on which modern economics relies to identify causal relationships, while rigorous, have limitations when used to study long-term, broad-based sociocultural change. These methods are better suited to assessing short-term, discrete policy shocks. Future research, therefore, needs to treat its conclusions more cautiously and to guard against the systematic biases they may produce—for example, possibly underestimating policies that have long-run effects on young people, or overestimating policies that produce only short-term "tempo" effects. Exploring empirical methods better suited to studying grand social change will be an important challenge.
- **Broadening the research agenda**: The research agenda needs to expand from a traditional focus on opportunity cost, prices, and income to the exploration of "softer" but possibly more fundamental factors such as social norms, endogenous preference formation, and media influence.

#### **Implications for Policymaking**

Based on the analysis throughout this paper, several thought-provoking implications can be drawn for public policy aimed at addressing low fertility.

- **Acknowledging the prevalence and complexity of the problem**: Low fertility is a general phenomenon in high-income countries, which suggests that its root is unlikely to be a policy failure unique to any one country, but rather a deeper, shared socioeconomic transformation. There is therefore **no simple "standard answer" that can be directly copied**.
- **There is no "quick fix"**: The evidence suggests that governments are unlikely to be able to quickly reverse the decline in fertility through modest cash transfers or incremental financial incentives. Fertility decisions are the result of long-term expectations and institutional arrangements, and short-term, piecemeal policy measures have difficulty producing transformative effects.
- **Possible directions for effective policy**: If completed fertility is to be raised effectively, the focus of policy needs to shift: **Target population**: Policy interventions should focus more on **influencing the new cohorts entering their childbearing years**, rather than on those in the later stage of their childbearing years whose life choices are already largely set. **Policy intensity**: What may be needed is larger-scale, systematic change capable of fundamentally altering individuals' lifetime cost-benefit calculations about childbearing, rather than piecemeal incentives.

#### **Final Conclusion**

Although we have still not found a definitive answer to "why fertility is so low in high-income countries," this study offers a broader analytical framework. It emphasizes that, to understand this phenomenon, we must broaden our vision, go beyond the traditional bounds of economics, and bring factors such as social influence, evolving institutional norms, and changing opportunity sets into consideration.
