This NBER working paper systematically explores the prevalence of **low fertility** in high-income countries. The authors argue that the root cause lies not in traditional economic factors—such as income or childcare costs—nor in any single policy, but in a deeper sociocultural transformation: a “**shift in priorities**.” In modern society, personal pursuits such as career ambitions, leisure, and self-fulfillment have come to be prioritized over **parenthood**.

**Title:** Why Is Fertility So Low in High Income Countries?

**Published by:** NBER Working Papers

**Authors:**

- <u>Melissa Schettini Kearney</u>: Neil Moskowitz Professor of Economics at the University of Maryland. Scholar affiliate and member of the board of the Notre Dame Wilson-Sheehan Lab for Economic Opportunities (LEO).
- <u>Phillip B. Levine</u>: Katharine Coman and A. Barton Hepburn Professor of Economics, Wellesley College.

**Publication Date:** July 2025

## Introduction

Birth rates are falling to historic lows in high-income countries and many others. In nearly all Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) member nations, the total fertility rate (TFR) has fallen below the **replacement level**, with many countries experiencing persistently low fertility below 1.5—including Japan, Italy, Canada, Greece, and the Netherlands. Countries in **East Asia**, such as South Korea, Singapore, and **China**, have seen TFRs drop to 1.0 or even lower.

Such low fertility has evoked concerns about potential economic and social ramifications—a shrinking workforce, diminished economic vitality, and the fiscal precariousness of social insurance systems. It has also raised broader, near-intractable questions: what shrinking population levels bode for global climate, living standards, and more broadly human welfare (Spears and Geruso 2025). Responding to those elevated stakes, considerable interest now centers on **why fertility declined so steeply and beyond what many demarcated as foreseeable boundaries**. This paper examines that question.

Our reading of the evidence leads us toward a central inference: the broad decline in fertility across the industrialized world—both heightened childlessness and now lower completed fertility among mothers—likely reflects a remaking of the manifest significance of **parenthood** in many adults’ lives rather than just specific ranges of costs or explicit institutional support, more so observable structural sources stretch with the composite of what we seek to label **“shifting priorities”** shaping how people direct money, care, and volitional energies for themselves.
