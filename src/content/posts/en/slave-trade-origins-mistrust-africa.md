Trust is a vital ingredient for economic development—mistrust toward people and institutions undermines cooperation and innovation. In this study, Nunn and his co‑author establish that the historical slave trade caused enduring mistrust within Africa, a culture of mistrust that persists to this day.

**Title:** The Slave Trade and the Origins of Mistrust in Africa

### Preface

There are many explanations for the widespread underdevelopment of modern African states. Nunn’s 2008 QJE paper, **[The Long‑term Effects of Africa's Slave Trades](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/123/1/139/1889789)**, used shipping records and historical slave‑trade data to find a strong negative correlation between the number of slaves a country exported historically and its present‑day economic performance. Yet rigorous evidence on the *mechanism* through which the historical slave trade affected today’s economic performance has been lacking.

Solid trust promotes cooperation and spurs innovation and economic growth, while mistrust acts as a drag on development. This AER paper argues that the historical slave trade generated inter‑group mistrust within Africa that has lasted to the present day, and this mistrust can serve as one mechanism explaining the link between the slave trade and Africa’s contemporary economic performance.

The source of this mistrust lies in how slaves were acquired: in the early stages, slaves were mainly captured through state‑organized raids and wars, but as the trade expanded, an insecure environment seeped down to the individual level—friends, family members, and others could all sell each other into slavery through kidnapping or deception. The risk of being betrayed in this way formed a rule‑of‑thumb of mistrust, transmitted as a cultural trait to the present.

The original English word in the paper for this concept is “*Mistrust*,” which differs somewhat from “*Distrust*”:

>Although “mistrust” and “distrust” are used interchangeably in everyday English, there are subtle differences between them:
>
> - **Mistrust** usually refers to a lack of trust based on the past behavior of a person or thing. This distrust is grounded in experience, meaning past conduct makes one doubt future reliability or integrity. Mistrust leans more toward gut feeling and emotional response; it may lack clearly articulated reasons.
> - **Distrust**, by contrast, is a deeper‑seated lack of confidence, typically grounded in specific reasons or evidence. It implies a firm belief that someone or something is untrustworthy. Distrust can be a suspicion about someone’s integrity or about information or situations, and it often involves more analysis and evaluation.
>
> In short, mistrust relates more to an uncertainty based on **emotion** and **intuition**, while distrust is a more **rational** lack of trust grounded in concrete **evidence**.

### How slaves were “sold”

- Early in the slave trade, nearly all slaves were taken during **large‑scale conflicts** or raids, creating an insecurity both within and between local communities.
- When people became commodities: individuals could profit from **betraying** others—or protect themselves—by means such as deception, kidnapping, or other small‑scale violence. People were able to exchange slaves for guns and iron weapons with Europeans or slave traders.
- **Slave‑trading alliances**: Slave merchants frequently formed strategic alliances with key groups within a village to secure a supply of slaves, so that neither villages, neighbors, nor even family members were safe. Merchants occasionally passed off the pretense of offering someone shelter only to sell that person into slavery.
- In the 1840s, a German missionary‑linguist gathered information on 144 former slaves living in Sierra Leone: more than 40 % had been enslaved because of kidnapping, fewer than 25 % because of war, and almost 20 % because they were betrayed by kin or friends. In certain areas, the practice of selling one’s own relatives to slave traders even became customary.
- The **judicial system** as a channel for acquiring slaves: individuals were accused—often by family members—of witchcraft, theft, adultery, or murder to ensnare victims. If convicted, the accused was sentenced to enslavement.
- **Leaders as slave traders**: chiefs—whether willingly or under coercion—frequently acted as slave dealers, often selling their own people into slavery. (As a result, the slave trade can also give rise to mistrust in political figures.)

### How mistrust from the slave trade becomes a culture

- **Anthropological perspective**: A culture can be seen as a rule‑of‑thumb for acting in an uncertain or complex environment. Research shows that when obtaining information is very costly or incomplete, relying on rules‑of‑thumb may be optimal. People may not seek the best possible action in every situation, but they pursue a way of acting that routinely reduces information costs—following a rule‑of‑thumb is generally efficient (though not optimal in every single case). Different behavioral rules evolve through a form of natural selection driven by the relative payoffs of those rules. (Intuitively: if trusting others might get you sold into slavery, then in a wide range of situations mistrusting others quickly becomes a simple and effective decision.)
- **The culture‑institution complementarity argument**: Individuals can inherit cultural norms from their elders and, through activities such as voting, help shape the evolution of political institutions. A culture of mistrust leads to weaker institutions, and weaker institutions in turn produce worse behavior and lower trust; you end up in a vicious cycle of mistrust.
- **Persistence of the slave trade itself**: The slave trade lasted over four hundred years; its consequences take a long time to dissipate, and cultural change happens slowly. There is evidence for this in the literature; for example, East Germans still favor government intervention more strongly than West Germans, though since reunification, East German attitudes are gradually shifting toward West German ones. Although Germany lasted as a divided country for only 45 years, the authors estimate it would take at least 20 to 40 years for such an attitude gap to vanish.
- **The genetic selection argument**: The preceding explanations all follow a “cultural learning” channel. But it is also possible that mistrust is, to some extent, innate. People who by nature would more readily trust others might be far more likely to change their behavior during the slave trade—trusting people could increase the odds of being captured versus not trusting anyone—leaving behind a pool of inherently less trusting individuals. The existence of a sizable population with intrinsically low trust levels persisted across Africa to the present day.

### Data and Variables

**Individual‑level data**

- Sourced from the Afrobarometer, with surveys covering 17 sub‑Saharan African countries.
- Dependent variables: trust in relatives, neighbors, and the locally elected government council, as well as in one’s own ethnic group and in people from other ethnic groups. Responses run: not at all, just a little, somewhat, a lot; in regression they can be coded 0–3 and treated as ordinal variables.

**Ethnic‑level data**

- The number of slaves from each ethnic group is derived from Nunn’s (2008) country‑level slave export database, covering the four African slave trades between 1400 and 1900 (Trans‑Atlantic, Indian Ocean, Red Sea, and Trans‑Saharan). These country‑level data were then disaggregated to the ethnic‑group level.

![img](https://www.un.org/sites/un2.un.org/files/2019/12/slave_trade_map_large.jpg)

- Because sufficiently detailed ethnic‑group data are available only for the Trans‑Atlantic and Indian Ocean trades, the empirical analysis does not include the other two trades (the Trans‑Saharan and Red Sea trades were far smaller in volume, so that omitting them makes little difference).

### Empirical Analysis

**Baseline regression**

- In the regression where the dependent variable is trust in neighbors, the coefficient is negative and statistically significant whether the explanatory variable is the number of enslaved people from the ethnic group or the slave‑trade intensity (the number of people taken, normalized by habitable area or by historical population).
- The slave trade exhibits a negative and significant influence on the level of trust in relatives, neighbors, the local government council, one’s own ethnic group, and people from other ethnic groups.

**Causal identification**

- After controlling for variables such as the historic environment of the ethnic group‘s location, pre‑colonial prosperity, or settlement patterns, the results remain significantly negative.
- When using the distance from the ethnic group’s home area to the coast as an instrumental variable, the analysis yields conclusions consistent with the baseline regression.
- Using parallel questions from the AsiaBarometer and instrumenting with respondents’ distance to the nearest coastline shows that the effect is statistically insignificant and economically meaningless, confirming that it is specifically Africa’s slave trade that accounts for the mistrust found there.

**Mechanism analysis**

- Is the effect of the slave trade on mistrust transmitted culturally? Could the slave trade instead have created poor institutions, and these weak institutions independently shape today’s mistrust?
- After also including the respondent’s attitudes toward the President/MP/National Legislature in the model, the absolute size of the coefficient on the main explanatory variable falls by about one half, yet it remains statistically very significant—suggesting that at least roughly half of the slave‑trade–mistrust relationship cannot stem from deteriorating trust in the local council (a nice and elegant test!).
- Regressing trust on the historic number of enslaved people from the respondent’s own ethnic group (measuring cultural influences—the *internal channel*) and, at the same time, the local-level measure of slaves taken from a respondent’s current area of residence (as a proxy for institutions / quality of the local political context—the *external channel*): both coefficients are negative and significant, and the internal-channel coefficient is notably large. This suggests that both culture‑based and institution‑based mechanisms are at work.

### Bonus notes

- This paper is very accessibly written; its language and logic are relatively reader‑friendly.
- It is a masterpiece of causal inference with cross‑sectional data. As one reviewer (pw) once put it, there are “high returns to replication”; the way the article uses the IV and handles the mechanism analysis is particularly worth studying closely!
