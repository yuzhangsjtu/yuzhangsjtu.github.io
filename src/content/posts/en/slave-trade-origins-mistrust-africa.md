### Preface

There are many explanations for the widespread underdevelopment of modern African states. Nunn's 2008 QJE paper, **[The Long-term Effects of Africa's Slave Trades](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/123/1/139/1889789)**, used shipping records and historical slave-trade data to find a strong negative correlation between the number of slaves a country historically exported and that country's present-day economic performance. However, exactly how the historical slave trade affected today's economic performance — what the mechanism is — still lacks rigorous evidence.

Good trust promotes cooperation and drives innovation and economic development, whereas mistrust acts as a drag on economic development. This AER paper argues that the historical slave trade gave rise to inter-group mistrust within Africa that persists to this day, which can serve as one of the mechanisms explaining the link between the slave trade and Africa's contemporary economic performance.

The source of this mistrust lies in how slaves were acquired: early slaves were captured mainly through state-organized raids and wars, but as the trade developed, the insecure environment penetrated down to the individual level, and friends, family members, and others could all sell one another into slavery through kidnapping or deception. This risk of being betrayed constituted a rule of thumb of mistrust, passed down in cultural form to the present.

The original word in the paper for the mistrust discussed here is "Mistrust," which differs somewhat from "Distrust":

> The words "Mistrust" and "Distrust" can be used interchangeably in everyday English, but there are still some subtle differences between them:
>
> - **Mistrust** usually refers to a lack of trust arising from someone's or something's past performance. This lack of trust is grounded in experience, meaning that past behavior makes one doubt future reliability or integrity. Mistrust is more about an intuitive and emotional reaction, and may have no clearly stated reason.
> - **Distrust**, by contrast, is a deeper lack of trust, usually based on specific reasons or evidence. It implies a strong conviction that someone or something is untrustworthy. Distrust can be a suspicion about someone's integrity, or about information or a situation, and is often accompanied by more analysis and evaluation.
>
> In sum, mistrust involves more of an uncertain feeling based on **emotion** and **intuition**, whereas distrust is a more **rational** lack of trust grounded in concrete **evidence**.

### How slaves were "sold"

- In the early days of the slave trade, almost all slaves were taken away during **large-scale conflicts** or raids. This led to an insecure environment both inside and outside the local community.
- When people became commodities: individuals could profit or protect themselves by **selling** others — for example, through deception, kidnapping, or other forms of small-scale violence, people could exchange slaves for guns and iron weapons from Europeans or slave traders.
- **Slave-trading alliances**: slave traders formed strategic alliances with key groups within a village in order to obtain slaves, making villages, neighbors, and family members all unsafe; they would even sell someone off by pretending to offer them shelter.
- In the 1840s, a German missionary and linguist gathered information on 144 former slaves living in Sierra Leone: more than 40% had become slaves through kidnapping, fewer than 25% had become slaves because of war, and nearly 20% had become slaves by being sold by relatives or friends. In some areas, a custom even developed of selling one's own kin to slave traders.
- The **judicial system** as a channel for acquiring slaves: slaves were obtained by accusing others (often family members) of crimes such as witchcraft, theft, adultery, or murder; if found guilty, the accused would be sentenced to enslavement.
- **Leaders as slave traders**: chiefs were often slave traders, and whether voluntarily or under coercion, would frequently sell their own people into slavery. (Thus the slave trade may also give rise to mistrust toward political figures.)

### How mistrust from the slave trade became a culture

- **Anthropological perspective**: culture is a rule of thumb for acting in an uncertain or complex environment. Some research shows that when the cost of obtaining information is very high or information is incomplete, it may be optimal for individuals to act according to rules of thumb. People may not seek out the best way of acting in every situation, but they will pursue ways to lower the information cost required for their actions; that is, acting by rules of thumb is often efficient (though not optimal in every case). Different rules of action evolve through a process of natural selection, a process determined by the relative payoffs of the different rules of thumb. (Intuitively, this means that if trusting others might get oneself sold, then in many situations distrusting others is a simple and effective decision.)
- **The complementarity of cultural norms and institutions**: individuals can inherit certain cultural norms from their elders, and through activities such as voting they make choices about the evolution of political institutions. A culture of mistrust leads to weaker institutions, and weak institutions in turn lead to worse behavior and lower levels of trust, trapping society in a vicious cycle of mistrust.
- **The persistence of the slave trade itself**: the slave trade lasted more than 400 years, and the dissipation of its consequences takes a long time, since cultural change occurs slowly. There is some documentary evidence for this; for example, East Germans are more favorable toward government intervention than West Germans, and after the reunification of the two Germanys, East Germans' attitudes have slowly moved closer to those of West Germans. Although postwar German division lasted only 45 years, the authors estimate that it will take 20 to 40 years for this difference to fade away.
- **The genetic selection argument**: the preceding explanations all follow the "cultural learning" pathway, but it is also possible that this mistrust is innate. People who are by nature more inclined to trust others were more likely to be carried off during the slave trade, so what remained were people who are by nature not inclined to trust others. A large number of people with low mutual-trust levels stayed in Africa, and this persists to this day.

### Data and variables

**Individual-level data**

- Sourced from the Afrobarometer, with surveys covering 17 sub-Saharan African countries.
- Dependent variables: the degree of trust in relatives, neighbors, and the local elected government council, and the degree of trust in one's own ethnic group and other ethnic groups; the categories are, respectively, not at all, just a little, somewhat, and a lot, which in the regressions can be assigned values of 0–3 and treated as ordinal variables.

**Ethnic-group-level data**

- The number of slaves per ethnic group comes from the country-level slave export data constructed by Nunn (2008), covering Africa's four slave trades from 1400–1900 (including the trans-Atlantic, trans-Indian Ocean, trans-Red Sea, and trans-Saharan trades); these data are disaggregated to the ethnic-group level.

### Empirical section

**Baseline regression**

- In the regression where the dependent variable is the level of trust in neighbors, whether the explanatory variable is the number of slaves carried off from the ethnic group or the slave-trade intensity (number of people taken / habitable area, or historical population), the result is significantly negative.
- The slave trade has a negative and significant effect on the levels of trust in relatives, neighbors, the local government council, one's own ethnic group, and other ethnic groups.

**Causal identification**

- After controlling for variables such as the ethnic group's historical environment of residence, pre-colonial prosperity, and settlement patterns, the result remains significantly negative.
- Using the distance from each ethnic group's place of residence to the coastline as an instrumental variable maintains conclusions consistent with the baseline regression.
- Using the relevant questions from the Asiabarometer and using the distance from the respondent's location to the coastline as an IV, the result is found to be insignificant and without real-world meaning, indicating that it is Africa's slave trade that constitutes the cause of the mistrust.

**Mechanism analysis**

- Is the effect of the slave trade on mistrust transmitted through culture? Could the slave trade also have led to bad institutions, and could these weak institutions also have shaped today's mistrust?
- After including the respondents' attitudes toward government councillors and the council in the model, the absolute value of the coefficient on the main explanatory variable is found to fall by half, but it remains very significant, indicating that at least half of the relationship between the slave trade and mistrust cannot be explained by the deterioration of the local council's credibility (brilliant!!).
- Using the number of slaves historically carried off from the respondent's ethnic group as the explanatory variable (to measure the influence of culture — the internal channel), while also including the historical number of slaves from the respondent's current place of residence (as a proxy for institutions — the external channel), the coefficients on both variables are significantly negative, with the coefficient of the internal channel being larger, indicating that both culture and institutions hold up as mechanisms.
