## Discourse

> A particular way of speaking and thinking based on certain shared assumptions, which（can）influence and shape people's understanding of and actions toward a topic.

"Language is thought， and discourse is power."

### 1 Origins and History

The concept of discourse originated in linguistics, referring to spoken or written communication. In the 1950s, J. L. Austin pointed out that written and spoken communication is not neutral, passive statement-making — "discourse" itself acts, and has a strong shaping effect on the world we live in. Mainstream sociology focuses on power and its impact on society and was not originally linked to linguistic research. Foucault first discovered and discussed the intrinsic connection between discourse and power. Since then, the concept of discourse, as well as "discursive practice," has increasingly captured sociologists' attention and interest.

### 2 Meanings and Interpretations

From the late 1950s onward, discourse began to be understood as a form of action, that is, an intervention in the world. The way we behave is affected by discursive constructions, for example:

- Describing a group expressing political demands as a "terrorist organization" versus "freedom fighters"
- Whether news coverage of a strike emphasizes its causes or its disruptive consequences

These influence how we choose to act.

Viewing discourse as a form of action significantly influences and alters our perception of language and everyday communication. There is no understanding of social structure or power relations, nor any study of culture or mass media, that remains untouched by this conceptual shift. Since then, sociologists have increasingly focused on how language is used and manipulated, how the frameworks for debating political issues are established, how certain specific ideas are deliberately excluded from topics under discussion, and how people's participation in debate is controlled and manipulated.

By examining the historical evolution of madness, criminality, the penal system, and medical institutions, Foucault showed that our social lives are governed by a series of discursive regimes, and that it is precisely through these discursive regimes that power is exercised. Discourse acts, in the way a paradigm does, to define the boundary of what people can discuss regarding a particular topic, as well as which types of discussion are permissible and which are forbidden. For instance, when discussing crime, we can only do so following legalking-legal standards, and complying with the law is accepted within this discursive framework as a given of everyday life. If someone proposed a regular practice of law resistance,* most people would consider it unfathomable. Why? Because **the discursive regime regulating crime exists prior to the individual**, and through the process of socialization, it gradually instills and internalizes these values and norms in society’s members, partially influencing people's attitudes and behaviors. In this way, discourse shapes and constructs each person's self-consciousness and identity. This reminds us that people cannot think, express or act with complete autonomy and freedom from all constraint. Human agency is always constrained and bounded.

Foucault pushed further: for him, discourse and discursive practice are the central issue when studying power. Knowledge and power, he argued, are closely intertwined rather than opposed.
