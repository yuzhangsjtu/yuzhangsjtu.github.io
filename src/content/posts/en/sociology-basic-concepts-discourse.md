## Discourse

> A particular way of speaking and thinking based on certain shared assumptions, which (can) influence and shape people's understanding of and actions toward a given topic.

"Language is thought; discourse is power."

### 1 Origin and History

The concept of discourse originated in linguistics, referring to oral or written communication. In the 1950s, Austin pointed out that written and oral communication is not a neutral, passive statement; "discourse" itself acts, and has an extremely strong shaping effect on the world we live in. Mainstream sociology focuses on power and its impact on society, and was not connected to linguistic research. Foucault was the first to discover and discuss the intrinsic connection between discourse and power. Since then, the concept of discourse, as well as "discursive practice," has attracted more and more attention and interest from sociologists.

### 2 Meaning and Interpretation

Beginning in the late 1950s, discourse came to be understood as a form of action, that is, an intervention in the world. The way we behave is influenced by discursive construction, for example:

- Whether a group expressing political demands is described as a "terrorist organization" or as "freedom fighters"
- Whether media coverage of a strike emphasizes its causes or the destructive consequences it brings about

These will influence our choices of action.

Viewing discourse as a form of action has greatly influenced and changed our view of language and everyday communication. Our understanding of social structures and power relations, as well as our study of culture and mass media, have all been profoundly affected by this conceptual shift. From then on, sociologists began to pay attention to how language is used and manipulated, how the frameworks for discussing political issues are established, how certain specific ideas are deliberately excluded from the topics of discussion, how the ways people participate in debate are controlled and manipulated, and so on.

Through his examination of the historical development of madness, crime, the prison system, and medical institutions, Foucault pointed out that our social life is governed by a series of discursive systems, and that power operates precisely by way of these discursive systems. Discourse is like a paradigm, stipulating the boundaries within which people discuss a particular issue, as well as what kinds of discussion are permitted and which are forbidden. For example, when discussing crime, we can only do so according to the standards of law and order; within this discursive framework, abiding by the law is regarded as a matter of course in everyday life. If someone were to suggest resisting the constraints of the law every day, most people would consider it unreasonable. Why? Because **the discursive system that governs crime exists prior to the individual**, and through the process of socialization it gradually instills these values and norms into members of society and causes them to internalize them, thereby partially influencing people's attitudes and behaviors. In this way, discourse shapes and constructs every person's self-consciousness and identity. This reminds us that people cannot think, express, and act in a completely autonomous manner free of any constraint; human agency is constrained and limited.

Foucault further regarded discourse and discursive practice as the core issue in the study of power. He argued that knowledge and power are closely connected rather than opposed to each other. For example, criminology, in order to study criminal behavior objectively, and psychiatry, in order to grasp objective knowledge about mental illness — although their original intention may have been to seek some kind of objective understanding — these two professional disciplines also at the same time **produce power relations**, swaying and shaping people's understanding of, and behavior toward, crime and mental illness. The discursive system of psychiatry artificially draws a clear boundary between reason and madness, making it seem reasonable and justified to isolate, manage, and treat mental illness through various specialized psychiatric institutions. Likewise, the discursive system of criminology not only describes and explains the characteristics and causes of criminal behavior; it also helps us establish new means and methods for defining and dealing with offenders.

### 3 Criticism and Discussion

Foucault held that discourse is unrelated to any specific social situation (such as social class), a point that runs counter to other research on power. A great deal of research shows that power is not a natural outcome, but rather something that must be striven for and won through effort. For example, in a patriarchal society, men hold the power to dominate women; or again, the suppression of the ruled class by the ruling class, and so on. The view that power is a natural "lubricant" of social relations clearly fails to see the reality that vast inequalities exist within power relations. Another criticism is that discourse analysis places excessive emphasis on language, speech, and text — this is a kind of "decorative sociology," which hides social relations behind culture and obscures the truly important topic, namely the sociological analysis of power and of subtle, balanced relations. This group of critics holds that, for social life, real social relations and material culture are more important than discursive systems.

### 4 Significance and Value

- Discursive contestation can change social attitudes. For example, discourse analysis of young single mothers shows that, contrary to the mainstream social view that regards teenage mothers as irresponsible, selfish welfare "parasites," teenage mothers are simply "slightly younger mothers" and therefore should have the legitimate right to obtain social support. This "alternative" discourse helps teenage mothers obtain the corresponding resources, and promotes a shift in public attitudes.
- Discourse analysis methods can be used to study the global political discursive system. For example, after the 9/11 attacks, the U.S. government introduced a new discourse about a global "war on terror." Within this discursive framework, the terrorists' attack was held to be directed not only at the United States, but at the "democratic system" of which the United States is the representative. Afterward, this discursive system constructed a space of public debate, in which a series of participants either tried to interpret this view or tried to argue for its legitimacy. Through these means and methods, the "counterterrorism" discourse sparked a fierce debate in the public sphere about "us" and "them," thereby constructing new identities and guiding people to distinguish "friend from foe."
- The changes in the discourse and rhetoric about war. The media increasingly emphasizes the professionalism and the "peacekeeping" mission of certain wars; reports on soldiers more often show scenes of them protecting civilians, while enemy casualties are scarcely mentioned at all. Battlefield photographs increasingly present generic themes rather than describing specific temporal details. In particular, the media increasingly tends to purchase battlefield photographs from commercial agencies and to use these images in a generic, symbolic way.
