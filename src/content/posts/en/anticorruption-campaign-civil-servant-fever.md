> This paper studies the impact of China's large-scale anticorruption campaign, launched in 2013, on the **willingness to apply for civil service positions**. Based on National Civil Service Exam data from 2012-2017, the authors exploit the staggered timing with which the **central inspection system** was implemented across departments to construct a difference-in-differences (DID) model, and find that departments that underwent inspections saw a significant cooling of application enthusiasm, with the number of applicants per position falling by about 37% on average. Further analysis shows that this effect works mainly through two channels, raising the **probability that corruption is detected** and **restricting officials' discretionary funds**, rather than by affecting officials' legal income. Theoretical and simulation analyses indicate that anticorruption actions may lower the average ability of new entrants into the bureaucracy while raising their prosociality. This paper provides important empirical evidence on how anticorruption campaigns affect civil service talent selection and governance capacity.

**Title:** China's Anticorruption Campaign and Civil Servant Fever

**Journal:** *The Journal of Law and Economics*

- <u>Weizheng Lai</u>: PhD candidate in economics at the University of Maryland. His research focuses on the intersection of development economics, political economy, and labor economics.

## Introduction

Civil service positions in China have always been highly sought after, a phenomenon known as "**Civil Servant Fever**." In traditional Chinese culture, entering the bureaucracy is seen as a path to **status** and **stability**. Modern research likewise shows that many talented individuals prefer government employment over entrepreneurship (e.g., [EJ-Good Gaokao Scores, Less Entrepreneurial Drive](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/ak8Kno7n-eYBL2aaC0mj2Q)). It is reported that in 2012 only **2%** of university graduates in **Singapore** were willing to apply for civil service jobs, while in **China** the share was as high as **76.4%**. At the same time, corruption is widespread within the bureaucracy, and it erodes administrative efficiency and harms economic development. Policymakers and scholars have long explored reforms to **curb corruption**. Take the classic study by Becker and Stigler (1974) as an example: they proposed that optimizing **punishment** and **compensation** mechanisms can effectively constrain officials' behavior.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250319165527584.png)

To help make sense of China's "Civil Servant Fever" and the impact of the anticorruption campaign, the figure above ⬆️ shows the trend of the "**application-to-admission ratio**" (i.e., number of applicants / number of admission slots) in the National Civil Service Exam from 2002 to 2017. As can be seen, the ratio peaked in 2013: on average about 70 people competed for a single position, and the admission rate was only about 1.4%, competition even fiercer than the admission rates of some **Ivy League** schools. But starting in 2013, this upward trend stopped abruptly and began to fall back. Coincidentally, 2013 was precisely the starting point of this unprecedented round of the anticorruption campaign. This contemporaneous trend reversal motivates the paper's core research question: **What effect does the anticorruption campaign have on the willingness to enter the bureaucracy (i.e., "Civil Servant Fever")?** In other words, does intensified anticorruption dampen people's enthusiasm for applying to the civil service? This is an area that few prior studies have directly explored. This research aims to fill that gap by combining a theoretical model with empirical analysis to rigorously estimate the causal impact of the anticorruption campaign on civil service application behavior, and to probe the mechanisms behind it. The paper focuses on China's anticorruption campaign launched in 2013, one of the harshest anticorruption actions since the founding of the People's Republic. Using China as a case, the study seeks to provide empirical evidence on how anticorruption policies affect entry into the bureaucracy, and to serve as a reference for similar questions in other contexts.

## Research Background

#### **Existing Research on Corruption and Anticorruption**

Corruption is widely believed to weaken government governance and economic development. A large literature studies how anticorruption measures affect the behavior of incumbent officials and economic performance. For example, research has examined how anticorruption improves **firm** performance or changes the efficiency of government spending. In the Chinese context, scholars have found that anticorruption actions are associated with improved company performance, local economic development, and so on (e.g., Xu and Yano 2017; Chen and Kung 2019; Kong and Qin 2021, among others). By contrast, however, the impact of **anticorruption** on the behavior of **potential entrants to officialdom** has received very little attention. This constitutes an important research gap: if anticorruption changes how enticing civil service positions are, will those who originally planned to apply be deterred? The question has far-reaching implications for human capital allocation and government personnel policy, yet past research has said little about it. This study targets exactly this gap.

#### **"Civil Servant Fever" and the Appeal of Officialdom**

The reason Chinese civil service positions are so hotly pursued lies largely in their **hidden privileges** and **benefits**. Beyond a respectable legal income, civil servants often also enjoy various kinds of gray income and perks. The literature provides ample evidence of this: after the Reform and Opening-Up, officials who controlled government resources had more rent-seeking opportunities, and corruption grew ever more rampant (Wedeman 2004); even for officials at the lowest level, informal income averaged the equivalent of 20% of legal income, and for ordinary officials the ratio reached as high as 67%. In addition, hidden perks such as discounted housing purchases for officials and publicly funded wining and dining were also very common. These facts show that before the anticorruption campaign, the hidden returns attached to civil service positions were quite considerable, seen by some as part of "corruption within the system." Entering the bureaucracy therefore meant not just a stable salary but also opportunities to obtain extra benefits, which undoubtedly intensified "Civil Servant Fever."

#### **China's Anticorruption Campaign**

The anticorruption campaign that began in 2013 is regarded as the most intense anticorruption action since 1949. Its scale was unprecedented: under the call to "**strike tigers and flies together**" (老虎苍蝇一起打), both high-ranking officials and grassroots cadres were strictly investigated and punished without exception. According to reports, by 2017 more than 335,000 officials had been placed under investigation for corruption, and high-profile "tiger-hunting and fly-swatting" cases frequently appeared in the press and on social media. The campaign was also accompanied by a series of new rules regulating official conduct. For example, the central government's "**Eight-Point Regulation**" (八项规定) aimed to curb publicly funded banquets and extravagant waste and to strictly control spending on official receptions and travel; rules banning the giving and receiving of gifts and high-end entertainment were rolled out as well. These measures focused on **tightening the power at officials' disposal** and **raising the cost of violations**. On the one hand, high-pressure anticorruption greatly increased the probability that corruption would be caught, so that officials who might once have escaped the law could no longer evade punishment. On the other hand, various disciplinary rules cut the public funds officials could dispose of, with gray expenditures such as official receptions and travel placed under strict limits. Notably, the campaign did not directly lower civil servants' legal wages and benefits: it aimed to **regulate behavior**, not to cut pay. As a result, under the campaign's high-pressure atmosphere, the gray benefits that once made civil service positions attractive became hard to obtain, while the corresponding risks and constraints clearly increased. The campaign thus offers an ideal setting for studying corruption governance: within a short period it significantly changed the **incentive structure** of officialdom, providing a natural experiment for observing **changes in people's willingness to apply for the civil service**.

## Research Contributions

The distinctive contribution of this study is to combine anticorruption governance with labor market choice, focusing on **how the anticorruption campaign affects new entrants into the bureaucracy**. Most past anticorruption research has emphasized behavioral changes among incumbent officials or economic performance (e.g., how greater government integrity affects economic growth), while **very few studies have examined the impact of anticorruption on potential civil servants**. This paper fills that gap by posing and answering the important question of how anticorruption affects enthusiasm for civil service applications.

To answer this question, the authors combine theoretical analysis with empirical evidence, which is another key innovation. First, the authors build a concise **incentive-based theoretical model** that clarifies through which channels anticorruption policy may affect individuals' career choices. The model highlights heterogeneity in job seekers' ability and prosocial motivation (prosociality), as well as the "power returns" and corruption risks unique to civil service positions. This theoretical framework enriches the literature on civil servants' self-selection behavior. Second, on the empirical side, the authors use a brand-new data source, a **department-level dataset from the National Civil Service Exam**, and cleverly exploit the fact that central inspections were **rolled out in batches** across departments, treating this as a natural experiment. Through a rigorous difference-in-differences econometric design, the study achieves causal identification of the anticorruption effect. Methodologically, the authors adopt the recently developed "robust DID" estimator (Sun and Abraham 2021) to avoid the bias that traditional two-way fixed effects methods can produce in the presence of heterogeneous treatment effects. Keeping up with methodological advances in this way ensures the reliability and robustness of the estimates.

More broadly, this study links **corruption governance** to the quality of **government human resources**, expanding our understanding of the **external effects of anticorruption policy**. For instance, by examining how anticorruption affects the composition of the civil service workforce, the authors provide new empirical evidence for the discussion of how corruption shapes talent flows and allocation.

## Research Methods

#### **Theoretical Model**

To guide the empirical analysis, the authors first set up a simplified occupational choice model. In the model, individuals have two heterogeneous characteristics: **ability** and **prosocial inclination** (prosociality). Each person faces two career options: **officialdom** (a civil service position) or **non-officialdom** (an ordinary market job). In the non-government sector, a person's income depends on their own ability; high-ability individuals earn higher returns in the private sector. In officialdom, every civil servant receives a fixed legal income (w) and controls a certain amount of discretionary public funds (m) that can be used to **exercise official power**. A civil servant can choose to use the public funds for **embezzlement** (lining their own pockets) or for **providing public services**. Choosing embezzlement carries the risk of being investigated and punished, with probability denoted q (the corruption detection rate); putting the funds into public services carries no detection risk but brings no extra monetary gain to the individual. In the model, prosocial inclination a affects decisions: highly prosocial people feel a large internal penalty (moral cost) when embezzling, while providing public services yields them utility; conversely, those low in prosociality care less about the moral cost of corrupt behavior. Under this setup, each person weighs the expected payoffs in officialdom versus non-officialdom and chooses the career that maximizes their utility.

The anticorruption campaign can be viewed as an exogenous shock that raises q or lowers m (i.e., raising the probability that corruption is detected or reducing officials' discretionary funds), and it may also indirectly affect legal income w (although China's campaign did not directly adjust civil servants' wages). The model predicts that if anticorruption policy lowers the **expected returns** of civil service positions (for example, corruption becomes easier to catch, or the funds available for embezzlement shrink), then rational individuals will be less willing to apply for the civil service. In particular, the model reveals three potential mechanisms:

- 1) **Stepping up corruption detection** (q rises), increasing the risk of corrupt behavior;
- 2) **Restricting the power/resources officials can abuse** (m falls), reducing gray benefits;
- 3) **Lowering legal income** (w falls).

In the anticorruption campaign, mechanisms 1 and 2 are considered the most likely to operate (since the campaign aimed to crack down hard on corruption and rein in privileges), while mechanism 3 is unlikely to be significant (since the campaign did not target wage cuts). Through the theoretical model, the authors make these mechanisms explicit and design the subsequent empirical tests accordingly.

#### **Empirical Design**

The study exploits the institutional setting in which, starting in 2013, central inspection teams inspected government departments in batches, treating this as a "**quasi-experiment**." Specifically, between 2013 and 2017, the Central Commission for Discipline Inspection conducted multiple rounds of inspections of central ministries and directly affiliated agencies. By 2017, about 11.3% of the central departments in the sample had undergone an inspection during this period. Departments that received an anticorruption inspection in a given year can be regarded as the **treatment group**, while departments not yet inspected serve as the **control group**. Because the timing of inspections was random to a considerable degree (not strongly correlated with departments' own characteristics), the inspections can be viewed as an exogenous shock, allowing a **difference-in-differences (DiD)** approach to identify the impact of anticorruption policy. The difference-in-differences comparison is: the change in a department before versus after its inspection, relative to the contemporaneous change in departments that were never or not yet inspected. If, under the parallel-trends assumption, departments' application intensity would have followed similar paths absent the anticorruption campaign, then any significant deviation appearing after an inspection can be attributed to the campaign's effect.

Econometrically, the authors adopt the difference-in-differences estimation procedure proposed by Sun and Abraham (2021) to accommodate the fact that different departments **received treatment at different times**. This method avoids the bias that the traditional two-way fixed effects DID can produce when treatment effects are heterogeneous, ensuring the estimator is robust to **differences in treatment timing**. The authors also conduct an **event-study analysis** to examine whether the treatment and control groups followed similar trajectories before inspections occurred, in order to validate the parallel-trends assumption. The results show no significant difference in application trends between the two groups before treatment, supporting the model's assumptions. In addition, given possible **spillover effects** (i.e., applicants to inspected departments switching to uninspected departments), the authors design a dedicated test to detect this phenomenon. Comparing changes in applications between inspected main departments and their uninspected subordinate units within the same system, they find no clear sign of applicant diversion; that is, spillovers are limited. Finally, the authors use propensity score matching (PSM) and Oster's (2019) method to check for bias from unobserved variables, subjecting the results to robustness checks that all support the main conclusions. In sum, the research design leverages the staggered rollout of anticorruption inspections and uses multiple methods to ensure credible causal identification, providing a powerful empirical tool for evaluating the impact of anticorruption policy.

## Data

This paper uses a novel set of **department-level data** to measure the intensity of civil service applications. The main data source is application and admission information from the **National Civil Service Exam** (NCSE), covering the exam-based recruitment of central departments during 2012-2017. Specifically, for almost all central Party and government organs and directly affiliated units, the data contain the number of job vacancies recruited each year (demand) and the number of applicants (supply), making it possible to compute indicators such as each department's annual **application-to-admission ratio** (applicants / admitted). These data essentially capture the full picture of how central government agencies recruit entry-level civil servants through the civil service exam, and are reasonably representative. It should be noted that China's National Civil Service Exam is mainly used for recruitment by central organs and vertically managed systems, while provincial and municipal governments hold their own provincial exams; even so, the national exam still attracts a large number of candidates nationwide, especially those hoping to work in central organs or their directly affiliated units. Because central organs are highly attractive to candidates in terms of staffing status (编制), compensation, and development opportunities, the data can effectively reflect the overall trend and intensity of "Civil Servant Fever."

The study also draws on other data to test the mechanisms:

- First, the authors use **data on prefecture-level city leaders** to measure changes in the intensity of corruption detection. The data record the tenures of municipal Party secretaries and mayors of prefecture-level cities nationwide from 2000 to 2015, along with removals from office due to corruption. With these data, one can observe whether officials' probability of falling for corruption rose after the anticorruption campaign began.
- Second, the authors hand-collected **"three public expenses" (三公经费) data** for the various departments (more precisely, for the "lines" (条线) of each administrative system), especially entertainment and travel costs (ETC, often regarded as high-risk areas for corruption and misuse of public funds). These data are used to measure the impact of anticorruption inspections on departments' freely disposable funds.
- Third, the authors collected departments' personnel expenditure data, including spending on wages and benefits, to test whether anticorruption affected civil servants' legal income levels.

In terms of data structure, the core analysis uses a **department-year** panel, where "department" refers to each central recruiting unit (including ministries themselves and their subordinate directly affiliated agencies). Each observation corresponds to a department's civil service exam situation in a given year. Because departments were inspected in different years, this data structure, combined with the timing of the policy shock, allows the difference-in-differences strategy described above to be applied. The data's cross-department coverage and time span ensure that the analysis can control for fixed department characteristics and aggregate annual shocks, thereby isolating the effect of the specific factor of anticorruption inspections. Overall, the data are novel and authoritative, enabling the study to characterize in depth the relationship between the anticorruption campaign and civil service application intensity.

## Interpreting the Results

#### **Main Findings**

**Civil service application fever cooled significantly.** The empirical results show that after the anticorruption campaign was launched, enthusiasm for civil service applications fell markedly. Using difference-in-differences estimation, the authors find that **once a department underwent an inspection, its average number of applicants dropped significantly**. Quantitatively, after an inspection the number of applicants per position **fell by about 69 people** compared with before, a decline equal to 37% of the pre-inspection average. This is a substantial drop, indicating that the campaign's impact on civil service recruitment was not subtle but quite dramatic. More importantly, the decline stems mainly from candidates' reduced willingness to sign up (a fall in supply), not from departments cutting recruitment quotas (a change in demand). In other words, the incentive changes brought by anticorruption led a sizable share of potential candidates to **choose not to apply** for civil service positions, rather than there merely being an adjustment on the position-supply side. This finding survives a series of robustness checks: controlling for potential confounders, using matched samples, and ruling out the possibility of candidates switching to uninspected departments all leave the direction and significance of the main result intact. One can therefore conclude with reasonable confidence that **China's anticorruption campaign effectively "cooled" the previously red-hot enthusiasm for civil service applications**. This result vividly reflects the significant impact of anticorruption policy on labor supply behavior.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250319171521888.png)

**Mechanism analysis**: To understand how anticorruption caused the drop in applicants, the authors test the three mechanisms proposed by the model: stronger corruption detection (q), restrictions on gray power-derived benefits (m), and changes in legal income (w). The evidence shows that the first two mechanisms indeed played important roles, while the third was essentially absent. The detailed analysis is as follows:

- **Stronger corruption detection**: Since the campaign began, **the probability that corrupt officials are caught has risen significantly**. Regression results using the prefecture-level official data show that after a province was inspected, the probability that a top local official (municipal Party secretary or mayor) was removed from office for corruption rose by about 1.2-2.1 percentage points. Given a baseline corruption-removal rate of only about 4%, this increase is very significant. If this holds for senior officials, it applies all the more to ordinary cadres, so one can infer that the campaign substantially strengthened the monitoring and investigation of officials at all levels (i.e., q rose sharply). Indeed, even senior officials who once held great power and might have been "spared the knife" (such as former Politburo Standing Committee member Zhou Yongkang) were brought to justice, and "anticorruption with no blind spots" heightened all potential civil servants' awareness of corruption risk. Consequently, those hoping to use civil service positions for private gain backed off because of the **sharply increased risk of corruption** and no longer joined the applicant pool.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250319171718669.png)
- **Gray power-derived benefits curtailed**: The campaign was equally effective in cutting the resources at officials' disposal. Compiling statistics on entertainment and travel expenses within departments' "three public expenses," the authors find that **once a department was inspected, its spending on publicly funded dining, travel, and the like fell by about 10.8% on average**. These funds are seen as gray money officials can use for private gain (for example, reimbursing personal consumption in the name of official business), so their contraction means **the room for officials to abuse their powers for extra benefits was squeezed**. This result corresponds to the m-reduction mechanism in the model. Further event-study analysis shows that the decline in such spending appeared immediately after inspections and was persistent. Evidently, the new anticorruption rules (such as the Eight-Point Regulation) genuinely restricted officials' privileges and hedonistic spending. For potential candidates, these hidden perks attached to civil service positions were much diminished, naturally weakening the positions' appeal and leading some of those previously enamored of "gray income" to give up on applying.![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250319171758821.png)
- **Legal income almost unchanged**: In contrast to the two changes above, **civil servants' legal income was not noticeably affected by the campaign**. Analysis of departments' personnel expenditure data shows that being inspected or not had no significant effect on departments' wage and benefit spending. This result is to be expected: the campaign focused on punishing corruption and regulating behavior, not on cutting civil servants' normal wages. The w mechanism in the model is therefore essentially inoperative: legal compensation remained stable and is not what drove applications down. This point matters because it shows that **the cooling of application enthusiasm is attributable mainly to the disappearance of corruption dividends**, not to changes in normal pay. In other words, those deterred by anticorruption stepped back not because wages fell (in fact wages did not change), but because "gray income" and privileges were gone or had become riskier.

Putting the evidence together, a clear conclusion emerges: **China's anticorruption campaign significantly lowered the expected returns of civil service positions through two channels, raising corruption risk and squeezing gray benefits, thereby reducing people's willingness to apply for the civil service**. Conversely, the fact that legal income did not change means anticorruption itself did not make civil service jobs so "austere" that no one wanted them; rather, it stripped away the illegitimate portion of the returns. In other words, anticorruption produced a **"crowding-out effect"**: it crowded out the talent who came mainly for the illicit gains. Under the combined influence of these mechanisms, Civil Servant Fever cooled markedly, confirming the predictions of the theoretical model.

#### **Effects on the Characteristics of New Recruits**

Beyond changes in quantity, the authors go further to explore how anticorruption may affect the **group quality** of those entering officialdom. Because direct data are hard to obtain (changes in new civil servants' ability and character cannot be observed directly), the authors turn to the model for inference and verify it through numerical simulations. The theoretical analysis predicts that **anticorruption may lower the average ability of new entrants to the civil service while raising their average prosocial motivation**.

The intuition is this: people with strong ability but a weak moral sense might originally have been attracted by officialdom's gray benefits (since they are better able to exploit privileges for profit), but under high-pressure anticorruption such people lean more toward non-public-sector careers where their ability earns high returns. Conversely, those willing to contribute to public service and uninterested in corruption may stay in and enter the civil service even with only middling ability. The upshot is that the newcomers entering officialdom are mostly people who are **cleaner and more willing to serve the public interest**, but their average market ability (such as business acumen and professional skills) may not match that of the cohorts who joined officialdom before. Simulating the model repeatedly under different parameters, the authors find that the simulation results **support this prediction**: as anticorruption intensifies, the average ability of newly recruited civil servants falls somewhat, while their average prosociality rises.

Of course, the authors also point out that the simulation is only a qualitative illustration, and the actual magnitudes should be treated with caution because the model is not calibrated to real data. Nevertheless, this finding alerts us that while the anticorruption campaign cleanses the civil service, it may bring changes to the talent structure: **fewer profit-savvy high performers enter officialdom, but those who remain may be more public-spirited**. This has complex implications for government governance capacity and requires further research and evaluation.

## Conclusions and Discussion

#### **Summary of Findings**

Combining theory and empirics, this paper analyzes in depth how China's anticorruption campaign, begun in 2013, affected "Civil Servant Fever." The conclusions show that **the campaign significantly reduced people's willingness to apply for the civil service**, cooling a fever that had long refused to break. The decline is mainly due to lower expected returns to corruption and higher risk, leading some who had treated the civil service as a route to gray income to give up. As for the specific mechanisms, the evidence clearly shows that the campaign **raised the probability of corruption detection** and **squeezed the gray funds at officials' disposal**, thereby lowering the expected returns of civil service positions. Meanwhile, civil servants' legal income was unaffected, meaning the campaign's "strike zone" was concentrated on illicit gains and did not harm normal compensation. Under this reshaped set of incentives, civil service positions became less attractive to certain groups and the number of applicants slid, confirming the authors' hypothesis.

#### **Policy Implications**

This finding carries policy implications on several fronts:

- First, it reveals the **potential costs of anticorruption policy**: while anticorruption raises the integrity of officialdom, it makes the civil service system less attractive to part of the talent pool. This may affect the state administrative system's talent reserves and **governance capacity**. If outstanding talent turns elsewhere because there is less "gravy" in officialdom, the government may face difficulty recruiting highly capable staff. For a country like China, where government functions are extensive, this could weaken administrative efficiency to some degree; it deserves attention and should be addressed through other means (such as improving the above-board, "sunshine" component of civil servants' compensation).
- Second, anticorruption pushing some talent toward the non-public sector may have positive effects from the standpoint of **economy-wide resource allocation**. As Murphy, Shleifer, and Vishny (1991) pointed out, too much talent pouring into rent-seeking sectors is bad for economic development. By "driving" some high-ability but profit-seeking individuals toward the more productive private sector, anticorruption may raise the vitality and efficiency of the whole economy. From a macro perspective, then, this reallocation of talent is not necessarily a bad thing and may to some extent optimize the allocation of human capital between officialdom and the market.
- Third, the results show that **in the past some people did indeed become civil servants for the corruption opportunities**. That being so, personnel system reform should consider how to **screen out applicants with impure motives**. This suggests to policymakers that beyond anticorruption itself, vetting at the recruitment stage needs strengthening, for example by increasing the weight given to assessing candidates' moral character and public service motivation, to prevent people whose "virtue does not match their talent" from entering the system.

Overall, this study highlights the complex effects of anticorruption policy: there are the direct integrity gains that anticorruption brings, but also indirect effects on officialdom's attractiveness to talent, and policymaking needs to strike a balance between the two.

#### **Limitations and Future Research Directions**

The authors also candidly acknowledge the study's limitations and look ahead to future research:

- First, regarding **external validity**: since the study focuses on the Chinese context, how far its conclusions apply to other countries should be treated with caution. Certain features of China's bureaucracy (such as officials' career advancement being tied to the political system, and the state's strong role in the economy giving civil service positions relatively high rents) are not necessarily the same elsewhere. Hence in countries with lower corruption or fewer civil service privileges, the effect of anticorruption on application willingness may be much weaker. However, if other countries exhibit similar phenomena of "high perks for low-level posts" or "the civil service as a gateway to political careers," some of this study's findings may carry over. Future work could use cross-country comparisons to test whether the effect of anticorruption on civil service recruitment is universal.
- Second, the study period is **relatively static**, with no major economic fluctuations. The authors note that **changes in the macro environment may alter the anticorruption effect**. For example, the post-pandemic economic downturn has increased employment pressure; against that backdrop, even with anticorruption ongoing, the stability of the civil service "iron rice bowl" has driven application fever back up. This reminds us that anticorruption is only one of many factors affecting applications; economic cycles and labor market conditions also significantly shape Civil Servant Fever. Future research could explore how the anticorruption effect differs across industry conditions and economic cycles, and how it interacts with other factors (such as job security and retirement security).
- Third, owing to data limitations, the paper's discussion of **bureaucratic workforce quality** relies mainly on model simulation and lacks direct empirical measurement. If follow-up research can obtain data on candidate quality and the performance of those recruited (for example, exam scores, probation-period evaluation results, or career performance), it will help verify whether anticorruption really changed the ability and moral composition of the civil service. This is very important for assessing the long-term governance effects of anticorruption.

In sum, this study reveals the profound impact of the anticorruption campaign on civil service application behavior. It both helps explain the changes in China's Civil Servant Fever in recent years and offers lessons for other countries thinking about how to balance anticorruption with attracting talent. While consolidating the existing conclusions, more research is needed in the future to analyze the full effects of anticorruption in depth, so that more complete anticorruption and personnel policies can be designed.

## Extra

- As the paper itself says, the study period is fairly short and does not take the post-pandemic situation into account. And the anecdotal "statistics" from my own circle are: more and more classmates and junior schoolmates are taking the civil service exam, and civil-service exam-prep providers are booming too. The chill has been passed on to everyone, and everybody is thinking of warming up inside the system.
- The paper does not seem to consider the problem of **concurrent event shocks**. 2013-2017 was also a period of breakneck mobile internet growth and "mass entrepreneurship and innovation" (**Internet Plus**). Although the DID design can mitigate this problem to some extent, if the analysis incorporated differences in outside employment potential across recruiting departments, it could alleviate the possible **overestimation** in this paper.
