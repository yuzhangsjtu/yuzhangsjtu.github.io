This study investigates the impact of China's large-scale anticorruption campaign, initiated in 2013, on the willingness to apply for civil service positions. Using data from the National Civil Service Exam (NCSE) between 2012 and 2017, the authors exploit the staggered implementation of central inspection tours across government departments to construct a difference-in-differences (DID) model. They find that departments subjected to inspection experienced a significant decline in application enthusiasm, with the number of applicants per position decreasing by approximately 37%. Further analysis indicates that this effect is primarily channeled through increasing the probability of corruption detection and restricting officials’ discretionary funds, rather than affecting officials' legal income. Theoretical and simulation analyses suggest that anticorruption actions may lead to a decrease in the average ability but an increase in the prosociality of new entrants into the bureaucracy. This paper offers important empirical evidence on the impact of anticorruption campaigns on the selection of civil service talent and governance capacity.

## Introduction

Civil service positions in China have historically been highly sought after, a phenomenon known as **"Civil Servant Fever."** In traditional Chinese culture, entering the bureaucracy is seen as a path to **status** and **stability**. Modern research also indicates that many talented individuals prefer government sector employment over entrepreneurship (e.g., [EJ-Good Gaokao Scores, Less Entrepreneurial Drive](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/ak8Kno7n-eYBL2aaC0mj2Q)). It is reported that in 2012, only **2%** of university graduates in **Singapore** were willing to apply for civil service jobs, whereas this proportion was as high as **76.4%** in **China**. Concurrently, corruption is widespread within the bureaucracy, eroding administrative efficiency and damaging economic development. Policy makers and scholars have long explored reforms to **curb corruption**. For instance, Becker and Stigler (1974) proposed that optimizing **penalty** and **compensation** mechanisms could effectively constrain official behavior.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250319165527584.png)

To facilitate understanding of China’s "Civil Servant Fever" phenomenon and the impact of the anticorruption campaign, the figure ⬆️ above illustrates the trend of the "**Application-Admission Ratio"** (number of applicants / number of recruited vacancies) in the NCSE from 2002 to 2017. It can be observed that the ratio peaked in 2013—averaging about 70 applicants competing for a single position, with an acceptance rate around 1.4%, making competition more intense than the admissions rates at some **Ivy League** universities. However, since 2013, this upward trend halted abruptly and began to decline. Coincidentally, 2013 was the starting point of this unprecedented anticorruption campaign. This contemporaneous trend reversal raises the core research question of this paper: **What is the effect of an anticorruption campaign on the willingness to enter the bureaucracy (i.e., "Civil Servant Fever")?** In other words, does strengthening anti-corruption measures dampen enthusiasm for applying to the civil service? This is a domain that few prior studies have directly explored. This research aims to fill this gap, rigorously estimating the causal impact of the anticorruption campaign on civil service application behavior through a combination of theoretical models and empirical analysis, and deeply probing the underlying mechanisms. This paper focuses specifically on China's anticorruption campaign launched in 2013, one of the most stringent anti-corruption actions undertaken since the founding of the People's Republic. Using China as a case study, the study aims to provide empirical evidence on how anti-corruption policies affect entry into the bureaucracy, and serve as a reference for similar issues in other contexts.

## Research Background

#### Existing Research on Corruption and Anti-Corruption

Corruption is widely believed to undermine government governance and economic development. Numerous studies examine the impact of anti-corruption measures on the behavior of incumbent officials and economic performance. For example, research has investigated how anti-corruption enhances **firm** performance or alters government expenditure efficiency. In the Chinese context, scholars have found associations between anticorruption actions and improved company performance, local economic development, etc. (e.g., Xu and Yano 2017; Chen and Kung 2019; Kong and Qin 2021; see). In contrast, however, the impact of anticorruption on **potential entrants to the bureaucracy** has largely escaped attention. This represents a significant research gap: if anti-corruption alters the attractiveness of civil service posts, will those originally intending to apply be deterred? This question holds profound implications for human capital allocation and government personnel policy, yet previous research has seldom addressed it. The present study directly targets this gap.

#### "Civil Servant Fever" and the Attraction of Officialdom

The intense popularity of Chinese civil service positions largely stems from their implicit **privileges** and **benefits**. Besides a respectable legal income, civil servants typically enjoy various grey incomes and perks. The literature provides ample evidence: after the Reform and Opening-Up policy was introduced, officials controlling government resources had more rent-seeking opportunities, and corruption intensified rapidly (Wedeman 2004); even for low-ranking local officials, unregistered income equivalent to, on average, 20 percent of regular income, elevating to 67 percent for an average regular official. Furthermore, implicit benefits such as housing purchase subsidies and dining publicly are common (Wedeman 2004, 2012; Fan and Grossman 2005, n.d. and reference within). This distributionally illustrates that prior to the anticorruption campaign, the implicit returns associated with civil service posts were substantial, considered by some as part of "endemic institutional corruption." Therefore, access to the bureaucratic structure portends permissible income plus corruption income prospects, inevitably surfeit feeding demand feeding "Civil Servant Fever" materially.
