> In 1877, England experienced a sharp and widespread decline in fertility. This inflection arrived so swiftly that neither existing economic-factor explanations nor cultural-diffusion theories could offer a convincing answer.
>
> To solve the puzzle, the authors adopt a cultural lens, employ cross-country comparisons and a difference-in-differences approach, and propose a new explanation: the sudden drop in English fertility in 1877 was largely driven by a rapid shift in cultural norms triggered by a “**trial over contraceptive advocacy**,” a shift that also manifested among populations of English descent overseas.

**Title:** Culture and the Historical Fertility Transition

**Journal:** *The Review of Economic Studies*

**Authors:**

- <u>Brian Beach</u>：Associate Professor of Economics and the Director of Graduate Studies for the Economics Department at Vanderbilt University. His research spans the fields of economic history, public economics, and health. Much of his work examines the extent to which government policies and institutions affect social welfare.
- <u>W Walker Hanlon</u>：Associate Professor, Department of Economics, Northwestern University. His research focuses on understanding how economies evolve over the long run using novel historical data.

## Preface

- The **fertility transition** and the **Industrial Revolution** are the two key turning points on the path to modern economic growth. The Industrial Revolution inaugurated sustained technological progress, but early output gains were largely offset by rapid population growth, leaving per capita income increases modest. Only once the fertility transition began and population growth slowed did per capita income rise persistently.
- In the 1970s, the European Fertility Project proposed a model of fertility transition in which fertility decline occurs when three preconditions are all met (Ready, Willing, and Able):
  - **Readiness**：Fertility control must fall within the calculus of conscious choice. That is, limiting births must be socially and morally acceptable; people need to regard family size as an economic matter that can be adjusted according to individual or household circumstances, rather than leaving it entirely to fate.
  - **Willingness**：For a household, the cost–benefit trade-off of fertility control must tilt toward having fewer children. In other words, rising costs of child-raising (e.g., greater spending on children’s education) or falling benefits (e.g., declining marginal value of child labour) increase households’ motivation to exercise deliberate fertility control.
  - **Ability**：Households must possess the knowledge and means to control fertility. This includes the spread of contraceptive information and the accessibility of contraceptive methods.
- Economists have mainly focused on how economic incentives affect fertility, with a vast body of work employing the Becker and Lewis (1973) **“quantity–quality”** trade-off framework and emphasising how rising demand for education shapes family size. Others concentrate on the role of women’s education and labour-market opportunities.
- More recently, a growing economics literature has begun to stress the influence of culture and social norms on economic decisions, including fertility; several cross-country studies find that fertility decline diffuses among culturally proximate countries. (Next read planned: *EJ* – the spread of fertility-decline behaviour from francophone regions to the rest of Europe, original title **Fertility and Modernity**.)
- Data for England and Wales from 1851 to 1891 (see the figure below) show that fertility began to fall sharply in 1877. This large, rapid and widespread change, visible across all regions of England, is hard to explain either with standard models based on economic factors such as rising returns to education, or with the slow diffusion of culture that has been emphasised recently. What, then, prompted English people suddenly to start having fewer children?![Screenshot 2024-04-25 at 14.16.29](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/Screenshot%202024-04-25%20at%2014.16.29.png)

## The Bradlaugh–Besant Trial as a Major Cultural Shock

The Bradlaugh–Besant trial of 1877, referred to hereafter as the BB trial or BB case.

> On Thursday, 5 April 1877, Annie Besant and Charles Bradlaugh stood trial for republishing Charles Knowlton’s *Fruits of Philosophy*, a book that not only set out methods of **birth control** but also stressed the economic and health benefits of family planning. Bradlaugh and Besant believed the book was particularly important for working‑class and poor families, who were often plunged into economic distress by having too many children. Yet the church and society of the time reacted with fierce hostility, regarding its content as a threat to morality.
>
> The pamphlet had been on sale for more than forty years before this reissue and was allegedly in direct opposition to the 1857 Obscene Publications Act, which banned all obscene literature. Besant and Bradlaugh, both prominent atheists and “free thinkers,” were quickly demonized by Victorian society. The trial lasted four days and ended in acquittal; it was covered heavily, if harshly, in the British press, and **contraception** was mentioned in the national conversation for the first time. The publicity led directly to a surge in sales of the book itself and brought the reality of birth control and sexual emancipation to people across the country.
>
> “One effect of the trial was that it legitimised newspaper coverage of a topic that had previously been socially taboo in Victorian society.”

## Identification Strategy

- **Cross‑country comparative analysis**：Compare fertility changes before and after 1877 among English‑origin and non‑English‑origin populations in England, Canada, the United States and South Africa. Although these countries all hosted sizeable English‑origin immigrant communities, they differed considerably in level of economic development, policy environment and so on. By examining English‑origin and non‑English‑origin groups across countries and by tracing how each group’s fertility evolved around 1877, the paper seeks to control for other factors and isolate the influence of **culture**.
- **DID**：The BB trial triggered wide and profound public discussion in British society, and that discussion spread largely through the press. To identify the effect of trial‑related reporting and debate on fertility, the paper uses a difference‑in‑differences strategy. Concretely, it exploits regional variation across England in newspaper coverage just before and after 1877. Some districts happened to acquire a new newspaper before the trial, so local residents had a greater chance of being exposed to related coverage and discussion; other districts gained a new paper only after the trial ended and were therefore less affected by the media.
- **IV**：Although DID alleviates the omitted‑variable problem to some extent, endogeneity bias may remain if omitted factors that are correlated with media exposure also change over time. For example, economically buoyant districts may have been both more capable of launching a new newspaper and more motivated to control fertility. To address this issue further, the paper uses an instrumental‑variable approach, instrumenting media exposure with the timing of new newspaper entry. This relies on two identifying assumptions: that the founding date of a newspaper is unlikely to be directly related to fertility in a specific year, and that once founded a newspaper permanently affects the local media environment and is correlated with the intensity of coverage during the trial.
- **Event Study**：Treat 1877 as the pivotal juncture and track fertility levels across different years before and after that date for both Britain and overseas English‑origin populations. By verifying whether fertility remained steady before the trial and fell markedly afterwards, the paper aims to establish that 1877 genuinely marked a moment of significant change.

## Results

- From 1877 onward, fertility in England and Wales dropped precipitously. A similar change is observed among the English‑origin population in Canada but not among their **francophone neighbours**. Parallel patterns emerge when comparing English‑origin with other non‑English European‑origin groups resident in the same locations in the United States and South Africa.
- The steep fertility decline that began in 1877 occurred only among the English and overseas populations of English descent!
- The BB case received extensive coverage inside Britain! The figure below plots the number of newspaper references to the BB case’s pivotal matter: it surged.![image-20240425145514907](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240425145514907.png)
- The BB case was also extensively covered in overseas English‑language newspapers; non‑English papers overseas showed little interest in the case. The Google N‑gram database shows a sharp rise in terms related to the trial in the years after 1877 (and entries referencing the case use British English idioms far more frequently than American English ones, indicating that culturally English populations took a stronger interest).![image-20240425145822137](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240425145822137.png)
- Within Britain, areas with higher media exposure experienced larger fertility declines.
- The BB case had no significant effect on the marriage rate; it mainly affected marital fertility. (Using the conceivable upper bound of the effect on the marriage rate for a counterfactual estimate shows that the contribution of a falling marriage rate to the fertility drop was negligible.)

## Conclusion

- The abrupt English fertility decline that began in 1877 was largely driven by a rapid shift in cultural norms sparked by the “**trial over contraceptive advocacy**,” a shift also visible among populations of English descent overseas;
- **Culture** and **linguistic links** can act as channels through which changing social norms spread across the world, producing rapid yet meaningful behavioural change;
- Economic fundamentals define the optimal family size, but cultural norms constrain households’ ability to get close to that optimum; the unravelling of cultural norms – or the dissemination of contraceptive knowledge – can then prompt swift changes in household behaviour. In other words, the fertility transition is not always a drawn‑out process; certain social events can bring about a rapid onset of fertility decline.

## Extras
- The BB trial’s aftermath brought a notable figure to prominence: Annie Besant, one half of the BB partnership, was dubbed the “contraceptive queen” by the press for her radical stance during the trial. Afterwards she devoted herself to the women’s suffrage movement and socialist causes, eventually becoming President of the Indian National Congress.
- Malthusian League：Founded in July 1877 – initially in the context of the “Knowlton Trial” of Annie Besant and Charles Bradlaugh – and dissolved in 1927, the Malthusian League argued that **excessively rapid population growth was the root cause of poverty and social problems**. It held that deliberate family limitation helped improve workers’ living conditions and reduce social strains. To this end the League undertook public lectures, publications, leafleting and other outreach activities aimed at disseminating contraceptive knowledge and fostering societal acceptance of family planning.
