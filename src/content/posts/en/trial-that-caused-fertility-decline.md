> In 1877, British fertility experienced a sharp and widespread decline. This turning point came about so quickly that neither existing economic-factor explanations nor cultural-diffusion theories could provide a convincing answer.
>
> To crack this puzzle, the authors take a cultural perspective and, using cross-country comparison and a difference-in-differences method, propose a new explanation: the sudden drop in British fertility in 1877 was largely driven by a rapid shift in cultural norms triggered by a "**trial over contraceptive advocacy**," a shift that was also reflected among populations of English descent overseas.

**Title:** Culture and the Historical Fertility Transition

**Journal:** *The Review of Economic Studies*

**Authors:**

- <u>Brian Beach</u>: Associate Professor of Economics and the Director of Graduate Studies for the Economics Department at Vanderbilt University. His research spans the fields of economic history, public economics, and health. Much of his work examines the extent to which government policies and institutions affect social welfare.

## Preface

- The **fertility transition** and the **Industrial Revolution** are the two key turning points on the path to modern economic growth. The Industrial Revolution inaugurated sustained technological progress, but early output gains were largely offset by rapid population growth, leaving per capita income growth limited. Only once the fertility transition began and population growth slowed did per capita income rise persistently.
- In the 1970s, the European Fertility Project proposed a model of fertility transition, holding that fertility decline occurs when three conditions are all present (Ready, Willing, and Able):
  - **Readiness**: Fertility control must fall within the calculus of conscious choice. This means that limiting births must be morally and socially acceptable; people must be willing to regard fertility as an economic behaviour that can be adjusted according to individual or household circumstances, rather than left entirely to fate.
  - **Willingness**: For a household, the cost-benefit trade-off of fertility control must favour having fewer children. That is, rising costs of raising children (such as increased spending on children's education) or falling benefits (such as a decline in the marginal value of children as labour) raise households' willingness to actively control fertility.
  - **Ability**: Households must possess the knowledge and means to control fertility. This includes the spread of contraceptive knowledge and the accessibility of contraceptive tools.
- Economists have mainly focused on how economic incentives affect fertility, with a large body of work adopting the Becker and Lewis (1973) "**quantity-quality**" trade-off framework and emphasising how rising demand for education affects family size. Others focus on the role of women's education and labour-market opportunities.
- More recently, a growing economics literature has begun to emphasise the influence of culture and social norms on economic decisions, fertility included, and some cross-country studies find that fertility decline diffuses among culturally proximate countries. (Planned for the next issue: EJ – the behaviour of fertility decline spreading from francophone regions to the rest of Europe, original title **Fertility and Modernity**.)
- Fertility data for England and Wales from 1851 to 1891 (see the figure below) show that fertility began to decline sharply from 1877. Such a large, rapid and widespread change, appearing across all regions of Britain, is hard to explain either with standard models based on economic factors such as rising returns to education, or with the slow cultural diffusion emphasised recently. So what made British people suddenly start having fewer children?![Screenshot 2024-04-25 at 14.16.29](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/Screenshot%202024-04-25%20at%2014.16.29.png)

## The BB Trial as a Major Cultural Shock

The Bradlaugh–Besant trial in 1877, hereafter referred to as the BB case or BB trial.

> On Thursday, 5 April 1877, Annie Besant and Charles Bradlaugh stood trial for republishing Charles Knowlton's *Fruits of Philosophy*, a book that not only set out methods of **birth control** but also stressed the economic and health benefits of family planning. Bradlaugh and Besant believed the book was especially important for working-class and poor families, who were often plunged into economic hardship by having too many children. Yet society and the church of the time expressed strong revulsion at its content, regarding it as corrupting to morality.
>
> This pamphlet had already been on sale for more than forty years before its republication, and was alleged to stand in direct opposition to the 1857 Obscene Publications Act, which banned all obscene literature. Both Besant and Bradlaugh were prominent atheists and "free thinkers," and were quickly demonized by Victorian society. The trial lasted four days and ended in acquittal; it was heavily reported by the British press, and although the coverage was harsh, it was the first time **contraception** had been mentioned on a national scale. The publicity led directly to a surge in sales of the book itself and brought the reality of birth control and sexual emancipation to all parts of the country.
>
> "One effect of the trial was to legitimise the reporting of a topic that had been taboo in Victorian society."
>

## Identification Strategy

- **Cross-country comparative analysis**: Compare fertility changes around 1877 among English-origin and non-English-origin populations in England, Canada, the United States, South Africa and elsewhere. Although these countries all hosted sizeable English-origin immigrant populations, they differed considerably in level of economic development, policy environment and other respects. By comparing English-origin and non-English-origin populations across countries, and by longitudinally tracing changes in the fertility of different population groups around 1877, the paper attempts to control for other factors and highlight the influence of **culture**.
- **DID**: The BB trial sparked wide and profound discussion in British society, and this discussion spread largely through the news media. To identify the effect of trial-related reporting and discussion on fertility, the paper adopts a DID method. Specifically, it exploits regional variation across Britain in the degree of news-media coverage around 1877. Some areas happened to acquire a new newspaper before the trial, so local residents were more likely to be exposed to the relevant coverage and discussion; other areas did not acquire a new newspaper until after the trial ended, and were therefore relatively less affected by the media.
- **IV**: Although DID can alleviate the omitted-variable problem to some extent, endogeneity bias may still remain if certain omitted factors happen to be correlated with the degree of media exposure and vary over time. For example, economically prosperous areas may be better positioned to found a new newspaper while also being more motivated to control fertility. To address this issue further, the paper adopts an instrumental-variable method. Specifically, it uses the founding time of a new newspaper as an instrument for media exposure. This rests on two identifying assumptions: first, that the founding time of a newspaper is unlikely to be directly related to fertility in a specific year; second, that once founded, a newspaper continues to affect the local media environment and is correlated with the intensity of coverage during the trial.
- **Event Study**: Treat 1877 as a key node and track the fertility levels of British and overseas English-origin populations across different years before and after that year. By observing whether fertility remained steady before the trial and declined significantly afterward, the paper seeks to confirm that 1877 did indeed mark an important point of change.

## Results

- From 1877 onward, fertility in England and Wales declined sharply. A similar change is observed among the English-origin population in Canada, but not among their **francophone neighbours**. A similar pattern is also observed when comparing English-origin populations with other non-English European-origin populations resident in the same locations in the United States and South Africa.
- The sharp fertility decline that began in 1877 occurred only among the English and overseas populations of English descent!
- The BB case received extensive coverage within Britain! The figure below shows the number of times the key terms of the BB case appeared in newspapers, revealing a surge!![image-20240425145514907](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240425145514907.png)
- The BB case was also extensively covered among overseas English-speaking populations, while overseas non-English newspapers took no interest in the case. The Google N-gram database shows a sharp increase in terms related to the trial in the years after 1877 (and entries expressing the BB case in British English are far more numerous than those in American English, indicating that culturally English populations took a greater interest in the case).![image-20240425145822137](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240425145822137.png)
- In Britain, areas with higher media exposure experienced larger fertility declines.
- The BB case had no significant effect on the marriage rate; it mainly affected the number of births after marriage. (Using a plausible upper bound on the effect on the marriage rate for a counterfactual estimate, the fertility decline brought about by a falling marriage rate was negligible.)

## Conclusion

- The sudden drop in British fertility in 1877 was largely driven by a rapid shift in cultural norms triggered by a "**trial over contraceptive advocacy**," a shift that was also reflected among populations of English descent overseas;
- **Culture** and **linguistic links** can serve as channels through which changing social norms spread across the world, producing rapid and meaningful behavioural change;
- Economic forces determine the optimal family size, but cultural norms constrain how close households come to that optimal fertility size; however, the collapse of cultural norms or the spread of contraceptive knowledge produces rapid changes in household behaviour. In other words, the fertility transition is sometimes not a long-term process; certain social events can cause a fertility decline to appear rapidly.

## Special Note

- Annie Besant (one half of BB) was dubbed the "contraceptive queen" by the press for her radical performance during the trial. After the trial ended, she turned to the women's rights movement and socialist causes, and eventually became President of the Indian National Congress.
- Malthusian League: The Malthusian League was originally founded during the "Knowlton Trial" of Annie Besant and Charles Bradlaugh in July 1877, and was dissolved in 1927. The Malthusian League argued that **excessively rapid population growth was the root cause of poverty and social problems**. It held that controlled limitation of births would help improve the living conditions of the working class and ease social pressures. To this end, the League actively undertook public lectures, published periodicals, distributed leaflets and other activities, aiming to spread knowledge of birth control among the public and promote societal acceptance of contraception.
