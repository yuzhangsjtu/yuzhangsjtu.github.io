> This is an analytical review published in ***Science***. Systematically analyzing the developmental trajectories of more than 34,000 world-leading performers across science, music, sport, chess, and other domains, it finds that **those who become famous young and those who ultimately reach the very peak of human capability are essentially two distinct groups**. Contrary to the traditional view of early specialization and massive **deliberate practice**, those who eventually reach the world's summit often performed unremarkably in their early years and progressed more slowly — but they ranged widely across multiple disciplines or events (i.e., **multidisciplinary practice**) and began specialized training later. The likely reasons: broad exploration helps find the best-matched lane, accumulates stronger "learning capital," and lowers long-run risks such as injury and **burnout**. The article accordingly criticizes talent systems that select on early results and intensify specialization, which may systematically miss the people with genuine long-term world-class potential.

**Title:** Recent discoveries on the acquisition of the highest levels of human performance

**Journal:** *Science*

- *Arne Güllich*: Professor of sport science at RPTU Kaiserslautern-Landau, Germany. Research spans child and youth sport, talent development and elite sport, sport development and organizations, school sport, and evaluation research.

## Introduction

Science has long been interested in how exceptional people achieve outstanding accomplishment. Decades of prior research focused mainly on **young people** and **sub-elite** performers — students, youth athletes, junior chess players, conservatory students. The traditional view holds that, within these populations, those who perform better early and invest heavily in specialized practice go on to higher achievement. In other words, individuals who stand out in **childhood** or **adolescence** and undergo intensive specialized training are seen as strong candidates for **future** excellence. On this logic, many elite schools, universities, conservatories, and youth sport academies select top young talent early and accelerate their development through intensified specialization — an **early specialization** model: identify the prodigy early, focus their training, and hope they peak as adults.

This traditional understanding raises two key questions:

- **First**, are the standout performers in youth and the standout performers at adult peak the same people? In other words, **do junior champions remain champions as adults**?
- **Second**, do the factors that predict youthful excellence (early results, training volume) **equally** predict adult peak excellence?

Only recently have these questions been studied systematically in world-class populations. Traditional research extrapolated from general or sub-elite samples, lacking direct tests of the developmental trajectories and predictors of those truly at the top of their fields as adults.

This review synthesizes developmental data from more than 34,000 adult international top performers across domains: **science** (e.g., Nobel laureates), **classical music** (history's most renowned composers), **sport** (Olympic and world champions), and **mind games** (chess grandmasters and world-ranked players). For example, science compares **Nobel laureates** with **nominated but unawarded** scientists; music covers the success of works by the world's most famous composers; sport covers Olympic medalists versus other national-level athletes; chess covers the development of top junior versus top adult players. Each domain includes longitudinal tracking of early and later performance, or retrospective measures of early experience.

**Findings**:

- Youth standouts and adult world-beaters are largely two **discrete** populations, with individual overlap typically below 15%;
- Compared with peers who ultimately reach only sub-elite levels, those who become world-class **masters** performed worse and progressed more slowly early on;
- The factors predicting early success (heavy domain-specific practice, little multidisciplinary practice, fast early progress) and those predicting adult world-class success (less domain-specific practice, more multidisciplinary practice, flatter early progress) show **opposite** patterns.

These highly consistent cross-domain findings challenge existing theories of expertise acquisition (such as **deliberate practice**) and motivate new explanatory hypotheses: the search-and-match hypothesis, the augmented learning capital hypothesis, and the circumscribed risks hypothesis.

## How to Define and Study "the World's Best"

(Below, "performers" follows the original.)

Not every domain suits the study of the upper limits of human performance. An ideal research domain should meet four **key criteria**:

- Objective, internationally standardized performance metrics exist;
- These metrics reliably distinguish performance levels — especially **the world's best** from **the next best**;
- Large numbers of participants strive long-term to improve;
- Predictor data spanning performers' full careers are available.

By these criteria, **sport** satisfies all four conditions, making it the ideal empirical proving ground — and the first domain in which the key questions of exceptional performance were studied comprehensively. Research on world-class performers in other domains is more limited, but existing evidence is highly consistent across fields.

The review includes 19 datasets totaling 34,839 adult international top performers (see Table 1). For comparison with prior research, it also reviews 66 representative studies of youth and **sub-elite performers**. The design combines prospective studies (tracking youth standouts into adulthood) and retrospective studies (tracing adult standouts back to their youth records). Combining the two allows a formula-based, precise quantification of the individual overlap between early and later top performers.

## Core Findings

#### **Early stars and late masters are two different groups**

Across all examined domains, **youth top performers and adult world-peak performers overlap very little — they are essentially discrete populations that diverge over time**. In **sport**, a large meta-analysis aggregating over fifty thousand athletes (including 3,375 international medalists) shows that fully 82% of international-level junior athletes failed to reach the equivalent level as adults, while 72% of international-level adult athletes had never reached international level as juniors. Combining prospective and retrospective data, about 87% of individuals in junior versus senior international championship populations are different people — an overlap of only 13%. Even narrowing the comparison to the more elite group of international medalists, the overlap is only about 12%.

The pattern holds elsewhere. In **chess**, analyzing FIDE public ratings, researchers compared the world's top-10 players under 14 with the adult world top 10: about 89% are different individuals. In **academics**, tracking top UK secondary-school graduates against elite-university graduates shows 91% non-overlap. In **cognition and careers**, the data are starker: a study tracking "prodigies" whose cognitive ability at age 12 ranked in the national top 1% found only 1% of them had incomes in the national top 5% in their thirties — 99% non-overlap between the groups.

Cross-domain differences are generally small, indicating high generality. **Classical music** lacks equivalent quantitative data, but descriptive research supports the same conclusion: many child prodigies never became top adult musicians, and many top adult musicians were not prodigies.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20251221163840868.png)

To be clear, this does not deny that early standouts have a **higher probability** than the general population of becoming adult elites.

The data show athletes who reach junior international level are 49 times more likely than those who don't to reach the equivalent adult level. But a key statistical fact: because the base of those who never reached junior international level vastly exceeds those who did (\>99% vs. \<1%), the majority of adult elites (\>70%) actually come from the larger "**not-early-elite**" group. This reveals a **paradox** of probability versus population composition: early standouts have higher individual odds, but because their group is so small, they end up a minority among the ultimately successful.

#### **World-class achievement correlates negatively with early performance**

(Here performers are split into **world-class** vs. **national-class**.)

Given that early and late elite populations differ, what characterizes the early trajectories of those who ultimately reach the world's peak? The finding: compared with peers who end up merely sub-elite, eventual world-class performers show **lower early performance** and slower early progress.

In **sport**, a study comparing 508 world-class athletes with 420 national-class athletes found that between ages 14 and 22, future world-class athletes' performance trajectories ran consistently below those of athletes who would only ever reach national class (see Figure 2A). Had selection occurred in adolescence, the future world champions would likely have been cut for underperforming their peers.

In **science**, a study comparing 330 Nobel laureates with 1,595 Nobel nominees (non-winners) reveals a similar pattern. **Laureates' citation rankings grew more slowly early in their careers**; they were less likely to win scholarships as students, and obtained their first full professorship later (see Figure 2B).

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20251221165157842.png)

In **chess**, comparing the world's top-3 players with those ranked 4–10: at age 14 the eventual top players' Elo averaged 62 points *lower* than the next tier's, yet at career peak it averaged 48 points *higher*.

Together the data paint a **tortoise-and-hare** picture: the ultimate winners were not the early front-runners. This directly challenges the conventional notion of **early lead advantage**, and hints that the predictors of early versus ultimate success may differ.

#### **Predictors of early performance and of ultimate achievement run opposite**

If early performance and final achievement are this misaligned, a natural inference follows: the factors predicting early success may differ from — even oppose — those predicting final success.

Synthesizing multiple meta-analyses, the review compares how a series of developmental predictors affect "youth high performance" versus "adult world-class performance." The effects point in almost exactly opposite directions.

For a child aiming at **strong results in adolescence**, the optimal strategy is: start early, enter a professional program early, produce results fast, pour all hours into the main event, don't get distracted. For future masters **aiming at the world's summit**, the optimal strategy is the reverse: no need to start the main event early or enter the professional system early; early progress can be gentler; relatively fewer practice hours in the main event — but spend ample time trying and participating in other domains.

Specifically, the data show:

- On **practice volume**: eventual world-class athletes accumulated significantly fewer weekly hours of structured practice in their main sport during childhood and adolescence than eventual national-class athletes — while spending significantly more time **in other sports**. On average, world-class athletes participated in two-plus other sports during childhood and adolescence, for as long as nine years;

- On **timing of entry into the professional system**: eventual world-class athletes entered elite youth academies or national junior selection squads significantly later than eventual national-class athletes.

The importance of **multidisciplinary practice** is corroborated in other domains. In science, **Nobel laureates**, compared with national-prize-level scientists, engaged more in multidisciplinary activity — both within science (ranging across subfields) and beyond it (arts, music, crafts). Paralleling world-class athletes' two extra sports, laureates pursued on average two additional avocations (think **Einstein** and his violin 🎻, or **Abhijit Banerjee** and his cooking).

In **classical music**, a study of 911 operas by the 59 most renowned opera composers found that — much as top athletes' performance correlates negatively with main-event practice volume — an opera's success correlates negatively with the number of **same-genre operas** the composer had previously written, and positively with the number of **works in other genres**. A composer's diversified experience across genres predicts success better than deep specialization within one.

More importantly, meta-analytic data show these predictors are interrelated, forming a robust, consistent pattern. The **world-class** profile is: **more multidisciplinary practice**, **less domain-specific practice**, and **slower early progress in the domain**. These three do not stand alone; they **co-occur** — gentler early progress travels with more multidisciplinary and less specialized practice.

## Mechanisms: Why Do "Casting a Wide Net" and "Taking It Slow" Work Better?

Why would a developmental pattern that seems to **lose at the starting line** be more likely to produce future world champions? The article argues existing frameworks — whether **deliberate practice** or early talent-screening — cannot fully explain the new findings, and proposes three complementary hypotheses as starting points for future research.

#### **Hypothesis 1: Search and match**

Borrowed from labor economics' theory of job matching: broad early sampling across domains greatly increases the chance of finding the field that **best matches** one's endowments, interests, and personality.

Everyone has a unique combination of abilities and preferences. A person mediocre in one domain may harbor undiscovered talent in another. Locked into a single domain too early, one may never find one's true calling. Conversely, **those who explore widely early are likelier to find the field that fits perfectly**. Once that made-for-each-other domain is found, intrinsic motivation and learning efficiency are maximized — and long-run breakout to peak excellence becomes likelier.

By contrast, the early specializer may simply be locked into an option that is decent but not optimal, capping their ultimate ceiling.

#### **Hypothesis 2: Augmented learning capital**

Rooted in transfer-of-learning theory: experience across multiple domains (multidisciplinary practice) accumulates richer **learning capital**, strengthening one's future capacity for long-term, deep learning in a chosen domain.

This capital may work through three channels:

- First, **diverse learning experiences** cultivate more flexible thinking, stronger pattern recognition across problems, and the ability to integrate knowledge and methods across fields. Simonton, the pioneer of creativity research, noted that creative potential is stimulated by diverse and challenging experiences;

- Second, diverse experiences raise one's ability to adapt to different learning tasks, contexts, methods, and information — making one a more adaptive learner able to exploit more learning opportunities;

- Third, experience with different learning designs and methods helps one understand which **learning principles** work best for oneself, yielding better-fitting learning solutions.

When the individual finally commits to one domain, this deep learning capital lets them learn faster and deeper — solving complex problems at a higher level and achieving innovative breakthroughs.

#### **Hypothesis 3: Circumscribed risks**

This view stresses the **downsides** and **risks** of **early specialization**. Premature, high-intensity single-domain training carries serious risks that can stall or **end** a career:

- **Burnout**: excessive pressure and monotonous repetition can drain passion and motivation prematurely. Overtraining and fatigue are documented drivers of youth-athlete dropout;

- **Injuries**: in sport, early specialization loads specific body parts and raises overuse injuries. Meta-analyses link early specialization to higher injury risk;

- **Opportunity cost**: pouring all hours into one domain forfeits time with family and friends, other interests, and broader education;

- **Declining enjoyment**: forced, involuntary high-intensity training can breed **aversion** to the domain, extinguishing intrinsic passion.

The broad-sampling path naturally avoids these risks, allowing healthier, more sustainable development — a solid base for a career spanning decades. Performers who reduce the risk of career derailment are likelier to develop peak excellence.

The authors stress that the three hypotheses are not mutually exclusive but likely complementary, jointly explaining why early multidisciplinary practice associates with ultimate world-class performance. Future research must test them further.

## The Challenge to Established Theory

These findings pose a major challenge to the long-dominant theory of **deliberate practice**. Proposed by psychologist Anders Ericsson and popularized as Malcolm Gladwell's **10,000-hour rule**, the theory holds that expertise accrues mainly through prolonged, voluminous, highly focused practice — implying the earlier you start and the more you practice, the likelier you reach the top.

The review's findings suggest the theory may only explain performance differences among **young people** and **sub-elite** populations, not the acquisition of the highest levels of performance. At the very top, the pattern of predictors is different — even reversed.

## Practical Implications: How Should We Develop the Next Generation?

- **Rethink selection criteria**: early performance should no longer be the main — let alone the only — criterion for selecting future top talent. The data are clear: selecting on early excellence captures only a minority of future adult top achievers, while most future top achievers sit outside the selected group. Early peak performance is not a wise selection criterion for identifying future world-class performers;

- Beware the **radiation effect**: when selection systems overweight early achievement, a harmful radiation effect follows — a **dysfunctional incentive structure** in which administrators and teachers are encouraged to select early standouts and then further accelerate their short-term performance. To meet the bar, youngsters, teachers, and parents are drawn into an arms race of premature specialization, single-subject focus, and maximized specialized practice — precisely the opposite of the developmental pattern associated with long-term excellence;

- **Reform development models**: for adolescents, education and training should emphasize empowering broad exploration rather than accelerating short-term results. The evidence indicates that moderate (not top-tier) early performance, moderate (not maximal) domain practice, plus substantial multidisciplinary practice, are the markers of long-run world-class potential. Concretely, teachers, coaches, and youth program managers might: reduce weekly single-domain practice hours and create opportunities to sample other fields. A soccer coach can encourage players to attend basketball, tennis, or gymnastics once or twice a week; a piano teacher can encourage a student to also try flute, violin, or percussion; a physics-olympiad teacher can encourage students to join extracurriculars in computer science, ecology, or philosophy;

- **Reform evaluation systems**: elite training programs — whose funding and survival often hinge on evaluations — should not be judged on participants' current or short-term results, but on participants' development over subsequent years up to peak performance. Only then does evaluation truly measure a program's effectiveness at producing long-term excellence.

## Discussion

To emphasize: the findings do not mean early performance is worthless, nor that early standouts cannot become world-class masters.

Compared with the general population, early standouts do have higher odds of adult excellence. In sport, athletes who reach junior international championship level are 49 times likelier than those who don't to reach adult international championship level.

The crux, however: first, even with higher odds, the great majority of early standouts (about 87%) still fail to maintain that level in adulthood; second, because the base of athletes who never reached junior international level dwarfs those who did (over 99%), the majority of adult international champions (over 70%) come from that larger group.

In other words, **early excellence is a positive signal — but neither a sufficient nor a necessary condition for success**. Most early prodigies do not become adult masters, and most adult masters were not early prodigies.
