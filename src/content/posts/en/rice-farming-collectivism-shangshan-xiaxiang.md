> The **rice culture theory** proposed by Talhelm and others argues that growing rice requires high-intensity labor and interdependent irrigation networks, which differ greatly from the individualistic work style of wheat farming, making people in rice-growing regions more collectivist. However, whether the relationship between rice cultivation and collectivism is **causal** or merely **correlational** still requires further clarification.
>
> In their latest study, Talhelm and colleagues took advantage of a social experiment from China’s “**Down to the Countryside**” movement, in which people were randomly assigned to two comparable rice and wheat farms. They found that individuals who farmed rice were indeed more collectivist than those who farmed wheat, and that this cultural difference could form within a single generation.

**Title:** People quasi-randomly assigned to farm rice are more collectivistic than people assigned to farm wheat

**Journal:** [Nature Communications](https://www.nature.com/ncomms)

**Authors:**

- Thomas Talhelm, Associate Professor of Behavioral Science at the University of Chicago Booth School of Business, a behavioral scientist with a strong interest in cultural studies, especially the cultural psychology of Chinese people. He has made outstanding contributions to systematizing and promoting the rice culture theory;
- Xiawei Dong, PhD candidate in Organizational Behavior at the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology Business School, who has collaborated extensively with Thomas Talhelm.

**Published online:** 27 February 2024

### Preface

Ten years later, Thomas Talhelm and his collaborators are back with the latest research on “rice theory”!

In 2014, Thomas Talhelm and colleagues from Beijing Normal University published an article in Science expounding the rice theory ([Science – Southern Rice, Northern Wheat: Crop cultivation and the origins of personality differences between Northern and Southern Chinese](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/6hmSVn06zLBTMFL8Yl96ig)), arguing that differences in rice and wheat cultivation led to personality differences between people in the north and south. Rice theory offered a concise framework and caused a great stir, but its big-brushstrokes narrative also drew considerable criticism, for example:

- [A North-South debate on thinking styles too? — The most probable internal and external causes for Chinese people’s preference for holistic thinking](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/S-5HW_I5vucfb3jY_wzRYg) (This article argues that **flood management** and **Five Elements thought** are the primary factors driving Chinese people’s tendency toward holistic thinking)
- [Zhejiang University School of Management Associate Professor Ruan Jianqing’s article challenges the “rice theory” published in a *Science* cover paper](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/ETFrWe5eFytbg8oMexRMOQ) (This article argues that Talhelm et al.’s sampling lacked proper stratification and that the regression specification was flawed; reanalysis using two other datasets does not support the rice theory)

These criticisms did not stop the dissemination of rice theory nor Thomas Talhelm’s continued research on the topic. Over the course of a decade, he conducted numerous international and within-China comparisons based on the rice theory, such as:

- [Is farming rice less happy than farming wheat? | Rice-wheat difference theory](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/5NYn8NuzhtsQT1VCCEq8qQ) (This article finds that residents of historically rice-farming areas report lower subjective well-being than those from wheat-farming areas)
- [A foreigner sat in a chair at a Starbucks to study what’s different about northern and southern Chinese](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/rBQy2qydwTZ73Y16o4tdPQ) (This article found that when a chair blocks the way, northerners are more willing to move it, while southerners prefer to walk around it)
- [Do traditional rice-growing societies have stronger social norms?](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/EV6gSS4FENB9QcxQdoScfA)

This time, utilizing a social experiment from the “**Down to the Countryside**” movement in which people were quasi-randomly assigned to two comparable rice and wheat farms, Talhelm ruled out the influence of temperature, latitude, and the numerous historical events that differ between northern and southern China. This provides new evidence for rice theory and makes the findings considerably more persuasive!

### The Down to the Countryside Movement as a Quasi-Random Experiment

#### Experimental Background

After the founding of the People’s Republic of China, the government created state farms nationwide to open up new farmland, provide work for veterans, and re-educate urban youths. In the northern part of Ningxia Province, the government established two farms only 56 kilometers apart, one cultivating rice and the other wheat.

![img](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/41467_2024_44770_Fig1_HTML.png)

(The two state farms are only 56 km apart, but tiny topographical differences allow **Lianhu Farm** (the Rice Farm in the figure) to cultivate rice while, in comparison, most land at **Qukou Farm** stands 50-100 meters above the nearby Yellow River, making large-scale irrigation uneconomical. The soil at Qukou Farm is also sandier, making it harder for paddy fields to retain water. Image from Google Earth.) (This figure truly embodies the idea of “just enough to convey the meaning, not a concern for aesthetics lol”)

The environments of Lianhu Farm and Qukou Farm are nearly identical, with similar temperature, rainfall, and overall area, rendering the two farms an ideal experimental group and control group.

#### How Randomness Was Guaranteed

**Ruling out self-selection of the sample:**

- Historical records and interviews with farm managers indicate that the government’s assignment of laborers to Lianhu Farm and Qukou Farm was random and did not consider individual interests or perceptions. Records show labor for both farms came primarily from: (a) ordinary citizens designated by the government (b) veterans assigned to the region by the government, or (c) sent-down youths assigned to Ningxia during the Cultural Revolution;
- In 1960, the government launched the **Youth “Support Ningxia”** movement. Zhejiang province, which lies in a major rice-growing region, sent numerous youths to participate. If officials consciously assigned youths based on skills or traits specific to a farm, then youths from Zhejiang should overwhelmingly have been assigned to Lianhu Farm to grow rice. Yet records show that a roughly equal number of Zhejiang youths were assigned to both farms;
- During the Cultural Revolution, many youths were sent to the countryside through a rushed process that was not deeply considered to match specific people to suitable destinations. Among the 234 farmers recruited for this study, only one was born in a rice-cultivating province. The vast majority of surveyed farmers on both farms (>99%) and their parents (98%) originated from predominantly wheat-producing provinces.

**Confirming comparability of the two farms:**

- The **environment** is comparable: The two farms have similar temperature, rainfall levels, and acreage;
- **Site** selection was incidental: A primary goal in creating state farms was opening new cultivated land. Before Lianhu Farm was established, that area was “uninhabited” wetland containing 72 lakes; the situation at Qukou Farm was similar—before the farm's construction, most of its land was uncultivated, unsuitable for rice, and characterized by dry, nutrient-poor sandy desert soil (sierozem, organic matter < 1%). The government’s construction of infrastructure and application of chemical fertilizers at Qukou Farm were needed just to make the land usable. In other words, these two farms were built on "blank slates"; the land itself did not possess traits shaped by particular agricultural activities, so naturally participants did not “inherit” any prior farming culture from it;
- **Policy and management** were consistent: The two farms belong to the same agricultural reclamation conglomerate, standard prescriptions apply to both, and when new policies are introduced they reach both farms simultaneously.

#### The Psychology Experiment Measuring Collectivism

**Experimental procedure:**

- In September and October 2017, 234 local farmers were recruited, together with local officials, to participate. Most participants in the final sample (73.5%) grew up on a farm, and the remaining quarter or so (26.5%) were assigned to their farm after childhood. Almost all participants (96.2%) engaged in agriculture in the year under question, with the remainder handling farm administration duties;
- Completing tasks and demographic questions took 30 to 40 minutes. Participants were compensated with a 30-yuan prepaid phone credit or a family-size bottle of liquid detergent and participated from within their home or their workplace environs.

**Experimental tasks:**

(The same ones as in the Science article—included below to elaborate the operationalizations)

- **Triad task:** Participants are shown three items, for example “train”, “bus”, and "track," and asked to pair two of them together. Prior literature finds that participants from individualistic Western cultural groups prefer the **analytic** pairing— in this example, “train" and “bus"—while East Asians more often prefer a **holistic-relation** match—assigning “train” with “track”;

- **Sociogram task:** Participants draw their own social network, typically using circles to represent themselves and their friends one-by-one. The node representing the self is normally drawn differently sized, or distinctively featured relative to friendship nodes. Previous research finds that on average, Americans draw themselves about 6 mm larger than others, Europeans draw themselves about 3.5 mm larger, and Japanese draw themselves slightly smaller. In collecting research data under conditions here, given that some participants found “friend" difficult to respond to meaningfully socially, in the final task it was changed to drawing the respondent family.
