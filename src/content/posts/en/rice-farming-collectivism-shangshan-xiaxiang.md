> The **rice culture theory** proposed by Talhelm and colleagues holds that growing rice requires high-intensity labor and interdependent irrigation networks, which differ substantially from the individual work style of wheat, and this makes people in rice regions more collectivistic. But whether the relationship between rice cultivation and collectivism is **causal** or **correlational** still needs further clarification.
>
> In their latest study, Talhelm and colleagues take advantage of a social experiment from the "**Down to the Countryside**" movement, in which people were randomly assigned to two comparable rice and wheat farms, and find that people who farm rice are indeed more collectivistic than people who farm wheat, and that this cultural difference can form within a single generation.

**Title:** People quasi-randomly assigned to farm rice are more collectivistic than people assigned to farm wheat

**Journal:** [Nature Communications](https://www.nature.com/ncomms)

**Authors:**

- Thomas Talhelm, Associate Professor at the University of Chicago Booth School of Business, a behavioral scientist with a strong interest in cultural research, paying particular attention to the cultural psychology of Chinese people. He has made outstanding contributions to systematizing and advancing the rice culture theory;
- Xiawei Dong, a PhD student in Organizational Behavior at the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology Business School, who has collaborated extensively with Thomas Talhelm.

**Published online:** 27 February 2024

### Preface

Ten years later, Thomas Talhelm and his collaborators are back with the latest "rice theory" research!

In 2014, Thomas Talhelm, together with scholars from Beijing Normal University, published an article in Science setting out the rice theory ([Science — Southern Rice, Northern Wheat: Crop cultivation and the origins of personality differences between people in northern and southern China](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/6hmSVn06zLBTMFL8Yl96ig)), arguing that the difference between rice and wheat cultivation gave rise to the personality differences between people in the north and south. Rice theory provided a concise framework and caused a great stir, but its broad-brush explanation also attracted many doubts, for example:

- [Is there a north-south divide in thinking styles too? — The most likely internal and external causes for Chinese people's preference for holistic thinking](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/S-5HW_I5vucfb3jY_wzRYg) (This article argues that **flood control** and the **Five Elements theory** are the main causes of Chinese people's tendency toward holistic thinking)
- [Associate Professor Ruan Jianqing of Zhejiang University's School of Management publishes a challenge to the "rice theory" proposed in the *Science* cover paper](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/ETFrWe5eFytbg8oMexRMOQ) (This article argues that Talhelm et al.'s sampling was not properly stratified and that the specification of the regression analysis was flawed; reanalysis using two other datasets does not support the rice theory)

These doubts did not stop the spread of the rice theory nor Thomas Talhelm's continued research on it. Over the course of a decade, he conducted many international comparisons and within-China comparisons based on the rice theory, such as:

- [Is farming rice less happy than farming wheat? | The rice-wheat difference theory](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/5NYn8NuzhtsQT1VCCEq8qQ) (This article argues that, compared with residents of historically wheat-growing regions, residents of rice-growing regions report lower happiness)
- [To study how northern and southern Chinese differ, a foreigner placed a chair in a Starbucks](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/rBQy2qydwTZ73Y16o4tdPQ) (This article found that, faced with a chair blocking the way, northerners are willing to move it aside, while southerners are more willing to walk around it)
- [Do traditional rice-growing societies have stronger social norms?](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/EV6gSS4FENB9QcxQdoScfA)

This time, by using a social experiment from the "**Down to the Countryside**" movement in which people were randomly assigned to two comparable rice and wheat farms, Talhelm ruled out the influence of temperature, latitude, and the many historical events that differ between northern and southern China, providing new evidence for the rice theory and making the results far more convincing!

### The Down to the Countryside Movement as a Quasi-Random Experiment

#### Experimental Background

After the founding of the People's Republic of China, the government created state farms across the country to open up new farmland, provide work for demobilized soldiers, and re-educate urban youth. In the northern part of Ningxia Province, the government established two farms only 56 kilometers apart, one growing rice and the other wheat.

![img](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/41467_2024_44770_Fig1_HTML.png)

(The two state farms are only 56 km apart, but minor differences in terrain allow **Lianhu Farm** (the Rice farm in the figure above) to grow rice. By contrast, most of the land at **Qukou Farm** sits 50-100 meters above the nearby Yellow River, making large-scale irrigation uneconomical. The soil at Qukou Farm is also sandier, making it hard for paddy fields to retain water. Image from Google Earth.) (This figure was really drawn just to get the point across; whether it looks nice doesn't matter lol)

The environments of Lianhu Farm and Qukou Farm are almost identical, with similar temperature, rainfall, and area, which makes the two farms an ideal experimental group and control group.

#### How Randomness Was Ensured

**Ruling out sample self-selection:**

- Historical materials and interviews with farm managers show that the government's assignment of laborers to Lianhu Farm and Qukou Farm was random and did not take individuals' interests or ideas into account. Records show that the laborers at the two farms came mainly from: (a) ordinary citizens designated by the government, (b) demobilized soldiers assigned to the region by the government, or (c) educated youth assigned to Ningxia during the Cultural Revolution;
- In 1960, the government launched the **Youth Supporting Ningxia** movement, and Zhejiang Province, located in a rice-growing region, sent many young people to Ningxia to take part. If government officials had consciously assigned youth to a farm based on their skills or traits, then youth from Zhejiang should have been assigned in large numbers to Lianhu Farm to grow rice. But records show that the number of Zhejiang youth assigned to the two farms was almost identical;
- During the Cultural Revolution, many young people were sent down to the countryside, and this process was hasty; there was no careful deliberation about sending particular people to suitable places. Among the 234 farmer participants recruited for this study, only one was born in a rice-growing province. The vast majority of farmers at both farms (> 99%) and their parents (98%) came from wheat-dominated provinces.

**Confirming the comparability of the two farms:**

- The **environment** is comparable: the two farms have similar temperature, rainfall, and area;
- The choice of **location** was random: one of the goals of the government's push to create state farms was to open up new arable land. Before Lianhu Farm was created, the area was a stretch of "uninhabited" wetland with 72 lakes; the situation at Qukou Farm was similar, with most of the land being uncultivated before the farm was built, unsuitable for growing rice, and consisting of desert soil (sierozem) that was dry and lacking in nutrients (organic matter < 1%). It was only the government's construction of infrastructure and use of chemical fertilizer at Qukou Farm that made the land usable. In other words, these two farms were built on "blank paper"; they themselves did not possess certain traits formed by particular agricultural activities, and naturally the participants would not "inherit" any culture originally embedded in the farmland;
- **Policy and management** were consistent: the two farms belonged to the same agricultural reclamation group, had the same incentive mechanisms, and when new policies were introduced, both farms received them at the same time.

#### The Psychology Experiment Measuring Collectivism

**Experimental procedure:**

- In September and October 2017, together with leaders of local departments, 234 farmers were recruited as participants. Most of the farmers in the sample (73.5%) grew up on the farm, and the remaining roughly one quarter (26.5%) were assigned to the farm after childhood. Almost all participants (96.2%) were engaged in farming in the year of the study, while the rest did administrative work on the farm;
- Completing the tasks and demographic questions took 30 to 40 minutes, and participants received a 30-yuan mobile phone top-up card or a family-size bottle of dish soap; the farmers took the tests at home or in the office.

**Experimental tasks:**

(The same as in the Science paper, excerpted here)

- **The triad task:** also called the triad task, in which participants are shown three items, such as train, bus, and track, and asked to pair two of them together. The existing literature finds that individualistic cultural groups from the West prefer the **analytic** match, that is, train and bus; whereas East Asians prefer the **holistic relational** choice, that is, train and track;

<img src="https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240307125600744.png" alt="image-20240307125600744" style="zoom:50%;" />

- **The sociogram task:** participants are asked to draw a diagram of their own social network, in which the nodes are circles representing themselves and their friends, and the circle representing oneself is often drawn at a different size from those of others. Some research finds that Americans draw themselves about 6 millimeters larger than others, Europeans draw themselves about 3.5 millimeters larger, and Japanese draw themselves slightly smaller; in testing, this study found it quite hard to get farmers to think of "friends," so in the end it was changed to having them draw family members;

<img src="https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240307125637405.png" alt="image-20240307125637405" style="zoom:50%;" />

- **The loyalty and nepotism task:** this measures whether people draw a clear distinction in how they treat friends versus strangers. A characteristic of collectivistic cultures is that people draw a clear line between friends and strangers. Participants are asked to imagine doing a business transaction with (a) an honest friend, (b) a dishonest friend, (c) an honest stranger, and (d) a dishonest stranger, where honesty causes the participant to make money while dishonesty causes them to lose money, and to indicate how much money they would spend to punish or reward them in different scenarios. Past experiments found that Singaporeans rewarded their friends far more than they punished them, which can be seen as a form of loyalty. Americans were more likely than Singaporeans to punish their friends for bad behavior. This study predicts that, compared with people in wheat regions, people in rice regions are less likely to punish their friends. (The detailed questionnaire can be found in the online supplement to the paper.)

<img src="https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2024/image-20240307130647424.png" alt="image-20240307130647424" style="zoom:50%;" />

**Analytic method:**

- Propensity score matching (PSM) was used for the regression analysis.

### Experimental Results

#### Basic results:

- Rice farmers engaged in less self-inflation when drawing the sociogram, that is, the circle representing themselves was smaller;
- Rice farmers punished strangers more harshly than wheat farmers did;
- In the triad task, rice farmers thought about the categorization task in a more holistic way than wheat farmers, with about 90% of rice farmers choosing the relational pairing (such as train and track);

#### Not growing rice in the current year does not affect the results:

- Lianhu Farm (which grows rice) provided a unique opportunity to test whether the difference between rice and wheat requires direct, recent rice-farming experience. In 2000, the rice fields began to face a shortage of irrigation water, so a crop-rotation plan was launched, with about one third of farmers growing **dryland crops** each year. In the year the authors collected their data, about one third (38.4%) of the participants at the rice farm did not grow rice. More importantly, the farmers were rotated by year, that is, this was a top-down arrangement applied evenly to all farmers, and did not depend on whether a farmer decided on their own to take part;
- Adding to the model the percentage of land an individual planted with rice in the most recent farming year, this variable was not significant. In other words, not growing rice in the current year does not affect the maintenance of one's collectivistic character.

### Discussion

- The participants were not truly randomly sampled, so the authors mitigated this problem by adding a series of demographic variables to the model and using methods such as generalized linear models and PSM;
- **Rice culture forms within a single generation:** as mentioned earlier, the early farmers at these two farms came from the Down to the Countryside movement, and they and their parents overwhelmingly came from wheat-producing regions, while the farms have existed for about 70 years; a span of several decades was enough for individuals to display stronger collectivism as a result of growing rice. These two farms provide an ideal experimental setting: the people farming at Lianhu Farm grow rice but are not exposed to southern China's clan culture, dialects, and government (factors that might reinforce collectivism), so it can be argued that this difference in character can form within a single generation relying solely on the difference between growing rice and growing wheat.
- *The data from this study provide the clearest evidence to date that rice farming is one cause of collectivism, and perhaps also one cause of East-West differences.* (the authors' original words)

### **Extra**

- The design of this quasi-random experiment is simple but very clever, the kind of research that belongs to the Big wow category!
- A scholar once said that government administrators are always, intentionally or unintentionally, creating many quasi-natural experiments; by using historical events and policy designs, social scientists can combine social reality with the questions they are interested in to carry out research.
