> Pricing political risk is a challenging task. Against the backdrop of “one country, two systems” potentially expiring in 2047, researchers exploit unique variation in lease-extension protection within Hong Kong’s real estate market to identify exposure to political risk. The results effectively reflect how markets price political uncertainty.
>
> The findings show that, compared with leases expiring on 30 June 2047 that are expected to receive a 50‑year extension, leases expiring after 1 July 2047 with no legally guaranteed extension trade at an average discount of up to 14.1%. Leases auctioned during the British colonial period suffer an even larger discount, with an additional reduction of about 8%, reflecting owners’ concern about “hold‑up risk” – the risk that future governments will breach obligations on these legacy leases.

### Preface

- Political factors have a significant impact on asset pricing and economic activity, as documented by a large body of literature; Hong Kong has practiced “one country, two systems” since its return to China in 1997, but this arrangement is set to expire in 2047. This signals potentially substantial political risk that may affect long‑term property prices.
- Empirically, pricing political risk is a challenging task because political risk often coincides with economic fluctuations, making it difficult to identify a causal effect.
- Hong Kong is a unique political and economic **intersection**:
  - Hong Kong sits at the nexus of conflict between China and the Western world, becoming the battleground for the fate of the “one country, two systems” political experiment;
  - Hong Kong’s land supply mainly relies on government leases rather than freehold titles, and whether a lease can be renewed upon expiry depends on the future regime, exposing property rights to substantial political risk;
  - Hong Kong has long had a well‑functioning free‑market economy with few distortions from external factors;
  - Property prices in Hong Kong are extremely high and have a major impact on residents’ welfare; and because of this, how the market prices real estate embodies residents’ long‑term views on political uncertainty.

- Real estate is a typical long‑term asset, and its value is mainly determined by the discounted cash flows it generates in the future. By affecting future cash flows and the discount rate, political risk can significantly alter the current price of real estate.

### Land tenure system and land policy in Hong Kong

For details see: [Land Tenure System and Land Policy in Hong Kong](https://www.landsd.gov.hk/en/resources/land-info-stat/land-tenure-system-land-policy.html)

- All land in Hong Kong belongs to the government; private parties own only the right to use and occupy the land for a specified period, i.e., a land contract or land lease. Upon lease expiry, title reverts to the government, which then decides whether to grant a renewal. This property‑right arrangement makes land‑lease value highly susceptible to future changes in land policy.
- Early land disposals were conducted mainly through auction and tender, with terms that included 75 years, 99 years, and 999 years. Beginning in the late 1960s, the Hong Kong government standardised the lease term for new grants to 75 years.

### Empirical research design

#### Treatment Groups

The treatment groups consist of land leases expiring at various points in time, especially those that mature after the political transition. The study focuses mainly on the price discounts of these leases in order to assess the impact of political risk. Specifically, they are divided into the following sub‑groups:

- **Leases expiring before 2047:** These leases expire before 2047 and, under existing policy, receive an automatic 50‑year extension. They are divided into sub‑groups by expiration year, such as leases expiring from 2029 to 2033, 2034 to 2039, and 2040 to 2046.
- **Leases expiring after 2047:** This is the main treatment group, comprising leases that expire after 1 July 2047. The renewal policy for these leases has yet to be specified, so the policy treatment they expect after the political transition is uncertain. These leases are also divided into sub‑groups by expiration year, such as 1 July 2047 – 2049, 2050 – 2052, 2053 – 2064, 2065 – 2097, and 2098 – 2135.
- **Leases expiring in the far future:** Another group comprises leases that expire in the far future (e.g., 999‑year leases), with maturities well beyond 2047, such as 2842 to 2959.

#### Control Group

The control group consists of land leases that expire on 30 June 2047. According to the current policy of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region (HKSAR), these leases will automatically be extended for 50 years, until 2097. Because these leases have explicit extension protection, they are less affected by the anticipated 2047 political change and can therefore serve as a relatively stable benchmark. Specifically, the control group includes:

- Leases that began after the 1985 effective date of the Sino‑British Joint Declaration and before Hong Kong’s handover in 1997 – these leases were automatically set to expire on 30 June 2047 (belonging to Type‑5 leases).
- Certain “+75‑year” leases on Hong Kong Island and old Kowloon that also expire in the second half of 2047 (partially belonging to Type‑2 leases).
- Leases in the New Territories and New Kowloon that originally expired in 1997 but were automatically extended to 30 June 2047 under the Sino‑British Joint Declaration (belonging to Type‑4 leases).

### Data

- **Residential transaction and amenity data:** These data were obtained by purchasing electronic transaction records from the Hong Kong Land Registry, covering all residential property transactions in Hong Kong from January 1992 to February 2020. The dataset includes land‑lease expiration years, transaction prices and dates, and property characteristics (such as building year, floor, unit number, amenities, etc.).
- **Land sales data:** To confirm the exact lease‑expiration dates, the researchers collected land auction data from the Land Registry website and matched them with residential transaction data using lot numbers.
- **Census data and local election data:** These publicly available datasets provide the socioeconomic characteristics of Hong Kong households, including quinquennial census data from 2001 to 2016, and the results of Hong Kong district‑council elections from 1999 to 2019.

#### Political risk and discounts

The study finds that properties whose land leases expire after 2047 with no legal protection (the treatment group) sell at a significant discount of around 14.1% relative to properties with extension protection (the control group). Moreover, leases granted by the colonial government before 2047 suffer an additional discount of about 8% compared with those.

#### Structural parameter estimates

Through a structural analysis of the real estate transaction data, the study estimates several key parameters:

- **Additional land‑rent rate:** After 2047, homeowners expect to pay an additional 21% of the net rental value when a lease is renewed.
- **Government default risk:** Homeowners do not anticipate any risk of colonial‑lease breach before 2047; after 2047, however, there is an expected breach risk of 1.6% per 61‑year period specific to colonial leases.
- **Lump‑sum land surcharge:** When a lease expires or is breached, colonial leases are expected to incur lump‑sum land surcharges of 6.2% (before 2047) and 20.8% (after 2047), respectively.

#### Discount‑rate estimation

- **Discount rate:** By incorporating Hong Kong real estate statistics, the researchers estimate a discount rate of 2.44%, which is similar to the findings in Giglio et al. (2015) – a paper that estimated “very‑long‑run discount rates” using data from the UK and Singapore.

#### The effect of local political sentiment

The study also examines how local political sentiment influences the perception of political risk:

- **Share of pro‑democracy seats:** In constituencies with a higher proportion of pro‑democracy seats, the discount on property prices is larger, reflecting that pro‑democracy supporters may hold more pessimistic views about Hong Kong’s political future.
- **Share of Mainland migrants:** Properties in constituencies with a higher share of Mainland migrants also exhibit a larger discount, which may reflect heightened unease among local residents about cultural pressure and political uncertainty.

#### The role of individual beliefs

- **Mainland buyers versus sellers:** Compared with local Hong Kong buyers, Mainland buyers appear more optimistic when purchasing and are willing to pay higher prices; compared with local Hong Kong sellers, Mainland sellers appear more pessimistic, tending to sell properties at lower prices.
