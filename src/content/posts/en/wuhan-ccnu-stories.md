January 29, the fifth day of the first lunar month, sunny.  

Today must be the first time in half a month that I've seen the sun.  

When I woke up this morning, the sky outside my window had changed color. The whole world looked as though the black-and-white filter had been peeled away, shedding its days of leaden gray. This Spring Festival has carried such a heavy sense of unreality. There is no festive atmosphere, and no scent of spring in the air.  

"Faintly the thunder rumbles, under the darkened sky" — there was no thunder, and I could not find Yukino’s garden either.  

![image-20220320135935826](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image/img/image-20220320135935826.png)

During my senior year, my internship took me all over Wuhan — Nanhu, Dongxihu, Jiangxia, Yangluo, Caidian, and East Optics Valley. At the time, Lines 7 and 11 and the southern extension of Line 2 were not yet open. Getting to Nanhu or East Optics Valley always required piecing together a combination of the metro, bus, and a shared bike. If a city has its own personality, my first impression of Wuhan was a foul-tempered one, and that impression began first and foremost with its traffic. In 2014, when Ruan Chengfa was the Party Secretary of Wuhan, the city had over ten thousand construction sites, and people gave him the nickname "Dig-Up-the-City." Yes: "Wuhan, different every day." The Xiongchu Avenue road-building crew could locate and slice through a water main (DN-whichever-number) with surgical precision; the Optics Valley traffic circle seemed to be under permanent reconstruction; construction barriers, dust and deafening noise could be seen everywhere. The whole city felt like an oversized county town with an absurdly high volume knob. The public transport during that era of frantic construction was a whole series of problems: the metro was packed, the buses were stuck in gridlock; it almost seemed impossible in the afternoon to pass Jiedaokou without sitting in bumper-to-bumper traffic. I once sighed any number of times that Optics Valley station was the most crushing metro station on earth. On Friday afternoons, when classes let out at most colleges, Optics Valley became a kingdom of youth. Upon exiting the station, the cellular overload from the sheer volume of phones would make your internet vanish; the crowd of people moved slowly, squeezing across pedestrian bridges or through underground tunnels until finally reaching World City — it felt as acute as surviving a military campaign. Every week in my senior year, I had to go to Optics Valley for class a few times. Once the bus hit Luxiang it crawled at turtle speed, private cars, e-scooters and pedestrians slotting abruptly into any spare inch they could find, the whole chaos enveloped in thick fumes of exhaust smoke. Every so often you would stumble upon a short-tempered driver and on meeting the traffic jam the verbal flood would let loose — he would open the window, stick his head out and start roaring the ritualized streetside curses of Wuhan — then sound a sharp *beep*. After that one *beep* would come a ripple-through cascade, horn following upon peevish horn, slicing clear and far off to Lumo Road. Anyway, my personal tactics were to pray softly that he would not tear through the starting-braking maneuvers of handbrake on a whim-dizzy escalator bus simulator… and mentally prepare. When the metro cannot unclog, you are squeezed, and at stations like South-Central Road and Han Street at rush hour then utterly pancaked for that full window of unstoppable abuse.
