Yahaha ~

January 29, the fifth day of the first lunar month, sunny.

Today must be the first time in half a month that I've seen the sun.

When I woke up this morning, the sky outside my window had changed color. The whole world looked as though the black-and-white filter had been peeled away, shedding its usual leaden gray. This Spring Festival has carried such a strong sense of unreality. There is no festive atmosphere, and no scent of spring in the air.

"Faintly the thunder rumbles, the sky is overcast" — there was no thunder, nor could I find Takao's courtyard.

![image-20220320135935826](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image/img/image-20220320135935826.png)

During my senior year, because of my internship, I traveled all over Wuhan — Nanhu, Dongxihu, Jiangxia, Yangluo, Caidian, and East Optics Valley. At the time, Line 7, Line 11, and the extension of Line 2 had not yet opened, so getting to Nanhu or East Optics Valley always required piecing together a combination of "metro + bus + shared bike." If a city has its own personality, then my first impression of Wuhan was that it was bad-tempered, and that impression came first and foremost from Wuhan's traffic. In 2014, when Ruan Chengfa served as Party Secretary of Wuhan, the city had more than ten thousand construction sites underway, and people gave this secretary the nickname "Dig-Up-the-Whole-City." Yes, Wuhan was different every day. The Xiongchu Avenue construction crew could always sever a water main with pinpoint precision (DN-such-and-such-number); the Optics Valley roundabout was forever under construction; construction barriers, dust, and roaring noise were everywhere. The whole city was like a big, very noisy county town. During that period of major construction, public transit always had serious problems: the metro was packed, the buses were jammed, and it seemed there was never a time in the afternoon when you could pass Jiedaokou without traffic. I marveled countless times that Optics Valley station was the most crowded metro station in the world. On Friday afternoons, when most schools had no classes, Optics Valley became a world of young people. When you exited the station, the base stations were overloaded, your phone lost its signal, the crowd flowed slowly, and you squeezed across the overpass or through the underground passage until you finally reached World City, as if you had been through a battle. In my senior year I went to Optics Valley for class several times a week, and once the bus reached Luxiang it shifted into turtle mode, with private cars, electric bikes, and pedestrians darting into every gap, inching forward amid heavy exhaust fumes. Sometimes you would run into a short-tempered driver, and when traffic jammed, he would roll down the window, stick out his head and curse — standard Wuhan profanity. One honk, followed by a long, domino-like cascade of honks, carrying all the way from Luxiang to Lumo Road. All I could do was silently pray that he wouldn't slam on the brakes, and then mentally prepare for the traffic jam on the Zhuodaoquan stretch on the way back. The metro doesn't jam, but it gets very crowded — at Zhongnan Road and Han Street, during rush hour the backpack on your shoulders could be squeezed flat; oh, and there were those pink handrails on Line 2, so off-putting you couldn't bear to grab them. It wasn't only the city buses that were bad-tempered, but the school shuttles too. When the shuttle turned at the Dong'er intersection, you could occasionally witness the same scene as the Optics Valley drivers, and there was also the legendary "God of Guizishan driving," whose bus played special music and had a magical trick of accelerating while heading down the mountain road.

Also infuriating were some of the teaching-affairs office staff I encountered in my freshman and sophomore years — the ones handling classroom bookings and course additions. They really made you question life, so much so that later I often wondered why the title "teacher" had become so generalized. Many of the staff in the teaching-affairs office are merely administrative personnel; they assist teaching activities but do not teach, and in how they handle things they often serve as negative examples — so why call them teachers?

Even though "Wuhan is bad-tempered" had become a stereotype in my mind, I still feel fortunate to have encountered so many novel things and kind people in Wuhan and on Guizishan.

### Courses and Lectures

It was as if I came with a built-in course-selection luck buff: in my freshman and sophomore years, when fighting to register for courses, I could always get plenty of general electives. In Mr. Jiannan's "Selected Lectures on Classic Texts of Secondary-School Chinese," when discussing "Li Sao" he talked about the accretion of Qu Yuan's image and the question of his sexual orientation, and about the problems with the teaching reference books compiled by the People's Education Press — it opened a window for us. Interestingly, near the end of April Mr. Jiannan said he would be leaving for a while and would return on June 9, and we all understood that he was going to set the questions for the college entrance exam. Then there was "A Cultural History of China," whose textbook was compiled by Mr. Feng Tianyu; in class he played many videos, running from clothing and artifacts to culture, spanning from the pre-Qin era to the Ming and Qing. The one that nearly went off the rails was "Studies in Film and Television Art" — when I selected it I thought class would just be watching movies, but it turned out we rarely saw film clips and instead got page after page of film and television theory. Well, that fit the course title, and the in-class exam was a disaster too. One Erya online course was "Logic and Critical Thinking," whose online lectures were recordings of Mr. Yang Wujin's course at Renmin University, full of many interesting little cases. There were also some fun courses, like "Parameter Analysis of Consumer Electronics" and "Computer Assembly and Maintenance" — not because the course content was interesting, but because of listening to the teachers tell stories from their own student days: the 286 era, soldering their own motherboards, going to Guangbutun to flash the BIOS. They were the PC enthusiasts of that time. Among the non-major courses, the one that left the deepest impression was Mr. Peng Xiaohui's "Introduction to Sexual Science," which may have been the hottest course on Guizishan. The class was held at noon, and back then my friends and I would push through the throngs from Building 6 to Building 7 and buy a little bun from the vending machine to grab seats. Mr. Peng usually wore a hat, like an amusing gentleman. He started from translation — sex to sexual to sexuality — talking about their collaborators and their website, their works and translations, and the LGBT community. The physiology of sex made up only part of the course; more of it was about sexual safety, the psychology and sociology of sex, and so on. It was on this basis that he wanted to rename the course "Introduction to Sexology." In class you could also sense the depth of his lecturing skill, honed over more than a decade of teaching this course — no fumbling, no convoluted phrasing, and a profound rapport when interacting with the teachers in the audience or the students asking questions. With Mr. Peng you could feel a scholar's reverence for teaching and learning.

The major courses were rather remarkable. Before the track split, the classes of Mr. Yang Xuan and Mr. Chen Shizhong were both quite interesting. The real-estate management major's courses were excellent. At the very beginning, the department chair taught "Real Estate Economics," with an intangible aura that made you afraid to let your mind wander or answer questions casually, for fear that some gap in your reasoning would be torn open by her piercing gaze. "Introduction to Construction Engineering and Reading Drawings" was taught by Ms. Dai Lu, the only engineering course in our major. Ms. Dai Lu was very gentle; in class, her voice and her brushstrokes had the feel of deep cultivation and real ability. Every line on the blackboard, every building component, was clear and beautiful. She printed out the materials needed for reading drawings and handed them to us for practice, and in class she answered questions clearly and brightly. In winter, for the first class of the morning, Ms. Dai would also arrive very early. Another of Ms. Dai's signatures was her little white Audi; she would let class out a few minutes early and, before the crowds got dense, drive her little Audi down Despair Slope and out the East Gate. Mr. Deng Baotong taught "Real Estate Law" and Mr. Dong Limin taught "Urban Economics" — having the textbook's compilers teach in person, with many of the cases in the books being ones the teachers had personally handled, naturally made it feel more intimate. Mr. Gao Binghua taught "Real Estate Appraisal" and "Real Estate Marketing"; he himself was a certified real-estate appraiser and a senior member of the real-estate broker association, took part in compiling the "Real Estate Appraisal Standards," and served as the head of the question-setting group for the certified real-estate appraiser qualification exam. Combined with his amusing Wuhan dialect, his lectures vividly presented all kinds of cases. Thinking back now, I've forgotten the appraisal knowledge, but I remember the jokes he made in class: he said Mr. Chai Qiang was an authority in the appraisal field, deliberately emphasizing that "everyone on earth knows it"; he reminisced about his days in the mathematics department, recalling how when the tennis courts in the East District were still fish ponds, he used to fish in them. Dean Deng taught "Real Estate Property Rights," with no textbook, few metaphysical concept introductions, and plenty of thought-provoking questions — joint development on allocated land, the handling of urban garages, the nature of educational land, and so on — dissecting for us, from the perspective of property rights, every aspect of these problems and the limitations of each solution. For the dean's classes, I felt that if you listened attentively, you didn't need to review; sure enough, the final exam had nothing requiring rote memorization. But the dean's classes had one serious problem (of course, this was my problem): I couldn't keep up with his pace and often couldn't understand his questions, and like the department chair's classes, there was a high-pressure aura.

Beyond the courses there were many lectures, and conveniently, the transport was easy. The 403 Art Center, similar to Beijing's 798, is a symbol of urban industrial gentrification. The 403 Mansion bookstore occasionally held lectures and meet-and-greets; there I heard a dialogue between Yu Xiuhua and Ms. Wei Tianzhen, with the bald Mr. Wei Tianwu taking photos to the side and the poet Zhang Zhihao sitting in the audience. Once there was a Milk Coffee meet-and-greet at Mansion, where everyone sang "Hello Tomorrow" and "I Don't Want to Say Goodbye" together, and at the end I got Milk Coffee's autograph, then gazed up at the words "Be in the great storm" on the bookstore's beam. In the winter of 2015, I attended Ms. Tian Yuan's lecture in the School of Journalism and Communication auditorium, sitting in the back rows where there seemed to be no seats left, listening to Guizishan's internet-famous teacher talk about her love story: she and her husband were each other's first loves, and it was even a long-distance relationship. Back when Ms. Tian Yuan went to Xi'an to see him, her academic-star boyfriend studied with her in the library, and she ended up playing with a dictionary by herself all afternoon. In the same auditorium, I also heard Mr. Wang Houxiong's lecture, "How to Become an Excellent Teacher." When Mr. Wang taught at the secondary-school level, he taught both physics and chemistry at the same time, which was truly impressive — yes, this is the same Wang Houxiong who is the chief editor of the "Complete Textbook Interpretation" and "Key and Difficult Points Handbook" series. One winter I also met Feng Tang of "Ten Miles of Spring Breeze"; back then he had not yet invented the term "middle-aged greasiness," and he talked about entrepreneurship and writing. About him, what left the deepest impression was the discussion of vision and water in "Everything Grows."

### 1404, 520, and the Alexander Academy of Sciences

1404 is the class number; Real Estate Management Class 1404 was officially established on February 16, 2016. As graduation approached in my senior year and we had to rehearse a program for the graduation gala, I suddenly realized that I had been class monitor for a long time but had barely participated in any class activities. The first League Day activity was a social with a certain water-supply-and-drainage major from HUST; I had a minor course that Saturday, so I didn't attend, but the League branch secretary Dr. Wang, who also had a minor course, went. Apparently the other side's League branch secretary looked a lot like Zheng Kai, and many of the girls in my class became his little fangirls. In many activities afterward I was quite peripheral: we came in third in the debate competition, with the debate-team members handling the whole thing; we won the championship in the basketball tournament, and all I had to do was deliver water and collect the prize and take photos afterward. In the first semester after the track split, one afternoon department chair Ms. Chen took us to tour the Country Garden development at Huashan; the land was apparently acquired ten years earlier. Ms. Chen chatted with her teacher while a bunch of us underlings listened in — a winding villa district, 600-square-meter fully furnished mansions; I was super envious of the home theaters! A major feature of my class was that it was quite harmonious. While other classes were squabbling and tearing each other apart over choosing advisors for their graduation theses, my class was perfectly serene. One day in the second semester of senior year, while I was still working at Worldunion, I "talked back" to the counselor in the grade group chat over the matter of having a chaperone, and then the former grade-council president jumped out, looking ready for a fight. Because I still had an appraisal report to compile, I didn't bother responding for the moment. I just thought it was a trivial matter — there's always a time in life when you step in poop, except that time it was horse manure. Only after getting off work and returning to the dorm did I learn that WeChat, QQ, and Weibo had already opened fire, guns trained outward, firepower especially concentrated. Later, Brother Nan told me that presenting a united front against outsiders had always been a tradition in real-estate management; even later, the handsome Da of the "Minion" crew said the Class of 2016's real-estate management class was the same. This was probably just the magnetic field of real-estate management. Actually, when I chose real-estate management during the major track split in the winter of 2015, it seemed to be a spur-of-the-moment thing: I was playing ping-pong with Dr. Wang at Youming, we got to talking about majors, and so I chose real-estate management. That casual, hasty decision led to long-lasting good fortune.

520 is the dorm number starting from my junior year. The room didn't change; the original dorm number was 518. On the morning the orientation system opened, I was still tutoring and didn't manage to grab the spot in 520 next door, but I did snag the last opening in 518. This room held many fateful encounters: people who didn't fit would leave, and the right people would come. Hansheng is a remarkable person, a science guy. When I had just added him on QQ, he loved to repost all kinds of status updates and articles, and his space was full of his posts. He was clearly a science guy, but his history was frighteningly good; during our bedtime chats, I just felt like I was a fake humanities student, having studied history for so many years yet remaining utterly impoverished in it. One year Christmas Eve fell on a Friday; the library was closed for retreat and the teaching buildings were sealed off for the postgraduate exam, so the two of us — one a single dog, one a long-distance dog — wandered around with our backpacks looking for a study room, found nowhere to settle, and finally decided to join the bustle at Optics Valley. It seemed to be then that we discovered Optics Valley had an Ali's Dream City offline store. In the summer of 2016, the heavy rains in Wuhan lasted a very, very long time. East 4, an old building said to have been built during the Cultural Revolution, was due for renovation; unable to bear the dampness of West 6, I stayed in East 4 until the very last deadline before moving. During the rainstorms I had classes in the YY Building and got soaked many times a day; at noon and in the evening I would study and eat with Rongrong in the activity room, where the secret knock was "I love studying, studying makes me happy." The heavy rain cleared up on July 7, and that day I went to Changsha. By the time I came back, it was already junior year, the room number had changed to 520, and soon after Rongrong moved in. Rongrong and I had many bedtime chats, sometimes going very, very late — thank goodness Hansheng didn't kill anyone in his sleep. Rongrong is a true academic star with a strong scholarly air, the president of the poetry society; he took me to their Dragon Boat poetry gathering and the poetry gathering at Sunshine Dock, and told me stories about the School of Chinese Language and Literature. Once, before winter break, it snowed heavily and the roads were thick with ice, and Rongrong and I "carefully, slippery floor" made our way to the East Gate to buy little gifts to take home and some Zhou Hei Ya; that night we watched "Devils on the Doorstep" in the dorm and kept up our bedtime chats. I miss the litsea sauce Rongrong shared and the time we spent preparing for the CET-6 in the activity room. In senior year I joked with Rongrong that since he'd won the top scholarship, he had to supply me with Zhou Hei Ya for life; he refused to the death, yet last summer when he came to the Nanjing Library to look up ancient texts, he brought Zhou Hei Ya on his own initiative and gave me a second mousepad, saying he'd supply all my mousepads for life — the most "till-the-end-of-time" promise I received in 2019. Brother Hou only moved in during the final days, but Brother Hou lived right next door and dropped by daily, so he had long been part of our dorm. Before winter break in junior year, Brother Hou went with us to eat at a seafood buffet, and afterward there was Haidilao too. Brother Hou gave me his postcards from Stanford and Berkeley, and for a long time afterward I kept the Berkeley emblem stuck to my desk. The provost said Brother Hou was my Youhei Sunohara, and I think so too — he's the cheerful Brother Hou. After graduation I often talk with Brother Hou on the phone; thank you, Brother Hou, for your patient guidance ~

The original intent in founding the Alexander Academy of Sciences was to establish an academic organization — and indeed, we did discuss academic questions in the group a few times... By chance, in Mr. Zhao Zheng of Beijing Normal University's "The Universe from Einstein to Hawking," I heard that Einstein had named his youthful study group the Olympia Academy, and for the sake of symmetry in grandeur, we established the Alexander Academy of Sciences. At its founding, the academy had three members: the director of the research department, the provost, and the dean. The provost and the director were both extremely clever and kind people; it was as if many wonderful qualities had naturally descended upon them. The director seemed to come with a built-in stealth attribute and was often unreachable — wouldn't answer the phone, wouldn't reply on WeChat. But the director was our study carry-team leader; in advanced math, linear algebra, and probability theory, as well as the wretched accounting, without her carrying me I might have failed. Once, on the way back from class, I got hit by a call from the counselor asking why the real-estate management class hadn't applied for the Outstanding Class Collective award. I was a bit stunned at the time and contacted the director, who said she'd forgotten; I half wanted to destroy her, but on second thought, it really didn't matter. Once on Girls' Day, Mr. Chen Feng had something come up and couldn't make it, so we just studied in the classroom — and the director launched into her brilliant maneuvering, organizing an impromptu program with a few friends, calling on people and singing, and just like that she livened up a whole class period. Really, that was amazing! Later that year on Boys' Day, the few boys in our class also received the care of the director's leadership. The provost was a makeup guru, and reportedly used to have many followers on Weibo. We first met, it seems, when forming a team (cox) for the Kangteng Cup, and somehow we just hit it off, and later we all gathered into the real-estate management class. During the winter of our minor courses, the provost recommended I watch "Clannad," a not-very-long anime that changed me in many, many ways; from then on, Ushio became the name of my daughter. With the provost I formed teams for many competitions and activities, as well as countless course presentations — a teammate I could trust unconditionally. The provost is a very serious and meticulous person, but she had her muddled moments too, like the day of the graduation photo when she missed her early-morning alarm ~ The night the thesis defense ended, the Alexander Academy of Sciences gathered at the vocational college and just sat there chatting simply, able to talk for a very, very long time about everything under the sun, and later at Youming too, making a pact to visit Nanjing when we had time after graduation, to stroll through the Confucius Temple and watch the Fire Cube in Xianlin.

Alexander academy of sciences, forever!

### The Good Eats

In Wuhan, the cheap and affordable hot dry noodles might be found in the cafeterias. It's said that HUST and Wuhan University had hot dry noodles for one yuan twenty and one yuan fifty; Guixiang Garden seemed to have them too, but I forget the price. What I ate most were the hot dry noodles from Chuannan on the second floor of East 1 — hot dry noodles with egg, no chili. This was probably the breakfast of choice for many people heading to the hall for duty or to Han Street for work. At the Chuannan window you could add pickled cowpea and cilantro, which was simply delightful. I also tried the hot dry noodles from Cailinji outside, and they weren't good; setting aside the price, much higher than at school, the taste really wasn't good. Actually, the first time Hansheng took me to eat hot dry noodles, I felt a bit like throwing up after finishing — the sesame paste in hot dry noodles is really dry once mixed in, and I'd recommend pairing it with soy milk. But later, seeing so many classmates picking at their hot dry noodles while hurrying to class, you come to discover its magic.

The best fish noodles, rice rolls, and braised chicken were also in the cafeterias. The rice rolls were at Guixiang Garden, one of a kind. There was a vacancy in my senior year, and on many evenings after finishing duty in the hall, in no hurry to eat, I would stroll from Guizhong Road to Guixiang Garden a little after six and occasionally see Dean Deng. Guixiang Garden's rice rolls were very special: they weren't cut but stacked together like a head of cabbage, the egg and ham were really cheap, and the marvelous part was the sauce in the little dish, better than many places I'd tried outside. Fish noodles were available at Xuezi, East 2, and Guixiang Garden; Guixiang Garden was too crowded and Xuezi's flavor fell a bit short, so what I ate most were East 2's — the leftmost window on the second floor, with five-grain fish noodles, tomato meatballs, shredded and minced pork, and chitterlings, really delicious, large portions and only half the price of outside. Boya Garden's signature dish was of course the braised chicken, and it seems in senior year there was another magical maneuver where the price dropped from 14 yuan per serving to 13 yuan per serving. Braised pork ribs, braised tofu — but I still liked the braised chicken best, and the same place's beef hotpot was great too. The braised chicken usually required queuing; after taking a number you'd find a quiet corner to sit down, and you'd often get an eyeful of couples showing off their affection. Boya Garden's braised chicken had no potatoes mixed in; instead it had vermicelli, tofu skin, and bok choy, with self-serve chili sauce, paired with rice that had little corn kernels in it — "a feast for the eyes, nose, and tongue" was pretty much like this.

![image-20220320140315246](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image/img/image-20220320140315246.png)

The cafeterias were close to the dorm area, and within a single day there seemed to be no clearly defined meal periods; many windows stayed open for a long time, and whether you went at nine or ten in the morning or three or four in the afternoon, you'd find plenty of windows serving food, and the same went for after nine at night. Sometimes after evening class, I'd go to Shanxi Qiaoxifu for noodles, and there was also the malatang at East 1 and Xuezi that was neither spicy nor numbing nor scalding. Oh, and there was the Southeast Gate, where the little Decadent Street was a wonderful supplement to the cafeterias — a small stall run by a couple from Zhejiang selling fried rice noodles and fried noodles; the Huizhou soup-dumpling stall also sold all sorts of congee — the congee was delicious and the soup dumplings were delicious, and if you cozied up to the owner and said you were from Anhui, you'd most likely be given a free soup dumpling. And across the way there was Creativity City, where I went many times to Haidilao and the Pang Brothers crab pot — adding extra chicken feet to the chicken-feet pot was truly perfect.

![image-20220320140333277](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image/img/image-20220320140333277.png)

Miyi — I first came to know it through the posts of Brother Xian and Onion; the shop below East 16 was facing the risk of relocation and closure. The first thing I tried was its signature panda milk-cap tea, and at the shop below East 16, taking a shameless sip before picking it up was a hard rule, which was no small challenge for an iron-blooded straight guy like me. The thick milk cap paired with black tea, sprinkled with Oreo dirt (the name Miss Rabbit gave it); when the black tea was nearly gone, you'd use a spoon to mix the milk cap and Oreo dirt and eat it. So, strictly speaking, the essence of panda milk-cap tea as a milk tea lies not in the taste of drinking it but in the "eating." Later Miyi moved to the second floor of the little woods in the East District, still a small storefront, the little room decorated with point cards and postcards covered in messages from all over the world; couples would chat, and students would play Gomoku. During this time, Miyi released their supreme work, Zhou Xue, which utterly crushed Nayuki's and Heytea's overbearing cheese-strawberry and cheesy berry-berry. Zhou Xue was so popular that by the time I came back from work to ask, it was already sold out. Sometimes I got lucky and bought one after work; after eating noodles at Xuezi, I'd shoulder my laptop and find a classroom in Building 8 to study in, quietly reading literature together with my Zhou Xue, and I'd feel a deep sense of satisfaction. Perhaps because Guizishan had condensed too much literary and artistic flavor, many of Miyi's drinks had affected names — after Zhou Xue they released Hao Mang and Xihuan Ni, and adding the Xia Zi I discovered not long ago, Miyi's Mount Rushmore was complete. Good rain knows its season, and good tea is the same; in the late spring and early summer of Guizishan, Zhou Xue, Hao Mang, and Xihuan Ni were all seasonal drinks. Now the main shop has moved to the ground floor of Block 4 of the International Exchange complex, the owner has already opened branches at Huazhong Agricultural University and Wuhan University of Technology, and I believe opening all over Wuhan's seven universities won't be a problem. Ps: On the little stool at Miyi's East District shop is written, "Big Doggo!"

### Novel Encounters

#### Kangteng Public Judge

Cox was probably the first competition team I joined in undergrad; its name came from the place where the team first met, the tiny café on the first floor of the School of Music. Although we didn't advance in the Kangteng Cup, we did go to the finals to be part of the public jury — in fact, for three years running I served as a public judge for Kangteng, watching contestants from all over show off their talents in the Wuhan University Humanities Hall. The one that left the deepest impression was the team from Sichuan University; they were really impressive. While most teams took the conventional route, the Sichuan University team played out a role-play from "Boonie Bears," turning a case analysis vividly into a humorous skit.

#### Cycling the East Lake, Riding Bikes with Dr. Wang

At some point a greenway was built around the East Lake, extending from the East Gate of Wuhan University to Moshan and connecting through to the OCT. One afternoon everyone made plans to go cycling — the provost and the director, Sister Xia, Brother Chenyang, and Brother Lei. Brother Hou was apparently busy with his romance at the time and wouldn't come no matter how we urged him over the phone. At the entrance to the greenway, something remarkable happened: our all-capable Dr. Wang turned out not to know how to ride a bike, so the ride added a new task — teaching Dr. Wang to ride. The whole group fussed over it the entire way and in the end still had to take a taxi back. One evening not long after, we went for a walk on the campus across the way, on the 912 athletic field, and in that very short span of time, Dr. Wang learned to ride a bike!

#### Brother Hou Takes Us on a Spring Outing

It must have been spring, or the changeover from spring to summer, when the weather was gradually warming up. One afternoon Brother Hou got into his little white Audi and took us to tour the Wuhan Zoo. It was a really run-down zoo — grubby giant pandas, Antarctic penguins about the size of ducks, and all sorts of parrots. The place we lingered longest that day was watching big bears play in the water and fight, which used up a fair bit of phone memory. Brother Hou drove very steadily, and passing over the Yingwuzhou Bridge, the view was wonderful — the very picture of "great rivers, great lakes, great Wuhan." I remember Ms. Dai Lu saying in class: "Clear and distinct are the Hanyang trees in the sunlit river; lush and verdant is the grass on Parrot Isle."

Ps: Brother Hou's little white Audi is the same model as Ms. Dai Lu's.

#### Shuttling Through the Streets

I didn't like staying in the dorm; I longed to stumble upon special secrets in unexpected moments on the streets of Wuhan. I went to many places alone. One afternoon in my freshman year I went to see the Yellow Crane Tower, but as luck would have it the tower was under renovation; not wanting to head back, I didn't go to nearby Hubu Alley but instead walked toward the far bank of the river. The Wuhan Yangtze River Bridge is said to have been built with Soviet aid, an integrated road-rail bridge, with a railway beneath the car lanes and a ferry terminal below that. Walking from Wuchang to Hanyang, the railing and handrail of the bridge's pedestrian walkway were covered in graffiti and edgy, angsty teenage writing; I have no idea of the age or inner monologue of those who wrote it at the time. During the summer of my junior year, I'd study in the hall during the day and ride out for a walk around dusk, not too far — a dozen kilometers, sometimes a few dozen. At the foot of Luojia Mountain I met an old man practicing calligraphy on the grid-pattern bricks with a brush dipped in water; I left my footprints at the national key laboratory and experimental fields at the foot of Lion Mountain, took photos, imagined the lives of the scientists inside, and cast in the admiration of a science idiot; on the streets at the foot of Nanwang Mountain beside the Wuhan Botanical Garden, at night you could see the long string of streetlights along the East Lake greenway, the lights at the center of the lake stretching far into the distance. A certain senior in my Moments queued up at dawn to buy breakfast for his fiancée on Liangdao Street; the photo had a tempting flavor to it, which suddenly sparked my curiosity and gave me the motivation to investigate. While working at Worldunion, one day a house-viewing appointment was scheduled for early morning; I reached the client's home a little after six, and after handling things I found that Liangdao Street was not far away, so the idea of "early eats" arose. I've forgotten what I ordered — probably some very greasy upscale shaomai — and there really were a lot of people queuing, and just like that, I checked it off.

#### My Secret Bases

Guizishan was really too crowded, and there really weren't many study rooms, but everyone could still find their own spot: some became sculptures in the library, some were the soul of the teaching-building study rooms, and many others prepared for exams in the cafeterias — while I had two secret bases. For many a final-exam season, after sorting out my exam materials, I needed a place to recite them. The elevator lobby on the fourth floor of the library, near the seminar rooms, became my first secret base; here I recited many materials, the most memorable being the head-spinning "Real Estate Law." Secret base number two was on the fourth floor of the Student Affairs Hall. Back then I was on duty in the hall, and on the half-days I wasn't scheduled I'd recite upstairs — a tiny spot, the platform outside the door of the Model UN. Most people going into the Wenhua Gong Shulin to handle business at the hall would only go up to the third floor, so the fourth-floor platform became an undisturbed, quiet place.

#### Claiming the Cherry Blossom Castle

"To watch a crush of crowds at Wuhan University is the dream of every cherry blossom." In March and April, Wuhan University is truly beautiful and truly crowded. To my regret, in four years in Wuhan, I never properly went to see the cherry blossoms; even the homebody Hansheng went once with Brother Hu through the online reservation channel. But fate always has its moments of unexpected encounter. Once a minor course was rescheduled, the class location moved from the Information Science campus to the Law School, so I had to get up early and cross most of Wuhan University to get to class. Following Meiyuan 2nd Road, I arrived around a little after seven at the intersection of WHU's School of Public Administration and the administration building, with great swaths of cherry blossoms beside the road, no pedestrians and no vehicles — at the flowering season you see only blossoms and no leaves, an eyeful of beauty. This was perhaps the perfect reward for rising early.

<br/>

It's true that Wuhan is not a gentle city, yet this city of eleven million people, in its own unique way, recounts its character and its past to everyone who has stayed there, and together they shape history. In these recent magical times, people pin their hopes on hot dry noodles, the songs sing of "the wind at Jiedaokou," Han Street and Qintai — as familiar place names flash by one after another, I finally understand that a sense of reality is the most important thing Wuhan gave me.

I want to find time in March or April to go to Wuhan University and properly watch the cherry blossoms; I miss my early-summer Zhou Xue, Xihuan Ni, and Hao Mang; and I absolutely must go ride the ferry I have yet to take.

World, do you hear me?
