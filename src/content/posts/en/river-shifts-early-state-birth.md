> Where does government (the state) come from? Debates abound. This paper explores the **cooperative theory** and the **extractive theory** of government origins, testing both through a natural experiment: the shifting of ancient rivers in southern Iraq. The authors find that when a sudden river shift made private irrigation untenable, a need for public canals and coordination emerged in the local area, thereby fostering state formation. This aligns with the cooperative theory and contradicts Marxist-inflected theories emphasizing elite predation. Moreover, river shifts also led to the provision of public goods (such as canals and defensive walls), the payment of tribute, and an increase in governmental administrative capacity, demonstrating that early government arose primarily from a need for cooperation to address **public affairs**.

## Theories on the Origins of the State

Theories concerning the origins of the state and government broadly fall into two camps: **cooperative theories** and **extractive theories** (also known as “social contract theory” vs. “conquest/extraction theory”):

- **Cooperative origin theories** view government as a cooperative organization that provides public goods and services. Their intellectual origins trace back to social contract thinkers like Hobbes, Locke, and Rousseau. According to Hobbes (1651), government, via a social contract, provides the public good of **peace** in the anarchic “state of nature” to prevent universal conflict. Citizens willingly relinquish some power to the government in return for peace and security, effectively “entrusting” the governement with sovereign power. Locke (1689) further emphasizes that the government’s power is constrained by the contract, its primary duties limited to necessary public matters such as dispute resolution. Generally, this school of thought sees individuals voluntarily banding together to form a government to correct “market failures” or collective action problems, providing public goods that the private sector cannot efficiently provide (classical economic theorists like Baumol (1952) and Samuelson (1954) also highlight the government’s role in public goods provision and externality correction). In other words, the cooperative theory emphasizes that members of society, out of **common interest** (such as security, water management, etc.), "contract" a government.
- **Extractive origin theories** posit that government arises from internal social power imbalances, where a powerful minority elite establishes governing institutions through conquest, violence, or exploitation in order to capture resources and control the population. The Marxist tradition views the state as an instrument of the ruling class for oppressing the ruled classes, emphasizing its emergence as the coercive agency necessary for maintaining **property ownership over the means of production** and the **exploitation of the labour masses.** Similarly, Olson (1993) offered the “roving bandits” theory, proposing that predators, to maximize long-term extraction benefits, will settle down and become “stationary bandits,” thus forming a state. In economic history and anthropology, such theories are also called “coercive theories” or “conflict models,” exemplified by archaeologist Carneiro (1970)’s emphasis on warfare and conquest arising from population pressure and competition for bounded lands leading to the state, and Service (1975)’s distinction of 'voluntary association' and 'coercive subjugation' origin models for states. To summarize, extractive theories stress the establishment of government by an elite through **coercive means**. The provision of public goods is only a secondary outcome of the elite’s bargain with the populace. For instance, elites may provide some public goods ex post-facto just to bolster their dominance—see e.g. Acemoglu & Robinson (2000).

Addressing these two theories, the paper encapsulates the former as “demand-driven” (the case social needs conduce states) and the latter as “extractive-driven” (elite grabs give rise to states) —and devises an empirical analysis aimed at distinguishing them.
