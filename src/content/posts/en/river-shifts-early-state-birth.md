> Where does government (the state) come from? The debate is wide-ranging. This paper examines the **cooperative theory** and the **predatory theory** of government origins, testing both through a natural experiment: the river shifts of ancient southern Iraq. The authors find that when a river suddenly changed course and made private irrigation unsustainable, a demand emerged locally for public canals and coordination, thereby fostering state formation. This is consistent with the cooperative theory and runs counter to Marxist theories emphasizing elite predation. Moreover, river shifts also led to the provision of public goods (such as canals and defensive walls), the payment of tribute, and an increase in governmental administrative capacity, demonstrating that early government arose primarily from the need to cooperate in solving **public affairs**.

## Theories on the Origins of the State

Theories concerning the origins of the state and government fall broadly into two camps: **cooperative theories** and **predatory theories** (also known as "social contract theory" vs. "conquest/predation theory"):

- **Cooperative origin theories** view government as a cooperative organization that provides public goods and services. Their intellectual roots can be traced back to social contract thinkers such as Hobbes, Locke, and Rousseau. According to Hobbes (1651), government, through a social contract, provides the public good of **peace** in the anarchic "state of nature," so as to avoid universal mutual conflict. To obtain peace and security, citizens are willing to cede part of their power to the government, thereby "entrusting" the government with the exercise of sovereign authority. Locke (1689) further emphasizes that the government's power is constrained by the contract, with its principal duties limited to necessary public matters such as dispute resolution. In general, this school holds that individuals voluntarily band together to form a government in order to correct "market failures" or collective action problems and to provide public goods that the private sector cannot efficiently supply (classical economic theory, such as Baumol (1952) and Samuelson (1954), likewise stresses the government's role in supplying public goods and correcting externalities). In other words, the cooperative theory emphasizes that members of society "contract" a government into being out of **common interest** (such as security, water management, and so on).
- **Predatory origin theories** hold that government arises from imbalances of power within society, where a powerful minority elite establishes governing institutions through conquest, violence, or exploitation in order to seize resources and control the masses. The Marxist tradition views the state as an instrument by which the ruling class oppresses the ruled class, emphasizing that the state emerges as a coercive force organized by rulers in order to maintain their **ownership of the means of production** and their **exploitation** of the laboring masses. Similarly, Olson's (1993) theory of "roving bandits" proposes that predators, in order to maximize the long-run gains from exploitation, will settle down and become "stationary bandits," thereby forming a state. In economic history and anthropology, such theories are also called "coercion theories" or "conflict models"; for example, the archaeologist Carneiro (1970) emphasizes that warfare and conquest driven by population pressure and territorial competition give rise to the state, while Service (1975) distinguishes two models of state origin, "voluntary association" and "coercive subjugation." In sum, predatory theories stress that elites establish government through **coercive means**, and that the provision of public goods is merely a secondary outcome of the game played between elites and the populace (for instance, elites may provide certain public services after the fact only in order to consolidate their rule; see Acemoglu & Robinson, 2000).

In response to these two theories, the paper summarizes the former as "demand-driven" (social needs give rise to government) and the latter as "predation-driven" (elite extraction gives rise to government), and designs an empirical study to test them. The authors point out that if the **cooperative theory** holds, then government should appear **where there are coordination problems that need solving**; conversely, if the **predatory theory** holds, government is more likely to appear **where resources are abundant and easy to extract**. This insight lays the foundation for the empirical design: find a setting in which the geographic distribution of cooperative demand and of predatory incentives diverges. The case the authors choose is river shifts in the irrigation-agriculture societies of ancient Mesopotamia, because a river shift causes certain areas to suddenly lose their water source (sharply increasing cooperative demand, while the surplus available for elite extraction actually declines), thus making it possible to distinguish the two theories.

## Historical Background and Research Design

#### **Geographic and Climatic Background**

The case study site of this paper is ancient southern Mesopotamia, that is, the alluvial plain of the Tigris-Euphrates valley in present-day southern Iraq. This region is part of the "Fertile Crescent," with a total area of about 30,000 square kilometers (roughly the size of Belgium or the U.S. state of Vermont). Geologically, this plain was formed by the long-term alluvial deposition of the Euphrates and Tigris rivers; its terrain is extremely low and flat and its rainfall scant, so much so that it has historically been entirely unsuited to rain-fed agriculture. Around 6000 BCE, most of the region was still a wetland and bay (delta) environment composed of high-water rivers and the sea, where humans subsisted mainly by fishing, hunting, and gathering wetland resources. Archaeological evidence indicates that before 4000 BCE, human settlements in southern Iraq were sparse and very small in scale. However, around 3500 BCE a series of major **environmental changes** occurred: the global climate grew drier, rainfall plummeted, the sea level of the Persian Gulf fell, river flows declined while riverbeds silted up and rose, and large tracts of formerly marshy land dried out. This climatic event transformed the ecology of southern Mesopotamia from a "land of waters" into an arid desert. From an agricultural standpoint, before roughly 3000 BCE only the narrow strips immediately adjacent to the river channels could be cultivated in the south; as the climate grew drier, maintaining agriculture in areas beyond the banks of the great rivers required building irrigation canals to channel river water toward the dry land. In other words, climate change made **irrigation** a necessary condition for the survival of local agriculture.

#### **Social Organization Background**

Before the early climatic changes (roughly before 3500 BCE), southern Mesopotamia had not yet developed governing organizations beyond the scale of the settlement. According to archaeological and historical research, the basic organizational unit of society in this period was the extended family or kin-based clan (extended lineage). These **clans** were composed of the descendants of a common ancestor, generally clustered into villages or neighborhoods and self-governing internally. Within the clan, elders and lineage heads mediated disputes and organized public affairs, often deciding important matters through consultation in the form of a clan assembly. For example, in the absence of state authority, various public goods (such as small irrigation ditches, public order, and so on) were provided and maintained mainly within the clan or neighborhood. The archaeologist Ur (2014) notes that even after the state later emerged, these basic clan functions continued to operate within their respective spheres. The rise of **cities** was an important step in the development of social organization in southern Mesopotamia: around 4000 BCE, **towns** formed from the fusion of multiple clans (such as Uruk) began to appear in the region, marking the concentration of population and the increasing complexity of society. Some cities developed governance structures transcending the clan level, such as citizen assemblies or temple economies managed by priest-leaders (Emberling, 2015). On the whole, however, until around 3000 BCE, governing entities in the sense of "states" had still not formed, and the coordination of public affairs in most communities remained at the self-governing level within the clan or the city. The American archaeologist Robert McC. Adams called this stage the era of "autonomous villages" and "temple city-states," a time when no large cross-clan polity yet existed.

#### **River Shifts as a Natural Experiment**

The hydrological characteristics of southern Mesopotamia provided the authors with ideal experimental conditions. The Euphrates and Tigris rivers changed course many times over historical periods. Research shows that since 5000 BCE these two rivers underwent six major shift events, shaping the rivers' present courses. A **river shift** is usually triggered by extreme upstream rainfall: when heavy rains in the highlands of Turkey and Syria cause the rivers to surge, the enormous flood breaks through the natural levees of the existing channel and cuts a new course across the plain, thereby rewriting the river's path. Such shifts often occur suddenly within a matter of weeks and are, for local people, an almost entirely exogenous shock. The first major shift examined in this paper occurred around 2850 BCE. Before that, archaeological reconstruction indicates that the Euphrates and Tigris had not yet divided to flow into the sea as they do today, but were merged into a single stream (sometimes called the "Ur River") crossing the plain. Over the long ages this main channel continuously silted up and rose, until at some point it breached its banks and changed course.

The **direct effect** of the shift was that areas formerly along the old channel lost their proximity to water: with irrigation lacking, the land became difficult to cultivate, and the farmers who had settled there faced a livelihood crisis. Historical records and archaeological materials show that some farmers, after the channel dried up, were forced to abandon farming and turn to nomadic herding. But within this paper's study area, there was another possibility: local communities could **cooperate** to dig artificial canals, channeling the remaining river water to land far from the new channel, in order to "re-irrigate" the dried-up fields. However, building large canals was far beyond what a single clan or family could accomplish; it required cross-community coordination and cooperation. Without an authority transcending the individual clans to organize it, such a large cross-community public works project would be very hard to carry through (because of "coordination problems" and issues of mutual trust). Therefore, for areas that had lost their river water, a river shift would significantly raise the demand for "**government**": only by establishing a higher-level organization (such as a cross-clan governing body) could labor be mobilized and resources allocated to build canals and re-irrigate the farmland.

Conversely, from the standpoint of the predatory theory, a river shift had the opposite effect on the incentives of the **elite**: the receding water source reduced agricultural output and shrank the surplus grain or tax revenue that could be levied, which in fact **weakened** the returns to a bandit-type elite from establishing a polity. If an ambitious ruler wanted to extract wealth, he would more likely go to a prosperous area where the water still remained, rather than stay in a place that had lost its water to establish a polity. Therefore:

- The cooperative theory predicts: states will appear preferentially in areas that, after a river shift, are water-poor but need **cooperative water management**;
- The predatory theory predicts: states will tend to appear in areas with **abundant water** and rich resources available for extraction (such as where the river did not shift, or where the new channel lies).

More importantly, historical river shifts were variable across **space** and **time**, and did not affect the entire region simultaneously. The Euphrates often breached and changed course in one stretch while other stretches retained their original flow. This means that in the same period, "shocked areas" (where the channel had receded) and "unshocked areas" (where the channel remained in place) coexisted on the plain. Taking the first shift around 2850 BCE as an example, archaeological reconstruction shows that the Euphrates shifted in its northern stretch at the time, while the southern river network temporarily remained unchanged. This created a natural control-experiment environment: we can compare the differences in change before and after the shift between **shifted areas** and **non-shifted areas**, and thereby identify the causal effect of river change on state formation—this is essentially a "difference-in-differences" (DID) analytical framework. Because the shift was triggered by a distant climatic event, and because archaeological analysis found no systematic correlation between where the shift occurred and the prosperity of human settlement before the shift, it can be regarded with reasonable confidence as an exogenous shock, rather than the result of human factors or endogenous evolution.

## Data and Methods

#### **Construction of the Archaeological Data**

The paper draws on a newly compiled archaeological panel dataset covering sociopolitical information on the southern Iraqi plain from 3900 BCE to 2700 BCE. The authors divide the study area into **5x5-kilometer** grid cells, totaling about 1,374 cells (as shown in Figure 1(d)). Each grid cell in each archaeological period serves as one observation. Archaeologists, on the basis of pottery styles and the like, partitioned this span into five consecutive sub-periods, each averaging about 240 years. From numerous archaeological survey reports and historical documents, the authors collected and organized data on the following main variables:

- **Settlements and cities:** whether a human settlement existed in each cell in each period, and the settlement's size. If a cell contained an archaeological site in that period, "settlement present" was recorded; if the site reached city scale (for example, if the site is known by a historical name or has a relatively large area), it was recorded as a "city." These data come from archaeological surveys' classification of sites. Summary statistics show that, on average, the sample has 0.32 settlements per cell per period, and about 7% of cells are regarded as cities.
- **The state polity:** whether each cell in each period fell under the governing domain of some **"state."** Here a "state" refers to an early polity in which a central city governed the surrounding countryside, equivalent to a "city-state" or early state. Based on archaeological and historical records, the authors compiled a list of all known states between 3900 and 2700 BCE together with their spatial extents. If a cell lay within a state's territory (usually in the hinterland adjacent to its capital city), it was recorded as "under state rule" in that period. On average, when there was no river-shift shock, only about 6% of cells belonged to a state polity. This is one of the key dependent variables, used to measure "state formation."
- **Public goods provision:** the authors selected irrigation canals and defensive works as representatives of public goods. The canal data come from regional archaeological mapping, which reconstructed the locations of the irrigation canal network in different periods. For each cell in each period, they recorded whether a canal passed through (or irrigation covered) it. Defensive works refer to the defensive walls built by cities; the authors collected all archaeologically confirmed wall constructions and their dates. Since walls were usually built around cities, this indicator in fact reflects whether city-level public goods were provided.
- **Tribute and state capacity:** to measure changes in society's payment of resources to government and in government's capacity to extract, the authors constructed two kinds of indicators. First, whether there is evidence that tribute was paid to rulers. Since written records appeared only around 3200 BCE, the authors used cuneiform tablet texts to gather records of tribute. They organized **5,885** published cuneiform tablet documents and searched for whether terms denoting tribute payment, such as "tribute" or "tax grain," appeared in them. If a city's tablet records in a given period contained tribute-related entries, the city was deemed to have "tribute payment present" in that period. Second, "state capacity" is approximated by the number of large public buildings constructed by the government, including temples, palaces, and ziggurats. The authors built a new database of such public-building remains, tallying the number of major buildings newly added by each major city during the study period in order to observe changes in the government's capacity to mobilize resources.

#### **Econometric Identification Strategy**

Based on the data above, the authors use a difference-in-differences method to estimate the causal effect of river shifts on the various sociopolitical indicators. Specifically, they treat grid cells "adjacent to a shifted river stretch" as the treatment group and grid cells "adjacent to an unshifted river stretch" as the control group, and compare the difference in outcomes between the two groups **before and after the shift**. Here the "river shift" event is defined using a **5-kilometer** threshold: if there was a river within 5 kilometers of a cell's center point in the previous period but not in the current period (the channel having receded), then that cell is defined as having experienced a "river-shift shock" in that period. The first major shift occurred around 2850 BCE, so for most cells it falls in the middle of the study period (roughly at the boundary between the fourth and fifth sub-periods). The model generally includes grid fixed effects (controlling for time-invariant features of each location) and period fixed effects (controlling for region-wide temporal trends), in order to isolate the net effect of the shift on the treatment group. To observe the dynamic effects directly, the authors also conducted an event-study analysis.

#### **Identification Assumptions and Validity**

The key assumption that the paper's identification strategy must satisfy is that the timing and location of river shifts are **independent** of other factors affecting state formation. To this end, the authors provide evidence on several fronts:

- **The location of shifts is uncorrelated with the human settlement pattern before the shift:** by tallying pre-period indicators such as population density and number of settlements near the shifted stretch, they found no systematic difference between the treatment and control groups before the shift. This means that shifts were not more likely to occur where there were more or fewer people, ruling out the possibility that "settlement activity caused the channel to shift."
- **No significant pre-shift trend:** the event-study results show that in the several periods before the shift occurred, the difference between the treatment and control groups in outcomes such as the appearance of a state was not significant.
- **The shift was triggered by external climate:** the authors cite climatological research showing that flooding of the Euphrates in this period was highly correlated with rainfall on the Anatolian plateau (and bore little relation to local factors). They found that the first shift event coincided with a phase of highly anomalous fluctuation in upstream rainfall. This suggests the shift originated from an external natural shock, and that other locally simultaneous shocks (such as war or technological change) are unlikely to have coincided with it.

In sum, these arguments allow river shifts to be treated as **exogenous** events satisfying "approximate randomness," thereby providing a basis for causal inference via DID. Of course, the authors also considered the issue of spatial correlation in the standard errors, using the Conley (1999) method to correct the standard errors and ensure the robustness of the inference.

## Empirical Results

#### **Effect on State Formation: The Appearance of New Governments**

First, the study examines whether river shifts fostered the emergence of new state polities. The regression results clearly show that river shifts **significantly raised** the probability of a state appearing in the affected area. For grid cells that experienced a river-shift shock, the probability of being incorporated under state rule increased by **about 14 percentage points**. Relative to the mere 6% of cells belonging to a state before the shift, this increase is quite considerable—nearly more than double the original. This means that many communities that previously had no state jurisdiction successfully formed new states (city-states) within about 200 years of losing their river water. Even more striking, the authors find that this phenomenon was driven mainly by "**the birth of new states**," rather than the territorial expansion of existing states. In other words, it was not that existing neighboring states seized the opportunity to occupy the water-deprived areas, but rather that the local communities organized themselves to establish independent political entities. This runs counter to the scenario predicted by the predatory theory (strong neighbors taking the chance to conquer), and instead fits the picture in the cooperative theory of "communities spontaneously forming a government to solve a common problem."

Panel A shows the event-study estimates of the difference in the probability of "being under state rule" before and after the shift. The vertical axis is the estimated difference relative to the control group, and the horizontal axis is centered on the shift period $t=0$. The results show that before the shift occurred ($t=-3,-2,-1$), the treatment and control groups were almost indistinguishable, with the estimated effect close to 0. After the shift occurred, the treatment group rapidly jumped sharply: at period $t=1$, the difference was about 0.14 and statistically significant. This demonstrates that there was no pre-shift trend, and that the probability of state formation rose markedly after the shift. Panel B further groups the sample by **population density** in a heterogeneity analysis. As can be seen, in high-population-density areas (red line) the probability of a state appearing after the shift surged by about 0.18, whereas in low-population-density areas there was almost no significant increase (only about 0.04). This indicates that communities tend to establish a state only where the population is sufficiently dense and the potential returns to cooperation are high; conversely, in sparsely populated places, after losing the water source people may choose to migrate or abandon agriculture rather than stay and form a state. This finding is consistent with the view of the political anthropologist James Scott (2018): sparse populations more easily "escape" state formation, while high-density communities are more likely to "accept" the state in order to solve a common predicament. On the whole, the dynamic pattern shown in Figure 5 accords with the cooperative-origin hypothesis: the water-management demand brought by the shift gave rise to new state polities.

#### **Public Goods Provision: Irrigation and Defensive Works**

After the river suddenly receded from the former settlements around 2850 BCE, the first significant reaction was a collective doubling-down on the **irrigation system**—the authors use "grid centroid within 5 km of a canal" as the indicator of irrigation coverage, and the coefficient obtained in the difference-in-differences is 0.12. This means that the probability of a shocked area being covered by the artificial canal network rose from a panel mean of 28% to about 40%, an increase of nearly half. Because the shift cut off the natural water-intake channels, farmers could continue to cultivate only by building and maintaining long-distance water-conveyance facilities, and such works exceeded the capacity of a single village, requiring coordination across villages and even across kin groups. The rapid rollout of the irrigation network thus became the most direct public-good evidence for measuring the "**state response**."

Besides irrigation, security and defense also strengthened in step. The authors marked all wall remains in the archaeological materials and used "whether the nearest city had a wall" as the outcome variable. The regression shows that the river's recession raised the probability of having a wall by 11%, whereas before the shock this probability was only 14%. In other words, within a single short archaeological period, the coverage of city-defense construction grew by nearly 80%. A wall is a costly collective investment hard for a single community to complete, and its significant increase indicates that the nascent polity focused not only on productive public goods (irrigation) but also took on the function of safeguarding the security of its residents and granaries, thereby strengthening the interdependence between "government and society."

At this point the paper has verified that in the early stage of state formation, the new government did indeed take on the responsibility of coordinating large-scale projects: **with one hand managing water, with the other managing public order**. This accords with the literature's description of early Mesopotamian states—for example, historians emphasize that water management was an important duty of ancient monarchs, while frequent warfare among city-states also prompted cities to build walls for defense (Postgate, 2017; Ur, 2014). By contrast, if a polity were predatory, we would not expect it to invest effort in building infrastructure in a resource-poor area. Therefore, this evidence further tends to support the **cooperative-origin** view.

#### **Tribute Payment and State Extractive Capacity**

According to the cooperative theory, once the government provides public services, the beneficiary populace may be willing to contribute resources in return (akin to a contract of "taxes in exchange for public goods"). Using tablet-document materials, this paper tests the flow of **tribute**, and finds that river shifts doubled the probability that tribute-payment records appeared in the relevant cities. In other words, in the new states fostered by the shift shock, archaeologists more often find in the tablet documents of that period records of grain, corvée labor, or goods paid to rulers. Since the tablets are mostly official records, this means that the government did indeed obtain more offerings/taxes from the populace at that time. This finding corresponds to the provision of public goods: building canals and walls requires the input of resources, and tribute is precisely the means of providing such resources. Members of society were willing to pay tribute perhaps precisely because they saw the role the government played in water management and defense, and so accepted a certain redistribution of resources.

To carry out the functions described above, the state also had to expand in organizational structure. The authors sum the three categories of administrative buildings—palaces, temples, and ziggurats—as a tangible indicator of "**state capacity**," and find that the river's recession increased the number of administrative buildings in the nearest city by 0.44, whereas the mean before the shift was only 0.70. An increase of more than 60% means that the originally sparse administrative core was significantly densified, providing the manpower and space responsible for collecting tribute, deploying labor, and managing the canals and walls. These grand buildings (especially temples and ziggurats) served as religious and administrative centers in early society, and their construction usually meant that the ruler had to extract a considerable surplus of resources to accomplish it. Thus the surge in public buildings is, from another angle, evidence of the new government's capacity to extract resources and invest them in long-term construction. In short, after a water-deprived area established a government, the state's finances became flush (tribute increased), and what soon appeared on the surface was the prosperous scene of forests of temples and a proliferation of palaces.

It is worth stressing that the rise in tribute and state capacity may superficially resemble the "elite extortion" described by the predatory theory—after all, it too involves the concentration of resources in the hands of rulers. But in the context of this study there is a key difference: resource extraction took place within a **reciprocal framework**, that is, **"the government provides public goods, the populace contributes resources."** This differs from pure violent predation, because tribute payment surged only after the government had effectively solved the survival problem (irrigation) facing the populace. As the authors put it, these results fit the meaning of a "social contract": there is a positive feedback relationship between the provision of public goods and the levying of resources, reflecting a certain degree of healthy interaction between state and society.

## Extrapolation

This section attempts to answer two questions:

- Why, amid cooperative opportunities that seem to exist everywhere, "establishing a state" remained extremely rare;
- Whether the causal mechanism obtained from the Iraqi river shifts holds across broader expanses of time and space.

#### **The Paradox of Scarcity**

Historians have noted that although human societies are full of affairs requiring collective action, cases in which an institutionalized state actually emerged are not common (Scott 2017). This fact is a stern test for any theory of state origins: if a theory claims that "high-return cooperation" gives birth to the state, it must explain why such situations are not ubiquitous. The authors therefore focus on "under what conditions the cooperative demand brought by a river shift is sufficient to make a community stay and establish a state," rather than simply reporting average effects.

First, they divide the sample by the pre-shift "**population externality**": if the settlement density around a grid cell was above the median, the state-building effect was significantly larger (DID = 0.18), whereas in low-density areas there was almost no effect. This means that only when neighboring settlements are numerous enough and potential beneficiaries are sufficiently concentrated is coordinating public irrigation worth the organizational cost. Further, they examine the **relative arrangement** of villages and landforms—if the villages were lined up in a north-south row, a single canal could irrigate multiple villages, and the post-shift state-formation effect was pronounced; if the villages were staggered east-west, requiring multiple independent canals, the effect vanished. Social spatial structure thus determines the returns to coordination, and also determines the necessity of state formation.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250425142413115.png)

The authors then test the difference in potential output of land under irrigation versus rain-fed modes, as well as the effect of riverbed height on the ease of drawing water. The results show that areas with large irrigation gains, and the Euphrates valley with its shallower riverbed and easier water intake, were more likely to see city-states appear; conversely, in places with low returns or high water-intake costs, even if the river receded it was hard to attract residents to organize a state. This shows that, compared with "migrating to the new channel or turning to nomadism," staying to dig canals and build a state was chosen only when the **net benefit** was sufficient to offset the organizational expense.

The heterogeneity results above jointly reveal that establishing a state was not the only feasible path of adaptation. If the shift made cultivation too difficult, people could entirely migrate to areas that still had a river, or abandon settlement and turn to nomadism. Only at the intersection of concentrated population, accommodating terrain, and ample returns did the "state" become the optimal solution. This also explains why, throughout prehistory and antiquity, although the Middle Eastern plains underwent river shifts many times, the cases that actually left behind enduring organized polities can be counted on one's fingers.

#### **The Extended Panel**

To test whether the mechanism still held after "states already existed," the authors extend the panel to 5000 BCE–1950 CE, covering six river shifts. In these later events, the study can no longer observe "the appearance of new states," but it can examine public goods provision. The results show that, whether in the Babylonian or the Assyrian era, river shifts still significantly raised the probability of building canals locally (+0.11), accounting for a quarter of the full-sample mean, indicating that even after the state had taken shape, the government would still increase its investment in public goods in the shocked areas, thereby maintaining its reciprocal relationship with the community.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250425142705551.png)

On the whole, the authors' findings exhibit **conditional generalizability**: the chain of river shift → rising returns to cooperation → state formation is not universally inevitable, but depends on a series of threshold variables such as population size, settlement geometry, land returns, and the cost of water works. When these thresholds are met, even in early societies without central authority, communities tend to establish city-states by extending their existing kin structures; when the thresholds are not met, people are more likely to choose migration or nomadism. By revealing these boundary conditions, the paper not only answers "why states are rare," but also provides operational criteria for extending the cooperative theory of state origins to other civilizations.

#### **How the City-State Operated Internally After Its Birth**

In this section, the authors turn their attention to "how the city-state operated internally after its birth." Using the 5,885 already-transliterated Sumerian cuneiform tablets, they conduct a keyword search of the linguistic clues to state administrative activity. Because these tablets all came from government scribes, and their contents are almost entirely records of resource collection, redistribution, and project scheduling, changes in word frequency can directly reflect the organizational structure and functional evolution of the early state.

The analysis first focuses on the frequency of appearance of two kinds of leader titles: **lugal** (literally "great man," usually translated as "king") and the more general **gal** ("clan head/chief"). Before the river shift, the word lugal is almost nowhere to be found in the tablets; after the shift, the proportion of mentions of lugal rose sharply and remained stable, while the appearance of gal, though present both before and after, rose by about 40% after state formation. This is interpreted as follows: the nascent city-state "scaled up" the hierarchical system that had originally operated only within the clan to the cross-clan level, with lugal serving as the state-level "grand clan head" and gal corresponding to the grassroots chiefs of the various kin groups.

![](https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/gh/yuzhangnju/image2025/image-20250425141935716.png)

Next, the authors examine the tablets' references to terms related to **canal** and **tribute**. The appearance rate of both was extremely low before the shift, and increased sharply after the state was established: the mentions of canals confirm the government's central role in cross-settlement water coordination, while the first large-scale appearance of tribute indicates that the provision of public goods and the levying of resources had formed a reciprocal cycle. This corroborates the macro findings in the earlier econometric results of "rising probability of irrigation" and "doubled tribute records," and also shows that the administrative archives and the material remains are highly consistent in their narrative.

The authors then put the tablet word frequencies, by city-period unit, back into the DID equation, and find that the "lugal," "canal," and "tribute" variables jumped significantly in treated cities; by contrast, cities that were not hit by the shift shock and still settled along the river lacked any synchronous change. From this, the paper proposes a micro-picture of cooperative governance: clans that had originally each governed themselves, in the face of the large irrigation problem caused by the shift, extended the familiar elder-clan-head hierarchy into a state architecture of "king–clan head–household"; the government's foremost task was to redistribute water across clans, resolve disputes, and organize corvée labor, while tribute served as the economic glue holding this new system together.

In sum, this section grounds the macro-econometric inference in micro textual evidence, depicting a picture of "the state as an enlarged clan": lugal replaced the single-clan chief as the supra-clan supreme arbiter; gal still played a coordinating role within each kin group; and government archives began to frequently record large-scale water works and tribute, marking the formal commencement of operation of the public-goods–fiscal reciprocity mechanism. These findings support the "cooperation-reciprocity" theory of state origins put forward by the authors at the outset, emphasizing that the state was an institutional innovation generated on top of the existing social structure in order to meet coordination demands at a larger scale.

## Conclusion

- **River shifts prompted the birth of new states:** the channel shift of 2850 BCE raised the probability of a shocked grid cell being incorporated into a city-state by 14 percentage points, and almost all of this came from newly established states rather than the expansion of old ones, showing that establishing a state was a direct response to cooperative demand;
- **Public goods and tribute rose in step:** in these new states, irrigation canal coverage increased by 12 percentage points and walls by 11 percentage points, and the probability of tribute records doubled, indicating that a reciprocal exchange formed between government and residents, rather than one-way predation;
- **The threshold for state-building is clear:** cooperative states appeared only in areas that, before the shift, had dense populations, high irrigation returns, and settlement layouts convenient for sharing a canal; where conditions were insufficient, people were more likely to migrate or turn to nomadism, which explains the historical scarcity of states.

To sum up in one sentence: river shifts triggered the birth of early Sumerian city-states by raising the returns to inter-settlement cooperation, accompanied by public goods provision, tribute exchange, and the expansion of administrative structure; this causal chain was activated only when population-geography conditions were met, and it recurred many times in later ages, providing solid archaeological and econometric evidence for the cooperative theory of state origins.
